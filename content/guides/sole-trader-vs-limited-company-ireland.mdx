A sole trader is the simplest business structure in Ireland with no formation costs, while a limited company offers liability protection but requires CRO registration and annual filings. Most Irish businesses earning under EUR 40,000 to EUR 50,000 choose sole trader; above that, the corporation tax rate of 12.5% often makes a limited company more tax-efficient.

Both structures are straightforward to establish, and you can start as a sole trader and incorporate later as your business grows. This guide breaks down the key differences in tax, liability, costs, and compliance so you can make an informed decision.

## What Is a Sole Trader

A sole trader is the simplest business structure in Ireland. You and your business are legally the same entity. There is no formal registration with the Companies Registration Office (CRO) required -- you simply register for tax with the Revenue Commissioners and begin trading.

As a sole trader, you report your business income on your annual **Form 11** income tax return. You are personally responsible for all business debts and liabilities. Setting up takes a matter of days and costs nothing beyond any business name registration fee if you trade under a name other than your own.

Most freelancers, contractors, tradespeople, and small service providers in Ireland start as sole traders.

## What Is a Limited Company

A limited company is a separate legal entity from its owners (shareholders) and directors. It must be registered with the **Companies Registration Office (CRO)** and is governed by the Companies Act 2014.

The company has its own tax obligations, files its own tax returns (**Form CT1** for corporation tax), and maintains its own bank account. Directors have specific legal duties including the filing of an annual return (**B1 form**) and financial statements with the CRO.

Setting up a limited company typically costs between EUR 250 and EUR 500 if you use a company formation agent, or you can register directly with the CRO using the **A1 form**.

## Tax Differences

This is where the two structures diverge most significantly.

### Sole Trader Tax

As a sole trader, all your business profits are treated as personal income. You pay:

- **Income Tax:** 20% on the first EUR 42,000 (single person, 2025 rates) and 40% on income above that threshold
- **USC (Universal Social Charge):** Ranges from 0.5% to 8% depending on income level
- **PRSI (Class S):** 4% on all income for self-employed individuals

At higher income levels, the combined marginal rate for a sole trader can exceed **50%**.

### Limited Company Tax

A limited company pays **corporation tax at 12.5%** on trading profits. This is one of the lowest rates in Europe and is a key reason many Irish businesses incorporate.

However, getting money out of the company and into your personal bank account triggers additional tax. If you pay yourself a salary, that salary is subject to income tax, USC, and employee PRSI -- plus the company pays **employer PRSI at 11.05%** on your wages.

Alternatively, you can take dividends, but dividends from an Irish company are subject to income tax at your marginal rate plus USC. There is no separate lower dividend tax rate in Ireland as there is in some other countries.

The real tax advantage of a limited company emerges when you can retain profits within the business rather than extracting them all as personal income.

## Liability Protection

As a sole trader, you have **unlimited personal liability**. If your business cannot pay its debts, creditors can pursue your personal assets -- your home, car, savings, and other property.

A limited company provides **limited liability**. Shareholders are only liable up to the amount they have invested in the company (typically the nominal value of their shares). If the company fails, your personal assets are generally protected, provided you have not given personal guarantees on company debts and have not engaged in reckless or fraudulent trading.

This distinction matters most for businesses that carry significant risk -- those with large contracts, employees, physical premises, or exposure to potential legal claims.

## Setup Costs and Complexity

| Factor | Sole Trader | Limited Company |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Registration cost | Free (tax registration only) | EUR 50 CRO fee (plus agent fees if used) |
| Time to set up | 1-3 days | 5-10 business days |
| Annual CRO return | Not required | Required (B1 form, EUR 20 filing fee) |
| Accountant fees | EUR 300-800/year typical | EUR 1,500-3,500/year typical |
| Audit requirement | None | Exempt if small company thresholds met |
| Business bank account | Optional (but recommended) | Required |

## Annual Compliance Requirements

### Sole Trader

- File **Form 11** income tax return by 31 October (or mid-November via ROS)
- Pay preliminary tax by 31 October
- Keep records for 6 years
- File VAT returns if VAT-registered

### Limited Company

- File **Form CT1** corporation tax return within 9 months of financial year end
- File **annual return (B1 form)** with the CRO within 28 days of the annual return date
- Prepare and file financial statements (abridged accounts permitted for small companies)
- File **Form 11** for each director's personal income
- Pay preliminary corporation tax (small company: one payment; large company: two instalments)
- Maintain statutory registers (directors, shareholders, etc.)
- Hold an AGM (can be waived for single-member companies)

The compliance burden for a limited company is significantly heavier, which is reflected in higher accountant fees.

## Which Structure Is Right for You

There is no universal answer, but here are some general guidelines:

**Stay as a sole trader if:**
- Your annual profits are under EUR 40,000-50,000
- You want minimal paperwork and compliance costs
- You are testing a business idea and want to keep things simple
- Your business carries low risk and you have no significant assets to protect

**Consider a limited company if:**
- Your annual profits consistently exceed EUR 50,000
- You want to retain profits in the business for reinvestment
- You need liability protection for higher-risk activities
- You plan to bring on investors or partners in the future
- You want to build a business that exists independently of you

Many Irish business owners start as sole traders and incorporate later when their income grows or their circumstances change. Your accountant can model the exact tax savings based on your projected income to help you decide the right time to make the switch.

<FAQSection>
<FAQItem question="Can I switch from sole trader to limited company in Ireland?">
Yes. You can incorporate a limited company through the CRO at any time. You will need to register the new company for taxes with Revenue, transfer business assets, and close your sole trader tax registration. Many Irish accountants offer incorporation packages.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="Do sole traders pay more tax than limited companies in Ireland?">
It depends on your profit level. Sole traders pay income tax (up to 40%), USC, and PRSI. Limited companies pay 12.5% corporation tax on profits. However, extracting profits from a company (via salary or dividends) incurs additional personal tax, so the net difference varies.
</FAQItem>
