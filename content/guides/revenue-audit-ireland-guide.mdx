A Revenue audit in Ireland is a formal examination of your tax affairs to verify that you have paid the correct amount of tax. Revenue selects businesses and individuals for audit using risk profiling, random selection, and sector-specific campaigns. Receiving an audit notification does not mean Revenue suspects you of wrongdoing -- it is a routine part of the Irish tax system.

This guide explains everything you need to know about Revenue audits in Ireland: why they happen, what the process involves, how to prepare, your rights during the audit, and what happens afterwards. Whether you are a sole trader, company director, or partnership, understanding the audit process helps you respond confidently and avoid unnecessary penalties.

## Why Revenue Conducts Audits

Revenue uses audits to maintain the integrity of the self-assessment system. The Irish tax system relies on taxpayers calculating and declaring their own liabilities, and audits provide the verification mechanism that keeps the system honest.

Revenue selects cases for audit through several methods:

**Risk profiling.** Revenue's computerised system, known as REAP (Risk Evaluation, Analysis and Profiling), analyses tax returns and flags anomalies. Common triggers include unusual fluctuations in income or expenses, claims that are inconsistent with industry norms, large deductions relative to turnover, and discrepancies between different tax returns (for example, income tax and VAT).

**Random selection.** A proportion of audits are selected randomly. This ensures that every taxpayer has some probability of being audited, regardless of their risk profile. Random audits reinforce voluntary compliance across the entire taxpayer base.

**Sector-specific projects.** Revenue periodically targets specific industries where non-compliance is known to be higher. These projects have historically focused on sectors such as cash-intensive businesses (pubs, restaurants, takeaways), construction, professional services, rental income, and the sharing economy.

**Third-party information.** Revenue receives data from numerous sources, including banks, the CRO, other government departments, and international tax authorities through automatic exchange of information agreements. Discrepancies between third-party data and your tax returns can trigger an audit.

**Information from other audits.** If Revenue audits one of your suppliers, customers, or business partners and discovers issues that relate to your affairs, they may open an audit on you as well.

## Types of Revenue Audit

Revenue conducts different types of audit depending on the scope and nature of the examination:

### Aspect Query

An aspect query is the lightest form of Revenue intervention. It involves a written query about a specific item on your tax return, such as a particular expense claim or a discrepancy in your figures. You respond in writing, providing the requested documentation or explanation. If Revenue is satisfied, the matter closes. If not, it may escalate to a full audit.

### Desk Audit

A desk audit is conducted entirely from Revenue's offices. Revenue requests copies of your records, accounts, and supporting documentation by post or electronically. The inspector reviews these remotely and may request further information or clarification. Desk audits are common for straightforward issues or lower-risk cases.

### Field Audit

A field audit involves Revenue inspectors visiting your business premises to examine your records on-site. This is the most comprehensive form of audit and allows Revenue to observe your operations, verify physical records, and cross-check systems. Field audits typically last one to three days but can extend over weeks or months for complex cases.

### Investigation

A Revenue investigation is distinct from a routine audit. Investigations are conducted by Revenue's Investigations and Prosecutions Division and involve cases where serious fraud, evasion, or criminal behaviour is suspected. Investigations can lead to criminal prosecution and imprisonment. If you receive notification of an investigation rather than an audit, you should seek specialist legal and tax advice immediately.

## The Audit Notification Letter

When Revenue selects you for audit, you receive a written notification letter. This letter tells you:

- The tax head or tax heads being audited (income tax, corporation tax, VAT, PAYE, etc.)
- The period under examination (usually one to three years, but can extend to six years or more)
- The date and time of the audit (for field audits)
- The records you need to have available
- Your rights, including the right to make a voluntary disclosure before the audit begins

The notification letter typically gives you **21 to 28 days notice** before a field audit commences. This is your window to prepare your records and, if necessary, make a disclosure.

### The Importance of the Notification Window

The period between receiving notification and the audit start date is critical. If you are aware of any errors or omissions in your tax affairs, this is your last opportunity to make a disclosure. A disclosure made after notification is classified as a **prompted qualifying disclosure**, which carries lower penalties than if Revenue discovers the errors during the audit itself.

However, a prompted disclosure does not carry the same benefits as an unprompted (voluntary) disclosure made before any Revenue contact. Penalties for prompted disclosures range from 10 to 50 per cent of the tax underpaid, compared to 3 to 10 per cent for unprompted disclosures.

## How to Prepare for a Revenue Audit

Preparation is essential. Well-organised records and a clear understanding of your tax position make the audit process smoother and reduce the risk of adverse findings.

### Gather Your Records

Revenue expects you to maintain records for **six years** from the end of the tax year to which they relate. For a field audit, have the following available:

- **Books of account:** Cash books, ledgers, journals, and computerised accounting records
- **Bank statements:** All business bank accounts for the audit period. Revenue will also ask about personal bank accounts if you are a sole trader
- **Invoices and receipts:** Both sales invoices issued and purchase invoices received
- **Payroll records:** P35 returns, payslips, employment contracts, and details of any payments to subcontractors
- **VAT records:** VAT3 returns filed, VAT invoices, credit notes, and the annual Return of Trading Details
- **Asset records:** Details of business assets purchased, depreciated, or disposed of
- **Contracts and agreements:** Lease agreements, loan agreements, and any contracts relevant to your business
- **Personal records (sole traders):** Revenue may examine personal bank accounts, mortgage applications, and lifestyle indicators to verify that declared income supports your standard of living

### Review Your Returns

Before the audit, review all tax returns filed for the audit period. Check for:

- Mathematical errors or transposition mistakes
- Claims that you cannot substantiate with documentation
- Timing differences (income or expenses recorded in the wrong period)
- Personal expenses incorrectly claimed as business expenses
- VAT claimed on non-deductible items (entertainment, food and drink, certain motor expenses)

If you identify errors, discuss them with your accountant immediately. Making a prompted disclosure before the audit begins results in significantly lower penalties than if Revenue discovers the errors themselves.

### Engage Your Accountant or Tax Agent

You have the right to be represented by a tax agent during a Revenue audit. Your accountant or tax adviser can:

- Attend the audit on your behalf or alongside you
- Liaise with Revenue before, during, and after the audit
- Prepare a disclosure if errors are identified
- Negotiate the settlement amount and penalty rate
- Advise on your appeal rights if you disagree with Revenue's findings

Most accountants have experience with Revenue audits and know what inspectors look for. Their presence can help ensure the audit proceeds efficiently and that your rights are protected.

## Your Rights During a Revenue Audit

Revenue's Code of Practice for Revenue Audit and other Compliance Interventions (2022) sets out your rights during an audit:

- **Right to be treated courteously and professionally** by Revenue staff
- **Right to be represented** by a tax agent, accountant, or solicitor
- **Right to know the reason** for the audit and the period under examination
- **Right to reasonable time** to gather records and prepare
- **Right to make a disclosure** before the audit begins, with reduced penalties
- **Right to privacy** -- Revenue must conduct field audits with minimum disruption to your business
- **Right to appeal** Revenue's findings to the Tax Appeals Commission
- **Right to complain** to Revenue's Customer Service unit or the Office of the Ombudsman if you believe you have been treated unfairly

Revenue inspectors must identify themselves, explain the audit process, and give you the opportunity to ask questions. They should work with you to arrange audit dates and times that minimise disruption to your business.

## What Revenue Inspectors Look For

Revenue inspectors are trained to identify indicators of non-compliance. Common areas of focus include:

**Income completeness.** Revenue checks whether all income has been declared by cross-referencing your returns with bank lodgements, third-party data, and industry benchmarks. Unexplained lodgements are a significant red flag.

**Expense validity.** Every business expense claimed as a deduction must be wholly and exclusively for the purpose of the trade. Revenue scrutinises expenses that could have a personal element, such as motor expenses, travel, meals, home office costs, and mobile phone bills.

**Director's loan accounts.** For companies, Revenue examines director's current accounts closely. Significant debits on the director's account may indicate undeclared income or benefits in kind.

**Benefits in kind.** Use of company assets for private purposes (company car, accommodation, loans) must be properly reported as benefits in kind on Form P11D. Under-reporting is common and is a frequent audit finding.

**VAT accuracy.** Revenue verifies that VAT has been correctly charged on sales, that input VAT claims are supported by valid VAT invoices, and that the correct rates have been applied.

**PAYE compliance.** For employers, Revenue checks that all employees are on the payroll, that PAYE, PRSI, and USC have been correctly calculated and remitted, and that no employees have been incorrectly classified as self-employed contractors.

**Subcontractor payments.** If you use subcontractors, Revenue checks RCT (Relevant Contracts Tax) compliance and verifies that subcontractors are genuinely self-employed rather than disguised employees.

## After the Audit: Outcomes and Settlements

Once the audit fieldwork is complete, Revenue will communicate their findings. The possible outcomes are:

### No Adjustments

If Revenue finds no errors or omissions, the audit closes with no additional liability. Revenue issues a letter confirming the audit is complete and that no adjustments are required. This is the best possible outcome.

### Additional Assessment

If Revenue identifies underpaid tax, they will raise an assessment for the additional amount due, plus interest from the original due date. They will also apply penalties based on the nature and severity of the error:

- **Careless behaviour (without significant consequences):** 15 to 20 per cent of the tax underpaid
- **Careless behaviour (with significant consequences):** 20 to 30 per cent
- **Deliberate behaviour:** 40 to 100 per cent

The penalty rate can be mitigated by cooperation during the audit. Taxpayers who engage openly and provide full information typically receive penalties at the lower end of the range.

### Settlement Agreement

In most cases, the audit concludes with a settlement agreement between you and Revenue. This agreement sets out the additional tax, interest, and penalties payable. Once you sign and pay the settlement, the matter is closed for the periods under audit.

If the total settlement (tax, interest, and penalties) exceeds EUR 50,000 and was not the result of an unprompted qualifying disclosure, your details will be published on Revenue's quarterly list of tax defaulters.

### Tax Appeals Commission

If you disagree with Revenue's findings, you have the right to appeal to the Tax Appeals Commission (TAC). You must submit a Notice of Appeal within 30 days of the assessment. The TAC is an independent body that will hear your case and make a determination. Either party can appeal a TAC decision to the High Court on a point of law.

## Revenue's Code of Practice for Revenue Audit

Revenue published its Code of Practice for Revenue Audit and other Compliance Interventions, most recently updated in 2022. This document is essential reading for anyone facing an audit. It sets out:

- The different types of compliance intervention (aspect query, profile interview, audit, investigation)
- The notification process and your rights
- The disclosure and penalty framework
- How settlements are calculated
- Publication criteria for the tax defaulters list

The Code of Practice is available on Revenue's website at revenue.ie and is free to download. Your accountant will be familiar with its provisions and can explain how they apply to your specific circumstances.

## Practical Tips for Managing an Audit

**Do not panic.** An audit notification does not mean Revenue suspects you of fraud. Many audits result in minor adjustments or no changes at all. Stay calm and focus on preparation.

**Respond promptly to all Revenue correspondence.** Delays in responding can create a negative impression and may lead Revenue to suspect you are hiding something.

**Be honest and cooperative.** If you have made genuine errors, acknowledge them early. Cooperation is specifically factored into the penalty calculation, and it can reduce your penalties significantly.

**Do not volunteer information beyond what is asked.** Answer Revenue's questions accurately and completely, but do not offer unsolicited information about other matters not under examination.

**Keep a record of all communications.** Document dates, times, and content of all conversations with Revenue inspectors. Retain copies of all correspondence.

**Do not destroy or alter records.** Destroying or falsifying records during an audit is a criminal offence that can lead to prosecution.

<FAQItem question="How likely am I to be audited by Revenue in Ireland?">
Revenue audits a relatively small percentage of taxpayers each year, but the exact probability depends on your risk profile. Cash-intensive businesses, those with inconsistent returns, and sectors targeted by Revenue projects face higher audit rates. Random selection means any taxpayer can be chosen. Maintaining accurate records and filing on time reduces your risk profile.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="Can I refuse a Revenue audit?">
No. Revenue has statutory powers to audit any taxpayer and to require production of records. Refusing to cooperate or obstructing an audit is an offence that can result in penalties and prosecution. However, you have the right to be represented by an accountant or solicitor and to request reasonable adjustments to the audit schedule.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="What penalties will I face if Revenue finds errors?">
Penalties depend on the nature of the error. Careless behaviour attracts penalties of 15 to 30 per cent of the underpaid tax. Deliberate behaviour attracts penalties of 40 to 100 per cent. These are in addition to the underpaid tax and interest at 0.0219 per cent per day. Making a disclosure before or at the start of the audit reduces penalties significantly.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="How far back can Revenue audit?">
Revenue can generally audit the previous four years of tax returns. However, if fraud or neglect is suspected, they can go back further -- up to six years or even beyond in cases of deliberate evasion. It is essential to retain all records for at least six years from the end of the relevant tax year.
</FAQItem>
