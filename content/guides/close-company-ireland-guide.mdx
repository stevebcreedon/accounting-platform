Closing a limited company in Ireland requires either a voluntary strike-off through the Companies Registration Office (CRO) using Form H15 or a formal liquidation process. The most common route for small companies is voluntary strike-off, which takes approximately 3 to 6 months and involves obtaining Revenue clearance, filing the H15 form, and waiting for the CRO to remove the company from the register. You cannot simply stop trading and walk away -- a company continues to exist and accumulate filing obligations until it is formally dissolved.

Many Irish business owners are surprised by how involved the closure process is. Even a dormant company that has never traded must go through the proper dissolution procedure. This guide covers both voluntary strike-off and formal liquidation, the obligations you must meet along the way, and the consequences of getting it wrong.

## Why You Cannot Just Walk Away

A limited company is a separate legal entity. It exists independently of its shareholders and directors. Once incorporated, it continues to exist until it is formally dissolved, which means:

- **Annual returns continue to be due.** The CRO requires an annual return (Form B1) every year, regardless of whether the company is trading. Missing an annual return incurs a penalty of EUR 100 per day (up to EUR 5,000 per year) and triggers loss of audit exemption.
- **Tax returns continue to be due.** Revenue expects a corporation tax return (Form CT1) for every accounting period until the company is formally dissolved and Revenue closes its file.
- **The company can accumulate liabilities.** Penalties, interest, and filing obligations continue to accrue. Directors can be pursued personally for these obligations.

Walking away from a company without closing it properly can result in thousands of euros in penalties, personal liability for directors, and potential restriction from acting as a director of any company for 5 years.

## Voluntary Strike-Off (Form H15)

Voluntary strike-off is the most common method for closing a small Irish company. It is suitable for companies that:

- Have no outstanding debts or liabilities (or can pay them off before closure)
- Have no assets to distribute (or can distribute them before closure)
- Are up to date with all CRO and Revenue filings
- Are not in the process of being wound up by the court

### Step 1: Settle All Debts and Liabilities

Before applying for strike-off, the company must settle all its debts. This includes:

- **Revenue debts:** All taxes (corporation tax, VAT, PAYE/PRSI) must be paid in full
- **Trade creditors:** All outstanding invoices from suppliers must be paid
- **Employee entitlements:** All wages, holiday pay, notice periods, and redundancy payments must be honoured
- **Loan repayments:** All bank loans and other borrowings must be repaid or formally written off
- **Lease obligations:** Any property or equipment leases must be terminated or assigned

If the company has debts that it cannot pay, voluntary strike-off is not appropriate. You will need to consider formal liquidation instead.

### Step 2: Distribute or Dispose of Assets

The company should distribute any remaining assets to the shareholders before applying for strike-off. Common assets to deal with include:

- **Cash in the bank:** Transfer to shareholders as a final distribution. This may be treated as a capital distribution rather than income for tax purposes, which can be more favourable.
- **Equipment and vehicles:** Sell or transfer to shareholders at market value
- **Intellectual property:** Transfer or assign to the appropriate parties
- **Contracts and agreements:** Terminate, assign, or allow to expire

Any distribution to shareholders may have tax implications. A final distribution of assets can be treated as a capital distribution, potentially qualifying for CGT treatment rather than income tax. However, Revenue's close company rules and anti-avoidance provisions must be considered. Your accountant should advise on the most tax-efficient approach.

### Step 3: File All Outstanding Returns

Before Revenue will issue a letter of no objection, all returns must be up to date:

- **Corporation tax returns (Form CT1):** File returns for all outstanding periods, including the final period up to the date of cessation
- **VAT returns:** File all outstanding VAT3 returns and deregister for VAT
- **PAYE/PRSI returns:** File all outstanding employer returns and deregister as an employer
- **Annual returns (Form B1):** File all outstanding annual returns with the CRO, including financial statements
- **Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT):** If applicable, file all outstanding returns

### Step 4: Obtain Revenue Clearance (Tax Clearance)

You must apply to Revenue for a letter of no objection to the strike-off. This confirms that Revenue has no objection to the company being dissolved. Revenue will only issue this letter if:

- All tax returns have been filed
- All taxes have been paid
- There are no outstanding audits or investigations
- There are no outstanding queries or correspondence

Apply for the letter through the Revenue Online Service (ROS) or by writing to your local Revenue office. Processing can take 2 to 6 weeks depending on the complexity of the company's tax affairs and Revenue's workload.

### Step 5: Pass a Special Resolution

The shareholders must pass a special resolution authorising the directors to apply for voluntary strike-off. A special resolution requires at least 75 per cent of the shareholders to vote in favour. For single-shareholder companies, this is straightforward -- you simply sign the written resolution.

The resolution should state that the company has no liabilities, has no assets or has distributed all assets, and that the directors are authorised to apply to the CRO for strike-off.

### Step 6: File Form H15 with the CRO

The Form H15 is the application for voluntary strike-off. It is filed with the CRO online via CORE or by post. The form requires:

- Company name and CRO number
- Confirmation that the company has no liabilities
- Confirmation that the company has no assets (or has distributed them all)
- A copy of the special resolution authorising the strike-off
- The Revenue letter of no objection
- Signatures of the directors

The CRO filing fee for a Form H15 is EUR 15 (online).

### Step 7: CRO Publication and Waiting Period

After accepting the H15 application, the CRO publishes a notice in the CRO Gazette and the Iris Oifigiuil (the official State gazette) stating that the company will be struck off the register unless cause is shown within 30 days.

This 30-day notice period gives creditors and other interested parties the opportunity to object to the strike-off. If no objection is received, the CRO proceeds to dissolve the company.

### Step 8: Dissolution

After the notice period expires and no objections have been received, the CRO strikes the company off the register and publishes a notice of dissolution. The company ceases to exist as a legal entity.

The entire process from filing the H15 to dissolution typically takes 3 to 6 months.

## Members' Voluntary Liquidation (MVL)

A members' voluntary liquidation is a more formal process for closing a company that is solvent (able to pay its debts). It is used when:

- The company has significant assets to distribute
- A formal winding-up process is preferred for legal certainty
- There are tax advantages to distributing assets through liquidation (particularly the potential to claim entrepreneur relief on capital gains)
- The shareholders want the protection of a formal liquidator handling the distribution

### The Process

1. **Declaration of solvency:** The directors must make a statutory declaration that the company is able to pay its debts within 12 months of the commencement of the winding up. This declaration must be made within 30 days before the passing of the winding-up resolution and must include a statement of the company's assets and liabilities.

2. **Special resolution:** The shareholders pass a special resolution to wind up the company voluntarily.

3. **Appointment of liquidator:** The shareholders appoint a liquidator at the same meeting or within 14 days. The liquidator must be a qualified insolvency practitioner.

4. **Liquidator takes control:** The liquidator takes control of the company's affairs, realises its assets, pays its debts, and distributes any surplus to the shareholders.

5. **Final meeting and dissolution:** Once the liquidation is complete, the liquidator calls a final meeting of the shareholders, presents a final account, and files the account with the CRO. The company is dissolved 3 months after the filing.

### Costs of MVL

A members' voluntary liquidation is significantly more expensive than a simple strike-off:

| Cost Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Liquidator fees | EUR 3,000-10,000+ |
| Legal fees | EUR 1,000-3,000 |
| Advertising and gazette notices | EUR 500-1,000 |
| Accountant fees (final returns) | EUR 500-2,000 |
| **Total** | **EUR 5,000-16,000+** |

The cost is justified when there are significant assets to distribute and when the tax savings from a formal liquidation (such as entrepreneur relief) outweigh the additional professional fees.

## Final Tax Returns and Revenue Obligations

Regardless of whether you close the company through strike-off or liquidation, several tax obligations must be met:

### Final Corporation Tax Return

The company must file a Form CT1 for the final accounting period, which runs from the end of the previous accounting period to the date of cessation. If the company ceased trading before its normal year-end, the final period may be shorter than 12 months.

Any profits in the final period are subject to corporation tax at 12.5 per cent (trading) or 25 per cent (non-trading). Any losses can potentially be carried back against profits of the preceding accounting period.

### VAT Deregistration

If the company is registered for VAT, you must:

- File a final VAT3 return covering the period up to the date of cessation
- Account for VAT on any assets retained or distributed to shareholders at market value
- Notify Revenue to deregister the company for VAT

### PAYE/PRSI

If the company had employees or paid director salaries:

- File all outstanding payroll returns
- Issue P45s (cessation certificates) to all employees and directors
- Make final PAYE/PRSI payments to Revenue
- Deregister as an employer

### Capital Gains Tax

Distributions to shareholders on a winding up or dissolution are generally treated as capital disposals. This means the shareholder is subject to capital gains tax (CGT) at 33 per cent on any gain above their base cost in the shares.

However, if the conditions for revised entrepreneur relief (Section 597AA TCA) are met, a reduced CGT rate of 10 per cent applies on qualifying disposals up to a lifetime limit of EUR 1 million. This relief can make a significant difference and is a key reason why some shareholders choose a formal MVL over a simple strike-off.

## Restoring a Struck-Off Company

If a company is struck off the register, it can potentially be restored in certain circumstances:

**Application by the company:** The company (through its former directors) can apply to the court for restoration within 6 years of the date of dissolution. The court may order restoration if it is just and equitable to do so.

**Application by a creditor or other party:** A creditor, member, or any person who appears to the court to have an interest can also apply for restoration, typically where the applicant has a claim against the company.

**Requirements for restoration:**
- All outstanding annual returns and financial statements must be filed with the CRO
- All CRO filing fees and late filing penalties must be paid
- All outstanding tax returns must be filed and taxes paid
- The court must be satisfied that restoration is appropriate

The cost of restoring a company includes court application fees (approximately EUR 400), legal fees (EUR 2,000 to EUR 5,000), outstanding CRO penalties (potentially thousands of euros), and professional fees for preparing outstanding returns.

Restoration is expensive and time-consuming. It is far better to follow the proper closure procedure from the outset.

## What Happens to Directors After Dissolution

After a company is dissolved:

- Directors are released from their ongoing obligations to the company
- However, directors can still be held liable for pre-dissolution breaches of duty
- If the company was dissolved while insolvent, the ODCE may still investigate the conduct of the directors
- Revenue can still pursue directors personally for unpaid taxes even after the company is dissolved

Directors should retain the company's books and records for at least 6 years after dissolution, as these may be needed for any future queries from Revenue, creditors, or the ODCE.
