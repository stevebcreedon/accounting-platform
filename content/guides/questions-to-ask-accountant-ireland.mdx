Choosing the right accountant is one of the most important decisions you will make for your Irish business. A good accountant does far more than file your tax returns. They help you structure your business efficiently, plan for tax, and avoid costly mistakes with Revenue. A poor choice can mean missed deadlines, overpaid tax, or compliance issues that take years to resolve.

The problem is that most business owners do not know what to ask when meeting a potential accountant for the first time. They end up choosing based on price alone or accepting whoever a friend recommended without assessing whether the fit is right for their specific situation. This guide gives you ten essential questions to ask before hiring an accountant in Ireland, explains what good answers look like, and highlights the red flags that should make you walk away.

## Before You Start: What to Bring to the Meeting

Before meeting potential accountants, prepare the following:

- A clear description of your business (what you do, how long you have been trading, your business structure)
- Your most recent set of accounts and tax returns (if you have them)
- Your current bookkeeping setup (software, spreadsheet, or shoebox)
- Your rough turnover and number of employees
- A list of specific challenges or questions you need help with
- Your budget expectations

Most accountants offer a free initial consultation of 30 to 60 minutes. This is enough time to ask the questions below and get a sense of whether the relationship will work.

## Question 1: What Are Your Professional Qualifications?

This is the single most important question, and you should ask it first. In Ireland, there is no legal restriction on who can call themselves an accountant for non-audit work. Anyone can set up as an accountant without any qualifications whatsoever.

The main recognised professional bodies in Ireland are Chartered Accountants Ireland (CAI), CPA Ireland, ACCA, and CIMA. Members of these bodies have completed rigorous training programmes, passed professional examinations, and are subject to ongoing continuing professional development (CPD) requirements, ethical standards, and disciplinary procedures.

**What a good answer looks like:** "I am a Fellow/Associate of Chartered Accountants Ireland" or "I hold a CPA Ireland practising certificate." They should be able to tell you their membership number and you can verify it on the relevant body's website.

**Red flags:** Vague answers like "I have years of experience" or "I studied accounting in college" without mentioning a professional body. A degree in accounting is not the same as a professional qualification. Also watch for claims of being "chartered" without specifying the body -- there are different types of chartered qualifications with different scopes.

## Question 2: Do You Have Experience with Businesses Like Mine?

Not all accountants are the same. Some specialise in sole traders, others in limited companies. Some focus on specific industries like construction, medical professionals, or technology startups. An accountant who understands your industry will know the specific tax reliefs, compliance requirements, and common pitfalls relevant to your business.

**What a good answer looks like:** "Yes, I work with several businesses in your sector. For example, I am familiar with the relevant contracts tax requirements for construction, or the R&D tax credit process for technology companies." They should be able to demonstrate specific knowledge without revealing confidential client details.

**Red flags:** "I work with all types of businesses" without any specific examples. While generalist accountants can be excellent, if your business has industry-specific requirements (construction CIS, farming schemes, medical consultant structures), you need someone who knows those areas well.

## Question 3: What Services Are Included in Your Fee?

This is where most misunderstandings occur. Some accountants quote a headline fee that covers only the annual accounts and tax return. Everything else -- VAT returns, payroll, management accounts, phone calls to discuss queries, letters to Revenue -- is charged separately.

Ask for a detailed breakdown of what is included and what costs extra. The key services to ask about:

- Annual accounts preparation
- Corporation tax return (CT1) or income tax return (Form 11)
- VAT return preparation and filing
- Payroll processing and Revenue submissions
- CRO annual return and filing
- Management accounts (monthly or quarterly)
- General tax queries by phone or email
- Tax planning meetings
- Revenue correspondence and representation

**What a good answer looks like:** A clear, written fee proposal that lists every service, whether it is included in the annual fee or charged separately, and what the additional charges are. The best accountants provide a fixed annual fee that covers all standard compliance work, with hourly rates for advisory work clearly stated.

**Red flags:** Reluctance to put fees in writing, vague statements like "it depends on the work involved," or a suspiciously low headline fee that clearly cannot cover all the services you need. If the quoted fee seems too good to be true, it almost certainly excludes services you will need.

## Question 4: How Do You Structure Your Fees?

Beyond what is included, understand how the accountant charges. The three main models in Ireland are:

**Fixed annual fee.** A set amount that covers all agreed compliance work for the year. This is the most predictable and usually the best option for small businesses. It allows you to budget accurately and removes the anxiety of calling your accountant with a question and watching the meter run.

**Hourly rate.** You pay for time spent. Rates for qualified accountants in Ireland range from EUR 100 to EUR 300 per hour depending on experience and location. This can work well for one-off advisory work but creates uncertainty for ongoing compliance.

**Monthly retainer.** A fixed monthly payment that covers all services. This is essentially the fixed annual fee divided into monthly instalments, often with management accounts and regular review meetings included.

**What a good answer looks like:** A clear explanation of their fee structure with no hidden charges. They should tell you about any minimum charges, what happens if your business grows and becomes more complex, and how fees are reviewed annually.

**Red flags:** Unwillingness to commit to any figure, hourly-only billing for standard compliance work (which creates an incentive to be slow), or fee structures that penalise you for asking questions.

## Question 5: Who Will Actually Handle My Work?

In larger practices, the partner or senior accountant you meet at the initial consultation is often not the person who does your day-to-day work. Your accounts may be prepared by a trainee or junior staff member, with the partner reviewing and signing off.

This is not necessarily a problem -- it is how most practices operate and can keep costs down. But you should know who your primary contact will be, who prepares your accounts, and who reviews them.

**What a good answer looks like:** "Your accounts will be prepared by [name], who is a qualified accountant with [X] years of experience. I will review everything before it is finalised, and I am available for any queries you have." Transparency about the process is what matters.

**Red flags:** Evasiveness about who does the work, or discovering after engagement that your file has been passed to someone you have never met without any explanation. A sole practitioner who claims to handle everything personally but has 200 clients may also be a concern -- there are only so many hours in a day.

## Question 6: How Often Will We Communicate?

Communication is one of the most common sources of frustration in the accountant-client relationship. Some business owners want a proactive accountant who calls them with tax-saving ideas. Others just want someone to file the returns and only contact them when necessary.

Clarify expectations on both sides:

- How often will you receive updates on your accounts?
- What is the typical response time for emails and phone calls?
- Will there be a scheduled annual review or tax planning meeting?
- How will urgent matters be handled?
- Is there a client portal for sharing documents and viewing progress?

**What a good answer looks like:** "We respond to emails within 24 hours on business days. We schedule an annual tax planning meeting in advance of year end. We issue quarterly reminders for key deadlines. You can reach me directly on my mobile for urgent matters."

**Red flags:** "We will be in touch when we need something from you" (reactive only), no clear response time commitments, or difficulty getting through to speak with someone. If they are hard to reach before you are a client, it will not improve afterwards.

## Question 7: What Accounting Software Do You Prefer?

Your accountant's software preference affects your bookkeeping workflow, the quality of your management information, and the efficiency of year-end work. In Ireland, the most common platforms are Xero, Sage Business Cloud, and QuickBooks Online.

Ideally, you and your accountant should use the same platform or at least compatible systems. If your accountant can access your accounting software directly, they can review your records in real time, prepare your accounts more efficiently, and provide timely advice based on current data.

**What a good answer looks like:** "We recommend Xero for most small businesses because of its Irish bank integrations and the ability for us to access your data directly. If you are already using Sage or QuickBooks, we can work with those too." They should explain why they prefer a particular system and how it benefits you.

**Red flags:** Insisting on paper records or desktop software that does not allow collaborative access. Accountants who do not use cloud software in 2026 may also be behind on other technology and efficiency improvements. An accountant who has no preference at all may not have invested in optimising their workflow.

## Question 8: What Is Your Approach to Tax Planning?

There is a difference between tax compliance (filing returns correctly and on time) and tax planning (structuring your affairs to minimise tax within the law). The best accountants do both.

Ask how proactive they are about identifying tax-saving opportunities. Do they just process what you give them, or do they actively look for ways to reduce your tax bill?

Key areas where an Irish accountant should add value include:

- Pension contributions and their tax relief at your marginal rate
- Salary vs dividend decisions for company directors
- Capital allowances on business assets
- The small benefit exemption (EUR 1,000 tax-free benefit per employee per year)
- Motor expenses and the appropriate claim basis
- The employment and investment incentive scheme (EIIS)
- Timing of income and expenditure around your year end
- Revenue's concessional arrangements and extra-statutory practices

**What a good answer looks like:** Specific examples of how they have helped similar clients save tax. A willingness to schedule a pre-year-end planning meeting to review your position and identify opportunities before the year closes.

**Red flags:** "We will make sure your returns are filed on time" with no mention of planning. An accountant who only does compliance is leaving money on the table for you. Also be wary of anyone promising aggressive or questionable tax schemes -- if it sounds too good to be true, it probably is.

## Question 9: Can You Provide References?

A confident accountant should be willing to provide references from existing clients, ideally businesses of a similar size and type to yours. You are not looking for a glowing review; you are looking for honest feedback on reliability, communication, responsiveness, and value for money.

**What a good answer looks like:** "I can put you in touch with two or three clients who are happy to share their experience. I will check with them first for data protection reasons." This shows confidence in the quality of their service and respect for client confidentiality.

**Red flags:** Outright refusal to provide any references, or offering references only from personal contacts rather than business clients. While data protection considerations are legitimate, an accountant who cannot find a single client willing to recommend them should give you pause.

## Question 10: Will There Be a Letter of Engagement?

A letter of engagement is a written agreement that sets out the terms of the professional relationship. It should cover:

- The services to be provided and any exclusions
- The fees and how they will be billed
- Your responsibilities (providing information, meeting deadlines)
- The accountant's responsibilities (professional standards, confidentiality)
- Data protection arrangements (GDPR compliance)
- How either party can terminate the engagement
- Professional indemnity insurance confirmation
- Complaints procedure

In Ireland, all members of the recognised accounting bodies are required to issue a letter of engagement before beginning work for a new client. This is not just best practice; it is a professional obligation.

**What a good answer looks like:** "Yes, we issue a letter of engagement to all new clients before we begin any work. It sets out exactly what we will do, what it will cost, and what we need from you."

**Red flags:** No letter of engagement, reluctance to put terms in writing, or an engagement letter that is so vague it does not actually clarify anything. Without a clear agreement, disputes about fees and scope are almost inevitable.

## Bonus: Additional Questions for Specific Situations

### If You Are a Startup

- Do you offer discounted rates for new businesses in their first year?
- Can you help with company formation and initial Revenue registrations?
- Are you familiar with the SURE scheme, EIIS, and startup-specific reliefs?
- Do you have experience with R&D tax credits?
- Can you advise on share option schemes (KEEP) for early employees?

### If You Are Switching Accountants

- How do you handle the professional clearance process with my current accountant?
- What information do you need from my previous accountant?
- Is there a charge for the handover and initial setup?
- How quickly can you take over my affairs?

### If You Have Employees

- Do you provide a full payroll service including Revenue submissions?
- Are you up to date with the latest PAYE Modernisation requirements?
- Can you handle employment contracts, benefit-in-kind calculations, and termination payments?
- What payroll software do you use?

## How to Evaluate the Answers

After meeting two or three accountants (which is the minimum you should consult), compare them across these criteria:

**Qualifications and experience.** Is the accountant professionally qualified and experienced with businesses like yours?

**Clarity on fees.** Did they provide a clear, written breakdown of costs? Can you predict what you will pay each year?

**Communication style.** Did they explain things in plain language? Did they listen to your questions and answer them directly? Did you feel comfortable asking follow-up questions?

**Proactive vs reactive.** Did they demonstrate a tax planning mindset, or did they focus solely on compliance?

**Technology.** Are they using modern cloud software that integrates with your workflow?

**Professionalism.** Were they punctual, prepared, and organised? Did they follow up after the meeting?

The cheapest accountant is rarely the best value. An accountant who charges EUR 500 more per year but saves you EUR 2,000 in tax through proactive planning is the better investment. Focus on value, not just cost.

## Making Your Decision

Take your time. A good accountant relationship can last decades, so it is worth investing effort in getting the choice right. After your consultations:

1. Review your notes from each meeting
2. Compare the fee proposals side by side
3. Check references if provided
4. Verify qualifications on the relevant professional body's website
5. Trust your instinct about who you felt most comfortable with

Remember that you are hiring someone who will have intimate knowledge of your financial affairs. Trust, communication, and competence matter more than saving EUR 100 on the annual fee.

<FAQItem question="Do I need a chartered accountant or will any accountant do?">
For basic bookkeeping and tax return preparation, a qualified CPA or ACCA accountant is just as competent as a chartered accountant. The key distinction is between qualified and unqualified practitioners. If your company requires a statutory audit, you need a Registered Auditor, which requires membership of a recognised accountancy body. For most small businesses, the accountant's experience with your type of business matters more than which specific body they belong to.

<FAQItem question="Can I change accountants if I am not happy?">
Yes, you can change accountants at any time. The process involves giving notice to your current accountant, requesting a professional clearance letter, and ensuring your new accountant receives all relevant records and working papers. The best time to switch is after your year-end accounts and tax returns have been filed, but you can switch at any point if the relationship is not working. Your new accountant will manage the handover process.

<FAQItem question="Should I choose a local accountant or an online accountant?">
Both can work well depending on your needs. A local accountant offers face-to-face meetings, local business knowledge, and may have connections with your bank, solicitor, and other professionals. An online accountant often charges lower fees, may be more tech-savvy, and offers greater convenience if you prefer communicating digitally. For complex business structures or situations where face-to-face meetings add value (such as tax planning or Revenue audits), a local accountant may be preferable. For straightforward compliance, online can be excellent value.
