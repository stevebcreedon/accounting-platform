Payroll processing in Ireland typically costs EUR 5 to EUR 15 per employee per pay run when outsourced to an accountant or payroll bureau. For a company with five employees on monthly payroll, this means EUR 300 to EUR 900 per year in processing fees alone. However, the total cost of running payroll extends well beyond processing fees -- it includes software, employer PRSI contributions, pension obligations, and the administrative time required for compliance. This guide covers every aspect of payroll costs so you can budget accurately and choose the right approach for your business.

Understanding the full cost of payroll is essential for any Irish employer. Underestimating these costs is one of the most common financial mistakes new employers make, often leading to cash flow problems in the first year of hiring.

## Payroll Processing Fees

### Accountant Payroll Fees

Most accountants offer payroll as an add-on service to their annual accounting package. Typical pricing:

| Pay Frequency | Cost Per Employee Per Run | Annual Cost (5 employees) |
|---|---|---|
| Weekly | EUR 5-15 | EUR 1,300-3,900 |
| Fortnightly | EUR 5-12 | EUR 650-1,560 |
| Monthly | EUR 5-10 | EUR 300-600 |

The per-employee cost decreases as the number of employees increases because there is a fixed overhead for each pay run (logging in, submitting to Revenue, running reports) regardless of headcount. A company with twenty employees on monthly payroll might pay EUR 3 to EUR 7 per employee per run rather than EUR 5 to EUR 10.

### What Is Included in Accountant Payroll Processing

A standard payroll processing service from your accountant includes:

- Calculating gross-to-net pay for each employee
- Applying PAYE, PRSI, USC, and Local Property Tax (LPT) deductions
- Processing Revenue Payroll Notifications (RPNs) to ensure correct tax credits and rate bands
- Submitting payroll submissions to Revenue through the enhanced reporting system
- Generating payslips for each employee
- Processing statutory deductions (pension contributions, court orders, union dues)
- Providing monthly or per-period payroll reports
- Filing Revenue submissions within the statutory timeframe

### What Is Usually Charged Separately

- **New employee setup:** EUR 30 to EUR 75 per employee (registering with Revenue, setting up in payroll software)
- **Employee termination processing:** EUR 50 to EUR 100 (final payslip, P45 equivalent notification, holiday pay calculation)
- **Payroll year-end reconciliation:** EUR 100 to EUR 300 (reconciling all submissions, correcting discrepancies)
- **Maternity/paternity leave calculations:** EUR 50 to EUR 100 per occurrence
- **Revenue correspondence:** Handling queries or audits related to payroll

## Payroll Bureau Services

A payroll bureau is a specialist payroll processing company, as distinct from an accountant who offers payroll as an add-on. Bureaus process payroll as their core business and often offer more competitive pricing for companies with larger headcounts.

### Typical Bureau Pricing

| Headcount | Cost Per Employee Per Month | Monthly Total |
|---|---|---|
| 1-5 employees | EUR 10-20 | EUR 50-100 |
| 6-20 employees | EUR 7-15 | EUR 42-300 |
| 21-50 employees | EUR 5-10 | EUR 105-500 |
| 50+ employees | EUR 3-8 | Negotiable |

Bureau pricing typically includes a minimum monthly fee (EUR 50 to EUR 100) regardless of headcount, plus a per-employee charge. This makes bureaus more expensive than accountants for very small companies (one to three employees) but more competitive for larger teams.

### Advantages of a Payroll Bureau

- **Specialist expertise:** Payroll is their core business, not an add-on. They stay current with every Revenue change, rate update, and legislative development.
- **Dedicated support:** A named payroll contact who understands your setup and responds quickly to queries.
- **Software included:** The bureau provides the payroll software and maintains it. You do not need to purchase your own licence.
- **Scalability:** Adding or removing employees is straightforward with no software reconfiguration.
- **Compliance focus:** Bureaus invest heavily in compliance because payroll errors directly affect their reputation and client retention.

## The True Cost of Employing Someone in Ireland

Payroll processing fees are a small fraction of the total cost of employing someone. Understanding the full employment cost is critical for budgeting and pricing decisions.

### Employment Cost Breakdown

For an employee earning EUR 35,000 gross salary per year:

| Cost Component | Amount | Percentage of Gross |
|---|---|---|
| Gross salary | EUR 35,000 | 100% |
| Employer PRSI (11.05%) | EUR 3,868 | 11.05% |
| Pension contribution (est. 3-6%) | EUR 1,050-2,100 | 3-6% |
| Employer liability insurance | EUR 200-500 | 0.6-1.4% |
| Payroll processing | EUR 60-180 | 0.2-0.5% |
| Training / CPD | EUR 200-500 | 0.6-1.4% |
| Equipment / workspace | EUR 500-2,000 | 1.4-5.7% |
| **Total employer cost** | **EUR 40,878-43,148** | **117-123%** |

The employer's true cost is typically 17 to 23 per cent above the gross salary. For budgeting purposes, a common rule of thumb is to add 20 per cent to the gross salary to estimate the total employment cost.

### Employer PRSI

Employer PRSI is the single largest additional cost of employment. At 11.05 per cent of the employee's gross earnings, it adds EUR 3,868 to the cost of employing someone on EUR 35,000. There is no cap on employer PRSI -- it applies to all earnings.

The PRSI rate has remained at 11.05 per cent for several years, but it is subject to change in annual Budgets. For employees earning less than EUR 441 per week (EUR 22,932 per year), the employer PRSI rate is reduced to 8.8 per cent.

### Pension Auto-Enrolment

Ireland is introducing a pension auto-enrolment system that will require employers to contribute to employee pensions. While the exact commencement date and contribution rates are being finalised, the expected framework involves:

- **Employee contribution:** Starting at 1.5 per cent of gross salary, rising to 6 per cent over a phased period
- **Employer contribution:** Matching the employee contribution (1.5 per cent rising to 6 per cent)
- **State contribution:** An additional top-up of EUR 1 for every EUR 3 contributed by the employee

For planning purposes, budget for an additional 3 to 6 per cent of gross salary in employer pension contributions within the next few years. On a EUR 35,000 salary, this is EUR 1,050 to EUR 2,100 per year.

## In-House vs Outsourced Payroll

### When to Run Payroll In-House

Running payroll in-house makes sense when:

- You have a dedicated office manager or bookkeeper who can handle the processing
- You have more than ten employees (the cost savings over outsourcing become meaningful)
- You need tight control over payroll timing and adjustments
- You have complex payroll requirements (multiple pay rates, shift patterns, commission structures) that require detailed knowledge of your operations

The cost of in-house payroll is primarily the software licence (EUR 200 to EUR 600 per year) plus the time of the person processing it. If your office manager spends two hours per month on payroll at an effective cost of EUR 25 per hour, the annual time cost is EUR 600. Total in-house cost: approximately EUR 800 to EUR 1,200 per year for software plus time.

### When to Outsource Payroll

Outsourcing makes sense when:

- You have fewer than ten employees and the time cost of learning and maintaining payroll knowledge is not justified
- You do not have a staff member with payroll competence
- You want to eliminate the risk of payroll errors and Revenue penalties
- Your accountant offers payroll at a competitive rate as part of a bundled engagement

For a company with five employees on monthly payroll, outsourced payroll costs EUR 300 to EUR 600 per year through an accountant. This is comparable to the in-house cost but eliminates the learning curve, error risk, and time commitment.

## Payroll Frequency Considerations

The frequency of your payroll directly affects processing costs and administrative burden.

### Weekly Payroll

- **Processing cost:** 52 pay runs per year (highest annual cost)
- **Employee preference:** Many employees in sectors like construction, retail, and hospitality prefer weekly pay
- **Administrative burden:** High -- weekly submissions to Revenue, weekly payslip generation
- **Cash flow impact:** Requires disciplined weekly cash management

### Fortnightly Payroll

- **Processing cost:** 26 pay runs per year (moderate annual cost)
- **A compromise:** Balances employee preference for regular pay with reduced administrative burden
- **Less common:** Fewer businesses use fortnightly payroll in Ireland compared to weekly or monthly

### Monthly Payroll

- **Processing cost:** 12 pay runs per year (lowest annual cost)
- **Standard for salaried staff:** Most office-based and professional employees are paid monthly
- **Administrative burden:** Lowest -- twelve submissions per year
- **Cash flow:** Requires employees to budget monthly, which some find challenging

Switching from weekly to monthly payroll can reduce your processing costs by 75 per cent. However, this must be done with employee agreement and appropriate notice. Some employment contracts specify pay frequency, and changing it requires the employee's consent.

## Revenue Penalties for Payroll Errors

Revenue takes payroll compliance seriously. Common penalties and consequences include:

**Late filing.** Employers must submit payroll data to Revenue on or before the pay date. Late submissions can result in interest charges and, for persistent offenders, escalating compliance interventions.

**Incorrect deductions.** Under-deducting PAYE, PRSI, or USC makes the employer liable for the shortfall. Revenue can assess the employer for the underpaid tax plus interest.

**Failure to operate PAYE.** If an employer pays employees without operating PAYE (off the books), Revenue can estimate the PAYE, PRSI, and USC that should have been deducted and assess the employer for the full amount plus penalties.

**Enhanced reporting non-compliance.** The enhanced reporting system requires employers to report certain non-taxable payments and benefits. Non-compliance can result in fixed penalties.

The cost of payroll errors can be substantial -- a single audit finding of incorrect PRSI classification for a director could result in back-payments of thousands of euros. Professional payroll processing is a cost-effective insurance policy against these risks.

## Payroll for Company Directors

Company directors in Ireland have specific payroll considerations:

**Director as employee.** If a director draws a salary, they must be on the company payroll with PAYE, PRSI, and USC deducted at source. Directors cannot simply withdraw money from the company without proper payroll processing.

**PRSI class.** Directors holding more than 15 per cent of the company's share capital are Class S PRSI contributors (self-employed rate) for their directorship income. Directors with less than 15 per cent are Class A (employee rate). The distinction affects both the PRSI rate and the social welfare entitlements.

**Benefit-in-kind (BIK).** If the company provides the director with a car, health insurance, or other benefits, these must be processed through payroll as benefit-in-kind. BIK calculations add complexity and may increase your payroll processing costs.

**Salary vs dividend.** The balance between director salary and dividend payments affects both the company's and the director's tax position. Your accountant advises on the optimal split, but the salary component must be processed through payroll.
