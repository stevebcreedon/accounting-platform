Running a restaurant, cafe, pub, or hotel in Ireland involves a unique set of accounting and tax challenges. The hospitality sector has its own VAT rates, specific employment rules, licensing requirements, and cash flow patterns that differ from most other businesses. Revenue pays close attention to the hospitality sector, and targeted compliance campaigns are common. This guide covers the specific accounting considerations for Irish hospitality businesses, from VAT treatment and tip taxation to staff costs, licensing, and managing the seasonal nature of the industry.

Whether you are opening your first cafe or managing an established restaurant, getting your accounting right from the start saves time, money, and stress. The margins in hospitality are tight, and understanding your costs and tax obligations is essential for profitability.

## VAT Rates for Hospitality in Ireland

The hospitality sector in Ireland benefits from a reduced VAT rate on many of its core activities. Understanding which rate applies to each element of your business is critical for compliance and pricing.

### The 9 Per Cent Rate

The 9 per cent VAT rate applies to:

- Restaurant and catering services (food and drink for consumption on the premises or prepared for takeaway with a service element)
- Hotel and guest house accommodation
- Admission to cinemas, theatres, museums, and art galleries
- Use of sporting facilities (gyms, swimming pools)
- Amusement services and theme parks
- Hairdressing services

This rate has had a turbulent history. It was temporarily reduced from 13.5 per cent to 9 per cent in 2011 to support the tourism industry, raised back to 13.5 per cent, reduced again during the COVID-19 pandemic, and has been subject to further changes. Always verify the current applicable rate with Revenue or your accountant, as it may change with each Finance Act.

### The 13.5 Per Cent Rate

Some hospitality-adjacent services are taxed at 13.5 per cent, including:

- Short-term car hire
- Cleaning services
- Certain repair and maintenance services

### The 23 Per Cent Rate

The standard 23 per cent VAT rate applies to:

- Alcohol (whether served at the bar, in the restaurant, or sold off-licence)
- Soft drinks served on premises
- Takeaway food that is not prepared as a catering service (for example, unheated food sold over a counter without plates or cutlery)
- Vending machine sales
- Conference and meeting room hire (in some circumstances)

### Split Bills and Composite Supplies

A typical restaurant bill may include items at different VAT rates: the food at 9 per cent and the wine at 23 per cent. Your EPOS system must be configured to apply the correct rate to each item. Revenue expects your VAT return to accurately reflect the split between different rates. Getting this wrong, even unintentionally, is a common audit finding.

For hotels, accommodation and breakfast as part of a B&B rate is charged at 9 per cent. However, if additional services are provided (minibar, laundry, room service), these may attract different rates. Conference packages that include accommodation, meals, and room hire should be split by VAT rate.

## Tips and Service Charges

The tax treatment of tips and service charges is an important area for hospitality businesses, particularly since the Payment of Wages (Amendment) (Tips and Gratuities) Act 2022.

### Cash Tips

Cash tips received directly by employees are the employee's own income. Legally, the employee is responsible for declaring this income to Revenue. In practice, many hospitality employees fail to declare cash tips, which creates a compliance risk for both the employee and the employer.

As an employer, you are not responsible for PAYE on cash tips that you have no control over. However, if you collect cash tips and distribute them to staff, you may be considered to be making a payment of wages, and PAYE obligations may arise.

### Electronic Tips (Card Tips)

Tips paid by card are processed through your payment system. Under the 2022 Act, you must distribute these tips to employees in a fair and transparent manner. Because card tips pass through your accounts, they are clearly identifiable as income, and PAYE, USC, and PRSI must be deducted.

Card tips should be recorded separately in your accounts. They are the employees' income, not the business's revenue. When distributing card tips, operate payroll deductions as you would for any other payment to employees.

### Service Charges

A mandatory service charge added to the bill is different from a voluntary tip. A service charge is revenue of the business and must be included in your VAT-able turnover. If you distribute the service charge to staff, it is treated as wages and subject to PAYE deductions.

The 2022 Act requires transparency: if you add a service charge, you must clearly display what percentage goes to staff. Customers have the right to know how their money is distributed.

### Contract Tips (Tips Through Platform Apps)

For hospitality businesses using delivery platforms or booking apps where tips are collected through the platform, the platform typically remits the tips to you. You must then distribute them to the relevant employees and operate PAYE.

## Licensing Requirements and Costs

Operating a licensed premises in Ireland requires a licence from the Revenue Commissioners (for the sale of alcohol) and compliance with the Licensing Acts.

### Types of Licences

- **Publican's licence:** Required for pubs and bars
- **Restaurant certificate:** Allows the sale of alcohol with meals in a restaurant that does not have a full publican's licence
- **Wine retailer's on-licence:** Allows the sale of wine for consumption on premises
- **Off-licence:** Required for selling alcohol for consumption off the premises
- **Special exemption orders:** Allow extended opening hours for specific events

### Licence Costs

The annual licence duty varies by type but is relatively modest compared to the overall cost of operating a licensed premises. The bigger costs are legal fees for licence applications and renewals, particularly if objections are raised.

From an accounting perspective, licence fees and related legal costs are deductible business expenses. The cost of the initial licence (if purchased) is a capital asset and not deductible as a revenue expense, though it may qualify for capital allowances in certain circumstances.

## EPOS Systems and Record Keeping

Electronic Point of Sale (EPOS) systems are effectively mandatory for hospitality businesses in Ireland. Revenue expects hospitality businesses to maintain contemporaneous records of all transactions, and an EPOS system is the most practical way to achieve this.

Your EPOS system should:

- Record every transaction with a timestamp
- Apply the correct VAT rate to each item
- Distinguish between cash, card, and other payment methods
- Track tips and service charges separately
- Produce end-of-day reports (Z-reads) showing total sales by category and payment method
- Retain transaction data for at least 6 years

Revenue auditors will examine your EPOS data closely. They compare Z-reads to bank lodgements, check for voids and refunds, and analyse patterns that might suggest under-reporting of cash sales. Gaps in Z-read sequences or missing end-of-day reports are red flags.

You must retain all EPOS data, including individual transaction records, for at least 6 years. Many modern cloud-based systems retain data indefinitely, but ensure you have reliable backups.

## Food Safety and HACCP Costs

All food businesses in Ireland must comply with food safety regulations and implement a HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points) plan. The costs associated with food safety compliance are fully deductible business expenses:

- HACCP training for staff
- Food safety consultancy fees
- Temperature monitoring equipment
- Food safety signage and documentation
- Environmental health officer inspection costs (if any)
- Pest control services
- Cleaning chemicals and hygiene supplies

These costs are ongoing and should be budgeted as regular business expenses.

## Staff Costs in Hospitality

Labour costs are typically the largest expense for hospitality businesses, often representing 30 to 40 per cent of turnover. Managing staff costs requires understanding minimum wage rules, employer PRSI, and industry-specific employment regulations.

### Minimum Wage

The national minimum wage in Ireland is reviewed annually. Sub-minimum rates apply to employees under 20, with the rate increasing with age. Hospitality businesses must comply with these minimum rates, and Revenue and the Workplace Relations Commission conduct compliance checks.

### Employer PRSI

Employer PRSI is currently 11.05 per cent on employee earnings above EUR 441 per week. For lower-paid employees (earnings below EUR 441 per week), the employer PRSI rate is 8.8 per cent. Given the high number of employees in hospitality and the prevalence of part-time work, employer PRSI is a significant cost.

### Joint Labour Committee (JLC) Rates

The hospitality sector has a JLC that sets minimum rates of pay, including Sunday premiums and other conditions. The current Employment Regulation Order (ERO) for the hospitality sector, if in force, may set rates above the national minimum wage. Check the current ERO through the Workplace Relations Commission website.

### Part-Time and Casual Workers

Hospitality businesses frequently employ part-time, casual, and seasonal workers. All employees, regardless of hours worked, are entitled to a written statement of terms, paid annual leave (pro-rata), public holiday entitlements, and PRSI coverage. You must operate PAYE on all payments, even for workers doing only a few hours per week.

### Staff Meals

Providing meals to employees during their shift is a common practice in hospitality. From a tax perspective, a light meal provided to staff during their working hours is generally not treated as a benefit-in-kind (BIK) and is not taxable for the employee. The cost of staff meals is a deductible business expense.

## Seasonal Cash Flow Management

Hospitality businesses in Ireland experience significant seasonal variations. Restaurants and hotels in tourist areas may earn 50 to 60 per cent of their annual revenue during the summer months. Pubs and restaurants in cities see peaks around Christmas and quieter periods in January and February.

Managing cash flow through the quiet season requires discipline:

**Build a cash reserve during peak season.** Set aside a portion of peak-season profits to cover fixed costs during the quiet months. Fixed costs (rent, insurance, loan repayments, permanent staff wages) do not reduce just because revenue drops.

**Adjust staffing levels.** Use part-time and casual workers to scale your labour costs with demand. However, be mindful of employees' rights regarding contracted hours and minimum notice periods.

**Negotiate supplier terms.** Many food and drink suppliers offer credit terms. Negotiate longer terms during quiet periods and take advantage of prompt payment discounts during peak season.

**Plan VAT and tax payments.** Your VAT liability will be higher during peak periods. Ensure you set aside the VAT collected during busy months so that you have funds available when the return is due.

**Review your menu and pricing.** Quiet periods are an opportunity to review food costs, eliminate low-margin items, and adjust pricing for the upcoming season.

## Revenue Compliance in Hospitality

Revenue conducts targeted compliance campaigns in the hospitality sector. Common areas of focus include:

**Unrecorded cash sales.** Revenue uses mark-up analysis (comparing your declared gross profit margin to industry norms), purchase-to-sales ratio tests, and bank deposit analysis to identify potential under-declaration of cash sales.

**Tip income.** Revenue is increasingly focused on whether tip income is being properly declared and taxed, particularly electronic tips that pass through the business's accounts.

**Employment status.** Revenue checks whether workers in hospitality are properly classified as employees (not contractors) and whether PAYE is being operated correctly.

**VAT rate application.** Applying the wrong VAT rate (for example, charging 9 per cent on alcohol sales instead of 23 per cent) is a common finding in hospitality audits.

The best approach is to maintain complete, accurate records, ensure your EPOS system is properly configured, and work with an accountant experienced in the hospitality sector.

## Allowable Expenses Summary

Hospitality businesses can deduct all ordinary and necessary business expenses, including:

- Food and drink costs (cost of goods sold)
- Staff wages and employer PRSI
- Rent and rates
- Utilities (electricity, gas, water)
- Insurance (public liability, employer's liability, premises, stock)
- Equipment and furniture (capital allowances)
- Marketing and advertising
- Cleaning and maintenance
- Accounting and legal fees
- EPOS and software subscriptions
- Licence fees and renewal costs
- HACCP and food safety compliance costs
- Bank charges and payment processing fees
- Waste disposal and recycling
