Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT) is a withholding tax system in Ireland that applies to payments made by principal contractors to subcontractors in certain industries. The principal contractor deducts tax from the payment at one of three rates (0 per cent, 20 per cent, or 35 per cent) and remits it to Revenue. The subcontractor then claims credit for the RCT deducted against their income tax or corporation tax liability.

RCT was introduced to address the high levels of tax non-compliance in the construction industry and has since been extended to forestry and meat processing. If your business operates in any of these sectors as either a principal contractor or a subcontractor, understanding RCT is essential to avoid penalties and ensure you are not overpaying tax. This guide explains how the system works, your obligations, and how to manage RCT through the Revenue Online Service (ROS).

## Which Industries Does RCT Apply To?

RCT applies to payments made under relevant contracts in three specific industries in Ireland.

### Construction

Construction is the primary industry covered by RCT. The definition of construction operations is broad and includes:

- Building, demolition, and alteration of structures
- Civil engineering works (roads, bridges, tunnels)
- Installation of heating, lighting, drainage, and ventilation systems
- Painting, plastering, and decorating
- Earthmoving, excavation, and land drainage
- Laying of pipes and cables
- Manufacturing and delivery of materials where the manufacturer also installs them

The key test is whether the work is carried out under a contract that relates to construction operations. Subcontractors performing any of these activities for a principal contractor are within the scope of RCT.

### Forestry

RCT applies to forestry operations, including land clearance, planting, thinning, felling, and haulage of timber from the forest to the nearest public road. The forestry sector was brought within RCT to address compliance issues similar to those in construction.

### Meat Processing

Payments by meat processing companies to subcontractors who carry out meat processing operations are within the scope of RCT. This includes slaughtering, deboning, and packaging operations performed by subcontract labour.

## The Three RCT Deduction Rates

Revenue assigns one of three deduction rates to each subcontractor based on their tax compliance history. The rate determines how much tax the principal contractor must withhold from each payment.

### 0 Per Cent Rate

The 0 per cent rate means no tax is deducted from payments to the subcontractor. This rate is assigned to subcontractors who have a strong record of tax compliance. To qualify for the 0 per cent rate, the subcontractor must meet all of the following conditions:

- All tax returns are up to date and filed on time
- All tax payments are up to date with no outstanding liabilities
- The subcontractor has been registered with Revenue for at least 3 years (or the principal of the business has been in business for at least 3 years)
- Revenue is satisfied with the subcontractor's overall compliance record

The 0 per cent rate is the most favourable and means the subcontractor receives the full contract payment without any RCT deduction. The subcontractor is still liable for income tax or corporation tax on the profits and must file their tax returns as normal.

### 20 Per Cent Rate

The 20 per cent rate is the standard rate applied to subcontractors who are registered with Revenue and have a reasonable compliance record but do not meet all the criteria for the 0 per cent rate. Common reasons for a 20 per cent rate include:

- The subcontractor has been registered for less than 3 years
- There are minor compliance issues (occasional late filing)
- The subcontractor is newly registered and has no compliance history

At the 20 per cent rate, the principal contractor deducts 20 per cent from the gross payment (excluding VAT) and remits it to Revenue. The subcontractor receives 80 per cent of the payment and claims credit for the 20 per cent withheld against their tax liability.

### 35 Per Cent Rate

The 35 per cent rate is the highest rate and is applied to subcontractors who are either not registered with Revenue or have a poor compliance history. This rate applies automatically if:

- The subcontractor is not registered for tax in Ireland
- Revenue cannot verify the subcontractor's identity or tax registration
- The subcontractor has serious compliance issues (outstanding returns, significant tax debts)

At 35 per cent, the principal contractor deducts more than a third of the payment. This creates a strong incentive for subcontractors to register with Revenue and maintain their compliance to qualify for a lower rate.

## Principal Contractor Obligations

If you are a principal contractor, you have specific obligations under the RCT system. Failure to meet these obligations can result in penalties and interest.

### Registering as a Principal Contractor

You must register as a principal contractor with Revenue if you engage subcontractors to carry out construction, forestry, or meat processing operations. Registration is done through ROS.

Principal contractors include not only construction companies but also government bodies, local authorities, semi-state bodies, and any person or company that engages subcontractors for relevant operations. If you are a homeowner hiring a builder for a domestic project, you are generally not a principal contractor. The obligation typically arises in a business-to-business context.

### Contract Notification

Before making any payment to a subcontractor, you must notify Revenue of the contract through ROS. The contract notification includes details of the subcontractor (name, tax reference number), the nature of the work, and the estimated value of the contract.

Revenue uses the contract notification to verify the subcontractor's identity and tax registration and to assign the appropriate deduction rate. You must wait for Revenue's response before making the payment.

### Payment Notification

Each time you make a payment to a subcontractor under a notified contract, you must submit a payment notification to Revenue through ROS. The payment notification includes:

- The subcontractor's details
- The gross payment amount (excluding VAT)
- The VAT amount (if applicable)
- The deduction rate assigned by Revenue
- The RCT amount deducted
- The net payment made to the subcontractor

Payment notifications must be submitted on or before the date the payment is made.

### Deduction Authorisation

After submitting the payment notification, Revenue issues a deduction authorisation confirming the rate to apply and the amount of RCT to deduct. You must retain the deduction authorisation as evidence that you applied the correct rate.

### Monthly RCT Return

Principal contractors must file a monthly RCT return (Form RCT Monthly Return) through ROS by the 23rd of the month following the month in which payments were made. The return summarises all payments made to subcontractors during the month, the deductions applied, and the total RCT remitted to Revenue.

### Payment of RCT to Revenue

The RCT deducted must be remitted to Revenue by the 23rd of the month following the month of deduction (aligning with the monthly return deadline). Late payment attracts interest at 0.0274 per cent per day.

## Subcontractor Obligations

If you are a subcontractor working in construction, forestry, or meat processing, you also have specific obligations under RCT.

### Tax Registration

You must be registered for tax with Revenue to receive a deduction rate of 0 per cent or 20 per cent. Without registration, the 35 per cent rate applies automatically, meaning you lose more than a third of each payment to RCT withholding.

Register for income tax (sole traders) or corporation tax (companies) and ensure your tax reference number is correct and up to date.

### Maintaining Compliance

Your deduction rate is directly linked to your tax compliance. To maintain the 0 per cent rate or to be considered for an upgrade from 20 per cent, you must:

- File all tax returns (income tax, VAT, corporation tax) on time
- Pay all taxes on time or within agreed arrangements
- Respond promptly to any Revenue correspondence
- Keep your tax registration details up to date

### Claiming Credit for RCT Deducted

RCT deducted from your payments is not a final tax. It is a payment on account of your income tax or corporation tax liability. When you file your annual tax return, you include the RCT deducted as a credit against your tax liability. If the RCT deducted exceeds your tax liability, you can claim a refund from Revenue.

For self-employed subcontractors filing a Form 11, the RCT credit is entered in the tax credits section. For companies filing a CT1, the RCT credit is included in the preliminary tax and corporation tax calculation.

### Requesting a Rate Review

If you believe your deduction rate is incorrect or if your compliance has improved, you can request a rate review through ROS. Revenue reviews rates periodically (at least quarterly) but you can request an ad hoc review at any time. Rate reviews typically result in a downgrade to a lower rate if your compliance has improved.

## RCT and VAT

RCT and VAT interact in a specific way. RCT is deducted from the gross payment before VAT, not from the total invoice amount including VAT. This means:

If a subcontractor invoices EUR 10,000 plus EUR 2,300 VAT (at 23 per cent), the total invoice is EUR 12,300. The principal contractor applies the RCT rate to the EUR 10,000 (the pre-VAT amount). At a 20 per cent RCT rate:

- RCT deduction: EUR 2,000 (20% of EUR 10,000)
- VAT: EUR 2,300
- Net payment to subcontractor: EUR 8,000 (EUR 10,000 minus EUR 2,000)
- Total payment from principal: EUR 10,300 (EUR 8,000 net plus EUR 2,300 VAT)

The VAT is paid in full to the subcontractor. The RCT deduction only affects the net payment.

### Reverse Charge VAT in Construction

Since 1 September 2008, a VAT reverse charge applies to construction services between VAT-registered subcontractors and principal contractors. Under the reverse charge, the subcontractor does not charge VAT on the invoice. Instead, the principal contractor self-accounts for VAT on the supply.

This means the subcontractor's invoice in the construction sector is typically for the net amount only, with no VAT line. The principal contractor accounts for both the output VAT and the corresponding input VAT on their own VAT return.

The reverse charge applies to the construction sector only, not to forestry or meat processing.

## Annual Rate Review

Revenue conducts an annual review of all subcontractor deduction rates, typically at the start of each year. The review considers the subcontractor's compliance record for the preceding period, including:

- Whether all tax returns were filed on time
- Whether all tax payments were made on time
- Whether there are any outstanding tax debts or payment arrangements
- The overall compliance history

Subcontractors who have improved their compliance may see their rate reduced (for example, from 35 per cent to 20 per cent, or from 20 per cent to 0 per cent). Conversely, subcontractors whose compliance has deteriorated may see their rate increased.

Rate changes take effect automatically after the annual review. Revenue notifies both the principal contractor and the subcontractor of any rate changes through ROS.

## Common Compliance Errors

Several common errors can cause problems for both principal contractors and subcontractors.

**Not submitting contract notifications.** Principal contractors must notify Revenue of each contract before making the first payment. Failing to notify can result in penalties and may mean the wrong deduction rate is applied.

**Late payment notifications.** Payment notifications must be submitted on or before the payment date. Late submission can attract penalties and creates a compliance risk.

**Applying the wrong rate.** Principal contractors must apply the rate specified in the deduction authorisation from Revenue. Applying a lower rate (or no deduction at all) can make the principal contractor liable for the shortfall.

**Not claiming RCT credits.** Subcontractors who do not claim credit for RCT deducted on their tax returns effectively overpay their taxes. Ensure all RCT deductions are captured in your annual return.

**Ignoring rate reviews.** Subcontractors should actively monitor their deduction rate and request reviews when their compliance improves. Remaining on a 20 per cent or 35 per cent rate when you qualify for a lower rate costs you cash flow throughout the year.

**Mixing RCT and non-RCT work.** Some subcontractors perform both construction and non-construction work. RCT only applies to relevant operations. Ensure your invoicing clearly distinguishes between RCT-applicable work and other services.

## Summary

Relevant Contracts Tax is a withholding tax system that applies to payments between principal contractors and subcontractors in construction, forestry, and meat processing in Ireland. Revenue assigns deduction rates of 0 per cent, 20 per cent, or 35 per cent based on the subcontractor's tax compliance history.

Principal contractors must register with Revenue, notify contracts, submit payment notifications, and file monthly RCT returns through ROS. Subcontractors must register for tax and maintain their compliance to secure the most favourable deduction rate.

RCT deducted is not a final tax but a payment on account. Subcontractors claim credit for all RCT deducted against their income tax or corporation tax liability and can claim refunds where deductions exceed the liability.

For businesses in the affected industries, understanding and complying with RCT is not optional. Non-compliance attracts penalties and interest, and a poor compliance record means a higher deduction rate, which directly affects your cash flow.
