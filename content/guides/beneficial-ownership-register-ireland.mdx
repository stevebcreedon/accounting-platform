Every Irish company must identify its beneficial owners and file that information with the Central Register of Beneficial Ownership of Companies and Industrial and Provident Societies (RBO). A beneficial owner is any individual who ultimately owns or controls the company, typically through holding more than 25 per cent of the shares or voting rights. This requirement stems from EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives and is implemented in Ireland through the European Union (Anti-Money Laundering: Beneficial Ownership of Corporate Entities) Regulations 2019.

This guide explains what beneficial ownership means, who must file, how to register with the RBO, the deadlines you must meet, and the penalties for non-compliance.

## What Is Beneficial Ownership

Beneficial ownership refers to the natural persons (individuals, not companies) who ultimately own or control a legal entity. The concept looks through the formal legal structure to identify the real human beings who benefit from or exercise control over the company.

Under the Regulations, a beneficial owner of a company is any individual who:

- **Holds more than 25 per cent of the shares** in the company, directly or indirectly
- **Holds more than 25 per cent of the voting rights** in the company, directly or indirectly
- **Exercises control over the company** through other means

### Direct and Indirect Ownership

Ownership can be direct (the individual personally holds shares) or indirect (the individual controls another entity that holds shares). For example, if Person A owns 100 per cent of Company X, and Company X owns 30 per cent of Company Y, then Person A is an indirect beneficial owner of Company Y because their effective interest exceeds 25 per cent.

Tracing beneficial ownership through multiple layers of corporate structure can be complex. Each company in the chain must identify and report its own beneficial owners, looking through the entire ownership structure to the ultimate natural persons.

### When No Individual Meets the 25 Per Cent Threshold

In some companies, no single individual holds more than 25 per cent of the shares or voting rights. In this situation, the company must determine whether any individual exercises control through other means, such as:

- The right to appoint or remove a majority of the board of directors
- The exercise of dominant influence through shareholder agreements
- Control through other contractual arrangements

If, after exhausting all possible means of identification, no beneficial owner can be identified, the company must register its **senior managing officials** as beneficial owners. For most Irish companies, this means the directors.

## Who Must File with the RBO

The obligation to file beneficial ownership information with the RBO applies to:

- All **private companies limited by shares** (LTDs)
- All **designated activity companies** (DACs)
- All **public limited companies** (PLCs)
- All **companies limited by guarantee** (CLGs)
- All **unlimited companies**
- All **industrial and provident societies** (co-ops)

The only exception is companies listed on a regulated market in the EU (or equivalent), which are subject to transparency requirements that already require disclosure of major shareholdings.

In practice, the vast majority of Irish companies must file with the RBO.

## Information Required for Registration

When filing with the RBO, the company must provide the following information for each beneficial owner:

- **Full name** (first name and surname)
- **Date of birth**
- **Nationality**
- **Residential address**
- **Statement of the nature and extent** of the interest held or control exercised
- **The date on which** the individual became a beneficial owner
- **PPS number** (for Irish residents) or, for non-residents, a passport or national identity card number and country of issue

The company must also confirm that it has taken all reasonable steps to identify its beneficial owners and that the information provided is accurate and complete.

## How to File with the RBO

Filing is done through the **RBO online portal** at rbo.gov.ie. The process is straightforward:

### Step 1: Register on the Portal

Create an account on the RBO portal. You will need the company's CRO number and a valid email address. The person filing (known as the presenter) is typically a director, the company secretary, or their accountant.

### Step 2: Identify Beneficial Owners

Before filing, determine who the beneficial owners are by examining the company's share register, constitution, and any shareholder agreements. Document the analysis, particularly where ownership is indirect or where control is exercised through means other than shareholding.

### Step 3: Enter Beneficial Owner Details

Log in to the portal and enter the required information for each beneficial owner. The portal validates the PPS number against Revenue records for Irish residents, so ensure the PPS number and name match exactly.

### Step 4: Submit the Filing

Review all information and submit. You will receive a confirmation email. There is **no filing fee** for RBO submissions.

### Step 5: Annual Confirmation

There is no annual return to the RBO. However, the company has an ongoing obligation to ensure the information on the register is accurate and up to date (see below regarding updates).

## Deadlines

### New Companies

A newly incorporated company must file its beneficial ownership information with the RBO within **five months of incorporation**. This deadline aligns broadly with the first CRO annual return date but is a separate filing obligation.

### Existing Companies

All existing companies should already have filed their initial beneficial ownership information. The original deadline for initial filing was November 2019, and any company that has not yet filed is in breach.

### Changes to Beneficial Ownership

If there is a change in the company's beneficial ownership (for example, a share transfer that changes who holds more than 25 per cent), the company must:

1. **Update its internal beneficial ownership register** within **14 days** of becoming aware of the change
2. **File the updated information with the RBO** without undue delay

In practice, this means you should file the update with the RBO as soon as possible after updating your internal register. Delays in filing changes leave the public register inaccurate and expose the company to penalties.

## The Company's Internal Register

In addition to filing with the RBO, every company must maintain its own **internal register of beneficial owners**. This register must contain all the information filed with the RBO plus any additional information obtained during the identification process.

The internal register must be kept at the company's registered office (or at another notified address within Ireland) and must be available for inspection by:

- The beneficial owners themselves
- Competent authorities (the Gardai, Revenue, the Central Bank, the FIU)
- Designated persons under AML legislation who are carrying out customer due diligence

The internal register must be retained for at least five years after the company is dissolved or the individual ceases to be a beneficial owner.

## Public Access to the RBO

The RBO is a **publicly accessible register**. Any member of the public can search the register and view the beneficial ownership information filed by any company. This was a deliberate policy choice under the EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives to promote transparency and deter the misuse of corporate structures.

The public can search the RBO at rbo.gov.ie by company name or CRO number. The information displayed includes the beneficial owners' names, month and year of birth, nationality, country of residence, and the nature and extent of their interest.

Full residential addresses and PPS numbers are not publicly accessible. These are only available to competent authorities.

### Restrictions on Access

In exceptional circumstances, a beneficial owner can apply to the Registrar to restrict access to their information. This is only granted where the beneficial owner can demonstrate that public access would expose them to a disproportionate risk of fraud, kidnapping, blackmail, extortion, harassment, violence, or intimidation.

Such applications are rare and are only granted in genuinely exceptional cases.

## EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives

The beneficial ownership register requirement derives from the **4th EU Anti-Money Laundering Directive** (2015/849) as amended by the **5th Anti-Money Laundering Directive** (2018/843). These directives require all EU member states to establish central registers of beneficial ownership for corporate entities and trusts.

The purpose is to increase transparency about who owns and controls companies, making it more difficult to use complex corporate structures to hide the proceeds of crime, evade tax, or finance terrorism.

Ireland implemented these requirements through the European Union (Anti-Money Laundering: Beneficial Ownership of Corporate Entities) Regulations 2019 (S.I. No. 110 of 2019).

The **6th Anti-Money Laundering Directive** (currently being finalised) will bring further changes, including potentially extending beneficial ownership registers to additional entity types and refining the definition of beneficial ownership.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Non-compliance with beneficial ownership obligations can result in serious penalties:

### For the Company

- Failure to file with the RBO or to maintain an internal register is an offence
- On summary conviction: a fine of up to **EUR 5,000**
- On conviction on indictment: a fine of up to **EUR 500,000**

### For Officers

- Directors and the company secretary who are in default can be held personally liable
- The same fine levels apply to officers as to the company
- Additionally, the court can make a disqualification order preventing an officer from acting as a director

### Practical Consequences

Beyond the statutory penalties, failure to file can:

- Create difficulties during due diligence for corporate transactions (mergers, acquisitions, funding rounds)
- Trigger concerns with banks and financial institutions during account opening or ongoing review
- Result in adverse findings during AML inspections by professional bodies
- Complicate the company's relationships with designated persons who must verify beneficial ownership as part of their CDD obligations

## Common Issues and Questions

**Nominee shareholders.** If shares are held by a nominee (someone who holds shares on behalf of another person), the beneficial owner is the person on whose behalf the shares are held, not the nominee. The company must look through nominee arrangements to identify the true beneficial owners.

**Trusts holding shares.** If a trust holds more than 25 per cent of a company's shares, the beneficial owners of the trust (settlor, trustees, beneficiaries, protector) may need to be identified as beneficial owners of the company, depending on the level of control they exercise.

**Changes during the year.** Beneficial ownership can change at any time through share transfers, new share issues, or changes in control arrangements. The obligation to update the RBO is ongoing and does not wait for any annual filing cycle.

**Foreign beneficial owners.** Non-Irish residents can be beneficial owners. They must provide their passport or national identity card number and country of issue instead of a PPS number.

**Newly incorporated companies.** Remember the five-month deadline from incorporation. This is separate from and additional to the CRO annual return deadline.
