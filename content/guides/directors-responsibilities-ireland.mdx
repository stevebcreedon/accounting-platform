Company directors in Ireland have eight statutory duties codified in Section 228 of the Companies Act 2014, along with fiduciary duties derived from common law. These duties apply to every director of every company registered in Ireland, regardless of whether the company is actively trading, dormant, or in the process of being wound up. Breach of these duties can result in personal liability, restriction from acting as a director, or disqualification.

Understanding your legal obligations as a director is essential before you incorporate a company or accept an appointment to a board. This guide sets out the eight statutory duties, explains fiduciary obligations, covers the personal liability scenarios that catch directors off guard, and outlines the compliance requirements that apply to Irish company directors.

## The Eight Statutory Duties

The Companies Act 2014 was the first Irish legislation to codify directors' duties in statute. Section 228 sets out eight principal duties that every director must observe:

### 1. Act in Good Faith in the Interests of the Company

A director must act in good faith in what the director considers to be the interests of the company. This means prioritising the company's interests over your own personal interests, the interests of individual shareholders, or the interests of any third party.

In practice, this duty requires you to make decisions that benefit the company as a whole, not just yourself as a shareholder or any particular group of stakeholders. If you are both a director and a majority shareholder, you must still consider the interests of minority shareholders and the company's creditors.

### 2. Act Honestly and Responsibly

A director must act honestly and responsibly in relation to the conduct of the affairs of the company. This is a broad duty that encompasses both the director's personal integrity and their approach to managing the company.

Acting honestly means not deceiving shareholders, creditors, or regulatory bodies. Acting responsibly means taking reasonable care in your management decisions and not being reckless or negligent in the conduct of the company's affairs.

### 3. Act in Accordance with the Company's Constitution

A director must act in accordance with the company's constitution and exercise powers only for the purposes allowed by law. The constitution sets out the rules governing the company's internal affairs, and directors must operate within those rules.

If you act outside the scope of the constitution, you may be personally liable for any resulting loss. If the constitution needs to be changed to accommodate a proposed course of action, it should be amended by special resolution of the shareholders before the action is taken.

### 4. Avoid Conflicts of Interest

A director must not place themselves in a position where their personal interests conflict, or may conflict, with the interests of the company. If a conflict arises, the director must declare their interest and, depending on the circumstances, may need to recuse themselves from the relevant decision.

Common conflict scenarios include:

- A director awarding a contract to a company they own or have an interest in
- A director competing with the company's business
- A director using company assets or information for personal gain
- A director taking a business opportunity that the company could have pursued

Section 231 of the Companies Act 2014 requires directors to declare any interest in a contract or proposed contract with the company. Failure to make this declaration is a criminal offence.

### 5. Do Not Use Company Property for Personal Benefit

A director must not use the company's property, information, or opportunities for their own benefit or the benefit of anyone else, unless the company's constitution permits it or the shareholders approve.

This duty covers tangible property (company vehicles, equipment, premises) and intangible property (trade secrets, customer lists, intellectual property). Even borrowing company assets temporarily without proper authorisation can constitute a breach.

### 6. Not to Fetter Discretion

A director must not agree to restrict their power to exercise an independent judgment unless this is permitted by the company's constitution or sanctioned by a shareholders' resolution. Each director must bring their own independent judgment to bear on every decision.

This means you cannot agree in advance to vote a particular way on board decisions, bind yourself to follow the instructions of a third party, or delegate your decision-making responsibility without retaining oversight.

### 7. Exercise Care, Skill, and Diligence

A director must exercise the care, skill, and diligence that would be exercised by a reasonable person who has both:

- The knowledge, skill, and experience that may reasonably be expected of a person in the same position
- The knowledge, skill, and experience that the director actually has

This is a dual objective/subjective test. You are measured against both a reasonable person in your position and your own actual capabilities. If you have specialist knowledge (for example, you are a qualified accountant serving as a director), you are held to a higher standard in areas related to your expertise.

### 8. Have Regard for the Interests of Employees and Members

A director must have regard to the interests of the company's employees in general, as well as the interests of its members (shareholders). This does not mean employees' interests override all other considerations, but they must be taken into account in decision-making.

This duty is particularly relevant in scenarios such as redundancies, restructuring, changes to terms and conditions of employment, or the sale of the business.

## Fiduciary Duties

In addition to the eight statutory duties, directors owe fiduciary duties to the company derived from common law. These overlap significantly with the statutory duties but have been developed through case law over centuries:

**Duty of loyalty:** Directors must act loyally towards the company and not place themselves in a position of conflict.

**Duty of care:** Directors must take reasonable care in managing the company's affairs and in making decisions on its behalf.

**Duty to act within powers:** Directors must exercise their powers for the purposes for which they were conferred and not for collateral purposes.

**Duty to account for profits:** If a director makes a personal profit from their position, they may be required to account for that profit to the company.

The codification of duties in Section 228 of the Companies Act 2014 was intended to make directors' obligations clearer and more accessible, but the common law fiduciary duties continue to apply alongside the statutory duties.

## Duty to Keep Proper Books of Account

Section 281 of the Companies Act 2014 imposes a specific and critically important duty on directors to ensure that the company keeps adequate accounting records. These records must:

- Correctly record and explain the transactions of the company
- Enable the financial position of the company to be determined at any time with reasonable accuracy
- Enable the directors to ensure that financial statements comply with the Act
- Enable the financial statements to be readily and properly audited

The accounting records must be kept at the company's registered office or at such other place as the directors think fit, and they must be available for inspection by the directors at all times.

If a company is wound up and it is found that proper books of account were not kept, every director and secretary who was in default is guilty of a criminal offence. The penalty is a fine of up to EUR 500,000 and/or imprisonment for up to 5 years.

This duty is one that Revenue and the Office of the Director of Corporate Enforcement (ODCE) take particularly seriously. Failure to keep proper books is often cited as a ground for restricting or disqualifying directors following a company's insolvency.

## Personal Liability Scenarios

While the limited company structure generally protects directors' personal assets, there are important exceptions where directors face personal liability:

### Reckless Trading (Section 610)

If a company goes into insolvent liquidation and it can be shown that a director was party to the carrying on of the company's business in a reckless manner, the court can declare the director personally liable for some or all of the company's debts.

A director is deemed to have been reckless if they ought to have known that their actions, or the company's actions, would cause loss to creditors. Indicators include continuing to trade when the company cannot pay its debts, taking on new obligations knowing the company cannot meet them, and failing to take steps to minimise potential loss to creditors.

### Fraudulent Trading (Section 722)

If a director carried on the business of the company with intent to defraud creditors or for any fraudulent purpose, the court can impose unlimited personal liability. Fraudulent trading is also a criminal offence, punishable by fine and imprisonment.

### Revenue Debts

Directors can be made personally liable for certain unpaid company taxes under Revenue's director liability provisions. This is most common for:

- **PAYE and PRSI:** Directors can be pursued for unpaid employee taxes that the company withheld but failed to remit to Revenue
- **VAT:** In certain circumstances, directors can be held liable for unpaid VAT
- **Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT):** Similar liability applies in the construction industry

Revenue typically pursues director liability where the company has gone into liquidation or is unable to pay, and where the directors failed to take reasonable steps to ensure the taxes were paid.

### Health and Safety

Under the Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005, directors can be personally liable if they consent to, connive in, or are negligent in relation to a health and safety offence committed by the company. Penalties include fines and imprisonment.

## Restriction and Disqualification

### Restriction (Section 819)

When a company goes into insolvent liquidation, the liquidator must report to the ODCE on the conduct of the company's directors. Unless the liquidator relieves the directors, the ODCE can apply to the High Court to have the directors restricted.

A restricted director can still act as a director, but only of a company that meets certain capitalisation requirements. A restricted person must ensure that any company they are a director of has a minimum allotted paid-up share capital of EUR 500,000 (for a PLC) or EUR 100,000 (for a private company).

Restriction lasts for 5 years from the date of the court order. During this period, the restricted director cannot be involved with any company that does not meet the capitalisation threshold.

To avoid restriction, a director must demonstrate that they acted honestly and responsibly in relation to the conduct of the company's affairs. This is the most common ground on which directors defend restriction applications.

### Disqualification (Section 842)

Disqualification is more severe than restriction. A disqualified person cannot act as a director of, or be involved in the management of, any company for the period of the disqualification.

Grounds for disqualification include:

- Conviction of an indictable offence in relation to a company
- Persistent default in filing requirements
- Fraudulent or reckless trading
- Being a director of an insolvent company while already restricted

Disqualification can be ordered for up to 5 years by the court, or indefinitely in the case of conviction for a serious offence.

## Directors' Compliance Statement

Section 225 of the Companies Act 2014 requires directors of certain companies to include a compliance statement in the company's annual report. This statement acknowledges the directors' responsibility to secure the company's compliance with its relevant obligations (company law, tax law, and other enactments).

The compliance statement must:

- Acknowledge that the directors are responsible for securing compliance
- Confirm that a compliance policy statement has been drawn up
- Confirm that appropriate arrangements and structures have been put in place to secure compliance
- State whether the arrangements have been reviewed during the financial year

This requirement applies to companies that exceed two of the following three thresholds: balance sheet total of EUR 12.5 million, turnover of EUR 25 million, and average number of 250 employees. Most small companies are therefore exempt, but the requirement signals the importance that Irish company law places on directors taking compliance seriously.

## Shadow and De Facto Directors

The duties and liabilities described above do not only apply to formally appointed directors. The Companies Act 2014 extends them to two additional categories:

**Shadow directors** (Section 221): A person in accordance with whose directions or instructions the directors of a company are accustomed to act. If you are not formally a director but you effectively control the board's decisions, you are a shadow director and subject to directors' duties.

**De facto directors** (Section 222): A person who, while not formally appointed as a director, occupies the position of director and carries out the functions of a director. If you act as if you are a director (signing contracts on behalf of the company, making management decisions, representing yourself as a director to third parties), you may be treated as a de facto director.

Both shadow and de facto directors can be subject to restriction, disqualification, and personal liability on the same basis as formally appointed directors.

## Practical Steps for Compliance

As a director of an Irish company, here are the practical steps to ensure you meet your obligations:

1. **Understand your duties.** Read Section 228 of the Companies Act 2014 and ensure you understand each of the eight statutory duties.

2. **Keep proper books of account.** Ensure the company maintains accurate and up-to-date accounting records. Engage a qualified accountant to assist.

3. **File on time.** Ensure the annual return is filed within 28 days of the annual return date. Set calendar reminders well in advance.

4. **Monitor the company's financial position.** Review management accounts regularly. If the company is experiencing financial difficulties, take immediate advice.

5. **Declare conflicts of interest.** If you have a personal interest in any contract or transaction involving the company, declare it in writing to the board.

6. **Attend board meetings.** Participate actively in board decisions. Absence does not absolve you of responsibility.

7. **Keep minutes.** Record all board decisions and the reasoning behind them. Good minutes can demonstrate that you acted honestly and responsibly.

8. **Take professional advice.** When facing complex decisions, take legal or financial advice. A director who acts on professional advice is more likely to be found to have acted responsibly.
