Switching accountants in Ireland is more straightforward than most business owners expect. There is no lock-in period, no penalty for leaving, and a well-established professional process for handing over your affairs from one accountant to another. Yet many business owners stay with an accountant they are unhappy with for years because they do not know how the process works, fear it will be disruptive, or worry about offending someone they have a personal relationship with.

Your accountant works for you. If the relationship is not delivering value, you have every right to change. This guide walks you through the entire process: recognising when it is time to switch, managing the professional clearance procedure, timing the transition, and ensuring a smooth handover.

## Signs It Is Time to Switch Accountants

Not every frustration warrants changing accountants. But if you recognise several of these patterns, the relationship is probably not working:

**Missed deadlines.** If your accountant has missed filing deadlines, causing you to incur late filing surcharges (5 to 10 per cent of your tax liability) or late CRO penalties (EUR 100 plus EUR 3 per day), this is a fundamental failure. Deadline management is the most basic service an accountant provides.

**Poor communication.** You cannot get a response to emails within a reasonable time (2 to 3 business days for routine queries). Phone calls go unreturned. You only hear from your accountant when they need something from you, never proactively. You feel like you are chasing your own accountant.

**No proactive advice.** Your accountant files your returns and sends you a bill, but never suggests ways to reduce your tax liability, restructure for efficiency, or plan ahead. Tax compliance is the minimum. Tax planning is where the real value lies.

**Unexplained fee increases.** Your fees have risen significantly without any explanation, additional services, or change in the complexity of your affairs. Annual fee reviews are normal, but increases should be justified and communicated in advance.

**You have outgrown them.** Your business has grown from a simple sole trade to a limited company with employees, VAT registration, and international sales. Your accountant was a good fit when you started, but may not have the expertise to handle your current complexity.

**Errors in your returns.** Mistakes in your tax returns, company accounts, or Revenue filings are serious. An occasional error is human; a pattern of errors suggests a competence issue. Errors that result in penalties or overpaid tax are unacceptable.

**Lack of technology adoption.** Your accountant insists on paper records, desktop software, or manual processes when cloud accounting platforms offer better efficiency, real-time data access, and collaborative working. In 2026, an accountant who does not use cloud software is limiting your business's financial visibility.

**They are hard to reach when it matters.** During your year end, at tax deadlines, or when Revenue contacts you, your accountant should be available and responsive. If they disappear at the times you need them most, the relationship is not working.

## The Professional Clearance Process

When you change accountants in Ireland, there is a formal procedure called professional clearance (sometimes called professional etiquette or professional handover). This process is governed by the ethical codes of the professional accounting bodies (CAI, CPA Ireland, ACCA, CIMA) and exists to protect clients and maintain professional standards.

Here is how it works:

### Step 1: You Engage Your New Accountant

Before anything else, find and engage your new accountant. Sign a letter of engagement with them. Until this is done, you do not have anyone to manage the transition.

Your new accountant should explain the handover process and manage it entirely on your behalf. You should not need to coordinate between the two firms yourself.

### Step 2: Your New Accountant Writes to Your Old Accountant

Your new accountant sends a professional clearance letter to your outgoing accountant. This letter:

- Confirms they have been appointed as your new accountant
- Requests confirmation that there are no professional reasons why they should not accept the appointment
- Requests the handover of your records and working papers

The professional clearance letter is a requirement of the professional bodies. Your new accountant cannot begin work until they have received a response.

### Step 3: Your Old Accountant Responds

Your outgoing accountant is professionally obligated to respond to the clearance letter promptly -- typically within 14 to 21 days. They must either:

- Confirm there are no professional reasons why the new accountant should not act (the standard response in the vast majority of cases)
- Disclose any professional reasons, which are limited to specific circumstances such as outstanding fees

The most common reason for a delayed or qualified clearance response is unpaid fees. If you owe your outgoing accountant money, settle this before or during the handover to avoid complications.

### Step 4: Records Are Transferred

Once clearance is received, your outgoing accountant transfers your records to your new accountant. This includes:

- **Your own records:** Source documents you provided (invoices, receipts, bank statements, contracts). These belong to you and must be returned.
- **Statutory records:** Company books, registers, and documents that belong to the company.
- **Working papers:** The accountant's own working papers (their analysis, computations, and notes). These technically belong to the outgoing accountant, but in practice, most will provide copies to facilitate a smooth handover. If there are outstanding fees, the outgoing accountant may exercise a lien (right to retain documents until payment is received).

Your new accountant will provide a checklist of what they need to receive to take over your affairs.

## What Your New Accountant Needs

To hit the ground running, your new accountant needs a comprehensive set of information. The more complete the handover, the smoother the transition. Key items include:

**Tax records and returns:**
- Copies of your most recent filed tax returns (Form 11 or CT1)
- Corporation tax computations for the last 2 to 3 years
- Any Revenue correspondence, including open queries or ongoing audits
- VAT registration details and recent VAT3 returns
- Payroll records and PAYE registration details

**Company records (for limited companies):**
- The last 2 to 3 years of signed financial statements
- CRO annual returns and confirmation of filing status
- Company constitution (formerly Memorandum and Articles of Association)
- Register of members, directors, and secretaries
- Share register and any share transfer documentation

**Bookkeeping and accounting data:**
- Access to your accounting software (Xero, Sage, QuickBooks) -- your new accountant will need to be added as an advisor
- Trial balance as at the last accounts date
- Fixed asset register with capital allowance computations
- Bank reconciliations
- Details of any provisions, accruals, or prepayments

**Other essential information:**
- Letter of engagement from the outgoing accountant (to understand scope of previous services)
- Tax agent link details (TAIN) so your new accountant can link to your Revenue record on ROS
- Details of any ongoing tax planning arrangements
- Pension contribution records
- Insurance details (professional indemnity, public liability)

## Timing the Switch

The timing of your switch matters. Getting it right avoids duplication of work, reduces costs, and prevents gaps in compliance.

**Best time: After year-end accounts are filed.** The cleanest point to switch is after your outgoing accountant has completed and filed your most recent year-end accounts and tax returns. This means your new accountant starts with a clean slate from the beginning of the next financial period.

**Acceptable time: During the year but before year end.** If the relationship has broken down and you cannot wait, switching mid-year is perfectly manageable. Your new accountant will need to pick up the bookkeeping from wherever your outgoing accountant left off, which may involve additional setup costs.

**Avoid: During year-end preparation.** Switching while your accounts are being prepared but not yet filed creates the most complexity. Your outgoing accountant may have partially completed work, and your new accountant will either need to redo it or rely on incomplete work from someone else. If possible, let your outgoing accountant finish the current year's filings before you switch.

**Avoid: Within 56 days of CRO annual return date.** For limited companies, switching close to the CRO annual return deadline risks missing the filing date, which carries a EUR 100 plus EUR 3 per day penalty and loss of the audit exemption for two years.

## The Tax Agent Link

When you switch accountants, the tax agent link on ROS needs to be updated. Your outgoing accountant's Tax Agent Identification Number (TAIN) is linked to your Revenue record, allowing them to file returns and access your tax information on your behalf.

Your new accountant will:

1. Request that you authorise a new agent link through ROS (you must do this yourself through your own ROS login)
2. The old agent link can remain active until the new one is established, or you can revoke it immediately
3. Once the new link is active, your new accountant can access your Revenue record and file returns on your behalf

The agent link process takes a few days to activate. Plan for this in your transition timeline.

## Step-by-Step Handover Checklist

Here is a practical checklist for managing the switch:

### Before You Switch

- [ ] Settle any outstanding fees with your current accountant
- [ ] Find and meet at least two potential new accountants
- [ ] Choose your new accountant and sign a letter of engagement
- [ ] Inform your current accountant that you are switching (a phone call is courteous, but not required)

### During the Handover

- [ ] Your new accountant sends the professional clearance letter
- [ ] Your outgoing accountant responds with clearance confirmation
- [ ] Your records are transferred to the new accountant
- [ ] Update your ROS agent link to your new accountant's TAIN
- [ ] Add your new accountant as an advisor on your accounting software
- [ ] Provide your new accountant with access to your bank feeds (if applicable)

### After the Switch

- [ ] Your new accountant reviews the records received and confirms completeness
- [ ] Any gaps in the handover are identified and resolved
- [ ] Your new accountant confirms all upcoming filing deadlines
- [ ] A tax planning meeting is scheduled to review your current position
- [ ] You feel confident that your affairs are in capable hands

## Costs of Switching

Switching accountants does involve some costs, though they are typically modest:

**Outstanding fees.** You must pay any amounts owed to your outgoing accountant. If work has been done but not yet billed (for example, they are partway through your year-end accounts), you may receive a final bill for work completed.

**New accountant setup.** Most new accountants charge a one-off setup fee (EUR 200 to EUR 500) to cover the time spent reviewing your records, setting up your file, and familiarising themselves with your business. Some absorb this cost into the first year's fee.

**Overlap period.** If you switch mid-year, there may be a brief period where both accountants are involved, which can result in some duplication of cost. The cleanest transitions minimise this overlap.

**Potential fee difference.** Your new accountant's annual fees may be higher or lower than your current accountant's. The decision should be based on value, not just cost. A more expensive accountant who provides proactive tax planning and responsive service delivers better value than a cheap accountant who misses deadlines and never calls.

## Common Concerns (and Why They Should Not Stop You)

**"My accountant will be offended."** Accountants are professionals. They understand that clients change providers. Most will handle the handover gracefully. Your accountant works for you; you do not owe them loyalty beyond paying for services rendered.

**"It will be too disruptive."** With proper timing and a competent new accountant managing the process, the disruption is minimal. Most handovers are complete within 4 to 6 weeks.

**"My old accountant knows my business."** This is a valid consideration, but your new accountant will quickly get up to speed. The records tell the story. Within one full year-end cycle, your new accountant will understand your business thoroughly.

**"I might lose records."** Your records belong to you. Your outgoing accountant is professionally obligated to return them. Keep copies of everything during the handover, and verify that all documents have been received by your new accountant.

**"There might be problems with Revenue."** Switching accountants has no effect on your Revenue record. Your tax obligations remain the same regardless of who prepares your returns. The only change is the agent link, which is a straightforward administrative process.

## Professional Ethics Rules

The professional clearance process is governed by ethical standards set by the accounting bodies. Key rules include:

**Duty to respond.** Your outgoing accountant must respond to a professional clearance letter within a reasonable timeframe. Failure to respond is a breach of professional ethics and can be reported to the relevant professional body.

**Confidentiality.** Your outgoing accountant cannot disclose confidential information about your affairs to your new accountant without your consent. The clearance process is limited to confirming whether there are professional reasons not to accept the engagement.

**No obstruction.** Your outgoing accountant cannot refuse to hand over your records (with the exception of a legitimate lien for unpaid fees on their own working papers). Deliberately obstructing a handover is a breach of professional standards.

**Lien rights.** If you owe fees, your outgoing accountant may retain their own working papers until payment is received. They cannot, however, retain your source documents (your invoices, receipts, bank statements) or statutory company records.

If your outgoing accountant is uncooperative, your new accountant can escalate the matter to the relevant professional body. In practice, this is rarely necessary.

## Making the Most of a Fresh Start

Switching accountants is an opportunity to reset your financial management. Use it to:

**Review your business structure.** Ask your new accountant whether your current structure (sole trader or limited company) is still the most tax-efficient for your income level and circumstances.

**Set up proper systems.** If your bookkeeping has been neglected, use the transition as an opportunity to set up proper cloud accounting software, establish regular bookkeeping routines, and create systems for receipt capture and expense management.

**Plan proactively.** Schedule a tax planning meeting with your new accountant to review pension contributions, capital allowances, and any other opportunities to reduce your tax bill for the current and upcoming year.

**Establish communication expectations.** Set clear expectations about response times, scheduled reviews, and how you prefer to communicate. Start the relationship on the right foot.
