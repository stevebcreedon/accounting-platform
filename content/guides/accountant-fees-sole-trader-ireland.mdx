A sole trader in Ireland typically pays between EUR 300 and EUR 800 per year for accounting services. The exact amount depends on the complexity of your business, the quality of your records, whether you are VAT-registered, and the level of service you choose. This guide breaks down what you should expect to pay, what is included at each price point, and practical ways to keep your fees as low as possible.

Understanding what drives your accountant's fees puts you in a stronger position when comparing quotes and negotiating a fair price. Most sole traders overpay not because their accountant charges too much, but because they create unnecessary work through poor record keeping or fail to compare what different accountants include in their standard package.

## Typical Fee Ranges for Sole Traders

The majority of sole traders in Ireland fall within one of three fee brackets, each reflecting a different level of complexity and service.

### Basic Package: EUR 300 to EUR 450

At this price point, you are typically working with a sole practitioner or small local firm. The service covers the essential compliance work that Revenue requires from every self-employed person.

A basic package generally includes:

- Preparation and filing of your annual **Form 11** income tax return via ROS
- Calculation of your income tax, USC, and PRSI liabilities
- Calculation of your preliminary tax payment for the following year
- A brief year-end review call or meeting to discuss your tax position
- Basic advice on allowable expenses

This level of service suits sole traders with a single income source, relatively few transactions (under 100 per year), no employees, and no VAT registration. You are expected to maintain your own records throughout the year and present them in an organised format.

### Mid-Range Package: EUR 450 to EUR 650

The mid-range bracket is where most sole traders land. At this level, your accountant takes on more of the heavy lifting and provides a more comprehensive service.

Additional inclusions at this price point typically cover:

- Review and reconciliation of your bookkeeping records
- Advice on capital allowances for equipment, vehicles, and other business assets
- Motor expense calculations (proportioned for business use)
- Home office deduction calculations if you work from home
- Handling of multiple income sources (trading income plus rental income or investment income)
- Communication with Revenue on your behalf for routine queries

The price moves toward the higher end of this range when your business has more transactions, when you have rental properties alongside your trade, or when your records require significant tidying before your accountant can work with them.

### Premium Package: EUR 650 to EUR 800+

At the premium end, you are paying for a more proactive, advisory-led service. This suits sole traders with growing businesses who want strategic guidance beyond basic compliance.

Premium packages typically include everything in the mid-range bracket, plus:

- Proactive tax planning advice throughout the year
- Assistance with Revenue audits or compliance checks
- Quarterly or six-monthly check-ins on your financial position
- Advice on whether and when to incorporate as a limited company
- Pension planning guidance and contribution optimisation
- Support with business financing applications or grant submissions

### VAT Returns as an Add-On

If you are VAT-registered, your accountant will charge separately for preparing and filing your VAT3 returns. Ireland requires bi-monthly VAT returns for most businesses, which means six returns per year plus the annual Return of Trading Details (RTD).

Typical VAT return costs for sole traders:

- **EUR 50 to EUR 100 per VAT3 return** if you provide well-organised records
- **EUR 100 to EUR 150 per return** if your accountant must reconcile from bank statements or invoices
- **Annual RTD filing:** EUR 50 to EUR 100

Over a full year, VAT return preparation adds EUR 350 to EUR 700 to your total accounting costs. Some accountants bundle VAT returns into an annual package at a slightly lower per-return cost.

## What Affects Your Fee

Several factors determine where you fall within the fee ranges. Understanding these allows you to take practical steps to reduce your costs.

### Record-Keeping Quality

This is the single biggest variable cost factor. If you use cloud bookkeeping software like Xero, Sage, or QuickBooks and keep your records up to date throughout the year, your accountant's work is largely review and compliance. They can complete your Form 11 efficiently because the data is clean and accessible.

If you hand over a shoebox of receipts, a stack of bank statements, and a vague memory of which expenses were business-related, your accountant must reconstruct your records before they can even begin the compliance work. This reconstruction time is either billed directly or reflected in a higher annual fee.

The difference in fees between a well-organised client and a poorly organised one with the same business complexity can be EUR 200 to EUR 400 per year. Investing two hours per month in basic bookkeeping can save you significantly more than that in accountant fees.

### Business Complexity

A sole trader with one income stream from a single trade is straightforward. Add rental income, foreign income, capital gains, or investment income, and the Form 11 becomes substantially more complex. Each additional income source requires its own calculations, reliefs, and disclosures.

Common complexity factors that increase your fee:

- **Rental income:** Adds EUR 100 to EUR 200 per property (mortgage interest relief, wear and tear, management fees, pre-letting expenses)
- **Capital gains:** Adds EUR 100 to EUR 300 depending on the nature and number of disposals
- **Foreign income:** Adds EUR 100 to EUR 200 (double taxation relief calculations, foreign tax credit claims)
- **Multiple trades:** Each separate trade requires its own profit and loss calculation

### Number of Transactions

A sole trader processing 50 invoices per year generates less bookkeeping work than one processing 500. Transaction volume directly affects the time your accountant spends reviewing and reconciling your records.

If your transaction volume is high, consider maintaining your own bookkeeping in software and handing your accountant a clean trial balance rather than raw data. This shifts the bulk of the data entry work to you (or a bookkeeper at EUR 25 to EUR 40 per hour) and keeps your accountant focused on the compliance and advisory work that justifies their higher hourly rate.

### Regional Differences

Dublin-based accountants typically charge 10 to 20 per cent more than firms outside Dublin, reflecting higher overhead costs (office rent, staff salaries). However, with remote working now standard in the accounting profession, geography matters less than it once did.

A sole trader in Cork or Galway can engage a Dublin accountant if they offer better value, and vice versa. The key is finding the right fit in terms of expertise and communication, not proximity. Most routine sole trader work can be handled entirely via email, phone, and cloud software with no need for face-to-face meetings.

## How to Reduce Your Accountant Fees

There are practical steps every sole trader can take to minimise their accounting costs without compromising on quality.

### Maintain Good Records Throughout the Year

The most effective way to reduce your fees is to keep organised, up-to-date records. Use bookkeeping software (Xero, Sage, or even a well-structured spreadsheet) and update it weekly or at least monthly.

At a minimum, maintain:

- A record of all income received with dates and sources
- Copies of all invoices issued
- A record of all business expenses with receipts
- Monthly bank reconciliations
- A log of motor expenses if you claim business mileage
- Separation of personal and business transactions (ideally through a dedicated business bank account)

### Use Cloud Accounting Software

Cloud software like Xero or Sage costs EUR 15 to EUR 40 per month but typically saves you more than that in reduced accountant fees. These tools allow your accountant to access your data directly, eliminating the back-and-forth of exchanging files and the risk of working from outdated information.

Many accountants offer discounted fees for clients who use their preferred software platform because it makes their workflow significantly more efficient.

### Separate Personal and Business Banking

A dedicated business bank account is not legally required for sole traders, but it dramatically simplifies your bookkeeping and reduces your accountant's work. When personal and business transactions are mixed in one account, your accountant must identify and separate them -- a time-consuming process that inflates your fee.

### Prepare a Year-End Pack

Before your annual meeting or submission, prepare a structured year-end pack containing:

- Your profit and loss summary for the year
- Bank statements for all business accounts
- A summary of capital purchases or disposals
- Details of any new income sources or changes to your business
- Outstanding invoices and debtor balances
- Any Revenue correspondence received during the year

This preparation saves your accountant time and demonstrates that you are an organised, low-maintenance client -- the type they are willing to offer competitive rates to retain.

### Compare Quotes Properly

When comparing accountant quotes, ensure you are comparing like with like. A quote of EUR 350 that includes only your Form 11 is not cheaper than a quote of EUR 550 that includes your Form 11, bookkeeping review, capital allowances calculation, and two advisory calls during the year.

Ask each accountant to specify:

1. Exactly what services are included in the quoted fee
2. How ad-hoc queries (phone calls, emails) are handled
3. What triggers additional charges
4. Whether the fee is fixed for the year or subject to review

## When to Consider a Full Package

As your sole trader business grows, the cost of individual services (Form 11 here, a VAT return there, an occasional phone call) can exceed the cost of a comprehensive annual package. If you find yourself needing your accountant's input more than two or three times per year beyond your annual return, ask about package pricing.

A good annual package provides certainty -- you know what you will pay, and your accountant knows what they are expected to deliver. It also encourages you to make use of the advisory component, which is where the real value lies. Many sole traders who pay per interaction avoid calling their accountant with questions, missing out on advice that could save them far more than the call would have cost.

## When to Upgrade to a Limited Company

One of the most valuable conversations a sole trader can have with their accountant is about incorporation timing. The general rule is that when your net trading profits consistently exceed EUR 75,000 to EUR 100,000, the tax savings from incorporating as a limited company often outweigh the additional accounting costs.

However, this threshold varies depending on your personal circumstances, pension contributions, and how much of your profits you need to draw as income. A good accountant will model both scenarios for you and advise on the optimal timing. This analysis typically costs EUR 200 to EUR 400 as a one-off exercise but can save thousands in annual tax over the following years.

Bear in mind that limited company accounting costs are significantly higher (EUR 1,500 to EUR 3,500 per year versus EUR 300 to EUR 800), so the tax savings must be sufficient to cover both the additional accounting fees and the increased administrative burden.

## Form 11 Preparation: What Is Involved

Understanding what your accountant does when preparing your Form 11 helps you appreciate why the fee is what it is and how your actions affect the cost.

The Form 11 is the annual income tax return filed by self-assessed individuals in Ireland. For sole traders, it captures all your income, expenses, reliefs, and credits for the tax year (January to December). It is filed via ROS (Revenue Online Service) and is typically due by mid-November of the following year (with an extended deadline for ROS filers).

Your accountant's work in preparing your Form 11 includes:

1. **Gathering and reviewing your records** -- income, expenses, bank statements, receipts
2. **Preparing your trading accounts** -- profit and loss calculation for your business
3. **Calculating capital allowances** -- depreciation of business assets for tax purposes
4. **Identifying all allowable deductions** -- motor expenses, home office costs, pension contributions, professional subscriptions
5. **Completing the Form 11** -- entering all figures across the relevant sections (trading income, rental income, employment income, capital gains, reliefs, credits)
6. **Calculating your tax liability** -- income tax at 20% and 40%, USC, PRSI
7. **Calculating preliminary tax** -- your advance payment for the current year
8. **Filing via ROS** -- electronic submission before the deadline
9. **Providing you with a summary** -- your liability, payment dates, and any planning points for the following year

Each step takes time, and the total time depends almost entirely on the quality and organisation of the records you provide.

## Comparing Sole Practitioner vs Larger Firm

Sole practitioners and small firms (two to five staff) generally offer the best value for sole traders. Their overhead costs are lower, they often provide a more personal service, and the person you speak to is typically the person doing your work.

Mid-tier and large firms charge more but may offer advantages if your business is complex or growing rapidly. They have specialists in areas like capital gains tax, Revenue audits, or cross-border transactions. However, for a straightforward sole trader business, these specialist capabilities are rarely needed, and you may find yourself paying for infrastructure you do not use.

The Big Four (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) are not appropriate for most sole traders. Their minimum fees typically start at EUR 2,000 to EUR 3,000 per year, and their service model is designed for larger businesses.
