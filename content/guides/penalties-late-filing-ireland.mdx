Filing your tax returns and company documents late in Ireland triggers automatic penalties from both Revenue and the Companies Registration Office (CRO). These penalties are not discretionary -- they apply as soon as you miss a deadline, and they can accumulate quickly. Understanding what is at stake helps you prioritise compliance and avoid unnecessary costs.

This guide covers every penalty you might face for late filing in Ireland, from Revenue surcharges and interest charges to CRO late filing consequences. It also explains how to appeal, how voluntary disclosure works, and the practical steps you can take to ensure you never miss a deadline.

## Revenue Surcharges for Late Tax Returns

Revenue applies an automatic surcharge to your tax liability when you file a return after the deadline. This surcharge is added on top of any tax you owe and cannot be reduced or waived.

The surcharge rates are straightforward:

- **5 per cent surcharge** if you file within 2 months of the due date, up to a maximum of EUR 12,695
- **10 per cent surcharge** if you file more than 2 months after the due date, up to a maximum of EUR 63,485

These caps apply per return. If you file multiple returns late in the same year, each return attracts its own surcharge independently. For example, if you file both your income tax return and your VAT annual Return of Trading Details late, you face two separate surcharges.

The surcharge applies to the full amount of tax payable on the return, not just the amount outstanding. Even if you have already paid most of your tax through preliminary tax or PAYE, the surcharge is calculated on the total self-assessed liability shown on the return.

### Which Returns Attract Surcharges

The surcharge regime applies to all self-assessment returns filed with Revenue, including:

- Form 11 (income tax for self-employed individuals)
- Form CT1 (corporation tax for companies)
- Form 46G (return of third-party information)
- Employer annual returns
- Capital gains tax returns

VAT returns (VAT3) do not attract the standard surcharge but are subject to their own penalty regime, including fixed penalties of up to EUR 4,000 for non-filing.

## Interest on Late Payment of Tax

Separately from the surcharge for late filing, Revenue charges interest on any tax that remains unpaid after the due date. The current interest rate is **0.0219 per cent per day**, which equates to approximately 8 per cent per annum.

Interest runs from the due date for payment until the date the tax is actually paid. There is no cap on the interest amount, and it cannot be appealed or reduced. It is calculated automatically by Revenue's systems.

For example, if you owe EUR 10,000 in income tax and pay it 6 months late, the interest charge would be approximately EUR 400. On larger liabilities, interest accumulates rapidly and can become a significant additional cost.

### Preliminary Tax and Interest

Interest also applies to underpayments of preliminary tax. If your preliminary tax payment is less than the required minimum (either 90 per cent of the current year's liability or 100 per cent of the prior year's liability for income tax), interest runs on the shortfall from the preliminary tax due date.

For corporation tax, companies must pay preliminary tax in one or two instalments depending on their size. Small companies (corporation tax liability under EUR 200,000 in the prior year) pay in a single instalment. Larger companies pay an initial instalment in month 6 of the accounting period and a top-up instalment in month 11. Interest applies to any shortfall from the relevant instalment dates.

## CRO Late Filing Penalties

The Companies Registration Office imposes its own penalties when companies file their annual return (Form B1) late. These penalties are distinct from Revenue penalties and apply to all registered Irish companies.

### Financial Penalties

The CRO charges a **late filing penalty of EUR 100 for each day** the return is overdue, up to a maximum of **EUR 20,000 per return**. This penalty is charged to the company and becomes a debt owed to the CRO.

If a company repeatedly files late, the CRO may refer the matter to the Director of Corporate Enforcement for investigation. Directors can be held personally liable for persistent non-compliance.

### Loss of Audit Exemption

The most significant consequence of late CRO filing for small companies is the **loss of audit exemption for two years**. Normally, small companies that meet certain size criteria can claim an exemption from having their accounts audited. However, if a company files its annual return late, it loses this exemption for the financial year in which the return was due and the following financial year.

The cost of a statutory audit for a small company typically ranges from EUR 3,000 to EUR 8,000 or more, depending on the complexity of the business. Losing audit exemption because of a late filing that could have been avoided is one of the most expensive mistakes an Irish company can make.

To qualify for audit exemption, a company must meet two of the following three criteria:

- Turnover does not exceed EUR 12 million
- Balance sheet total does not exceed EUR 6 million
- Average number of employees does not exceed 50

Even if your company meets all three criteria, a single late B1 filing will cost you the exemption for two years.

### Strike-Off Risk

If a company fails to file annual returns for two or more consecutive years, the CRO may commence involuntary strike-off proceedings. This means the company is removed from the register and ceases to exist as a legal entity. Assets of a struck-off company vest in the State.

Restoring a company to the register after strike-off is possible but involves a High Court application, which is costly and time-consuming.

## Revenue's Publication of Tax Defaulters

Revenue publishes a quarterly list of tax defaulters on its website. Inclusion on this list occurs when a settlement exceeds **EUR 50,000** (including tax, interest, and penalties) and the settlement was not the result of a qualifying voluntary disclosure.

Being published as a tax defaulter causes reputational damage that can affect your business relationships, access to credit, and ability to tender for public contracts. The list is searchable online and remains publicly available.

## Revenue's Collection and Enforcement Powers

When tax remains unpaid, Revenue has extensive powers to collect the debt, including:

- **Attachment orders:** Revenue can instruct your bank, customers, or other debtors to pay money they owe you directly to Revenue instead
- **Sheriff:** Revenue can instruct the Sheriff to seize and sell your assets to satisfy the tax debt
- **Solicitor collection:** Revenue can engage solicitors to pursue the debt through the courts
- **Taxpayer information notices:** Revenue can require banks and other financial institutions to provide information about your accounts
- **Court proceedings:** Revenue can take legal action to recover the debt, including seeking judgment against you

These powers are used regularly. Revenue collects over EUR 500 million annually through enforcement activities.

## How to Appeal Revenue Penalties

If you believe a penalty or surcharge has been applied incorrectly, you can appeal to the **Tax Appeals Commission (TAC)**. The TAC is an independent body that hears appeals against Revenue decisions.

To appeal, you must:

1. First raise the issue directly with your Revenue office
2. If not resolved, submit a formal Notice of Appeal to the TAC within 30 days of the Revenue decision
3. Provide supporting documentation and grounds for your appeal
4. Attend a hearing or agree to have the matter determined on the papers

Note that you cannot appeal the interest charge itself -- interest is a statutory obligation that runs automatically. You can only appeal the underlying tax assessment if you believe the amount of tax is incorrect.

## Voluntary Disclosure to Revenue

If you discover that you have underpaid tax or made errors in previous returns, making a voluntary disclosure to Revenue can significantly reduce the penalties you face. A voluntary disclosure is an unprompted communication to Revenue that reveals a previously undisclosed tax liability.

### Qualifying Voluntary Disclosure

A qualifying voluntary disclosure must be made before:

- Revenue notifies you of an audit or investigation
- Revenue raises a query about the specific matter
- Revenue obtains information about the matter from a third party

If you meet these conditions, the benefits include:

- **No publication** on the tax defaulters list
- **Reduced penalties** (typically 3 per cent to 10 per cent of the tax due, depending on whether the error was a careless or deliberate default)
- **No criminal prosecution**

### Non-Qualifying Voluntary Disclosure

If Revenue has already notified you of an audit, you can still make a disclosure, but it will be treated as a non-qualifying voluntary disclosure. The penalty reductions are smaller, and you may still be published if the settlement exceeds the EUR 50,000 threshold. However, a non-qualifying disclosure generally results in lower penalties than if Revenue discovers the underpayment during the audit.

## Practical Tips to Avoid Late Filing

Prevention is always better than dealing with penalties. Here are practical steps to ensure you file on time:

**Set calendar reminders well in advance.** Do not wait until the deadline week. Set reminders at least 4 weeks, 2 weeks, and 1 week before each filing deadline. This gives you time to gather information and resolve any issues.

**Know your key dates.** Income tax (Form 11) is due by 31 October (mid-November via ROS). Corporation tax (Form CT1) is due 9 months after the accounting period end. CRO annual returns are due within 28 days of your Annual Return Date. VAT returns are due by the 23rd of the month following the period end (via ROS).

**Use a tax agent or accountant.** A qualified accountant manages your filing calendar as part of their service. They know the deadlines, have systems to track them, and will ensure returns are filed on time. The cost of engaging an accountant is almost always less than the cost of late filing penalties.

**File early, even if estimated.** If you cannot finalise your figures in time, consider filing an estimated return and amending it later. While you should aim for accuracy, filing an estimated return on time avoids the surcharge. You can then file an amended return when your final figures are available.

**Pay preliminary tax on time.** Even if your return is not yet due, ensure preliminary tax payments are made by the relevant deadlines to avoid interest charges.

**Keep records organised throughout the year.** If your records are in order, preparing returns is faster and less stressful. Use accounting software to track income and expenses in real time rather than scrambling at year end.

**Check your CRO Annual Return Date.** Many companies do not know their Annual Return Date. Log in to the CORE system on the CRO website to check your company's ARD and set reminders accordingly.
