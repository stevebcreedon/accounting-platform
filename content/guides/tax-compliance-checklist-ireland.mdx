Running a tax-compliant business in Ireland means filing the right returns, paying the correct amounts, and meeting every deadline set by Revenue. Miss one filing and you face automatic surcharges. Miss a payment deadline and interest starts accruing at 0.0219 per cent per day. This checklist covers every tax obligation an Irish business is likely to encounter, from income tax and corporation tax to VAT, PAYE, and the less common filings that catch businesses out.

Whether you are a sole trader, partnership, or limited company, use this guide to ensure nothing falls through the cracks.

## Income Tax Compliance (Sole Traders and Partnerships)

If you are self-employed or a partner in a partnership, income tax is your primary tax obligation. Here is what you need to file and pay.

### Form 11 -- Annual Income Tax Return

The Form 11 is the annual self-assessment income tax return for self-employed individuals. It covers all sources of income: trading profits, rental income, investment income, capital gains, and employment income (if any).

**Filing deadline:** 31 October each year for the previous tax year. If you file and pay through ROS, the deadline extends to mid-November (the exact date varies slightly each year but is typically around 14 to 16 November).

**What to include:**
- Trading income (profit from your business)
- Rental income from Irish and foreign property
- Investment income (dividends, deposit interest)
- Employment income (if you also have PAYE employment)
- Capital gains and losses
- Tax credits and reliefs claimed
- Preliminary tax payment details

### Preliminary Tax

Preliminary tax is an advance payment of your expected income tax liability for the current year. It must be paid by **31 October** (mid-November via ROS) of the tax year to which it relates.

**Minimum payment to avoid interest:** You must pay at least:
- 90 per cent of your current year liability, OR
- 100 per cent of your prior year liability, OR
- 105 per cent of the pre-prior year liability (only available if paying by direct debit)

If your preliminary tax payment is less than the required minimum, interest runs on the shortfall from the due date.

### Pay and File

Under the pay and file system, your payment deadline and filing deadline coincide. You should aim to file your Form 11 and pay any balance due in a single ROS session to ensure both obligations are met simultaneously.

## Corporation Tax Compliance (Limited Companies)

Limited companies pay corporation tax rather than income tax. The compliance obligations are different and have their own set of deadlines.

### Form CT1 -- Corporation Tax Return

The Form CT1 is the annual corporation tax return for Irish companies. It covers the company's profits (trading and non-trading), capital gains, and claims for reliefs and credits.

**Filing deadline:** The CT1 must be filed within **9 months of the end of the accounting period**. For a company with a 31 December year end, the filing deadline is 23 September of the following year (21 days extension for ROS electronic filing).

**What to include:**
- Trading profits
- Investment and rental income
- Capital gains
- Close company surcharges (if applicable)
- R&D tax credit claims
- Group relief claims
- Associated company information
- Directors' remuneration details

### Preliminary Corporation Tax

Companies must pay preliminary corporation tax in advance. The timing depends on the company's size:

**Small companies** (prior year corporation tax liability under EUR 200,000): Pay a single instalment by day 21 of the month before the end of the accounting period. For a December year-end company, this is 21 November.

**Large companies** (prior year corporation tax liability EUR 200,000 or more): Pay in two instalments:
- **First instalment:** 50 per cent of estimated liability, due in month 6 of the accounting period
- **Second instalment:** Top-up to 90 per cent of estimated liability, due in month 11 of the accounting period

## VAT Compliance

If your business is registered for VAT, you have regular filing and payment obligations throughout the year.

### VAT3 Returns -- Bi-Monthly Filing

Most Irish businesses file VAT returns on a bi-monthly basis. The VAT3 return reports output VAT (charged on sales) and input VAT (paid on purchases) for the two-month period.

**Filing deadlines (via ROS):**

| Period | Deadline |
|--------|----------|
| January/February | 23 March |
| March/April | 23 May |
| May/June | 23 July |
| July/August | 23 September |
| September/October | 23 November |
| November/December | 23 January |

**What to check before filing:**
- All sales invoices have been included with correct VAT rates
- All purchase invoices with valid VAT numbers have been captured
- Reverse charge transactions are correctly accounted for
- Credit notes have been properly applied
- No VAT has been claimed on non-deductible items (entertainment, certain motor expenses)

### Annual Return of Trading Details (RTD)

In addition to bi-monthly returns, every VAT-registered business must file an annual RTD. This summarises total supplies and purchases broken down by VAT rate.

**Filing deadline:** 23rd of the month following the end of your annual accounting period. For most businesses, this is 23 January (for a calendar year accounting period).

The RTD is one of the most commonly missed returns. Revenue uses it to cross-check your bi-monthly VAT3 figures against the annual totals. Discrepancies can trigger an audit.

## PAYE and Employer Obligations

If you employ anyone, including yourself as a company director, you have extensive payroll tax obligations.

### Payroll Submissions

Under Enhanced Reporting Requirements (ERR), employers must submit payroll information to Revenue in real time. Each time you run payroll, you must submit:

- **Payroll Submission Request (PSR):** Reports each employee's gross pay, tax deducted, PRSI, USC, and net pay
- Revenue processes the submission and confirms it matches the Revenue Payroll Notification (RPN) for each employee

Payroll submissions must be made **on or before the payment date**. You cannot process payroll retrospectively.

### Monthly PAYE/PRSI/USC Remittance

The PAYE, PRSI, and USC deducted from employees' pay must be remitted to Revenue **by the 14th of the following month** (by the 23rd if filing and paying via ROS).

For example, PAYE deducted in January must be paid to Revenue by 14 February (or 23 February via ROS).

### Employer Annual Compliance

At the end of each tax year, employers should review:

- All payroll submissions are complete and accurate
- Any corrections have been filed
- Benefits in kind have been reported (company car, health insurance, etc.)
- Share option exercises have been reported
- Any ex-gratia or termination payments have been correctly treated

### Enhanced Reporting Requirements (ERR)

Since 1 January 2024, employers must report additional non-taxable benefits and payments to Revenue. This includes:

- Small benefit exemption payments (up to EUR 1,000 per year)
- Travel and subsistence payments
- Remote working daily allowance (EUR 3.20 per day)

These must be reported within the payroll submission for the period in which they are paid.

## Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT)

If your business operates in the construction, forestry, or meat processing sectors and engages subcontractors, you must operate the RCT system.

### Principal Contractor Obligations

- **Register as a principal contractor** with Revenue
- **Notify Revenue** of each contract with a subcontractor before the first payment
- **Deduct RCT** at the rate specified by Revenue (0 per cent, 20 per cent, or 35 per cent depending on the subcontractor's compliance history)
- **File monthly RCT returns** by the 23rd of the month following the return period
- **Remit RCT deducted** to Revenue on time

### RCT Compliance Pitfalls

The most common RCT error is failure to notify Revenue of a contract before making the first payment. This results in the highest withholding rate (35 per cent) being applied and potential penalties.

## Dividend Withholding Tax (DWT)

If your company pays dividends to shareholders, you must deduct **Dividend Withholding Tax at 25 per cent** from the gross dividend and remit it to Revenue.

**Filing deadline:** The DWT return must be filed and the tax remitted within **14 days of the end of the month** in which the distribution was made.

Certain shareholders are exempt from DWT, including Irish-resident companies, pension funds, and qualifying non-resident shareholders who provide a declaration of non-residence. You must retain these declarations for six years.

## Professional Services Withholding Tax (PSWT)

If you are a designated body (government department, local authority, HSE, state body) or certain other organisations, you must deduct **PSWT at 20 per cent** from payments to professionals (accountants, solicitors, consultants, etc.) for professional services.

If your business receives payments subject to PSWT, the tax withheld is credited against your income tax or corporation tax liability.

## Local Property Tax (LPT)

If your business owns property in Ireland, you must pay Local Property Tax. LPT is a self-assessed tax based on the market value of the property as at the valuation date.

**Current valuation date:** 1 November 2021 (for the period 2022 to 2025)
**Payment methods:** Lump sum, direct debit, or deduction at source

While LPT is primarily a personal tax, it applies to all residential properties regardless of the owner. Companies that own residential properties must comply.

## Annual Planning Calendar

Use this month-by-month checklist to stay on track:

**January:** Pay November/December VAT by 23rd. Review PAYE submissions for the prior year. File RTD if calendar year accounting period.

**February:** Remit January PAYE by 23rd (ROS). Begin preparing prior year accounts.

**March:** Pay January/February VAT by 23rd. File corporation tax preliminary payment if month 6 (June year-end companies).

**April:** Remit March PAYE by 23rd. Check CRO annual return date if applicable.

**May:** Pay March/April VAT by 23rd. Review preliminary tax estimates for current year.

**June:** Remit May PAYE by 23rd. Mid-year review of VAT and PAYE compliance.

**July:** Pay May/June VAT by 23rd. Companies with December year-end file preliminary CT (large companies, first instalment).

**August:** Remit July PAYE by 23rd. Begin gathering Form 11/CT1 documentation.

**September:** Pay July/August VAT by 23rd. File CT1 for December year-end companies by 23rd. Pay final preliminary CT for large December year-end companies.

**October:** Remit September PAYE by 23rd. File Form 11 by 31st (mid-November via ROS). Pay income tax balance and preliminary tax.

**November:** Pay September/October VAT by 23rd. Small companies with December year-end pay preliminary CT by 21st.

**December:** Remit November PAYE by 23rd. Year-end payroll closing. Prepare for January filings.

## Getting Organised for Compliance

**Use accounting software.** Cloud-based accounting software such as Sage, Xero, or QuickBooks automates VAT calculations, PAYE processing, and financial reporting. The upfront cost is recovered many times over in saved time and avoided errors.

**Engage a qualified accountant.** A good accountant does not just prepare your annual return -- they manage your entire tax compliance calendar, ensure deadlines are met, and identify opportunities for legitimate tax savings. The cost of an accountant is almost always less than the cost of non-compliance.

**Maintain contemporaneous records.** Keep invoices, receipts, bank statements, and contracts organised as you go. Do not wait until year end to sort through a shoebox of receipts. The better your records, the easier your compliance and the lower your risk during a Revenue audit.

**Reconcile regularly.** Reconcile your bank accounts monthly. Review your VAT figures before each bi-monthly return. Check your payroll submissions against your bank payments each pay period.

**Set multiple reminders.** For each deadline, set reminders at 4 weeks, 2 weeks, and 1 week before. Calendar reminders, accounting software alerts, and your accountant's own reminder system should all work together.

**Review with your accountant quarterly.** A quarterly review meeting ensures nothing is missed, identifies issues early, and keeps your accountant informed about business changes that may affect your tax position.

- Employers must submit payroll to Revenue in real time and remit PAYE, PRSI, and USC by the 23rd of the following month via ROS
- Use a planning calendar with reminders at 4 weeks, 2 weeks, and 1 week before each deadline to ensure nothing is missed

<FAQItem question="What happens if I miss a tax deadline in Ireland?">
Late filing triggers an automatic surcharge of 5 per cent of your tax liability (rising to 10 per cent if more than 2 months late). Late payment incurs interest at 0.0219 per cent per day (approximately 8 per cent per annum). There are also fixed penalties for certain failures, such as EUR 4,000 for failing to file a VAT return. Multiple missed deadlines increase your risk of being selected for a Revenue audit.
</FAQItem>

The cost of professional support is almost always less than the cost of penalties and interest for non-compliance.
