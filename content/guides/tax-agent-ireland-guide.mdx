A tax agent in Ireland is a professional who is authorised to deal with Revenue on your behalf. They can access your tax records, file returns, make payments, and handle correspondence through the Revenue Online Service (ROS). For most small business owners, their accountant acts as their tax agent, though the two roles are technically distinct.

Understanding how the tax agent system works in Ireland is important because it determines who can access your tax information, what they can do on your behalf, and how you maintain control over your Revenue record. This guide explains what a tax agent does, how to appoint one, what the TAIN system means, and how the role differs from an accountant or bookkeeper.

## What Is a Tax Agent?

A tax agent is a person or firm that is registered with Revenue and authorised to act on behalf of taxpayers. Revenue uses the term "agent" to describe anyone who has been granted access to a taxpayer's record through the ROS agent link system.

In practice, most tax agents in Ireland are:

- Qualified accountants in practice (members of CAI, CPA Ireland, ACCA, or CIMA)
- Tax advisors and consultants
- Firms specialising in tax compliance and planning

The key distinction is between someone who advises you on tax matters and someone who is formally linked to your Revenue record with authority to act on your behalf. An accountant who prepares your returns but does not have an agent link still needs you to file the returns yourself through your own ROS login. An accountant who is linked as your tax agent can file returns directly.

## The TAIN System

Every registered tax agent in Ireland has a Tax Agent Identification Number (TAIN). This is a unique number assigned by Revenue that identifies the agent within the ROS system. When your agent links to your Revenue record, their TAIN is associated with your tax reference number (PPS number for individuals, tax registration number for companies).

The TAIN system serves several purposes:

**Identification.** Revenue can identify exactly which agent is acting for which taxpayer. This creates a clear audit trail for all actions taken on your record.

**Access control.** Only an agent whose TAIN is linked to your record can access your tax information and file returns on your behalf. This protects your data from unauthorised access.

**Communication routing.** When Revenue sends correspondence about your tax affairs, they can copy your linked agent automatically. This ensures your agent is kept informed of any queries, assessments, or compliance actions.

**Accountability.** Revenue holds agents to professional standards. If an agent consistently files incorrect returns or fails to meet obligations, Revenue can investigate and, in serious cases, revoke the TAIN.

## How to Appoint a Tax Agent

The process for appointing a tax agent involves both you (the taxpayer) and the agent. Neither party can establish the link unilaterally -- both must take action.

### Step 1: Your Agent Initiates the Link Request

Your accountant or tax advisor initiates the agent link process through their ROS agent account. They select your tax reference number and request to be linked as your agent. This sends a notification to your ROS account.

### Step 2: You Authorise the Link

You log into your own ROS account and approve the agent link request. This step ensures that you explicitly consent to the agent accessing your tax record. Revenue requires the taxpayer to actively authorise the link -- your agent cannot add themselves without your knowledge or consent.

For individuals, you log in using your own ROS certificate. For companies, a director or company secretary must authorise the link using the company's ROS certificate.

### Step 3: The Link Becomes Active

Once you authorise the request, the agent link is activated. From this point, your agent can access your tax record, file returns, and communicate with Revenue on your behalf. The activation is usually immediate but may take up to 48 hours in some cases.

### Step 4: Confirmation

Both you and your agent receive confirmation that the link is active. You can verify the active agent link at any time by logging into your ROS account and checking the agent management section.

## What Your Tax Agent Can Access and Do

Once linked, your tax agent has broad access to your Revenue record. The specific capabilities include:

**View your tax record.** Your agent can see your tax registration details, filed returns, assessments, payments, and any outstanding liabilities. They have the same view of your record that you have through your own ROS login.

**File tax returns.** Your agent can prepare and submit tax returns on your behalf, including:

- Form 11 (income tax for self-employed individuals)
- CT1 (corporation tax for companies)
- VAT3 (VAT returns)
- PREM (employer payroll returns)
- Form 46G (third-party returns)
- Other ancillary returns and declarations

**Make payments.** Your agent can set up and process tax payments through ROS, including direct debit mandates. However, you should ensure clear agreement about who authorises payments, as the agent acts on your behalf.

**Correspond with Revenue.** Your agent can send and receive messages through the ROS message system, respond to queries, submit documentation, and request Revenue confirmations (such as tax clearance certificates) on your behalf.

**Access Revenue tools.** Your agent can use Revenue's online tools on your behalf, including the preliminary tax calculator, the Income Tax estimator, and the MyEnquiries system for queries.

### What Your Tax Agent Cannot Do

Despite broad access, there are limitations:

- Your agent cannot change your personal details (address, bank account, marital status) without your authorisation
- Your agent cannot register you for a new tax head without your knowledge (though in practice they may do so as part of their service)
- Your agent does not have access to your ROS digital certificate -- they use their own agent certificate
- Your agent's access is limited to the specific tax reference numbers you have linked

## Tax Agent vs Accountant vs Bookkeeper

These three roles are related but distinct. Understanding the differences helps you know who to engage for which purpose.

### Tax Agent

A tax agent is defined by their relationship with Revenue. They hold a TAIN, are linked to your Revenue record, and can file returns and access your tax information through ROS. The role is administrative and compliance-focused.

Not all accountants are tax agents. An accountant can prepare your tax returns and hand them to you to file yourself through your own ROS login. They only become your tax agent when they are formally linked to your Revenue record.

### Accountant

An accountant provides a broader range of services than a tax agent. They prepare your financial statements, provide tax planning advice, handle CRO compliance (for companies), and offer business advisory services. Most practising accountants in Ireland also act as tax agents for their clients, but the accountant role encompasses much more than filing returns.

The key additional value an accountant provides over a pure tax agent is:

- Financial statements preparation (profit and loss, balance sheet)
- Tax planning and advice (not just compliance)
- Business structure recommendations
- Cash flow management and forecasting
- Revenue audit representation with professional expertise
- CRO compliance and company secretarial

### Bookkeeper

A bookkeeper handles the day-to-day transaction recording that creates the data both accountants and tax agents rely on. A bookkeeper does not typically hold a TAIN or have access to your Revenue record through ROS. Their role is to maintain accurate financial records, not to interpret them for compliance or advisory purposes.

In some cases, experienced bookkeepers do file VAT returns through ROS using the business owner's own login credentials (with permission). This is an informal arrangement rather than a formal agent relationship.

### The Typical Small Business Arrangement

For most Irish small businesses, the arrangement works like this:

1. The bookkeeper (or the business owner) maintains day-to-day records in accounting software
2. The accountant prepares annual accounts and tax returns from those records
3. The accountant, acting as tax agent, files the returns through ROS using their TAIN

Some accountants provide all three functions. Others work alongside a separate bookkeeper. The important thing is that the person filing your returns with Revenue is qualified, authorised (via the agent link), and accountable.

## Self-Service ROS vs Agent-Managed

You do not need a tax agent to use ROS. Every taxpayer in Ireland can register for their own ROS account and manage their tax affairs directly. The question is whether self-service or agent-managed is the better approach for your situation.

### When Self-Service Works

- Your tax affairs are straightforward (simple PAYE income, no self-employment)
- You have a good understanding of Irish tax obligations
- You are comfortable navigating the ROS system
- You have the time to manage deadlines and filing requirements

### When an Agent Makes Sense

- You are self-employed or run a limited company
- You have multiple sources of income
- You are registered for VAT and/or PAYE
- You want professional assurance that your returns are correct
- You prefer to delegate compliance to focus on your business
- You need tax planning advice alongside filing services

The cost of having an agent manage your ROS filings is included in your accountant's annual fee. You are not paying separately for the agent link -- it is simply the mechanism by which your accountant files returns on your behalf.

## Revenue's Requirements for Tax Agents

Revenue sets expectations for tax agents through published guidance and the Tax Agent Charter. Key requirements include:

**Registration.** All tax agents must be registered with Revenue and hold a valid TAIN. Revenue may refuse or revoke registration if an agent fails to meet professional standards.

**Competence.** Revenue expects tax agents to have adequate knowledge and expertise to prepare accurate tax returns and provide reliable advice. While Revenue does not mandate specific qualifications, they can take action against agents who consistently file incorrect returns.

**Compliance.** Tax agents must ensure their own tax affairs are fully compliant. An agent who is not tax-compliant themselves cannot hold a tax clearance certificate, which Revenue considers a basic professional standard.

**Confidentiality.** Agents must maintain the confidentiality of taxpayer information accessed through the agent link. This obligation exists under both Revenue's guidelines and data protection law (GDPR).

**Cooperation.** Revenue expects agents to cooperate with compliance interventions, respond to queries in a timely manner, and facilitate audits when required.

## Responsibilities and Obligations

### Your Responsibilities

Even with a tax agent, you remain legally responsible for your own tax obligations. The agent acts on your behalf, but the legal responsibility for accurate returns and timely payment sits with you. This means:

- You should review returns before your agent files them (or at minimum, understand what is being filed)
- You are responsible for providing complete and accurate information to your agent
- If your agent makes an error, Revenue holds you responsible (though you may have a civil claim against the agent)
- Penalties and interest for late filing or underpayment are charged to you, not your agent

### Your Agent's Responsibilities

Your tax agent has professional and ethical obligations:

- Act in your best interests within the law
- Maintain professional competence and keep up to date with tax legislation
- Communicate with you about your tax position, deadlines, and liabilities
- File returns accurately and on time
- Respond to Revenue queries in a timely manner
- Maintain professional indemnity insurance
- Follow the ethical standards of their professional body

If your agent fails in these responsibilities, you can report them to their professional body and, in serious cases, to Revenue.

## Revoking Agent Access

You can revoke your tax agent's access at any time. The process is straightforward:

1. Log into your ROS account using your personal or company ROS certificate
2. Navigate to the agent management section
3. Select the agent link you wish to revoke
4. Confirm the revocation

The revocation takes effect immediately. Your former agent can no longer access your tax record, file returns, or communicate with Revenue on your behalf. They retain copies of any documents or working papers they created, but their access to your live Revenue record is terminated.

Common reasons for revoking agent access include:

- Switching to a new accountant (the new agent link replaces the old one, or you can have both active during a transition period)
- Bringing tax compliance in-house
- Dissatisfaction with the agent's service
- The agent retiring or closing their practice

When switching accountants, it is good practice to maintain the old agent link until the new one is fully active, ensuring there is no gap in agent coverage. Your new accountant will advise on the timing.

## Cost of a Tax Agent

There is no separate fee for appointing a tax agent. The agent link is simply the mechanism by which your accountant or tax advisor files returns and accesses your Revenue record on your behalf. The cost is included in whatever fee you pay for their professional services.

If you engage a standalone tax filing service (rather than a full accountancy service), typical fees in Ireland are:

- **Form 11 filing only:** EUR 200 to EUR 500 per year
- **CT1 filing only:** EUR 400 to EUR 800 per year
- **VAT3 filing:** EUR 50 to EUR 150 per return

These are lower than full accountancy fees because they cover only the filing function, not the preparation of accounts, tax planning, or advisory services. For most business owners, the full accountancy service provides better value because the filing is just one part of what you need.
