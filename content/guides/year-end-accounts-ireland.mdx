Year-end accounts are the financial statements prepared at the end of your business's accounting period. In Ireland, every business needs year-end accounts, whether you are a sole trader preparing figures for your Form 11 income tax return or a limited company meeting your obligations under the Companies Act 2014 and to the Revenue Commissioners.

Preparing for year end does not have to be stressful. With good bookkeeping throughout the year and a clear process for gathering the information your accountant needs, you can close your books efficiently and use the resulting accounts to make better business decisions for the year ahead.

## What Year-End Accounts Are

Year-end accounts are the complete set of financial statements that summarise your business's financial performance and position over a 12-month accounting period. They typically include:

- **Profit and loss account (income statement):** Shows your revenue, costs, and resulting profit or loss for the year
- **Balance sheet (statement of financial position):** Shows what your business owns, owes, and is worth at the year-end date
- **Notes to the accounts:** Provide additional detail on specific figures in the financial statements
- **Directors' report:** Required for limited companies, summarising the company's activities and financial performance

For sole traders, year-end accounts are simpler. You prepare a profit and loss account and a basic balance sheet, primarily for the purpose of completing your Form 11 income tax return. There is no legal requirement to file these publicly, but Revenue can request to see them during an audit.

For limited companies, year-end accounts are more formal. They must comply with the Companies Act 2014 and be filed with the Companies Registration Office (CRO) as part of your annual return.

## Sole Trader vs Company Requirements

### Sole Traders

As a sole trader in Ireland, your year-end obligations are:

- Prepare a profit and loss account and balance sheet for your accounting period
- Use these accounts to complete your annual Form 11 income tax return
- File your Form 11 and pay any balance of tax by 31 October (or mid-November if filing through ROS)
- Retain all supporting records for at least 6 years

Your accountant will typically prepare your year-end accounts and Form 11 together. The cost for a sole trader with straightforward affairs is usually EUR 500 to EUR 1,500, depending on the complexity of your business.

You choose your own accounting year end. Most sole traders use either 31 December (aligning with the calendar year) or 31 March, but you can choose any date. Once chosen, you should use it consistently each year.

### Limited Companies

Limited companies face more extensive year-end requirements:

- Prepare full financial statements complying with the Companies Act 2014
- File an annual return (Form B1) with the CRO within 56 days of your annual return date, accompanied by financial statements
- File a corporation tax return (Form CT1) with Revenue within 9 months of your financial year end
- Pay any balance of corporation tax

Your company's financial year end is set when the company is incorporated. The first accounting period can be up to 18 months, but subsequent periods are normally 12 months. You can change your year end, but this requires approval from Revenue and the CRO.

## Choosing Your Accounting Year End

For sole traders, the choice of year end can have tax implications. If your year end does not align with the calendar year (1 January to 31 December), the basis of assessment rules determine which year's profits are taxed in which tax year. The standard basis is the "preceding year basis," meaning your tax for 2026 is based on the profits of the accounting period ending in 2026.

The most common year-end dates for Irish businesses are:

- **31 December:** Aligns with the tax year. Simplest for sole traders.
- **31 March:** Popular because it gives you 7 months (until 31 October) to file your return and pay your tax
- **30 September:** Gives 13 months before tax filing deadline, but preliminary tax still due by 31 October
- **Company-specific dates:** Often set by the date of incorporation

Your accountant can advise on the best year end for your specific circumstances, taking into account your cash flow, seasonal patterns, and tax planning opportunities.

## Preparing for Year End

The quality of your year-end accounts depends entirely on the quality of your records throughout the year. The following steps will help you close your books efficiently:

### 1. Reconcile Your Bank Accounts

Ensure every transaction on your bank statements is recorded in your accounting records. Reconcile all bank accounts, credit card accounts, and any other financial accounts to the year-end date. Identify and investigate any unreconciled items.

### 2. Review Your Debtors (Accounts Receivable)

List all customers who owe you money at year end. For each debtor:

- Confirm the balance is correct
- Chase any overdue amounts
- Identify any bad debts that should be written off
- Consider whether a provision for doubtful debts is appropriate

### 3. Review Your Creditors (Accounts Payable)

List all suppliers you owe money to at year end. Ensure all invoices received before year end are recorded, even if not yet paid. Check for any supplier statements to confirm your balances match.

### 4. Count Your Stock (If Applicable)

If your business holds stock (inventory), conduct a physical stock count as close to your year-end date as possible. Record the quantities and values of all stock items. Stock is valued at the lower of cost and net realisable value. Identify any obsolete or damaged stock that should be written down.

### 5. Identify Prepayments and Accruals

**Prepayments** are expenses you have paid in advance that relate to the following period. For example, if your annual insurance premium of EUR 1,200 is paid in September and covers September to August, three months (EUR 300) relates to the current year and nine months (EUR 900) is a prepayment that belongs in the next year.

**Accruals** are expenses that relate to the current period but have not yet been invoiced or paid. Common accruals include your accountant's fee for preparing the year-end accounts, utility bills for the final weeks of the period, and any bonuses or commissions earned but not yet paid.

### 6. Review Fixed Assets

Check your fixed asset register against the physical assets in your business. Have you purchased any new assets during the year? Have you disposed of or scrapped any assets? Your accountant will calculate depreciation and capital allowances based on this information.

### 7. Gather Supporting Documentation

Your accountant will need:

- Bank statements for all accounts for the full year
- A list of debtors and creditors at year end
- Stock valuation (if applicable)
- Details of any fixed asset purchases or disposals
- Loan agreements and statements
- Lease agreements
- Directors' loan account details (for companies)
- Details of any unusual or one-off transactions
- Payroll summaries for the year

## Working with Your Accountant

Your relationship with your accountant is a partnership. The more organised and timely you are in providing information, the smoother the year-end process will be. Many accountants report that the biggest cause of delays in preparing year-end accounts is not the complexity of the work but the time it takes clients to provide complete records.

### Timing

Engage your accountant early. Do not wait until the filing deadline is approaching to start the year-end process. Ideally, provide your records to your accountant within 2 to 3 months of your year end. This gives them enough time to prepare the accounts, discuss any issues with you, and file within the deadline.

### Communication

Tell your accountant about anything unusual during the year: a major new contract, a dispute with a customer, a legal claim, a change in business activity, or a significant asset purchase. These events often have accounting and tax implications that your accountant needs to consider.

### Questions

When you receive your draft accounts, review them. Ask your accountant to explain any figures you do not understand. Common questions worth asking:

- How does this year compare to last year?
- Are there any areas of concern?
- What is my effective tax rate?
- Are there any tax-saving opportunities I should consider for next year?
- Is my business structured in the most tax-efficient way?

## CRO Filing Requirements

Every Irish limited company must file an annual return (Form B1) with the Companies Registration Office. The annual return includes basic company information (directors, registered office, share capital) and must be accompanied by financial statements.

### Filing Deadlines

Your annual return date (ARD) is set when the company is incorporated. It falls on the anniversary of the company's incorporation date each year. You must file your annual return within 56 days of the ARD.

**Late filing consequences:**

- **Financial penalty:** EUR 100 plus EUR 3 per day for every day the return is late, up to EUR 1,200 per return
- **Loss of audit exemption:** If you file late, you lose the audit exemption for the following two financial years, which means paying for an audit that could cost EUR 3,000 to EUR 8,000
- **Risk of strike-off:** Persistent non-filing can result in the CRO initiating strike-off proceedings against your company

### Abridged vs Full Accounts

Small companies (turnover under EUR 12 million, balance sheet under EUR 6 million, fewer than 50 employees -- meeting at least two of these three criteria) can file abridged accounts with the CRO. Abridged accounts contain a reduced balance sheet and limited notes but do not include a profit and loss account. This means your revenue and profit figures are not publicly visible.

Most Irish SMEs file abridged accounts. Full accounts must still be prepared for Revenue purposes and for the company's own records.

## Audit Exemption

Small companies that meet the size thresholds are exempt from the requirement to have their accounts audited. To claim the exemption, the company must:

- Meet at least two of the three size criteria (turnover, balance sheet, employees)
- Include a statement on the balance sheet confirming eligibility
- Not have any shareholder holding 10 per cent or more of voting rights who has requested an audit

Audit exemption saves significant money. Audit fees for small Irish companies typically range from EUR 3,000 to EUR 8,000. Even with the exemption, your accountant still prepares the accounts, but the work involved is less extensive than a full audit.

## Tax Return Preparation

Your year-end accounts form the basis of your tax return. However, accounting profit is not the same as taxable profit. Your accountant makes a number of adjustments to convert accounting profit to taxable profit:

**Add back:**
- Depreciation (replaced by capital allowances for tax purposes)
- Entertainment expenses (never tax-deductible in Ireland)
- Non-trade expenses
- Any personal expenses included in the accounts

**Deduct:**
- Capital allowances (the tax equivalent of depreciation)
- Losses brought forward from previous years
- Certain reliefs and credits

The resulting taxable profit is what your corporation tax (for companies) or income tax (for sole traders) is calculated on.

### Key Tax Deadlines

- **Sole traders:** Form 11 due by 31 October (ROS deadline mid-November)
- **Companies:** Form CT1 due within 9 months of financial year end
- **Preliminary tax (companies):** Due within 6 months of the start of the accounting period (small companies)
- **CRO annual return:** Due within 56 days of the annual return date

## Common Year-End Adjustments

Your accountant will make several standard adjustments when preparing your year-end accounts:

**Depreciation:** Writing down the value of fixed assets over their useful life. Common rates in Ireland are 20 per cent for computer equipment, 12.5 per cent for furniture, and 20 per cent for motor vehicles.

**Bad debt provision:** Setting aside an amount for debts that may not be collected. Specific provisions for identified doubtful debts are allowable for tax.

**Stock adjustment:** Adjusting the stock figure to reflect the actual quantity and value at year end, including write-downs for obsolete or damaged items.

**Accruals and prepayments:** Matching expenses to the period they relate to, as described above.

**Director's loan account:** For companies, reviewing the balance on any director's loan account and ensuring any loans to directors comply with the Companies Act 2014.

**Pension contributions:** Ensuring any pension contributions are properly recorded and the tax relief is claimed.

## Timeline for Year-End Process

A typical timeline for a company with a 31 December year end:

| When | What |
|------|------|
| January | Finalise December bank reconciliation |
| January-February | Complete stock count, finalise debtors and creditors lists |
| February | Provide records to accountant |
| March-April | Accountant prepares draft accounts |
| April | Review and approve draft accounts |
| May-June | Sign accounts, file annual return with CRO |
| September | File CT1 with Revenue, pay balance of corporation tax |

Starting earlier gives you more time to resolve any issues and avoids the stress of last-minute filing.
