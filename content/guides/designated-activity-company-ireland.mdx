A designated activity company (DAC) in Ireland is a private limited company that has an objects clause in its constitution restricting the activities the company can carry on. Unlike an LTD (private company limited by shares), which can carry on any lawful activity, a DAC can only engage in the specific activities defined in its objects clause. The DAC replaced the old-style private limited company under the Companies Act 2014 and is primarily used in regulated industries, joint ventures, and special purpose vehicles where restricting the company's scope of activity is required or advantageous.

Most small businesses in Ireland do not need a DAC. The standard LTD company type is simpler, more flexible, and suitable for the vast majority of trading businesses. However, understanding the difference between the two is important when incorporating, as certain situations specifically call for a DAC structure.

## Company Types Under the Companies Act 2014

The Companies Act 2014 reorganised and simplified the types of companies that can be formed in Ireland. Before the Act came into force on 1 June 2015, the most common private company type was the private company limited by shares, which had both a memorandum of association and articles of association, including an objects clause.

The 2014 Act introduced a new default private company type -- the LTD -- and created several other company types to cover different needs:

| Company Type | Abbreviation | Key Feature |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Private company limited by shares | LTD | No objects clause, single-document constitution, most flexible |
| Designated activity company limited by shares | DAC | Has objects clause, two-part constitution (memorandum and articles) |
| Designated activity company limited by guarantee | DAC | Has objects clause, limited by guarantee rather than shares |
| Company limited by guarantee | CLG | Non-profit, no share capital, limited by members' guarantee |
| Public limited company | PLC | Can offer shares to the public, minimum share capital EUR 25,000 |
| Unlimited company | ULC/PUC | No limit on members' liability |

For small businesses, the choice is almost always between LTD and DAC. The other types serve specific purposes: CLG for charities and non-profits, PLC for companies seeking public listing, and unlimited companies for specific structuring reasons.

## LTD vs DAC: Key Differences

The differences between an LTD and a DAC are more technical than practical for most businesses, but they are significant in certain contexts:

### Constitution

**LTD:** Has a single-document constitution. There is no memorandum of association and no articles of association -- everything is in one document. The constitution is simpler and easier to amend.

**DAC:** Has a two-part constitution consisting of a memorandum of association and articles of association, similar to the old-style company format. The memorandum contains the objects clause defining what the company can do.

### Objects Clause

**LTD:** Has no objects clause. An LTD company has full and unlimited capacity to carry on any lawful activity. The company can change its business without amending its constitution.

**DAC:** Has an objects clause that restricts the company's activities to those specified in the memorandum. If the company engages in activities outside its objects clause, those actions may be ultra vires (beyond the company's powers). While the Companies Act 2014 provides some protection for third parties dealing with the company in good faith, operating outside the objects clause can still create legal issues.

### Number of Directors

**LTD:** Minimum of 1 director, maximum of 25.

**DAC:** Minimum of 2 directors, maximum of 25. This is a significant practical difference for single-person businesses -- a DAC always requires at least two directors.

### Company Secretary

Both LTD and DAC companies must have a company secretary. In both cases, the sole director (LTD) or a sole director of a two-director company (DAC) cannot also be the secretary.

### Designation in Name

**LTD:** The company name must end in "Limited" or "Ltd" (or the Irish equivalents).

**DAC:** If limited by shares, the company name must end in "Designated Activity Company" or "DAC" (or Irish equivalents). If limited by guarantee, it ends in "Designated Activity Company Limited by Guarantee" or "DAC Limited by Guarantee."

### Audit Exemption

Both LTD and DAC companies can avail of the audit exemption if they meet the qualifying criteria (turnover below EUR 12 million, balance sheet total below EUR 6 million, and average employees below 50). The audit exemption thresholds are the same for both company types.

### Shareholder Numbers

**LTD:** Minimum 1 shareholder, maximum 149.

**DAC (limited by shares):** Minimum 1 shareholder, maximum 149.

The shareholder limits are identical in practice.

## When You Need a DAC

Despite the LTD being the default and most popular choice, there are specific situations where a DAC is required or preferred:

### Regulated Industries

Certain regulated industries and licensing bodies require the company to have an objects clause restricting its activities. This is a form of protection that ensures the company can only carry on the specific activity it is licensed to perform. Examples include:

- **Licensed credit institutions:** Banks and credit unions regulated by the Central Bank may be required to operate as a DAC
- **Insurance companies:** Certain insurance undertakings must operate within a defined objects clause
- **Special purpose vehicles (SPVs) in securitisation:** The Central Bank's securitisation regulations may require the SPV to be a DAC with a restricted objects clause

### Joint Ventures

When two or more parties form a company for a specific joint venture or project, a DAC can be useful because:

- The objects clause clearly defines the scope of the joint venture, preventing mission creep
- Each party knows exactly what activities the company can carry on
- It provides comfort to all parties that the company will not be used for purposes beyond the agreed scope

### Lender Requirements

Some lenders, particularly in property finance and project finance, require the borrowing entity to be a DAC. This restricts the company's activities to the specific project or property being financed, which provides comfort to the lender that the company will not take on unrelated risks that could jeopardise its ability to repay the loan.

### Legacy Companies

When the Companies Act 2014 came into force on 1 June 2015, existing private companies limited by shares that did not convert to the new LTD type within the transition period were automatically re-registered as DACs. This means many older Irish companies are DACs simply because they did not actively convert to the LTD type during the 18-month transition period that ended on 30 November 2016.

These companies continue to operate as DACs unless they choose to convert to an LTD, which is a straightforward process involving a special resolution and filing with the CRO.

## Forming a DAC

The process of forming a DAC is similar to forming an LTD company, with some additional requirements:

### Step 1: Prepare the Constitution

A DAC requires a two-part constitution:

**Memorandum of association:**
- The company's name (ending in "DAC" or "Designated Activity Company")
- The objects of the company (the specific activities it is authorised to carry on)
- A statement that the liability of members is limited
- The authorised share capital and its division into shares

**Articles of association:**
- The internal regulations of the company (similar to the LTD constitution)
- Provisions for directors' meetings, shareholders' meetings, share transfers, dividends, and other governance matters

The objects clause is the critical element. It should be drafted carefully to cover all intended activities while being specific enough to serve its restrictive purpose. Overly narrow objects clauses can cause practical problems if the company needs to expand its activities later.

### Step 2: Appoint Directors and Secretary

A DAC must have at least two directors (compared to one for an LTD) and a separate company secretary. At least one director must be resident in the European Economic Area (EEA), or the company must hold a Section 137 bond.

### Step 3: File Form A1 with the CRO

The Form A1 is the same form used for all company types. File it online via CORE at core.cro.ie. The registration fee is EUR 50 online or EUR 100 by post.

### Step 4: Post-Incorporation Steps

The post-incorporation steps are the same as for an LTD company:

- Register for tax with Revenue using Form TR2
- Open a company bank account
- Register beneficial owners with the RBO
- Note the Annual Return Date (6 months after incorporation)

## Converting Between LTD and DAC

### Converting from DAC to LTD

A DAC can convert to an LTD company by:

1. Passing a special resolution to re-register as an LTD
2. Adopting a new single-document constitution (replacing the memorandum and articles)
3. Filing the conversion documents with the CRO
4. The CRO issues a new certificate of incorporation reflecting the LTD status

The conversion is relatively straightforward and does not create a new company -- the same CRO number and legal entity continue, just under a different company type.

**Why convert?** If the original reason for the DAC structure no longer applies (the regulatory requirement has changed, the joint venture has ended, or the lender no longer requires it), converting to an LTD simplifies the company's governance. An LTD has a simpler constitution, requires only one director, and has no objects clause restriction.

### Converting from LTD to DAC

An LTD can convert to a DAC by:

1. Passing a special resolution to re-register as a DAC
2. Adopting a two-part constitution (memorandum with objects clause and articles of association)
3. Filing the conversion documents with the CRO

This conversion is less common but may be required if the company enters a regulated industry or takes on financing that requires a DAC structure.

## Ultra Vires and the Objects Clause

One of the most important legal concepts relating to DACs is the doctrine of ultra vires (beyond the powers). If a DAC carries on an activity that is not covered by its objects clause, that activity may be considered ultra vires.

Under the old company law (pre-2014), ultra vires acts were void and could not be ratified, which caused significant problems for third parties dealing with the company. The Companies Act 2014 provides protection for third parties dealing with the company in good faith -- a transaction is not invalid merely because it is outside the company's objects clause, provided the third party was acting in good faith and did not know the transaction was ultra vires.

However, ultra vires acts can still create internal legal issues:

- Directors who authorise ultra vires acts may be in breach of their duty to act in accordance with the company's constitution
- Shareholders can seek an injunction to restrain ultra vires activities
- The transaction may be challengeable in certain circumstances

For these reasons, it is important that a DAC's objects clause is drafted broadly enough to cover all current and reasonably anticipated activities, while still being specific enough to satisfy any regulatory or contractual requirement.

## Companies Act 2014: Overview of Company Types

For completeness, here is a summary of all the company types available under the Companies Act 2014 and their key characteristics:

| Feature | LTD | DAC (Shares) | DAC (Guarantee) | CLG | PLC |
|---------|-----|-------------|----------------|-----|-----|
| Objects clause | No | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Constitution | Single document | Memorandum + Articles | Memorandum + Articles | Memorandum + Articles | Memorandum + Articles |
| Min directors | 1 | 2 | 2 | 2 | 2 |
| Max members | 149 | 149 | 149 | No limit | No limit |
| Share capital | Yes | Yes | No | No | Yes (min EUR 25,000) |
| AGM required | Can dispense | Required | Required | Required | Required |
| Can pass written resolutions | Yes | Limited | Limited | Limited | No |
| Audit exemption | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes | No |
| Name ending | Limited/Ltd | DAC | DAC Ltd by Guarantee | CLG | PLC |

### Which Type Is Right for Your Business

**Choose LTD if:** You are starting a small business, incorporating as a sole trader, or setting up any standard trading company. The LTD is simpler, more flexible, and suitable for the vast majority of businesses.

**Choose DAC if:** Your industry regulator requires it, your lender requires it, you are setting up a joint venture with a defined scope, or you specifically need an objects clause to restrict the company's activities.

**Choose CLG if:** You are setting up a non-profit organisation, charity, sports club, or community organisation that does not distribute profits to members.

**Choose PLC if:** You intend to offer shares to the public or seek a stock exchange listing. Note the minimum share capital requirement of EUR 25,000.

For most readers of this guide who are Irish small business owners, sole traders considering incorporation, or startup founders, the LTD company is the right choice.
