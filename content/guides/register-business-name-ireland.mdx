To register a business name in Ireland, you file an RBN1 form with the Companies Registration Office (CRO) through their CORE online system at core.cro.ie. The filing fee is EUR 20 for online submissions or EUR 40 for paper filings. Registration is required by law whenever a sole trader, partnership, or company trades under a name other than their own legal name or registered company name.

Business name registration is one of the first practical steps for many new businesses in Ireland. It gives you the legal right to trade under your chosen name, provides a level of protection against others using the same name, and is often required before you can open a business bank account or apply for certain permits. This guide covers when registration is required, how the process works, what rules apply to choosing a name, and how to maintain your registration over time.

## When Is Business Name Registration Required?

Under the Registration of Business Names Act 1963 (as amended), you must register your business name with the CRO if you carry on business under a name other than your own true surname (for sole traders), the true surnames of all partners (for partnerships), or your registered company name (for limited companies).

### Sole Traders

If your name is Mary Kelly and you trade as "Mary Kelly Consulting," you do not need to register a business name because you are trading under your own name. However, if you trade as "Kelly Digital Marketing" or "Emerald Consulting," you must register that name because it differs from your true surname.

Adding descriptive words to your surname also triggers the registration requirement. "Kelly & Associates" or "Mary Kelly & Co" would require registration because these are not simply your true name.

### Partnerships

A partnership must register if it trades under any name other than the true surnames of all the partners. For example, if Sarah Murphy and John O'Brien form a partnership and trade as "Murphy O'Brien Accountants," they do not need to register. But if they trade as "Liffey Accountancy Services," registration is required.

### Limited Companies

A company must register any trading name or business name that differs from its registered company name as filed with the CRO. For example, if your company is registered as "Greenfield Technology Solutions Limited" but you trade publicly as "Greenfield Tech," you need to register "Greenfield Tech" as a business name.

## How to Register: Step by Step

### Step 1: Check Name Availability

Before filing your application, check whether your proposed business name is already in use. You can search the CRO's register of business names and company names at core.cro.ie. The search is free and returns all registered names that match or are similar to your query.

A few important points about name availability:

- The CRO will refuse a name that is identical to an existing registered business name or company name
- Names that are very similar to existing names may also be refused if they could cause confusion
- The CRO's check is based on registered names only -- it does not check trademarks, domain names, or social media handles
- Even if a name is available on the CRO register, someone else may have trademark rights to it. Consider checking the Irish Patents Office trademark register and the EU Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO) database as well

### Step 2: Prepare Your RBN1 Form

The RBN1 is the application form for registration of a business name. You can complete it online through the CORE system or download a paper form from the CRO website. The form requires:

- **The proposed business name:** The exact name you intend to trade under
- **Nature of the business:** A description of your principal business activity
- **Date of commencement:** The date you started or intend to start trading under this name
- **Principal place of business:** The address where the business is mainly carried on
- **Applicant details:** Your name, address, nationality, and date of birth (for individuals); registered name and number (for companies)
- **Details of all persons carrying on business:** If a partnership, details of all partners

### Step 3: Submit Online via CORE

The recommended method is to submit your application through the CRO's CORE system at core.cro.ie. You need to create a CORE account if you do not already have one. The online process is straightforward:

1. Log in to CORE
2. Select "Business Name" from the filing options
3. Complete the RBN1 form online
4. Pay the EUR 20 filing fee by credit or debit card
5. Submit the application

### Step 4: CRO Review and Registration

After submission, the CRO reviews your application. For online applications, processing typically takes 5 to 10 working days. The CRO checks that:

- The name is not identical or too similar to an existing registered name
- The name does not contain restricted words
- The form is correctly completed with all required information

If the CRO has any concerns, they will write to you (or your agent) requesting clarification or amendment. If the application is straightforward, you will receive a Certificate of Business Name Registration, which confirms your right to trade under that name.

### Paper Submission Alternative

You can also submit a paper RBN1 form by post to the CRO at Bloom House, Gloucester Place Lower, Dublin 1. The filing fee for paper submissions is EUR 40 (double the online fee). Processing times for paper applications are longer, typically 2 to 4 weeks.

## Naming Rules and Restrictions

The CRO has specific rules about what names can and cannot be registered as business names.

### Prohibited Names

The following types of names will be refused by the CRO:

- **Names identical to existing registrations:** An exact match with an existing registered business name or company name will be refused
- **Misleading names:** Names that suggest a connection to the government, a state body, or a professional body where none exists (for example, "Revenue Approved Tax Services")
- **Offensive names:** Names that are offensive, obscene, or contrary to public order
- **Names suggesting corporate status:** A sole trader or partnership cannot include "Limited," "Ltd," "plc," "Teoranta," or "Teo" in a business name, as these imply incorporation

### Restricted Words

Certain words require the prior written consent of a relevant authority before they can be included in a business name:

- **"Bank," "banker," or "banking":** Requires consent from the Central Bank of Ireland
- **"Insurance," "assurance," or "re-insurance":** Requires consent from the Central Bank of Ireland
- **"Society," "co-operative," or "co-op":** May require clarification from the Registrar
- **"University," "institute," or "college":** May require consent from the Department of Education
- **"Charity" or "charitable":** May require evidence of charitable status

### Best Practices for Choosing a Name

When choosing your business name, consider these practical factors:

- **Keep it short and memorable:** A concise name is easier for customers to remember, spell, and search for online
- **Consider the domain name:** Check whether a matching .ie or .com domain is available before committing to a name
- **Avoid overly generic names:** "Irish Business Services" may be technically registrable but is difficult to distinguish and brand
- **Think about scalability:** If you might expand beyond your initial services, avoid a name that is too narrow (for example, "Dublin Plumber" limits you geographically and by trade)
- **Check social media availability:** Ensure the name is available as a handle on platforms relevant to your business

## Business Name vs Company Name vs Trading Name

These terms are often confused. Here is the distinction in Irish law:

### Business Name

A name registered under the Registration of Business Names Act. It is the name under which a sole trader, partnership, or company carries on business when that name differs from their legal or registered name. Registered with the CRO using the RBN1 form.

### Company Name

The official name of a limited company as registered on its Certificate of Incorporation. This is the legal name of the company and appears on the CRO register. A company name is chosen at the time of incorporation and can be changed subsequently with CRO approval.

### Trading Name

An informal term that typically refers to the name a business uses in day-to-day operations. If a trading name differs from the owner's name or the company's registered name, it must be registered as a business name with the CRO.

In practice, a limited company might have all three: a registered company name ("ABC Solutions Limited"), a registered business name ("ABC Solutions"), and be commonly known by a trading name ("ABC").

## Duration and Renewal of Registration

Business name registration in Ireland is not permanent. The renewal requirements differ depending on whether the registration is held by an individual or a body corporate.

### Individuals and Partnerships

For sole traders and partnerships, business name registration must be renewed every year. The CRO sends a renewal notice, and the renewal fee must be paid to keep the registration active. If you fail to renew, the CRO will remove the name from the register, and it becomes available for someone else to register.

### Companies

For limited companies holding a business name registration, renewal is required every 28 months. The same principle applies -- failure to renew leads to removal from the register.

### Cancellation

If you stop trading under a registered business name, you should notify the CRO using form RBN3 (Cessation of Business Name). This formally removes the name from the register and avoids unnecessary renewal notices and fees.

## Display Requirements

Once registered, you have a legal obligation to display your business name and the name of the proprietor (or all partners) in a conspicuous position at your principal place of business. This requirement applies to:

- Your business premises (a sign or notice visible to visitors)
- All business correspondence (letters, emails, invoices)
- All business documents (contracts, terms of business, quotations)
- Your website (typically in the footer or contact page)

For sole traders, you must show both the business name and your own name. For example, your invoices should state "Emerald Consulting, a business name of Mary Kelly" or similar wording that makes the proprietor's identity clear.

Failure to comply with display requirements can result in a fine under the Registration of Business Names Act.

## Common Questions About Business Names and Trademarks

Registering a business name with the CRO is not the same as registering a trademark. A CRO business name registration simply records the fact that you are trading under that name. It does not give you exclusive rights to the name across all industries or geographies.

A trademark registration, on the other hand, gives you exclusive legal rights to use that name (or logo) in connection with specific goods or services. Trademarks in Ireland are registered through the Irish Patents Office (IPOI) or, for EU-wide protection, through the European Union Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO).

If your business name is important to your brand and you want to prevent others from using it, consider trademark registration in addition to CRO business name registration. A trademark provides much stronger legal protection and can be enforced through the courts.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Not registering at all.** Some sole traders begin trading under a business name without registering it. This is a legal offence under the Registration of Business Names Act and can result in a fine. It also means you may not be able to open a business bank account in that name or enter into contracts under it.

**Confusing business name registration with company formation.** Registering a business name does not create a limited company. If you want the benefits of limited liability, you need to incorporate a company through the CRO using the A1 form -- a completely separate process.

**Not checking trademarks.** Your business name might be available on the CRO register but could infringe an existing trademark. A trademark holder can take legal action to prevent you from using their mark, even if the CRO registered your business name without objection.

**Letting registration lapse.** If you forget to renew your business name registration, the CRO removes it from the register. Someone else could then register the same name. Set a reminder for your renewal date.

**Not updating details.** If your address, the nature of your business, or the proprietor details change, you must notify the CRO. Trading with outdated registration information is a compliance issue.
