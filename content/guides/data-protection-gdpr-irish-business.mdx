The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) applies to every Irish business that collects, stores, or processes personal data -- and that includes virtually every business in Ireland. Whether you have a mailing list with ten email addresses or a customer database with thousands of records, GDPR imposes specific obligations on how you handle that data. The Data Protection Commission (DPC) in Ireland is the supervisory authority responsible for enforcing these rules, and it has the power to impose significant fines for non-compliance.

This guide provides a practical overview of GDPR for Irish small businesses. It explains what the regulation requires, how the DPC operates, and the concrete steps you should take to ensure your business complies with data protection law.

## What GDPR Means for Small Businesses

GDPR is an EU regulation that has been directly applicable in Ireland since 25 May 2018. It is supplemented by the Data Protection Act 2018, which gives effect to certain GDPR provisions in Irish law. Together, these laws establish a comprehensive framework for how personal data must be handled.

Personal data is any information that relates to an identified or identifiable individual. This includes obvious identifiers like names, email addresses, and phone numbers, but also extends to IP addresses, location data, online identifiers such as cookies, and even opinions about an individual.

For small businesses, the most common types of personal data you process include:

- Customer names, addresses, and contact details
- Employee records including PPS numbers, payroll data, and performance reviews
- Email subscriber lists
- Website analytics data (if it can identify individuals)
- CCTV footage (if you have cameras on your premises)
- Invoicing and payment records

The key principle of GDPR is that individuals (known as data subjects) have rights over their personal data, and organisations that process that data (known as data controllers) must respect those rights and handle data responsibly.

## The Data Protection Commission (DPC)

The DPC is Ireland's independent supervisory authority for data protection. Based in Dublin and Portarlington, the DPC is responsible for:

- Monitoring and enforcing GDPR compliance across Ireland
- Handling complaints from individuals about data protection breaches
- Conducting investigations and audits of organisations
- Issuing guidance on data protection best practice
- Imposing administrative fines for breaches of GDPR

Because many large technology companies have their European headquarters in Ireland, the DPC has a particularly prominent role in EU data protection enforcement. However, it also handles complaints and investigations involving small and medium-sized Irish businesses.

You can contact the DPC through its website at dataprotection.ie, by phone, or by post. The DPC also publishes guidance documents, case studies, and FAQs that are specifically relevant to small businesses.

## Lawful Basis for Processing Personal Data

Under GDPR, you must have a lawful basis for every processing activity involving personal data. There are six lawful bases, but three are most relevant to small businesses:

### Consent

The individual has given clear, specific, and informed consent to the processing of their personal data for a stated purpose. Consent must be freely given, meaning you cannot make a service conditional on consent to unrelated processing. Consent must also be as easy to withdraw as it was to give.

For email marketing, consent is the standard lawful basis. You must obtain explicit opt-in consent before adding someone to your mailing list, and you must provide an unsubscribe option in every email.

### Contract

Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract with the individual, or to take steps at the individual's request before entering into a contract. For example, you need to process a customer's address to deliver goods they have ordered, and you need their payment details to process the transaction.

### Legitimate Interest

Processing is necessary for your legitimate interests (or those of a third party), provided those interests are not overridden by the individual's rights and freedoms. For example, you may have a legitimate interest in processing customer data for fraud prevention or for internal administration.

When relying on legitimate interest, you should document a Legitimate Interest Assessment (LIA) that weighs your interests against the impact on the individual.

## Privacy Notice Requirements

GDPR requires you to provide individuals with clear, transparent information about how you process their personal data. This information is typically provided through a privacy notice (sometimes called a privacy policy) on your website and, where relevant, in other communications.

Your privacy notice must include:

- **Your identity and contact details** as the data controller
- **What personal data you collect** and for what purposes
- **The lawful basis** for each processing activity
- **Who you share data with** (third parties, processors, international transfers)
- **How long you retain data** (retention periods for each category)
- **Individual rights** and how to exercise them
- **The right to complain** to the DPC
- **Whether data is used for automated decision-making** or profiling

The privacy notice must be written in clear, plain language. Avoid legal jargon and make the notice easily accessible -- typically linked from your website footer and referenced in any forms that collect personal data.

## Data Subject Rights

Under GDPR, individuals have specific rights regarding their personal data. You must be able to respond to requests to exercise these rights within **one month** of receiving the request:

**Right of access.** Individuals can request a copy of all personal data you hold about them, along with information about how and why you process it. This is commonly known as a Subject Access Request (SAR).

**Right to rectification.** Individuals can request correction of inaccurate or incomplete personal data.

**Right to erasure (right to be forgotten).** Individuals can request deletion of their personal data in certain circumstances, such as when the data is no longer necessary for its original purpose, or when they withdraw consent.

**Right to restriction.** Individuals can request that you limit how you process their data, for example while a dispute about accuracy is being resolved.

**Right to data portability.** Individuals can request their personal data in a commonly used, machine-readable format so they can transfer it to another controller.

**Right to object.** Individuals can object to processing based on legitimate interest or for direct marketing purposes. If someone objects to direct marketing, you must stop processing their data for that purpose immediately.

## Data Breach Notification

A personal data breach is any security incident that leads to the accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to personal data. Examples include a stolen laptop containing customer data, a hacking incident that exposes your database, or accidentally sending a mass email with all recipients visible in the To field.

Under GDPR, you must:

- **Notify the DPC within 72 hours** of becoming aware of a breach that poses a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms
- **Notify affected individuals without undue delay** if the breach is likely to result in a high risk to their rights and freedoms
- **Document all breaches** in an internal breach register, regardless of whether they are reported to the DPC

The notification to the DPC can be made through the online breach notification form on the DPC's website. You should include details of the nature of the breach, the categories and approximate number of data subjects affected, the likely consequences, and the measures taken to address the breach.

Not every breach needs to be reported to the DPC. If the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to individuals, you only need to document it internally. However, when in doubt, it is safer to report.

## Record of Processing Activities (ROPA)

GDPR requires organisations to maintain a Record of Processing Activities. While there is a formal exemption for organisations with fewer than 250 employees, this exemption does not apply if the processing is likely to result in a risk to individuals, the processing is not occasional, or the processing includes special categories of data (such as health data) or criminal conviction data.

In practice, the DPC recommends that all organisations, regardless of size, maintain a ROPA. This is a written document that records:

- The purposes of each processing activity
- The categories of personal data processed
- The categories of data subjects
- The categories of recipients
- Transfers to third countries (if any)
- Retention periods
- A general description of security measures

A ROPA does not need to be complex. A spreadsheet listing each type of personal data you collect, why you collect it, where you store it, who can access it, and how long you keep it is sufficient for most small businesses.

## Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs)

A Data Protection Impact Assessment is required when a type of processing is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals. This is particularly relevant when:

- Using new technologies
- Processing data on a large scale
- Systematically monitoring a publicly accessible area (such as extensive CCTV)
- Processing special categories of data on a large scale

Most small businesses will not routinely need to conduct DPIAs. However, if you are implementing new technology such as facial recognition, AI-based profiling, or large-scale monitoring, you should assess whether a DPIA is required.

## Appointing a Data Protection Officer (DPO)

Under GDPR, you must appoint a DPO if:

- You are a public authority or body
- Your core activities require regular and systematic monitoring of data subjects on a large scale
- Your core activities consist of processing special categories of data on a large scale

Most small businesses do not need to appoint a DPO. However, even if not legally required, it is good practice to designate someone within your organisation as the person responsible for data protection matters. This person does not need to have the formal title of DPO but should have sufficient knowledge of GDPR to handle queries and oversee compliance.

## Practical Steps for GDPR Compliance

Here are the concrete steps every Irish small business should take:

**Audit your data.** Identify what personal data you collect, where it is stored, who has access to it, why you process it, and how long you keep it. This audit forms the basis of your ROPA.

**Write a privacy notice.** Publish a clear, accessible privacy notice on your website that covers all GDPR requirements. Review and update it whenever your processing activities change.

**Review your consent mechanisms.** If you collect email addresses or other data based on consent, ensure your opt-in process meets GDPR standards: clear, specific, informed, and freely given. Pre-ticked boxes are not valid consent.

**Implement data security measures.** Use appropriate technical and organisational measures to protect personal data. This includes encryption, password policies, access controls, regular backups, and staff training.

**Establish a breach response plan.** Know what to do if a data breach occurs. Document your response procedure, including who to notify, how to assess the risk, and how to report to the DPC within 72 hours.

**Respond to data subject requests.** Have a process for handling access requests, erasure requests, and other rights. You must respond within one month.

**Review third-party processors.** If you use third-party services that process personal data on your behalf (such as email marketing platforms, cloud storage, or payroll providers), ensure you have a data processing agreement in place with each provider.

**Train your staff.** Ensure all employees who handle personal data understand their GDPR obligations. Training does not need to be formal or expensive but should cover the basics of data protection, recognising breaches, and handling data subject requests.

## Common GDPR Mistakes by Small Businesses

**No privacy notice.** Many small businesses still do not have a privacy notice on their website. This is a basic GDPR requirement and one of the first things the DPC will check.

**Buying email lists.** Purchasing email lists and sending marketing emails to people who have not consented is a clear GDPR violation. Every recipient of marketing emails must have opted in specifically to receive communications from your business.

**Keeping data indefinitely.** GDPR requires that personal data be kept only for as long as necessary for its purpose. Define retention periods for each category of data and delete data when the retention period expires.

**Ignoring subject access requests.** When an individual requests access to their data, you must respond within one month. Ignoring or delaying a response is a breach of GDPR and can result in a complaint to the DPC.

**No data processing agreements.** If third parties process personal data on your behalf, you need a written data processing agreement. Using a cloud-based CRM, email platform, or accounting software without a processing agreement leaves you exposed.

## Fines and Enforcement

The DPC has the power to impose administrative fines for GDPR breaches. The maximum fines under GDPR are:

- **Up to EUR 10 million or 2 per cent of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher) for breaches of certain provisions, such as failing to maintain records or failing to notify a breach
- **Up to EUR 20 million or 4 per cent of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher) for breaches of key provisions, such as processing without a lawful basis or failing to respect data subject rights

In practice, fines imposed on small businesses are typically much smaller than these maximums. The DPC considers factors such as the nature and seriousness of the breach, whether it was intentional or negligent, the number of data subjects affected, and what steps the business took to mitigate the damage.

The DPC also has corrective powers short of fines, including issuing warnings, reprimands, and orders to bring processing into compliance. For most small businesses, a DPC investigation is more likely to result in corrective orders and recommendations than in a large fine.

However, the reputational damage of a data protection complaint or investigation can be significant, particularly for small businesses that rely on customer trust.
