Farming in Ireland benefits from one of the most generous sets of tax reliefs available to any sector. Revenue and successive governments have recognised the unique challenges of agricultural income, which fluctuates with weather, commodity prices, and EU policy changes. As a result, Irish farmers have access to income averaging, stock relief, enhanced capital allowances, stamp duty exemptions, and agricultural relief for Capital Acquisitions Tax that are not available to other businesses. This guide covers every accounting and tax consideration specific to farming in Ireland, from day-to-day bookkeeping through to succession planning.

Understanding these reliefs is essential because many of them must be actively claimed. They are not applied automatically. A farmer who does not claim stock relief, income averaging, or the young trained farmer reliefs is paying significantly more tax than necessary.

## Income Averaging for Farmers

Income averaging is one of the most valuable tax reliefs available to Irish farmers. Farm income can vary dramatically from year to year due to weather, disease, commodity price fluctuations, and the timing of EU payments. Without income averaging, a single good year could push you into the higher tax bracket, resulting in a disproportionately large tax bill.

### How Income Averaging Works

Under income averaging, your taxable farming profit for the current year is calculated as the average of your farming profits over the current year and the four preceding years. This five-year averaging smooths out the peaks and troughs of agricultural income.

For example, if your farming profits over five years were EUR 20,000, EUR 15,000, EUR 60,000, EUR 25,000, and EUR 30,000, your taxable profit in year five would be the average: EUR 30,000. Without averaging, you would pay tax on EUR 30,000 in that year anyway, but in the year your profit was EUR 60,000, averaging would have reduced your taxable income from EUR 60,000 to the five-year average, potentially keeping you below the higher rate threshold.

### Eligibility

Income averaging is available to individuals (not companies) whose farming profits make up a significant portion of their total income. You cannot use income averaging if:

- Your other income (non-farming) exceeds EUR 40,000
- You are a company or partnership where any partner is a company
- You opt out (averaging is elective, and you can withdraw, though there are consequences)

### Opting In and Out

You elect for income averaging on your Form 11. Once elected, it continues until you opt out. If you opt out, you must wait four years before opting back in. Revenue also requires you to pay any deferred tax that resulted from the averaging when you withdraw.

Income averaging requires careful planning. Your accountant should model the impact of averaging each year to ensure it remains beneficial. In years of consistently high income, averaging may not help. In years of volatile income, it can save thousands in tax.

## Stock Relief for Farmers

Stock relief allows farmers to reduce their taxable income by a percentage of the increase in the value of their farming stock (livestock, crops, and produce) during the accounting period. This relief recognises that an increase in stock values is often unrealised and does not represent cash in hand.

### General Stock Relief (25 per cent)

All farmers can claim general stock relief at 25 per cent. This means that if your closing stock is worth EUR 20,000 more than your opening stock, you can deduct EUR 5,000 (25 per cent of EUR 20,000) from your taxable farming income.

### Young Trained Farmer Stock Relief (100 per cent)

Farmers who qualify as young trained farmers can claim 100 per cent stock relief, effectively eliminating any tax liability arising from increases in stock values. To qualify, you must:

- Be under 35 years of age at the start of the tax year
- Hold a relevant agricultural qualification (at least a Level 6 on the National Framework of Qualifications in agriculture)
- Have submitted a business plan to Teagasc

The 100 per cent relief is available for the first four years of farming. This is an enormously valuable relief for young farmers building up their herds or flocks.

### Claiming Stock Relief

Stock relief is claimed on your annual tax return. You must maintain accurate stock records showing opening and closing values. Revenue accepts valuations based on the standard methods: cost price, market value, or the deemed cost basis commonly used in Irish farming accounts.

## Capital Allowances on Farm Assets

Capital allowances allow you to write off the cost of farm buildings, equipment, and machinery against your taxable income over time. For farmers, the standard capital allowance rate is 12.5 per cent per year over 8 years for plant and machinery (tractors, harvesters, milking equipment, feeding systems, etc.).

### Farm Buildings

Capital allowances on farm buildings are available at a rate of 15 per cent per year for the first 6 years and 10 per cent in year 7, giving a full write-off over 7 years. This applies to buildings used for the intensive production of livestock (such as pig houses, poultry houses, and calf sheds), greenhouses, and other qualifying farm structures.

General farm buildings such as hay sheds, slurry tanks, and silage pits qualify for industrial buildings allowances. The conditions and rates can be complex, so professional advice is recommended.

### Accelerated Capital Allowances

Young trained farmers may qualify for accelerated capital allowances, allowing them to write off the cost of certain equipment and buildings more quickly. This front-loads the tax relief, providing a cash flow benefit in the early years of farming.

### Energy-Efficient Equipment

Accelerated capital allowances are also available for energy-efficient equipment. If you invest in qualifying equipment from the SEAI's approved list (such as energy-efficient motors, lighting, or heating systems), you can claim the full cost as a capital allowance in the year of purchase rather than spreading it over 8 years.

## Young Trained Farmer Stamp Duty Exemption

Young trained farmers who meet the qualifying conditions (under 35, holding a relevant agricultural qualification, and farming the land on a commercial basis for at least 5 years) are exempt from stamp duty on the purchase of agricultural land. Given that stamp duty on non-residential property is 7.5 per cent, this exemption can save a young farmer purchasing EUR 500,000 of land a total of EUR 37,500.

The exemption must be claimed at the time of the land purchase, and you must submit a declaration confirming that you meet the qualifying conditions. If you cease farming the land within 5 years, the stamp duty becomes payable with interest.

## Agricultural Relief for Capital Acquisitions Tax

Agricultural relief is one of the most significant tax reliefs for farm families. When agricultural property (land, buildings, livestock, and machinery) is transferred by gift or inheritance, agricultural relief reduces the taxable value of the agricultural assets by 90 per cent.

For example, if a farm valued at EUR 1,000,000 is inherited, agricultural relief reduces the taxable value to EUR 100,000. If this falls within the relevant CAT tax-free threshold (EUR 335,000 for gifts/inheritances from parent to child), no CAT is payable at all.

### Qualifying Conditions

To qualify for agricultural relief, the beneficiary must either:

- Be a farmer, meaning that at least 80 per cent of their gross assets (after receiving the gift or inheritance) consist of agricultural property, or
- Spend at least 50 per cent of their normal working time farming the property (the active farmer test)

The property must be agricultural property as defined in the Capital Acquisitions Tax Consolidation Act, which includes land, crops, trees, buildings used for farming, livestock, bloodstock, and farm machinery.

The beneficiary must retain the property for at least 6 years (or replace it with other agricultural property) to maintain the relief. Disposing of the property within 6 years triggers a clawback.

## Farm Restructuring Relief

Farm restructuring relief provides stamp duty relief on the sale, purchase, or exchange of agricultural land carried out under a Teagasc farm restructuring plan. The purpose is to encourage farm consolidation by allowing farmers to swap or trade parcels of land to create more efficient holdings without incurring stamp duty costs.

To qualify, the restructuring must be carried out under a qualifying farm restructuring plan prepared by Teagasc. The land must be used for farming for at least 5 years after the restructuring.

## EU CAP Payments

Irish farmers receive payments under the EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP). The main payments include:

### Basic Income Support for Sustainability (BISS)

The BISS payment (formerly the Basic Payment Scheme) is the main direct payment to Irish farmers. Payments are based on the number of eligible hectares farmed and a national average payment rate. BISS payments are taxable income and must be included in your farming accounts.

### GLAS (Green, Low-Carbon, Agri-Environment Scheme)

GLAS payments are received for environmental actions undertaken on the farm, such as maintaining traditional hay meadows, planting hedgerows, managing watercourses, and other biodiversity measures. GLAS income is taxable and is included in farming profits.

### TAMS (Targeted Agricultural Modernisation Scheme)

TAMS provides capital grants of up to 40 per cent (60 per cent for young trained farmers) towards the cost of qualifying farm investments including animal housing, dairy equipment, low-emission slurry spreading equipment, and farm safety improvements. TAMS grants reduce the capital cost of the asset for capital allowance purposes. If you spend EUR 100,000 on a new shed and receive EUR 40,000 in TAMS funding, you claim capital allowances on the net cost of EUR 60,000.

### Eco-Schemes and ACRES

The Agri-Climate Rural Environment Scheme (ACRES) provides annual payments for environmental actions. Like other scheme payments, ACRES income is taxable and included in farming profits.

## Milk Quota and Payment Entitlement Disposal

Although milk quotas were abolished in 2015, farmers who sold milk quota before that date may have received capital sums that were subject to CGT. Payment entitlements under the Basic Payment Scheme (now BISS) are a capital asset. If you sell or transfer payment entitlements, the disposal may be subject to CGT at 33 per cent.

When payment entitlements are transferred with land as part of a family succession, agricultural relief and business relief for CAT may apply, and the disposal may qualify for CGT retirement relief if the farmer is over 55.

## Farm Partnerships

Farm partnerships can offer significant advantages for Irish farming families. Revenue recognises both standard partnerships and registered farm partnerships, each with different tax treatments.

A registered farm partnership allows two or more individuals to farm together and share profits according to an agreed ratio. Each partner is taxed individually on their share of the profits. This can be advantageous for income splitting between spouses or between generations, potentially keeping both partners within the standard rate tax band.

Registered farm partnerships also qualify for an additional stock relief of EUR 15,000 per year for the first three years. The partnership must be registered with the Department of Agriculture and comply with the relevant regulations.

## Succession Planning for Farms

Succession planning is critical for farming families. The combination of agricultural relief (90 per cent reduction for CAT), farm restructuring relief, CGT retirement relief, and the young trained farmer reliefs makes it possible to transfer a farm to the next generation with minimal or no tax liability, provided the transfer is properly planned.

### CGT Retirement Relief

If you are over 55 and dispose of qualifying business assets (including farmland) to your child, you may be exempt from CGT on the disposal. For disposals to a child, the relief applies without a monetary limit up to the age of 66. Between 66 and 70, a cap of EUR 3 million applies. Over 70, a cap of EUR 750,000 applies.

### Succession Farm Partnership Tax Credit

The succession farm partnership tax credit provides a credit of EUR 5,000 per year for up to 5 years (total EUR 25,000) to encourage farm transfers within succession farm partnerships. The partnership must be registered, and the transfer must be to a qualified young farmer.

### Teagasc Advisory Services

Teagasc provides free and subsidised advisory services for farm succession planning, including financial analysis, tax planning, and mediation where family dynamics are complex. Their farm advisors can prepare business plans required for young trained farmer reliefs and TAMS applications. Taking professional advice early in the succession process is strongly recommended to maximise the available tax reliefs.

## Record Keeping for Farmers

Farmers must maintain records for at least 6 years, including:

- Stock records (opening and closing valuations, births, deaths, purchases, sales)
- Crop records (acreage, yields, sales)
- Machinery and equipment records (purchase dates, costs, disposals)
- EU scheme payment documentation
- Lease agreements for rented land (conacre or long-term)
- Receipts for all purchases and expenses
- Bank and loan statements
- Farm partnership agreements

Revenue conducts regular audits of farming accounts, particularly where stock relief or income averaging is claimed. Accurate and complete records are your best protection.
