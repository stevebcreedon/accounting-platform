Financial statements are the formal records of your business's financial activity. In Ireland, every business needs to understand three core financial statements: the profit and loss account (income statement), the balance sheet (statement of financial position), and the cash flow statement. For limited companies, the Companies Act 2014 sets out exactly what must be included, while sole traders prepare accounts primarily for Revenue and their own decision-making.

Whether you run a small sole trader business or a limited company, understanding your financial statements is essential for making informed decisions, meeting your tax obligations, and satisfying the requirements of the Companies Registration Office (CRO) if applicable. This guide breaks down each statement, explains what the numbers mean, and outlines your statutory obligations as an Irish business owner.

## The Profit and Loss Account

The profit and loss account, also known as the income statement, shows your business's revenue, costs, and resulting profit or loss over a specific period, typically a financial year. It answers the fundamental question: did your business make money during this period?

### Revenue (Turnover)

Revenue is the total income your business earned from its normal trading activities during the period. For a shop, this is total sales. For a consultancy, this is fees billed. Revenue is recorded net of VAT if you are VAT-registered. Only income from your core business activity belongs here. Other income, such as interest earned on a deposit account or a one-off gain from selling equipment, is reported separately.

### Cost of Sales

Cost of sales represents the direct costs of producing or delivering the goods and services you sold. For a retailer, this includes the purchase price of stock sold. For a construction firm, it includes materials and direct labour. Cost of sales does not include overheads such as rent or insurance.

Revenue minus cost of sales gives you your **gross profit**. This figure tells you how much margin you earn on your core activity before accounting for running costs.

### Operating Expenses

Operating expenses are the day-to-day costs of running your business that are not directly tied to producing goods or services. Common operating expenses for Irish businesses include:

- Rent and rates
- Salaries and wages (including employer PRSI at 11.05 per cent)
- Insurance premiums
- Light, heat, and telephone
- Motor and travel expenses
- Professional fees (accountant, solicitor)
- Advertising and marketing
- Depreciation of assets
- Office supplies and postage
- Bank charges and interest

Gross profit minus operating expenses gives you your **net profit** (or operating profit). This is the figure that most closely reflects how well your business is actually performing.

### Tax

For limited companies, corporation tax at 12.5 per cent on trading income is deducted from net profit to give profit after tax. Sole traders do not pay corporation tax but instead pay income tax, PRSI, and USC through their annual Form 11 return.

## The Balance Sheet

The balance sheet, formally called the statement of financial position, provides a snapshot of what your business owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the residual value belonging to the owner or shareholders (equity) at a specific date. Unlike the profit and loss account, which covers a period, the balance sheet captures a single moment in time.

### Assets

Assets are divided into two categories:

**Fixed assets** (non-current assets) are items your business owns for long-term use rather than for resale. Common fixed assets for Irish SMEs include property, vehicles, equipment, computer hardware, and fixtures and fittings. Fixed assets are shown at cost minus accumulated depreciation.

**Current assets** are items your business expects to convert into cash within 12 months. These include:

- **Stock (inventory):** Goods held for resale
- **Trade debtors (accounts receivable):** Money owed to you by customers
- **Prepayments:** Expenses paid in advance, such as insurance premiums
- **Cash at bank and in hand:** Your bank balances and petty cash

### Liabilities

Liabilities are also divided into two categories:

**Current liabilities** are amounts your business owes that are due within 12 months:

- **Trade creditors (accounts payable):** Money you owe to suppliers
- **Accruals:** Expenses incurred but not yet invoiced, such as an accountant's fee for year-end work
- **VAT payable:** VAT collected but not yet remitted to Revenue
- **PAYE/PRSI due:** Employment taxes deducted but not yet paid over
- **Corporation tax payable:** For limited companies, the tax due on profits
- **Short-term loans or overdraft:** Bank borrowings repayable within a year

**Long-term liabilities** (non-current liabilities) are amounts due after more than 12 months, such as term loans, hire purchase agreements, and mortgages on business property.

### Equity

Equity represents the owners' stake in the business. For a sole trader, this is called the proprietor's capital account. For a limited company, equity comprises share capital (the amount invested by shareholders) plus retained earnings (accumulated profits that have not been distributed as dividends).

The balance sheet always balances: **Assets = Liabilities + Equity**.

## The Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement tracks the actual movement of cash into and out of your business during a period. It reconciles the profit shown in your profit and loss account with the actual change in your bank balance. This is important because profit does not equal cash. You can be profitable on paper but struggling to pay bills if your customers are slow to pay.

The cash flow statement is divided into three sections:

**Operating activities:** Cash generated from day-to-day trading. This starts with your net profit and adjusts for non-cash items (like depreciation) and changes in working capital (like increases in debtors or stock).

**Investing activities:** Cash spent on or received from buying or selling fixed assets. If you purchased a van for EUR 25,000, this appears here as a cash outflow.

**Financing activities:** Cash from or to providers of finance. This includes bank loans drawn down, loan repayments, share capital introduced, and dividends paid.

While sole traders are not required to prepare a formal cash flow statement, it is an invaluable tool for understanding where your cash is going. Many Irish accountants prepare one as part of management accounts even when not legally required.

## Statutory Requirements Under the Companies Act 2014

If you operate a limited company in Ireland, the Companies Act 2014 governs what financial statements you must prepare and file. Every company must prepare annual financial statements comprising at least a profit and loss account, a balance sheet, and accompanying notes.

### Small Company Exemptions

The Companies Act 2014 provides significant relief for small companies. A company qualifies as small if it meets two of the following three conditions:

- **Turnover** does not exceed **EUR 12 million**
- **Balance sheet total** does not exceed **EUR 6 million**
- **Average number of employees** does not exceed **50**

Small companies can prepare **abridged financial statements** for filing with the CRO. Abridged accounts contain a reduced balance sheet and limited notes but do not include a profit and loss account. This means your competitors and the general public cannot see your revenue or profit figures, which many small business owners in Ireland value highly.

### Audit Exemption

Small companies that meet the size thresholds above are also exempt from the requirement to have their accounts audited by a registered auditor. This saves a significant cost, as audit fees for small Irish companies typically range from EUR 3,000 to EUR 8,000 or more.

To claim audit exemption, the company must include a specific statement on its balance sheet confirming that it meets the conditions and that no member holding 10 per cent or more of voting rights has requested an audit.

Even with audit exemption, your accountant will still prepare your financial statements and you must still file them with the CRO and Revenue.

### Filing with the CRO

All Irish limited companies must file an annual return (Form B1) with the Companies Registration Office within 56 days of their annual return date. The annual return must be accompanied by financial statements. Late filing results in a penalty of EUR 100 plus EUR 3 per day up to a maximum of EUR 1,200 per return. Persistent late filing can result in the loss of audit exemption for the following two years and potential strike-off proceedings.

### Filing with Revenue

Financial statements also form the basis of your corporation tax return (Form CT1) filed with Revenue. The CT1 is due within 9 months of your financial year end. Your accountant uses the financial statements to calculate your corporation tax liability, including any adjustments required for tax purposes (such as adding back depreciation and claiming capital allowances instead).

## Reading Your Financial Statements

Preparing financial statements is only half the job. Understanding what they tell you about your business is equally important. Here are the key things to look for:

### Gross Margin

Your gross margin is gross profit expressed as a percentage of revenue. If your revenue is EUR 500,000 and your gross profit is EUR 200,000, your gross margin is 40 per cent. Track this percentage over time. A declining gross margin could indicate that your costs are rising faster than your prices, or that you are discounting too heavily.

### Net Profit Margin

Net profit margin is net profit as a percentage of revenue. This tells you how much of every euro in sales you actually keep after all expenses. For most Irish SMEs, a net profit margin of 10 to 20 per cent is healthy, though this varies significantly by industry.

### Current Ratio

The current ratio compares your current assets to your current liabilities. A ratio above 1.0 means you have enough short-term assets to cover your short-term debts. A ratio below 1.0 is a warning sign that you may struggle to pay your bills as they fall due. Most lenders and suppliers look for a current ratio of at least 1.5.

### Debtor Days

Debtor days measures how long, on average, it takes your customers to pay you. The formula is (trade debtors divided by revenue) multiplied by 365. If your debtor days are 60 but your payment terms are 30 days, you have a collection problem that needs attention.

### Return on Capital

Return on capital employed (ROCE) measures how efficiently you are using the capital invested in your business. It is calculated as net profit divided by total capital employed (equity plus long-term liabilities). A higher ROCE indicates more efficient use of capital.

## Sole Trader vs Company Accounts

Sole traders are not required to file financial statements with any public body. However, you must prepare accounts to complete your Form 11 income tax return accurately. Revenue can request to see your accounts during an audit, and you are required to retain all accounting records for at least 6 years.

In practice, most sole traders in Ireland prepare a simplified profit and loss account and a basic balance sheet, often with the help of their accountant. While less formal than company accounts, they serve the same purpose: understanding your profitability, managing your cash, and calculating your tax liability.

Limited companies face more extensive requirements but also benefit from the credibility that audited or independently prepared accounts provide. Banks, suppliers, and potential business partners often request copies of your filed accounts before extending credit or entering into contracts.

## Working with Your Accountant

Your accountant prepares your financial statements based on the records you provide. The quality of your accounts depends directly on the quality of your bookkeeping throughout the year. To get the most value from your financial statements:

- Keep your bookkeeping up to date throughout the year rather than doing a full year in January
- Reconcile your bank accounts monthly
- Keep all receipts and invoices organised, whether digitally or in paper form
- Provide your accountant with a list of debtors and creditors at year end
- Inform your accountant of any unusual transactions, such as asset purchases, loans, or changes in business activity
- Ask your accountant to explain any figures you do not understand. Financial statements are there to help you manage your business, not just to satisfy legal requirements

Your accountant should also provide commentary on your financial statements, highlighting trends, areas of concern, and opportunities. If your accountant simply hands over the accounts without discussion, consider whether you are getting full value from the relationship.

## Common Mistakes with Financial Statements

**Confusing profit with cash.** A profitable business can run out of cash if too much money is tied up in stock or unpaid invoices. Always consider the cash flow statement alongside the profit and loss account.

**Ignoring the balance sheet.** Many business owners focus solely on revenue and profit but overlook the balance sheet. The balance sheet reveals whether your business is building value or accumulating debt.

**Not comparing periods.** A single set of accounts tells you very little. Compare this year to last year, and this quarter to the same quarter last year, to identify trends and potential problems.

**Mixing personal and business expenses.** This is particularly common among sole traders. Personal expenses that slip into the business accounts distort your profit figure and can cause problems during a Revenue audit.

**Not filing on time.** Late filing of accounts with the CRO results in penalties and can lead to loss of audit exemption. Late filing of your CT1 with Revenue incurs surcharges on your tax liability.
