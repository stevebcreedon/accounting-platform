A business partnership in Ireland is a relationship between two or more persons carrying on a business in common with a view to profit. Partnerships are governed by the Partnership Act 1890, and each partner is personally liable for all debts and obligations of the partnership. Unlike a limited company, a partnership has no separate legal identity -- it is simply a collection of individuals trading together.

Partnerships are most common among professionals such as solicitors, accountants, doctors, and architects, as well as in family businesses and small trading enterprises. This guide covers the different types of partnerships available in Ireland, how to register, your tax obligations, and why a written partnership agreement is essential.

## Types of Partnerships in Ireland

Irish law recognises three types of partnerships:

### General Partnership

A general partnership is the most common type. It is formed when two or more individuals agree to carry on a business together with a view to making a profit. No formal registration is required to create a general partnership -- it can arise simply from the conduct of the parties.

Key characteristics:

- **No limit on the number of partners** (the previous limit of 20 was abolished for most businesses, though it remains for certain banking partnerships)
- **All partners have joint and several liability** for the debts of the partnership
- **All partners are entitled to participate** in the management of the business (unless the partnership agreement provides otherwise)
- **Profits and losses are shared equally** between partners (unless agreed otherwise)
- **No separate legal identity** -- the partnership cannot own property or enter into contracts in its own name (in practice, it does so in the names of the partners)

### Limited Partnership

A limited partnership is formed under the Limited Partnerships Act 1907 and must be registered with the Companies Registration Office (CRO). It consists of:

- **At least one general partner** who has unlimited liability and is responsible for managing the business
- **At least one limited partner** who contributes capital but has liability limited to the amount of their contribution, and who must not take part in the management of the business

If a limited partner participates in the management of the partnership, they lose their limited liability status and become liable as a general partner.

Limited partnerships are relatively uncommon in Ireland for trading businesses. They are more frequently used in investment fund structures.

### Investment Limited Partnership (ILP)

The Investment Limited Partnership Act 1994 created a specific type of limited partnership for investment fund purposes. ILPs are regulated by the Central Bank of Ireland and are used almost exclusively in the financial services sector. They are not relevant for most small businesses.

## The Partnership Act 1890

The Partnership Act 1890 is the primary legislation governing general partnerships in Ireland. Despite its age, it remains the default legal framework for partnerships where no written agreement exists.

Key provisions of the Act that apply in the absence of a partnership agreement include:

- **Equal sharing of profits and losses** (Section 24(1)): Every partner is entitled to share equally in the capital and profits of the business, and must contribute equally towards the losses
- **No salary for partners** (Section 24(6)): No partner is entitled to receive a salary for acting in the partnership business
- **Unanimous consent for new partners** (Section 24(7)): No new partner can be introduced without the consent of all existing partners
- **Right to participate in management** (Section 24(5)): Every partner may take part in the management of the business
- **Partnership at will** (Section 26): Where no fixed term is agreed, any partner can dissolve the partnership by giving notice to the other partners
- **Expulsion** (Section 25): No majority of partners can expel any partner unless a power to do so has been conferred by express agreement

These default rules apply automatically if you do not have a written partnership agreement. Many of them are unsuitable for modern business relationships, which is why a written agreement is strongly recommended.

## Setting Up a Partnership: Step by Step

### Step 1: Choose Your Partners

A partnership is fundamentally a relationship of trust. Each partner is an agent of the partnership and can bind the other partners to contracts and obligations. Choose your partners carefully, considering:

- Complementary skills and experience
- Shared values and business goals
- Financial capacity to contribute capital and withstand losses
- Track record and reputation
- Compatibility in work ethic and decision-making style

### Step 2: Draft a Partnership Agreement

While not legally required, a written partnership agreement is essential for any partnership. Without one, the default rules of the Partnership Act 1890 apply, which may not reflect what the partners actually intend.

A partnership agreement should cover:

**Financial arrangements:**
- Capital contributions from each partner
- How profits and losses will be shared (if not equally)
- Drawing entitlements (how much each partner can withdraw and when)
- How partner salaries (if any) will be determined
- How interest on capital contributions will be handled
- Banking arrangements and who can sign cheques or authorise payments

**Management and decision-making:**
- How day-to-day management decisions will be made
- Which decisions require unanimous consent vs majority vote
- How disputes will be resolved (mediation, arbitration, or court proceedings)
- Authority limits for individual partners (maximum spend without partner approval)
- Roles and responsibilities of each partner

**Admission, retirement, and expulsion:**
- How new partners can be admitted
- Procedure for a partner wishing to retire
- Grounds and procedure for expelling a partner
- How the outgoing partner's share will be valued and paid
- Non-compete and non-solicitation clauses for departing partners
- Gardening leave provisions

**Dissolution:**
- Events that will trigger dissolution (death, bankruptcy, mutual agreement)
- How assets will be distributed on dissolution
- How the business name and goodwill will be dealt with

**Other provisions:**
- Restrictions on partners carrying on competing businesses
- Obligations regarding time commitment and duties
- Insurance requirements
- Confidentiality obligations
- Holiday and leave arrangements

A solicitor should draft or review any partnership agreement. The cost is typically EUR 500 to EUR 2,000 depending on the complexity of the arrangement.

### Step 3: Register the Partnership Name with the CRO

If the partnership trades under a name other than the surnames of all the partners, the business name must be registered with the Companies Registration Office under the Registration of Business Names Act 1963.

The registration process:

- File the RBN1 form online via CORE at core.cro.ie
- Pay the registration fee of EUR 20 (online) or EUR 40 (paper)
- The CRO checks the name for similarity with existing registered names
- Processing takes approximately 5 business days

Business name registration does not give the partnership any trademark or intellectual property rights. It simply creates a public record of who is trading under that name.

### Step 4: Register with Revenue

The partnership must register with the Revenue Commissioners. While the partnership itself is registered for reporting purposes, each partner is taxed individually on their share of the profits.

**Partnership registration:**
- File a Form TR1 (Partnership) with Revenue to register the partnership
- This registers the partnership for VAT (if applicable) and provides a partnership tax reference number

**Individual partner registration:**
- Each partner must also be registered for self-assessment income tax
- Each partner files their own Form 11 annual income tax return
- Each partner pays their own income tax, USC, and PRSI on their share of the partnership profits

### Step 5: Open a Partnership Bank Account

Open a bank account in the partnership name. Most Irish banks offer partnership accounts, though the requirements are similar to business accounts:

- Partnership agreement (or a letter signed by all partners confirming the arrangement)
- Identification and proof of address for all partners
- Details of the business activities and expected turnover
- Confirmation of who is authorised to operate the account

## Tax Treatment of Partnerships

One of the defining features of an Irish partnership is that it is "transparent" for tax purposes. This means:

- **The partnership itself does not pay income tax or corporation tax.** The profits are allocated to the individual partners according to their profit-sharing ratio.
- **Each partner pays income tax on their share** at their personal marginal rate (20% on the first EUR 42,000 for a single person, 40% on the balance, plus USC and PRSI).
- **Each partner files their own Form 11** with Revenue, reporting their share of the partnership profits along with any other income they may have.
- **The partnership files a Form 1 (Firms)** with Revenue, which is an information return showing the total partnership income and how it is allocated among the partners. This is not a tax payment form.

### Tax Example

Consider a two-person equal partnership with annual profits of EUR 120,000:

| | Partner A | Partner B |
|---|---|---|
| Share of profits | EUR 60,000 | EUR 60,000 |
| Income tax (20% on EUR 42,000) | EUR 8,400 | EUR 8,400 |
| Income tax (40% on EUR 18,000) | EUR 7,200 | EUR 7,200 |
| USC (approximately) | EUR 2,700 | EUR 2,700 |
| PRSI (4%) | EUR 2,400 | EUR 2,400 |
| **Total tax per partner** | **~EUR 20,700** | **~EUR 20,700** |
| **Combined tax** | | **~EUR 41,400** |

Compare this with the same EUR 120,000 profit in a limited company, where corporation tax at 12.5 per cent would apply to retained profits. The appropriate structure depends entirely on the circumstances of the partners.

### VAT for Partnerships

If the partnership's taxable turnover exceeds the VAT thresholds (EUR 80,000 for goods, EUR 40,000 for services), the partnership must register for VAT. The partnership files VAT returns in its own name using its own VAT number. VAT is a partnership-level obligation, not an individual partner obligation.

## Joint and Several Liability

Perhaps the most significant legal characteristic of a general partnership is the principle of joint and several liability. This means:

- **Every partner is personally liable for all debts and obligations of the partnership**, not just their proportionate share
- **A creditor can pursue any one partner** for the full amount of a debt, regardless of how many partners there are
- **The partner who pays can seek contribution** from the other partners, but if the other partners cannot pay, the paying partner bears the full loss
- **This liability extends to personal assets** -- a partner's home, savings, car, and other personal property can be seized to satisfy partnership debts

This is in stark contrast to a limited company, where shareholders' liability is limited to the amount they have invested. The unlimited liability of partners is the primary reason many businesses choose to incorporate as a limited company instead.

### Liability for Partners' Actions

Under the Partnership Act 1890, every partner is an agent of the firm. This means that if one partner enters into a contract or incurs a liability while acting within the apparent scope of the partnership business, all partners are bound by that action.

For example, if Partner A signs a lease for business premises without consulting Partner B, both partners are liable under the lease (assuming the landlord reasonably believed Partner A was acting on behalf of the partnership).

This agency principle makes the choice of partners and the terms of the partnership agreement critically important. Limitations on individual partners' authority should be clearly set out in the agreement.

## Dissolving a Partnership

A partnership can be dissolved in several ways:

**By agreement.** The partners can agree to dissolve the partnership at any time. The partnership agreement should set out the procedure for dissolution by agreement.

**By notice.** Where no fixed term has been agreed, any partner can dissolve the partnership by giving notice to the other partners. There is no minimum notice period under the Partnership Act 1890, though the partnership agreement should specify a reasonable notice period.

**By operation of law.** A partnership is automatically dissolved by the death or bankruptcy of any partner, unless the partnership agreement provides otherwise. This is one of the most important reasons to have a written agreement -- without one, the death of a partner triggers immediate dissolution.

**By court order.** A partner can apply to the court for a dissolution order on grounds including a partner's permanent incapacity, conduct prejudicial to the business, persistent breach of the partnership agreement, or where the business can only be carried on at a loss.

### What Happens on Dissolution

When a partnership is dissolved:

1. Partnership assets are sold or distributed
2. Debts and liabilities of the partnership are paid from the assets
3. Any surplus is distributed to the partners according to their entitlements
4. The business name registration with the CRO should be cancelled
5. Revenue must be notified of the cessation of the partnership
6. Each partner files a final Form 11 covering the period up to dissolution

If the partnership's assets are insufficient to cover its debts, the partners are personally liable for the shortfall (joint and several liability applies).

## Partnership vs Limited Company: When to Choose a Partnership

A partnership can be the right choice in specific circumstances:

**Choose a partnership if:**
- You are in a profession where partnerships are the traditional structure (law, accounting, medicine)
- You want simplicity in setup and administration
- All partners want active involvement in management
- The business has low financial risk and low debt
- Professional indemnity insurance adequately covers liability risk
- You want tax transparency (each partner taxed at their own rate)

**Choose a limited company if:**
- You need liability protection
- You want to retain profits in the business at the 12.5% corporation tax rate
- You plan to bring in passive investors
- You want the business to have a separate legal identity that survives changes in ownership
- You want to access certain grants or funding only available to companies

Many businesses that start as partnerships eventually incorporate as limited companies as they grow and the liability and tax advantages of incorporation become more significant.
