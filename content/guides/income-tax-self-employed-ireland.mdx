Self-employed people in Ireland pay income tax on their business profits through the self-assessment system. You file a Form 11 annual return with Revenue, pay preliminary tax during the year, and settle any balance by the pay-and-file deadline of 31 October, extended to mid-November if you file and pay through ROS.

Income tax is just one of three charges on your self-employed income. You also pay Universal Social Charge (USC) and Pay Related Social Insurance (PRSI) Class S. Together, these can bring your effective tax rate well above 50 per cent on higher earnings. This guide explains how each charge works, what you can deduct, and how to manage your tax obligations efficiently as a self-employed person in Ireland.

## Income Tax Rates and Bands

Ireland operates a two-rate income tax system. The rate you pay depends on your total income and your personal circumstances.

### The Standard Rate: 20 Per Cent

The first portion of your income is taxed at the standard rate of 20 per cent. The amount taxed at this rate depends on your personal circumstances and is known as the standard rate cut-off point.

### The Higher Rate: 40 Per Cent

Income above the standard rate cut-off point is taxed at 40 per cent. There is no additional rate or top rate beyond this.

### Standard Rate Cut-Off Points

The standard rate cut-off point determines how much of your income is taxed at 20 per cent before the 40 per cent rate applies.

| Personal Circumstances | Standard Rate Cut-Off Point |
|------------------------|----------------------------|
| Single person | EUR 42,000 |
| Married couple / civil partners (one income) | EUR 51,000 |
| Married couple / civil partners (two incomes) | Up to EUR 84,000 |
| Single parent (one-parent family) | EUR 46,000 |

For married couples with two incomes, the cut-off point can be increased by the lower of EUR 33,000 or the income of the second spouse. This means the maximum standard rate band for a two-income couple is EUR 84,000 (EUR 51,000 plus EUR 33,000).

### Practical Example

A single self-employed person with taxable profits of EUR 60,000 would pay income tax as follows:

- First EUR 42,000 at 20 per cent = EUR 8,400
- Remaining EUR 18,000 at 40 per cent = EUR 7,200
- Total income tax = EUR 15,600

This does not include USC or PRSI, which are calculated separately.

## Universal Social Charge (USC)

USC is a tax on gross income that applies to all self-employed individuals whose total income exceeds EUR 13,000 per year. If your income is EUR 13,000 or below, you are exempt from USC entirely. Once you exceed this threshold, USC applies to your entire income from the first euro.

### USC Rates

| Income Band | USC Rate |
|-------------|----------|
| First EUR 12,012 | 0.5% |
| EUR 12,012 to EUR 25,760 | 2% |
| EUR 25,760 to EUR 70,044 | 4% |
| Above EUR 70,044 | 8% |

Self-employed individuals with income above EUR 100,000 pay a surcharge of 3 per cent on income above EUR 100,000, bringing the top USC rate to 11 per cent on that portion. This surcharge applies only to self-employed income, not to PAYE income.

### USC Calculation Example

For a self-employed person with gross income of EUR 60,000:

- EUR 12,012 at 0.5 per cent = EUR 60.06
- EUR 13,748 (EUR 25,760 minus EUR 12,012) at 2 per cent = EUR 274.96
- EUR 34,240 (EUR 60,000 minus EUR 25,760) at 4 per cent = EUR 1,369.60
- Total USC = EUR 1,704.62

## PRSI for Self-Employed People

Self-employed individuals in Ireland pay PRSI at Class S, which covers you for certain social insurance benefits. Class S contributions entitle you to the State Pension (Contributory), Maternity Benefit, Adoptive Benefit, Paternity Benefit, and certain other benefits. However, Class S does not cover you for Jobseeker's Benefit or Illness Benefit (short-term).

### PRSI Class S Rate

The PRSI Class S rate is 4 per cent of all reckonable income, with a minimum annual payment of EUR 500. If 4 per cent of your income is less than EUR 500, you pay EUR 500. There is no upper earnings limit for PRSI.

For the same self-employed person with income of EUR 60,000, PRSI would be EUR 2,400 (4 per cent of EUR 60,000).

## Total Tax Burden: A Complete Example

Combining all three charges for a single self-employed person with taxable profits of EUR 60,000:

| Tax | Amount |
|-----|--------|
| Income tax | EUR 15,600 |
| USC | EUR 1,704.62 |
| PRSI (Class S) | EUR 2,400 |
| **Total** | **EUR 19,704.62** |
| **Effective rate** | **32.8%** |

Before applying tax credits, the effective rate on EUR 60,000 is approximately 32.8 per cent. Tax credits then reduce the income tax portion. The main personal tax credit for a single person is EUR 1,875 (the personal tax credit) plus EUR 1,875 (the earned income tax credit for self-employed), reducing the net income tax by EUR 3,750.

After credits: EUR 19,704.62 minus EUR 3,750 = EUR 15,954.62, giving an effective rate of approximately 26.6 per cent.

## The Form 11 Annual Return

The Form 11 is the self-assessment income tax return for self-employed individuals, proprietary directors, and anyone with non-PAYE income exceeding EUR 5,000. It is a comprehensive return that covers all sources of income.

### What the Form 11 Covers

The Form 11 requires you to declare:

- All self-employment income and business profits
- Any PAYE employment income
- Rental income from Irish and foreign property
- Investment income (dividends, interest, foreign income)
- Capital gains
- Claims for tax credits and reliefs
- Details of your business expenses and deductions
- Information about your spouse or civil partner's income (if filing jointly)

### Filing Deadline

The Form 11 must be filed by 31 October of the year following the tax year. For example, the Form 11 for the 2026 tax year is due by 31 October 2027. If you file and pay through ROS, the deadline extends to approximately mid-November (the exact date varies each year but is typically around 14 to 16 November).

Late filing of the Form 11 results in a surcharge on your income tax liability. The surcharge is 5 per cent (up to EUR 12,695) if the return is filed within 2 months of the deadline, and 10 per cent (up to EUR 25,390) if filed more than 2 months late.

### Filing Through ROS

Revenue strongly encourages all self-employed individuals to file their Form 11 through the Revenue Online Service. ROS provides pre-populated fields based on information Revenue already holds, validation checks to catch errors before submission, and the extended filing deadline.

You need a ROS digital certificate to file online. If you do not already have one, apply through the ROS website. A verification PIN is posted to your registered address, which typically takes 5 to 7 working days.

## Preliminary Tax

Self-employed people must pay preliminary tax for the current year before the pay-and-file deadline. Preliminary tax is essentially a payment on account of your expected tax liability for the current year.

### When to Pay

Preliminary tax is due by 31 October of the tax year (extended to mid-November for ROS filers). This is the same deadline as filing the prior year's Form 11, so you are simultaneously filing last year's return and paying this year's preliminary tax.

### How Much to Pay

You must pay at least one of the following amounts to avoid interest:

**Option 1: 100 per cent of the prior year's liability.** Pay an amount equal to your total income tax, USC, and PRSI for the previous tax year. This is the safest option as it is based on known figures.

**Option 2: 90 per cent of the current year's liability.** Estimate your current year's income and pay at least 90 per cent of the tax that will ultimately be due. This requires accurate forecasting but can reduce your preliminary tax payment if your income has decreased.

**Option 3: 105 per cent of the pre-preceding year's liability.** This option is available only if you pay by direct debit. You can pay 105 per cent of the tax liability for the year before the prior year.

### Interest on Underpayment

If your preliminary tax payment is insufficient under all three options, Revenue charges interest at 0.0219 per cent per day (approximately 8 per cent per annum) on the underpaid amount from 1 November until the balance is paid.

## Allowable Business Expenses

One of the most important aspects of managing your income tax as a self-employed person is claiming all allowable business expenses. These expenses reduce your taxable profit, directly lowering your tax bill.

### What Qualifies as an Allowable Expense

An expense is allowable for tax purposes if it is incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of the trade. Revenue applies this test strictly. The expense must be revenue in nature (not capital), genuinely incurred, and not of a personal or domestic nature.

### Common Allowable Expenses

**Office and premises costs:** Rent, rates, insurance, heating, lighting, cleaning, and repairs to business premises. If you work from home, you can claim a proportion of household expenses based on the area used for business and the time spent working.

**Professional fees:** Accountancy fees, legal fees related to the business (not capital transactions), and subscriptions to professional bodies relevant to your trade.

**Travel and subsistence:** Business travel costs including mileage, public transport, parking, and overnight accommodation when travelling for business. You cannot claim for travel between home and your normal place of business (commuting).

**Marketing and advertising:** Website costs, online advertising, business cards, brochures, and other promotional expenses.

**Telecommunications:** Business phone, broadband, and mobile costs. If you use a personal phone for business, claim the business proportion.

**Staff costs:** Wages, salaries, employer PRSI, pension contributions, and training costs for employees.

**Materials and stock:** Cost of goods purchased for resale, raw materials, and consumables used in the business.

**Insurance:** Business insurance premiums, including professional indemnity, public liability, and employer's liability.

**Bad debts:** Debts that are genuinely irrecoverable and have been written off in your accounts.

### Non-Allowable Expenses

Certain expenses are specifically disallowed:

- Entertainment and hospitality (client meals, gifts, events)
- Personal expenses (clothing unless a uniform, personal phone usage)
- Depreciation (replaced by capital allowances)
- Capital expenditure (claimed through capital allowances instead)
- Fines and penalties
- Your own salary drawings (sole traders cannot pay themselves a salary)

## Capital Allowances

When you purchase assets for your business, such as equipment, machinery, vehicles, or furniture, you cannot deduct the full cost as a business expense in the year of purchase. Instead, you claim capital allowances, which spread the tax relief over the useful life of the asset.

### Standard Rate: 12.5 Per Cent Over 8 Years

The standard capital allowance rate for plant and machinery is 12.5 per cent per annum on a straight-line basis, giving full relief over 8 years. This applies to most business assets, including computers, office furniture, tools, and equipment.

### Motor Vehicles

Capital allowances on motor vehicles are restricted based on the cost and CO2 emissions of the vehicle. The maximum qualifying cost for capital allowances on cars is EUR 24,000. Even if you purchase a car costing EUR 40,000, you can only claim allowances on the first EUR 24,000. The allowance is further restricted if the car is used partly for private purposes.

### Accelerated Capital Allowances

Energy-efficient equipment qualifies for accelerated capital allowances, allowing you to write off the full cost in the first year. The equipment must be from a category specified by the Sustainable Energy Authority of Ireland (SEAI) and must meet certain energy efficiency criteria.

## Tax Credits for Self-Employed People

Tax credits reduce your income tax liability on a euro-for-euro basis. Self-employed individuals can claim several credits.

### Personal Tax Credit

Every individual is entitled to a personal tax credit. For a single person, this is EUR 1,875. For a married couple, the combined credit is EUR 3,750.

### Earned Income Tax Credit

Self-employed individuals and proprietary directors can claim the earned income tax credit of EUR 1,875. This credit was introduced to bring self-employed tax treatment closer to parity with PAYE workers, who benefit from the PAYE employee tax credit.

The earned income tax credit and the PAYE employee tax credit are mutually exclusive on the same income. If you have both self-employment income and PAYE income, the combined credits cannot exceed EUR 1,875 from each source.

### Other Available Credits

Additional credits that may be relevant include the home carer tax credit (EUR 1,800) for married couples where one spouse works in the home, the single person child carer credit (EUR 1,750), and age-related credits for those aged 65 and over.

## Payment Methods

Self-employed tax can be paid through several methods:

**ROS direct debit:** Set up a direct debit through ROS for automatic payment on the due date. This is the most reliable method and ensures you never miss a deadline.

**ROS single debit instruction:** Make a one-off electronic payment through ROS when filing your return.

**Debit or credit card:** Pay through ROS using a debit or credit card. Note that credit card payments may incur a surcharge.

**By cheque:** Revenue still accepts cheque payments, but processing takes longer and you do not receive the ROS extended deadline.

## Summary

As a self-employed person in Ireland, you pay income tax at 20 per cent and 40 per cent, USC at rates from 0.5 per cent to 11 per cent, and PRSI Class S at 4 per cent. Your total effective tax rate depends on your income level, personal circumstances, and the credits and reliefs you claim.

The Form 11 is your annual self-assessment return, due by 31 October (mid-November via ROS). You must also pay preliminary tax for the current year by the same deadline. Claiming all allowable business expenses and capital allowances is essential to minimising your tax liability.

The earned income tax credit of EUR 1,875, combined with the personal tax credit of EUR 1,875, provides EUR 3,750 in credits for single self-employed people. Work with a qualified accountant to ensure you are claiming every deduction and credit available and that your preliminary tax calculations are accurate.
