Cash flow is the movement of money into and out of your business. In Ireland, cash flow problems are the single most common reason small businesses fail, even when those businesses are profitable on paper. Understanding the difference between cash flow and profit, and actively managing both, is one of the most important skills any Irish business owner can develop.

A business can show a healthy profit in its accounts while simultaneously running out of cash to pay suppliers, staff, or Revenue. This happens because profit is an accounting concept based on when income is earned and expenses are incurred, while cash flow tracks when money actually moves through your bank account. This guide covers everything Irish SMEs need to know about managing cash flow effectively.

## Cash Flow vs Profit: Why They Are Different

Profit and cash flow are related but distinct concepts that every Irish business owner must understand. Your profit and loss account might show EUR 80,000 in net profit for the year, but your bank balance might tell a very different story.

Several factors cause profit and cash to diverge:

**Timing of payments.** If you invoice a customer in March but they do not pay until May, you record the revenue in March (for profit purposes) but do not receive the cash until May. Meanwhile, you still need to pay your own suppliers, staff, and Revenue.

**VAT.** When you invoice a customer, you add VAT to the bill. That VAT belongs to Revenue, not to you, but it sits in your bank account until you file your VAT return. Conversely, when you pay a supplier, you pay VAT on top of the purchase price, reducing your cash even though the VAT element is reclaimable.

**Capital expenditure.** Buying a van for EUR 30,000 reduces your cash by EUR 30,000 immediately. However, in your profit and loss account, only the annual depreciation charge (perhaps EUR 6,000 per year over five years) appears as an expense. Your profit looks healthy, but your cash has taken a significant hit.

**Stock purchases.** If you buy EUR 50,000 of stock in December but only sell EUR 20,000 of it by year end, your profit and loss account only shows EUR 20,000 as cost of sales. The other EUR 30,000 sits on your balance sheet as an asset, but the cash is already gone.

**Tax payments.** Your preliminary tax, VAT, and PAYE/PRSI obligations create large cash outflows at specific times during the year that do not correspond to when the related income was earned.

## Creating a Cash Flow Forecast

A cash flow forecast is a projection of the money you expect to receive and spend over a future period. It is the single most useful tool for managing your business finances. Every Irish SME should maintain a rolling cash flow forecast, updating it weekly or at minimum monthly.

### The 13-Week Rolling Forecast

The 13-week rolling cash flow forecast is the standard approach used by Irish businesses and recommended by banks, accountants, and Enterprise Ireland. It covers a quarter at a time and rolls forward each week, so you always have 13 weeks of visibility ahead.

To build your 13-week forecast:

**Step 1: Start with your opening bank balance.** This is the actual cash in your business bank account at the start of the forecast period.

**Step 2: List all expected cash inflows week by week.** These include:

- Customer payments (based on when you expect to receive payment, not when you invoice)
- Cash sales
- Loan drawdowns
- Grant receipts (from Enterprise Ireland, Local Enterprise Office, or other agencies)
- Tax refunds (VAT refunds if you are in a repayment position)
- Any other incoming cash

**Step 3: List all expected cash outflows week by week.** These include:

- Supplier payments
- Wages and salaries (net pay)
- PAYE/PRSI payments to Revenue
- VAT payments
- Preliminary tax payments
- Rent and rates
- Insurance premiums
- Loan repayments
- Equipment purchases
- Professional fees
- Utility bills
- Any other outgoing cash

**Step 4: Calculate the net cash flow for each week** (inflows minus outflows) and the running bank balance.

**Step 5: Review and update weekly.** Replace projected figures with actual figures as each week passes, and extend the forecast by one week so you always have 13 weeks ahead.

### Annual Cash Flow Forecast

In addition to the 13-week forecast, prepare an annual cash flow forecast at the start of each financial year. This longer-term view helps you plan for seasonal patterns, large tax payments, and capital expenditure. Your accountant can help you build this based on your previous year's actual figures and your plans for the year ahead.

## Improving Cash Collections

The fastest way to improve your cash flow is to collect the money you are owed more quickly. Irish SMEs are particularly affected by late payment, with the average payment period in Ireland running to 55 days despite standard 30-day terms.

### Set Clear Payment Terms

State your payment terms on every invoice and in your terms and conditions. Common terms in Ireland are:

- **Payment on receipt** (for small, one-off jobs)
- **Net 14 days** (increasingly common for small businesses)
- **Net 30 days** (the Irish standard)
- **Net 60 or 90 days** (only if you have the cash reserves to support it)

Shorter payment terms give you faster access to cash, but be aware that some larger customers may push back. Negotiate terms that work for both parties.

### Invoice Promptly

Send your invoice on the day you complete the work or deliver the goods. Every day you delay sending an invoice is a day added to your payment cycle. If you complete a project on a Friday but do not invoice until the following Tuesday, you have added four days to your cash collection timeline for no reason.

### Follow Up Systematically

Implement a structured follow-up process for unpaid invoices:

- **Day 1:** Send the invoice with clear payment terms
- **Day 21:** Send a friendly reminder that payment is due in 7 days
- **Day 31:** Send a firm reminder that payment is overdue
- **Day 45:** Make a phone call. A personal call is far more effective than email for overdue payments
- **Day 60:** Send a formal letter before action
- **Day 90:** Consider referring to a debt collection agency or solicitor

### Offer Multiple Payment Methods

Make it as easy as possible for customers to pay you. Accept bank transfers, card payments, and direct debits. Many Irish businesses still rely solely on bank transfers, but offering card payment through a terminal or online payment link can significantly reduce payment times.

### Consider Deposits and Milestone Payments

For larger projects, request a deposit before starting work and structure milestone payments throughout the project. A typical structure might be 30 per cent deposit, 30 per cent at midpoint, and 40 per cent on completion. This approach spreads your cash inflows across the project timeline rather than concentrating them at the end.

## Managing Payment Terms with Suppliers

Just as your customers' payment behaviour affects your cash inflow, your payment terms with suppliers affect your cash outflow. Negotiate the best terms you can with your suppliers, but always pay on time. A reputation as a reliable payer gives you leverage to negotiate better terms and ensures continued supply.

### Irish Prompt Payment Legislation

The European Communities (Late Payment in Commercial Transactions) Regulations 2012 (S.I. No. 580 of 2012) implement the EU Late Payment Directive in Ireland. Key provisions include:

- The default payment period is 30 days from receipt of invoice, unless otherwise agreed in the contract
- Public bodies must pay within 30 days (no contractual extension beyond 30 days)
- Between businesses, the payment period can be extended by contract, but any term exceeding 60 days must be justified and not grossly unfair
- Late payment interest accrues automatically at the ECB reference rate plus 8 percentage points
- A fixed compensation of EUR 40 is payable for each late payment, plus reasonable recovery costs

While few Irish SMEs actually invoke these provisions against their customers (for fear of damaging the relationship), knowing they exist gives you a legal basis for demanding timely payment.

## Planning for Tax Payments

Tax payments are among the largest cash outflows for Irish businesses, and they follow a predictable schedule. Planning for them is essential to avoid cash flow crises.

### Key Tax Payment Dates

**VAT:** Bi-monthly VAT3 returns are due by the 23rd of the month following each two-month period (if filing via ROS). If you owe EUR 5,000 in VAT every two months, that is EUR 30,000 per year leaving your account in six lumps.

**PAYE/PRSI:** If you have employees, you must pay PAYE, PRSI, and USC to Revenue by the 23rd of each month (via ROS) for the previous month's payroll. Miss a PAYE deadline and interest accrues immediately.

**Preliminary tax:** Sole traders and company directors must pay preliminary tax by 31 October each year (or the ROS extended deadline in mid-November). Your preliminary tax must be at least 90 per cent of your current year liability or 100 per cent of your prior year liability. For a sole trader earning EUR 60,000, this could be a single payment of EUR 15,000 to EUR 20,000.

**Corporation tax:** Companies must pay preliminary corporation tax within 6 months of the start of their accounting period (for small companies) or in two instalments for larger companies.

### Building a Tax Reserve

The most effective way to manage tax payments is to set aside money as you earn it, rather than scrambling to find it when the bill arrives. Open a separate business savings account and transfer a percentage of your income into it each week or month.

A reasonable rule of thumb for sole traders is to set aside 30 to 35 per cent of your net profit for income tax, PRSI, and USC. For limited companies, set aside 12.5 per cent of trading profits for corporation tax, plus the full amount of any VAT collected and PAYE/PRSI deducted.

## Seasonal Cash Flow Challenges

Many Irish businesses experience seasonal fluctuations in cash flow. Retail businesses see peaks before Christmas and troughs in January and February. Construction slows during winter months. Tourism businesses in the West of Ireland may earn 60 to 70 per cent of their annual revenue between May and September.

To manage seasonal cash flow:

- Use your annual cash flow forecast to identify the lean months
- Build cash reserves during peak periods to cover quiet months
- Negotiate with suppliers for extended payment terms during your off-season
- Consider seasonal staffing to match your labour costs to your revenue cycle
- Discuss seasonal overdraft facilities with your bank before you need them

## Financing Options for Irish SMEs

When cash flow is tight despite good management, external financing can bridge the gap. Options available to Irish businesses include:

**Bank overdraft.** A flexible borrowing facility attached to your current account. You only pay interest on what you use. Irish banks typically charge 8 to 12 per cent on overdraft facilities, and they require annual renewal.

**Invoice financing (factoring).** A finance company advances you typically 80 to 85 per cent of the value of your invoices immediately, and the balance (minus fees) when your customer pays. This converts your debtors into immediate cash. Several Irish providers offer this service, and it is particularly popular with businesses that have long debtor payment cycles.

**Term loans.** A fixed loan repaid over an agreed period, suitable for specific purchases or investments. Interest rates vary from 5 to 10 per cent depending on the security offered and the lender.

**Microfinance Ireland.** For businesses that may not qualify for bank lending, Microfinance Ireland provides loans of EUR 5,000 to EUR 25,000 to sole traders and small companies. Applications are typically made through your Local Enterprise Office.

**Credit guarantee scheme.** The Strategic Banking Corporation of Ireland (SBCI) operates a credit guarantee scheme that provides a government guarantee to participating banks, making it easier for SMEs to access credit.

## Warning Signs of Cash Flow Problems

Early identification of cash flow problems gives you time to take corrective action. Watch for these warning signs:

- Regularly paying suppliers late or asking for extensions
- Relying on your overdraft as a permanent source of funding rather than a short-term facility
- Struggling to meet payroll on time
- Delaying VAT or PAYE payments to Revenue
- Taking on work at lower margins just to generate cash
- Growing revenue but a shrinking bank balance
- Increasing debtor days (your customers are taking longer to pay)
- Directors lending personal funds to the company to cover shortfalls

If you recognise two or more of these signs in your business, speak with your accountant immediately. Early intervention is far easier and less costly than dealing with a full cash flow crisis.

## Building a Cash Flow Culture

Managing cash flow is not just an accounting exercise. It requires a mindset shift across your entire business. Everyone involved in spending money, invoicing customers, or managing stock needs to understand the importance of cash flow.

Review your cash flow forecast weekly. Share relevant information with your team. Celebrate prompt customer payments. Question every significant expenditure by asking not just whether you can afford it, but when the cash will leave your account and whether you will have enough to cover your other obligations at that time.

Irish businesses that build cash flow management into their weekly routine are far more likely to survive difficult periods and take advantage of growth opportunities when they arise.
