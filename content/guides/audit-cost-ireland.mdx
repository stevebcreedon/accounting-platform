A statutory audit in Ireland typically costs between EUR 3,000 and EUR 15,000 for small to medium companies, with fees for larger or more complex companies reaching EUR 50,000 or more. However, most small Irish companies do not need an audit at all. The Companies Act 2014 provides an audit exemption for companies that meet certain size thresholds, and understanding these thresholds can save your company thousands of euros every year. This guide explains when an audit is required, what it costs, how to qualify for exemption, and what the audit process involves.

The audit exemption is one of the most valuable provisions in Irish company law for small businesses. If your company qualifies, you avoid a significant annual expense while still meeting all your statutory obligations through the preparation of financial statements and filing of annual returns.

## When Is an Audit Required in Ireland?

Under the Companies Act 2014, all Irish companies are required to have their financial statements audited by a registered statutory auditor unless they qualify for an exemption. The key exemptions are based on company size.

### Small Company Audit Exemption

A company qualifies for audit exemption if it meets two of the following three conditions in the current and preceding financial year:

| Threshold | Limit |
|---|---|
| Turnover | Not exceeding EUR 12 million |
| Balance sheet total | Not exceeding EUR 6 million |
| Average number of employees | Not exceeding 50 |

You must meet two of these three thresholds in both the current and the preceding financial year. A company that exceeds two of the three thresholds in one year but qualifies in the next year does not regain the exemption until it qualifies in two consecutive years.

### Companies That Cannot Claim Audit Exemption

Certain companies cannot claim audit exemption regardless of their size:

- **Public limited companies (PLCs)** must always have an audit
- **Companies holding a banking or insurance licence**
- **Investment companies or UCITS**
- **Companies limited by guarantee** with turnover exceeding EUR 250,000
- **Companies where a member holding at least 10 per cent of shares requests an audit** (minority shareholder right)
- **Companies that filed their CRO annual return late** in the current or preceding financial year (late filing disqualifies the company from claiming audit exemption for two years)

### Group Companies

Group company audit exemption has additional rules. A subsidiary can claim audit exemption only if the group as a whole qualifies as a small group (meeting the aggregate thresholds) and the parent company guarantees the subsidiary's liabilities under Section 357 of the Companies Act 2014.

In practice, group structures often require at least the parent company to be audited, even when individual subsidiaries might otherwise qualify for exemption.

## What Does an Audit Cost?

Audit fees vary substantially based on company size, complexity, industry, and the audit firm engaged.

### Fee Ranges by Company Size

| Company Size | Typical Audit Fee | What Drives the Fee |
|---|---|---|
| Small (turnover under EUR 5m) | EUR 3,000-7,000 | Basic audit procedures, limited transactions |
| Medium (turnover EUR 5m-20m) | EUR 7,000-15,000 | More substantive testing, larger sample sizes |
| Large (turnover EUR 20m-50m) | EUR 15,000-30,000 | Comprehensive testing, multiple locations, complex transactions |
| Very large (turnover EUR 50m+) | EUR 30,000-100,000+ | Full audit programme, specialist areas, regulatory requirements |

### What Affects the Audit Fee

**Company size and transaction volume.** More transactions mean more testing. The auditor must examine a statistically significant sample of transactions to form their opinion, and larger companies require larger samples.

**Industry sector.** Certain industries carry higher audit risk and require specialist audit procedures. Financial services, construction (revenue recognition on long-term contracts), property (asset valuations), and manufacturing (stock valuation) typically attract higher audit fees than straightforward service businesses.

**Quality of internal controls.** Companies with strong internal controls (segregation of duties, authorisation procedures, regular reconciliations) allow the auditor to rely more heavily on those controls and reduce the amount of substantive testing required. Weak controls mean more testing, which means higher fees.

**Quality of financial records.** If the company's bookkeeping is well-maintained, the trial balance is clean, and year-end adjustments are documented, the audit proceeds efficiently. If the auditor must first help the company prepare or correct its records, the additional time increases the fee.

**Prior year issues.** If the previous audit identified significant issues (material adjustments, control weaknesses, going concern questions), the current year audit will include additional procedures to verify that those issues have been addressed. This adds to the fee.

**Number of locations.** Companies with multiple offices, branches, or warehouses may require audit procedures at each location, adding travel time and cost.

**Timing.** Audits scheduled well in advance and completed during the audit firm's quieter periods (typically January to March) may attract lower fees than those rushed through during peak season (September to December).

### Audit Fee vs Accounts Preparation Fee

It is important to distinguish between the audit fee and the accounts preparation fee. These are two separate services:

- **Accounts preparation:** Your accountant prepares the statutory financial statements from your bookkeeping records. This is the work described in the annual accounting package (EUR 1,500 to EUR 3,500 for most small companies).

- **Audit:** A registered statutory auditor independently examines the prepared financial statements to form an opinion on whether they give a true and fair view. This is the additional cost on top of accounts preparation.

Some companies have the same firm prepare their accounts and conduct the audit. While this is permitted for companies below certain thresholds, there are independence considerations, and some argue that a separate auditor provides more rigorous oversight.

## The Audit Process

Understanding what happens during an audit helps you understand why it costs what it does.

### Planning Phase

The auditor assesses the company's business, identifies key risks, sets materiality levels, and designs the audit programme. For a first-year audit, this phase is more time-intensive as the auditor must understand the business from scratch.

### Fieldwork Phase

The auditor performs the substantive testing and controls testing designed in the planning phase. Typical audit procedures include:

- **Bank confirmation:** Writing directly to the company's banks to confirm balances
- **Debtor circularisation:** Writing to a sample of customers to confirm amounts owed
- **Creditor confirmation:** Writing to key suppliers to confirm amounts payable
- **Stock observation:** Physically attending a stock count (for companies with significant inventory)
- **Revenue testing:** Examining a sample of sales invoices, delivery documentation, and customer payments
- **Purchase testing:** Examining a sample of supplier invoices, goods receipts, and payments
- **Payroll testing:** Verifying payroll calculations, PRSI deductions, and Revenue submissions
- **Fixed asset verification:** Examining supporting documentation for asset additions and disposals
- **Journal entry testing:** Reviewing manual journal entries for unusual or unauthorised postings
- **Related party review:** Identifying and verifying transactions with connected parties
- **Going concern assessment:** Evaluating whether the company can continue operating for at least twelve months

### Completion Phase

The auditor reviews the results of all testing, discusses any findings with management, obtains a management representation letter, and forms their audit opinion. The audit report is then issued and included in the company's financial statements filed with the CRO.

## Audit Opinions

The auditor's report contains one of four types of opinion:

| Opinion | Meaning | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Unqualified (clean) | Financial statements give a true and fair view | No issues -- the best outcome |
| Qualified (except for) | Mostly fair, but with specific exceptions | Indicates a limitation or disagreement on specific items |
| Adverse | Financial statements do not give a true and fair view | Serious -- indicates material misstatements |
| Disclaimer | Auditor unable to form an opinion | Serious -- usually due to lack of evidence or scope limitation |

For most small companies, an unqualified opinion is the norm. If issues arise during the audit that could lead to a qualification, the auditor will discuss these with management first and give the company an opportunity to make corrections before the report is finalised.

## Audit Exemption: How to Qualify and Maintain It

Given the significant cost savings, maintaining audit exemption should be a priority for every qualifying small company.

### Key Actions to Maintain Exemption

**File your CRO annual return on time.** This is the most common reason companies lose their audit exemption. A single late filing disqualifies the company for two years. The CRO annual return (B1 form) must be filed within 28 days of the annual return date, with the EUR 20 filing fee paid.

Set up a diary system or rely on your accountant to manage this deadline. The cost of a late filing is not just the EUR 100-per-day penalty (up to EUR 5,000) -- it is the loss of audit exemption, which costs EUR 3,000 to EUR 7,000 per year in audit fees for the following two years.

**Stay within the size thresholds.** Monitor your turnover, balance sheet total, and employee count. If you are approaching the thresholds, consider whether business decisions (timing of large contracts, year-end stock levels, headcount planning) might affect your qualification.

**No shareholder objection.** A shareholder holding at least 10 per cent of shares can require the company to have an audit. In practice, this is most relevant for companies with external minority shareholders. Owner-managed companies where the directors hold all shares will not face this issue.

### What Happens If You Lose Exemption

If your company loses audit exemption (through growth, late filing, or shareholder request), you must:

1. Appoint a registered statutory auditor
2. Have the financial statements for the relevant year(s) audited
3. Include the audit report in your CRO filings

The additional cost is the audit fee (EUR 3,000 to EUR 7,000 for most small companies) plus any additional work your accountant must do to prepare the financial statements to audit-ready standard.

## Voluntary Audit

Some companies choose to have a voluntary audit even when they qualify for exemption. Reasons include:

**Bank or lender requirements.** Some banks require audited accounts as a condition of lending, particularly for larger facilities.

**Investor confidence.** Companies seeking external investment may find that audited accounts provide credibility and reassurance to potential investors.

**Internal assurance.** Directors who are not involved in day-to-day financial management may want independent assurance that the accounts are accurate.

**Tender requirements.** Some public sector contracts and larger commercial tenders require audited accounts as part of the qualification criteria.

If you choose a voluntary audit, the cost is the same as a mandatory audit. There is no discounted rate for voluntary audits because the audit procedures and reporting requirements are identical.

## Audit vs Independent Review

An alternative to a full statutory audit is an independent accountant's review. This provides a lower level of assurance than an audit but is significantly cheaper.

| Feature | Statutory Audit | Independent Review |
|---|---|---|
| Level of assurance | Reasonable assurance (high) | Limited assurance (moderate) |
| Procedures | Substantive testing, controls testing, confirmations | Primarily enquiry and analytical procedures |
| Report | Audit opinion (true and fair view) | Review conclusion (nothing has come to attention) |
| Typical cost | EUR 3,000-15,000 | EUR 1,000-3,000 |
| Statutory requirement | Yes (if above exemption) | No (voluntary only) |

An independent review is not a substitute for a statutory audit when one is required. However, it can be useful for companies that want some external assurance without the full cost and formality of an audit.

## Choosing an Auditor

When selecting an audit firm, consider:

**Registration.** The auditor must be a registered statutory auditor with IAASA (Irish Auditing and Accounting Supervisory Authority). Only individuals and firms on the public register can conduct statutory audits in Ireland.

**Experience with your industry.** An auditor familiar with your sector will complete the audit more efficiently and identify industry-specific risks.

**Independence.** The auditor must be independent of the company. This means they should not have significant financial or personal relationships with the directors. In practice, it is common for smaller companies to have their audit conducted by the same firm that prepares their accounts, but the audit team should be separate from the accounts preparation team.

**Fee transparency.** Get a clear, written quote specifying the audit fee, any additional charges (travel, specialist consultations), and the expected timeline.
