Anti-money laundering (AML) obligations in Ireland are set out in the Criminal Justice (Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing) Acts 2010 to 2021. These laws require certain businesses, known as designated persons, to identify their customers, monitor transactions, and report suspicious activity. If your business falls within the scope of the legislation, non-compliance can result in criminal prosecution, significant fines, and reputational damage.

This guide explains which Irish businesses must comply with AML obligations, what customer due diligence involves, how to report suspicious transactions, and the practical steps you should take to meet your legal requirements.

## Who Must Comply: Designated Persons

AML obligations in Ireland do not apply to every business. They apply specifically to **designated persons** as defined in the Criminal Justice Acts. Designated persons include:

- **Credit institutions** (banks and building societies)
- **Financial institutions** (investment firms, insurance companies, payment institutions)
- **Auditors, external accountants, and tax advisers**
- **Independent legal professionals** (solicitors and barristers when acting in certain financial transactions)
- **Trust or company service providers**
- **Property service providers** (estate agents and letting agents)
- **Dealers in high-value goods** when accepting cash payments of EUR 10,000 or more
- **Virtual asset service providers** (cryptocurrency exchanges and wallet providers)
- **Gambling service providers** (bookmakers, casinos, and online gambling operators)

If your business falls into any of these categories, you are a designated person and must comply with the full range of AML obligations. The Central Bank of Ireland supervises credit and financial institutions, while other designated persons are supervised by their relevant professional body or by the Department of Justice.

### Small Business Impact

Many Irish small businesses are affected by AML obligations without realising it. Accountancy practices, tax advisory firms, solicitor practices, estate agents, and any business that deals in high-value cash transactions all fall within scope. Even a sole practitioner accountant must have AML policies and procedures in place.

If you are unsure whether your business is a designated person, check with your professional body or the relevant supervisory authority.

## Customer Due Diligence (CDD)

Customer due diligence is the cornerstone of AML compliance. CDD requires you to identify and verify the identity of your customers before establishing a business relationship or carrying out an occasional transaction above certain thresholds.

### Standard CDD

Standard CDD involves three key steps:

**Step 1: Identify the customer.** Obtain the customer's name, address, date of birth (for individuals), and company registration number (for legal entities). For companies, you must also identify the beneficial owners -- the individuals who ultimately own or control the company.

**Step 2: Verify the identity.** Verify the customer's identity using reliable, independent sources. For individuals, this typically means a government-issued photo ID (passport or driving licence) and proof of address (utility bill or bank statement dated within the last six months). For companies, verify by reference to the CRO register, certificate of incorporation, and constitutional documents.

**Step 3: Understand the business relationship.** Obtain information about the purpose and intended nature of the business relationship. Understand what the customer does, where their funds come from, and what transactions you can expect to see.

### When CDD Must Be Applied

You must carry out CDD in the following situations:

- When establishing a new business relationship
- When carrying out an occasional transaction of EUR 15,000 or more (or linked transactions that together exceed EUR 15,000)
- When you suspect money laundering or terrorist financing, regardless of the transaction amount
- When you have doubts about the accuracy of previously obtained customer identification information

You must complete CDD before establishing the business relationship. If you cannot satisfactorily complete CDD, you must not proceed with the business relationship or transaction, and you must consider whether to make a suspicious transaction report.

### Simplified Due Diligence

In certain lower-risk situations, you may apply simplified due diligence, which involves reduced verification measures. This may be appropriate when dealing with listed companies, regulated financial institutions, or public authorities. However, simplified due diligence is only permitted where a documented risk assessment supports the lower risk classification.

### Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)

Enhanced due diligence must be applied in higher-risk situations, including:

- **Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs):** Individuals who hold or have held prominent public functions, such as heads of state, senior politicians, senior government officials, judicial or military officials, senior executives of state-owned enterprises, and their family members and close associates
- **Complex or unusual transactions** that have no apparent economic or lawful purpose
- **Customers from high-risk third countries** identified by the EU or the Financial Action Task Force (FATF)
- **Correspondent banking relationships**

EDD measures include obtaining additional information about the customer's source of wealth and source of funds, conducting additional verification checks, obtaining senior management approval for the business relationship, and applying enhanced ongoing monitoring.

## Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)

PEPs require special attention under AML law because their position may make them vulnerable to corruption. The definition of a PEP extends beyond the individual to include their family members and known close associates.

When you identify a customer as a PEP, you must:

- Obtain **senior management approval** to establish or continue the business relationship
- Take adequate measures to establish the **source of wealth** and **source of funds**
- Apply **enhanced ongoing monitoring** to the relationship

The PEP classification is not limited to Irish officials. It includes foreign PEPs, domestic PEPs, and persons entrusted with prominent functions by international organisations. PEP status does not automatically mean the person is high risk, but it does trigger mandatory enhanced due diligence measures.

## Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs)

If you know, suspect, or have reasonable grounds to suspect that a transaction or activity is related to money laundering or terrorist financing, you must make a **Suspicious Transaction Report (STR)** to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) of An Garda Siochana and to Revenue.

### What Triggers a Report

Suspicion can arise from a variety of circumstances:

- Transactions that are unusual for the customer's normal pattern of business
- Customers who are reluctant to provide identification or who provide false or misleading information
- Transactions that have no apparent economic or lawful purpose
- Cash-intensive activities disproportionate to the nature of the business
- Funds coming from or going to high-risk jurisdictions
- Customers who appear to be acting on behalf of undisclosed third parties

### How to File an STR

STRs are filed with the FIU at the Garda National Economic Crime Bureau (GNECB). You can submit reports through the goAML online reporting system, which is the FIU's secure electronic reporting platform.

Your STR should include:

- The identity of the customer and any other persons involved
- The nature and details of the suspicious transaction or activity
- The reasons for your suspicion
- Any supporting documentation

### Tipping Off

It is a criminal offence to **tip off** a person that an STR has been made or that an investigation is underway. You must not inform the customer or any third party that you have filed a report. Tipping off can result in criminal prosecution and imprisonment.

This obligation means that after filing an STR, you should continue to deal with the customer normally while awaiting direction from the FIU. If in doubt about how to proceed, seek legal advice.

## Record Keeping Requirements

Designated persons must retain AML records for a minimum of **five years** after the end of the business relationship or the completion of the transaction. Records to be retained include:

- Copies of all CDD documentation (identification documents, verification records)
- Transaction records sufficient to permit reconstruction of individual transactions
- Correspondence and records relating to the business relationship
- Records of any STRs filed

Records must be retained in a manner that allows them to be made available to competent authorities (the FIU, Revenue, the Central Bank, or your supervisory authority) promptly on request.

## AML Policies and Procedures

Every designated person must have documented AML policies, controls, and procedures. These must be proportionate to the nature and size of your business and should cover:

- **Customer due diligence procedures:** How you identify and verify customers, including specific procedures for PEPs, EDD, and simplified due diligence
- **Transaction monitoring:** How you monitor ongoing business relationships for unusual or suspicious activity
- **Reporting procedures:** How STRs are identified, escalated, and filed
- **Record keeping:** How CDD and transaction records are maintained and retained
- **Risk assessment:** A documented assessment of the money laundering and terrorist financing risks your business faces, considering customer types, geographic areas, products and services, and delivery channels
- **Staff training:** How you train staff on AML obligations and ensure they can recognise and report suspicious activity

These policies must be reviewed and updated regularly to reflect changes in the law, regulatory guidance, and your business's risk profile.

## Training Obligations

All employees who are in a position to encounter money laundering or terrorist financing in the course of their work must receive AML training. Training must cover:

- The legal framework (Criminal Justice Acts, EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives)
- Your business's AML policies and procedures
- How to identify suspicious transactions and activity
- How to report suspicions internally and through STRs
- The obligation not to tip off
- The consequences of non-compliance

Training must be provided to new employees before they take up their duties and must be refreshed periodically. Records of training provided must be maintained.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Non-compliance with AML obligations in Ireland can result in severe penalties:

- **Criminal prosecution:** Failure to comply with CDD, record-keeping, or reporting obligations is a criminal offence. On summary conviction, penalties include fines of up to EUR 5,000 and/or imprisonment for up to 12 months. On conviction on indictment, penalties include unlimited fines and/or imprisonment for up to 5 years.
- **Administrative sanctions:** The Central Bank can impose significant administrative sanctions on regulated financial institutions for AML failures, including fines of up to EUR 10 million or 10 per cent of annual turnover.
- **Professional consequences:** Professional bodies (such as Chartered Accountants Ireland, the Law Society, or the PSRA for estate agents) can take disciplinary action, including suspension or removal from the register.
- **Reputational damage:** AML enforcement actions are typically published, causing significant damage to the reputation of the business and its principals.

## Practical Compliance Steps

**Conduct a risk assessment.** Assess the money laundering and terrorist financing risks specific to your business. Consider your customer types, the services you provide, geographic exposure, and delivery channels.

**Document your policies.** Write clear, practical AML policies and procedures. These do not need to be lengthy but must cover CDD, monitoring, reporting, record keeping, and training.

**Implement CDD consistently.** Apply CDD to every new customer and maintain records. Use a checklist to ensure you collect all required information and verification documents.

**Screen for PEPs and sanctions.** Check customers against PEP lists and sanctions lists. Commercial screening tools are available, but for small businesses, manual checks against publicly available lists can be sufficient.

**Monitor ongoing relationships.** Review customer activity periodically to identify changes in risk or unusual patterns. The level of monitoring should be proportionate to the risk.

**Train your team.** Ensure everyone in your business who handles customer relationships or financial transactions understands AML obligations and knows how to report suspicious activity.

**Keep records meticulously.** Retain all CDD documentation and transaction records for at least five years after the relationship ends. Store them securely and in an organised manner.

**Report promptly.** If you suspect money laundering or terrorist financing, file an STR with the FIU immediately. Do not wait to gather additional evidence or consult others beyond your Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO).
