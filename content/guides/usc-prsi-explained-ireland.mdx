Universal Social Charge (USC) and Pay Related Social Insurance (PRSI) are two separate charges on income in Ireland that sit alongside income tax. USC is a tax on gross income that funds general government expenditure, while PRSI is a social insurance contribution that funds specific benefits like the State Pension, Maternity Benefit, and Jobseeker's Benefit.

Together with income tax, USC and PRSI determine your total tax burden. Understanding how each charge works, what rates apply, and which benefits they fund is essential for business owners, whether you are self-employed, employing staff, or both. This guide explains the full picture for Irish businesses.

## What Is Universal Social Charge (USC)?

USC was introduced on 1 January 2011, replacing the income levy and the health levy. It is a tax on gross income before pension contributions or most other deductions. USC applies to almost all forms of income, including employment income, self-employment profits, rental income, and investment income.

### Who Pays USC?

USC applies to individuals whose total income exceeds EUR 13,000 per year. If your total income from all sources is EUR 13,000 or below, you are completely exempt from USC. Once your income exceeds EUR 13,000, USC applies from the first euro of income, not just the amount above the threshold.

Certain income is exempt from USC regardless of the total, including Department of Social Protection payments, income already subject to DIRT (Deposit Interest Retention Tax), and income from a medical card holder aged 70 or over (subject to a reduced rate).

### USC Rates for 2026

USC is charged at progressive rates on different bands of income:

| Income Band | USC Rate |
|-------------|----------|
| First EUR 12,012 | 0.5% |
| EUR 12,012 to EUR 25,760 | 2% |
| EUR 25,760 to EUR 70,044 | 4% |
| Above EUR 70,044 | 8% |

### Self-Employment USC Surcharge

Self-employed individuals with income exceeding EUR 100,000 pay an additional surcharge of 3 per cent on the portion of income above EUR 100,000. This brings the effective USC rate on self-employment income above EUR 100,000 to 11 per cent (8 per cent standard rate plus 3 per cent surcharge).

This surcharge applies only to non-PAYE income. If you have both PAYE employment income and self-employment income, only the self-employment portion above EUR 100,000 is subject to the surcharge.

### Reduced USC Rates

Reduced rates apply in certain circumstances:

**Medical card holders aged under 70** with total income of EUR 60,000 or less pay reduced rates of 0.5 per cent on the first EUR 12,012 and 2 per cent on the balance.

**Individuals aged 70 and over** with total income of EUR 60,000 or less also qualify for the reduced rates.

If your income exceeds EUR 60,000, the standard rates apply regardless of age or medical card status.

### USC Calculation Example

For an employee earning EUR 50,000 per year:

- EUR 12,012 at 0.5% = EUR 60.06
- EUR 13,748 (EUR 25,760 minus EUR 12,012) at 2% = EUR 274.96
- EUR 24,240 (EUR 50,000 minus EUR 25,760) at 4% = EUR 969.60
- **Total USC = EUR 1,304.62**

For a self-employed person earning EUR 120,000:

- EUR 12,012 at 0.5% = EUR 60.06
- EUR 13,748 at 2% = EUR 274.96
- EUR 44,284 at 4% = EUR 1,771.36
- EUR 49,956 (EUR 70,044 to EUR 120,000) at 8% = EUR 3,996.48
- Plus surcharge: EUR 20,000 (above EUR 100,000) at 3% = EUR 600
- **Total USC = EUR 6,702.86**

## What Is Pay Related Social Insurance (PRSI)?

PRSI is a social insurance system that funds specific state benefits. Unlike USC, which is a general tax, PRSI contributions directly entitle you to social welfare benefits. The benefits you can access depend on your PRSI class and the number of contributions you have made.

PRSI is collected by Revenue through the payroll system (for employees) or through the self-assessment system (for self-employed individuals). The Department of Social Protection maintains your contribution record and determines your eligibility for benefits.

### PRSI Classes Explained

PRSI classes determine the rate you pay and the benefits you are entitled to. The class depends on the nature of your employment and your earnings level.

#### Class A: Most Employees

Class A is the main PRSI class and covers the vast majority of private-sector employees, public servants recruited after April 1995, and community employment scheme participants. Class A provides access to the widest range of benefits.

**Employee rate:** 4 per cent of all reckonable earnings (no upper limit). Employees earning EUR 352 or less per week are exempt.

**Employer rate:** 8.8 per cent on weekly earnings of EUR 441 or less; 11.05 per cent on weekly earnings above EUR 441.

**Benefits covered:** State Pension (Contributory), Jobseeker's Benefit, Illness Benefit, Maternity Benefit, Paternity Benefit, Adoptive Benefit, Carer's Benefit, Invalidity Pension, Treatment Benefit, and Occupational Injuries Benefits.

#### Class S: Self-Employed

Class S applies to self-employed individuals, including sole traders, partners in partnerships, and proprietary directors who are not employees of the company. Class S provides access to a narrower range of benefits than Class A.

**Rate:** 4 per cent of all reckonable income, with a minimum annual contribution of EUR 500.

**Benefits covered:** State Pension (Contributory), Maternity Benefit, Adoptive Benefit, Paternity Benefit, Parent's Benefit, and Treatment Benefit.

**Benefits NOT covered:** Jobseeker's Benefit, Illness Benefit, Carer's Benefit, Invalidity Pension, and Occupational Injuries Benefits.

The exclusion of Jobseeker's Benefit and Illness Benefit is a significant gap for self-employed individuals. If your business fails or you become ill, you may not have the same social insurance safety net as an employed person. This is worth considering when planning your financial protection.

#### Class J: Low Earners and Certain Employments

Class J applies to employees earning less than EUR 38 per week and to people in certain types of employment, such as subsidiary employment. Class J provides no benefit entitlements and is essentially a record-keeping class.

#### Class M: Certain Public Servants and Others

Class M applies to employees with no liability to contribute, including people aged 66 and over and certain public servants. Like Class J, Class M provides limited or no benefit entitlements.

### PRSI Credit for Low Earners

Employees earning between EUR 352.01 and EUR 424 per week benefit from a PRSI credit that reduces their PRSI liability. The maximum credit is EUR 12 per week for employees earning EUR 352.01. The credit reduces by one-sixth for every EUR 1 of weekly earnings above EUR 352.01, reaching zero at EUR 424.

This tapered credit ensures that low-earning employees are not disproportionately affected by the 4 per cent PRSI rate.

## How USC and PRSI Interact with Income Tax

Income tax, USC, and PRSI are three separate charges calculated independently but collected together. Understanding how they interact helps you calculate your total tax liability.

### Order of Calculation

For employees, the calculation order is:

1. **Gross pay** is determined
2. **Income tax** is calculated based on gross pay, tax credits, and standard rate cut-off point (from the RPN)
3. **USC** is calculated on gross pay (before pension deductions but after certain exempt income)
4. **Employee PRSI** is calculated on gross pay
5. **Net pay** = Gross pay minus income tax minus USC minus employee PRSI

For self-employed individuals, all three charges are calculated on taxable income (after allowable business expenses) and reported on the Form 11.

### Total Marginal Rate

The combined marginal rate of income tax, USC, and PRSI determines the total tax on each additional euro earned. For most workers:

| Income Level | Income Tax | USC | PRSI | Total Marginal Rate |
|-------------|------------|-----|------|-------------------|
| Up to EUR 42,000 (single) | 20% | 4% | 4% | 28% |
| EUR 42,000 to EUR 70,044 | 40% | 4% | 4% | 48% |
| Above EUR 70,044 | 40% | 8% | 4% | 52% |
| Self-employed above EUR 100,000 | 40% | 11% | 4% | 55% |

These rates demonstrate why tax planning is so important. At the higher income levels, the State takes more than half of each additional euro earned.

### Pension Contributions and Tax Relief

Occupational pension contributions and certain personal pension contributions reduce your income tax liability but do not reduce USC. This means that while pension contributions are tax-efficient for income tax purposes, you still pay USC on the full income before the pension deduction.

PRSI is also calculated on income before pension contributions for employees. For self-employed individuals, PRSI Class S is charged on reckonable income, which is broadly the same as taxable income.

## Social Insurance Benefits Linked to PRSI

Your PRSI contributions build up an entitlement to specific social welfare benefits. The benefits available depend on your PRSI class and the number of contributions you have made.

### State Pension (Contributory)

The State Pension (Contributory) is the primary long-term benefit funded by PRSI. To qualify, you need a minimum of 520 paid PRSI contributions (10 years) at Class A or Class S. The maximum personal rate is EUR 277.30 per week (2026 rate).

The pension is calculated using either the yearly average method or the total contributions approach (TCA), whichever gives the higher amount. Under TCA, you need 2,080 contributions (40 years) for the maximum rate.

### Maternity Benefit

Available to Class A and Class S contributors with sufficient contributions. The standard rate is EUR 274 per week for 26 weeks. Self-employed individuals are eligible, which is particularly relevant for sole traders and proprietary directors.

### Jobseeker's Benefit

Available only to Class A contributors. Self-employed people paying Class S are not eligible for Jobseeker's Benefit. This is one of the most significant differences between PRSI classes and a key consideration for business owners deciding on their business structure and remuneration approach.

### Treatment Benefit

Available to Class A and Class S contributors. Provides free or subsidised dental, optical, and hearing treatments. You need a minimum number of PRSI contributions to qualify, typically 260 contributions.

## Common Misconceptions About USC and PRSI

Several misconceptions about USC and PRSI can lead to errors in tax planning and compliance.

**Misconception: USC and PRSI are the same thing.** They are entirely separate charges with different purposes, rates, and bases. USC is a tax on income that funds general government spending. PRSI is a social insurance contribution that funds specific benefits.

**Misconception: Self-employed people do not pay PRSI.** Self-employed individuals pay PRSI at Class S (4 per cent). They have paid PRSI since 1988 and are entitled to certain benefits including the State Pension (Contributory) and Maternity Benefit.

**Misconception: There is an upper earnings limit for PRSI.** There is no upper earnings limit for either employee PRSI (Class A) or self-employed PRSI (Class S). You pay 4 per cent on all reckonable earnings with no cap.

**Misconception: USC only applies to high earners.** USC applies to all individuals with total income exceeding EUR 13,000. Given that this threshold is relatively low, the vast majority of working adults pay USC.

**Misconception: Pension contributions reduce USC.** Pension contributions reduce your income tax liability but do not reduce USC. USC is charged on gross income before pension deductions.

## USC and PRSI for Business Owners

As a business owner in Ireland, your USC and PRSI position depends on how you structure your remuneration.

### Sole Traders

Sole traders pay income tax, USC, and PRSI Class S on their business profits. All three charges are declared and paid through the Form 11 self-assessment return. The 3 per cent USC surcharge applies to self-employment income above EUR 100,000.

### Company Directors

A company director who draws a salary pays income tax, USC, and Class A PRSI through the payroll (PAYE) system, just like any other employee. However, a proprietary director (one who controls more than 15 per cent of the company) may also be liable for Class S PRSI on any non-PAYE income such as dividends or rental income.

Directors should work with their accountant to optimise the split between salary and dividends, taking account of income tax, USC, PRSI, corporation tax, and the close company surcharge.

### Employers

Employers pay employer PRSI as an additional cost on top of each employee's gross salary. This is not deducted from the employee but is a direct cost to the business. Employer PRSI is the largest social insurance contribution in the system and a significant employment cost.

## Summary

USC and PRSI are distinct charges that sit alongside income tax in the Irish tax system. USC is a progressive tax on gross income at rates from 0.5 per cent to 8 per cent (plus a 3 per cent surcharge for self-employed income above EUR 100,000). PRSI is a social insurance contribution that funds specific state benefits, with the class and rate depending on your employment status.

For employees, PRSI Class A at 4 per cent provides access to the widest range of benefits including Jobseeker's Benefit and Illness Benefit. For self-employed individuals, PRSI Class S at 4 per cent covers the State Pension and certain other benefits but excludes Jobseeker's Benefit and Illness Benefit.

The combined marginal rate of income tax, USC, and PRSI can reach 52 per cent for employees and 55 per cent for self-employed individuals at higher income levels. Understanding how these charges interact is essential for tax planning and for making informed decisions about business structure and remuneration.
