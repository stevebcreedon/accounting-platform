Capital Gains Tax (CGT) in Ireland is charged at 33 per cent on the profit you make when you dispose of an asset. For business owners, CGT is most relevant when selling a business, disposing of commercial property, transferring shares, or selling other capital assets. Revenue collects CGT through self-assessment, and you must calculate, declare, and pay the tax yourself.

Every business owner should understand CGT because it directly affects the after-tax return on your most significant transactions. Selling a business you built over decades, disposing of an investment property, or transferring shares in your company all trigger CGT. However, several valuable reliefs exist that can substantially reduce the bill. This guide explains how CGT works in Ireland, how to calculate your liability, and which reliefs may apply to your situation.

## How Capital Gains Tax Works

CGT applies when you make a chargeable gain on the disposal of an asset. A disposal includes selling an asset, gifting it, exchanging it, or receiving an insurance payout for its loss or destruction. The gain is calculated as the difference between the disposal proceeds and the allowable cost of the asset.

### Who Pays CGT?

Irish-resident individuals and companies pay CGT on gains from worldwide disposals. Non-residents pay CGT only on gains from the disposal of specified Irish assets, including Irish land and buildings, minerals in Ireland, and shares deriving their value from such assets.

Companies pay CGT on capital gains at the corporation tax rate of 33 per cent (not the 12.5 per cent trading rate). The gain is included in the company's corporation tax return (CT1) rather than a separate CGT return.

### The 33 Per Cent Rate

The standard CGT rate in Ireland is 33 per cent. This rate has applied since December 2013 and applies to most chargeable gains. There are no different rates for short-term or long-term gains in Ireland, unlike some other jurisdictions.

A reduced rate of 10 per cent applies to qualifying gains under the Revised Entrepreneur Relief (see below).

### Annual Exemption

Each individual is entitled to an annual CGT exemption of EUR 1,270. This means the first EUR 1,270 of chargeable gains in any tax year is tax-free. The exemption is per person, not per asset, so it can only be used once per year regardless of how many disposals you make.

Married couples each have their own EUR 1,270 exemption, giving a combined annual exemption of EUR 2,540 for jointly-owned assets.

The annual exemption is modest and has not been increased for many years. It provides limited relief but should always be taken into account in your CGT calculations.

## Calculating Your Capital Gain

The chargeable gain is calculated using a straightforward formula:

**Gain = Disposal proceeds - Allowable costs**

### Disposal Proceeds

The disposal proceeds are the amount you receive for the asset. If the disposal is a sale at arm's length, the proceeds are the sale price. If the disposal is a gift or a sale at less than market value (between connected persons), Revenue treats the market value at the date of disposal as the proceeds.

### Allowable Costs

The following costs can be deducted when calculating your gain:

**Original purchase price:** The amount you paid to acquire the asset, including any incidental costs of acquisition such as solicitor's fees, stamp duty, and auctioneer's fees.

**Enhancement expenditure:** Capital expenditure that enhanced the value of the asset and is reflected in the asset at the time of disposal. This includes the cost of extensions to property, improvements to land, or capital invested in developing an asset. Routine maintenance and repair costs are not enhancement expenditure.

**Disposal costs:** Incidental costs of making the disposal, including solicitor's fees, auctioneer's fees, and advertising costs.

**Indexation relief:** For assets acquired before 1 January 2003, you can index the purchase price and enhancement expenditure to account for inflation. The indexation factor is based on the consumer price index and is applied from the year of acquisition to 2002 (indexation relief is frozen at 2003 levels). For assets acquired after 1 January 2003, no indexation relief is available.

### Calculation Example

You purchased a commercial property in 2015 for EUR 300,000, including acquisition costs. In 2026, you sell it for EUR 500,000 with disposal costs of EUR 10,000.

- Disposal proceeds: EUR 500,000
- Less acquisition cost: EUR 300,000
- Less disposal costs: EUR 10,000
- Chargeable gain: EUR 190,000
- Less annual exemption: EUR 1,270
- Taxable gain: EUR 188,730
- CGT at 33%: EUR 62,280.90

No indexation relief applies because the asset was acquired after 2003.

## Payment Dates and Filing

CGT payment dates are particularly strict, and late payment attracts interest. The tax year is divided into two periods for CGT purposes.

### Initial Period: 1 January to 30 November

For gains arising between 1 January and 30 November, CGT must be paid by 15 December of the same year. This is a payment on account and must be made even before the annual tax return is filed.

### Later Period: 1 December to 31 December

For gains arising in December, CGT must be paid by 31 January of the following year.

### Filing the Return

Capital gains must be declared on your annual income tax return (Form 11 for self-assessed individuals) or your corporation tax return (CT1 for companies). The filing deadline is 31 October of the year following the tax year (mid-November via ROS).

If you are not otherwise required to file a Form 11 (for example, if you are a PAYE employee), you must file a Form CG1 specifically for capital gains. The CG1 is due by 31 October of the following year.

### Interest on Late Payment

Revenue charges interest at 0.0219 per cent per day (approximately 8 per cent per annum) on CGT paid late. Interest runs from the payment due date (15 December or 31 January) until the date of actual payment.

## Entrepreneur Relief (Revised)

Entrepreneur Relief, formally known as the Revised Entrepreneur Relief, is the most valuable CGT relief for Irish business owners. It provides a reduced CGT rate of 10 per cent (instead of 33 per cent) on qualifying gains, up to a lifetime limit of EUR 1 million.

### Qualifying Conditions

To claim Entrepreneur Relief, you must meet the following conditions:

**Qualifying business assets:** The disposal must be of qualifying business assets, which include shares in a qualifying company, or assets used in a qualifying business that you carry on as an individual (sole trader or partner).

**Ownership period:** You must have owned the qualifying business assets for a continuous period of at least 3 years in the 5 years immediately before the disposal.

**Active involvement:** For shares in a company, you must have been a working director of the company for at least 3 continuous years in the 5-year period before disposal, and you must have spent at least 50 per cent of your working time in the service of the company during that period.

**Minimum shareholding:** You must have owned at least 5 per cent of the ordinary share capital of the company for at least 3 continuous years in the 5-year period before disposal.

### The EUR 1 Million Lifetime Limit

The 10 per cent rate applies to a lifetime limit of EUR 1 million in qualifying gains. Once you have used your EUR 1 million lifetime limit, any further gains are taxed at the standard 33 per cent rate. The lifetime limit applies to all qualifying gains across all disposals, not per transaction.

### Calculation Example

You sell your company for EUR 1,500,000. Your original investment was EUR 200,000. The gain is EUR 1,300,000.

- First EUR 1,000,000 at 10% (Entrepreneur Relief): EUR 100,000
- Remaining EUR 300,000 at 33%: EUR 99,000
- Less annual exemption at 33%: EUR 419.10
- Total CGT: EUR 198,580.90

Without Entrepreneur Relief, the CGT would be EUR 428,450.70. The relief saves EUR 229,869.80.

## Retirement Relief

Retirement Relief exempts certain disposals from CGT entirely, provided specific conditions are met. It is designed to allow business owners to dispose of their business or farm on retirement without a CGT liability.

### Disposal to Any Person (Age 55 or Over)

If you are aged 55 or over and dispose of qualifying business assets to any person, the disposal is exempt from CGT provided the market value of the assets does not exceed EUR 750,000. If the market value exceeds EUR 750,000, the full gain is chargeable (not just the excess).

### Disposal to a Child (Any Age for Disposer)

If you dispose of qualifying business assets to your child (including a niece or nephew who has worked in the business), there is no upper limit on the market value for CGT purposes. The disposal is fully exempt from CGT regardless of the value, provided you meet the other qualifying conditions.

### Qualifying Conditions

To claim Retirement Relief, the qualifying conditions include:

- You must be aged 55 or over at the date of disposal (for disposals to non-family members)
- You must have owned the business assets for at least 10 years ending on the date of disposal
- The assets must have been used in a qualifying business throughout the 10-year period
- For shares, the company must be a trading company and you must have been a working director for at least 10 years

### Interaction with Entrepreneur Relief

Retirement Relief and Entrepreneur Relief can interact. If a disposal qualifies for full Retirement Relief, no CGT is payable and Entrepreneur Relief is not needed. If Retirement Relief does not fully cover the gain (for example, because the market value exceeds EUR 750,000), Entrepreneur Relief can potentially be applied to any remaining taxable gain.

## Transfer of Business to a Company

When a sole trader or partnership incorporates their business by transferring it to a company, the transfer is technically a disposal for CGT purposes. However, Relief under Section 600 of the Taxes Consolidation Act 1997 allows the gain to be rolled over into the shares received in the company.

The conditions for this relief include transferring the business as a going concern, receiving shares in the company as the whole or part of the consideration, and the company assuming the trade of the business.

The effect is that no CGT is payable at the time of incorporation. Instead, the gain is deferred until the shares in the company are eventually disposed of, at which point the original base cost of the business assets is used to calculate the gain on the share disposal.

## CGT on Property for Business Owners

Property disposals are one of the most common CGT events for business owners. Whether you are selling a business premises, an investment property, or development land, CGT applies to the gain.

### Business Premises

When you sell a property that has been used in your business, the gain is subject to CGT at 33 per cent. If you have claimed capital allowances on the property (for example, industrial buildings allowance), the allowances claimed may be clawed back and added to the gain.

### Principal Private Residence Relief

Your family home is exempt from CGT under the principal private residence (PPR) relief. If you have used part of your home exclusively for business (for example, a home office), that portion of the gain may not qualify for PPR relief. Revenue applies a proportional approach based on the area used for business.

### Development Land

Gains on the disposal of development land are subject to CGT at 33 per cent. The windfall tax on development land profits, which previously applied at 80 per cent, was abolished in 2015.

## CGT for Companies

When a company makes a capital gain, the gain is subject to corporation tax at 33 per cent (the CGT rate, not the 12.5 per cent trading rate). The gain is calculated in the same way as for individuals, using disposal proceeds minus allowable costs.

Companies do not receive the EUR 1,270 annual exemption. Entrepreneur Relief and Retirement Relief are reliefs for individuals and do not apply to gains made by companies.

However, companies can benefit from the Substantial Shareholding Exemption (participation exemption) in certain circumstances when disposing of shares in subsidiary companies. This relief can exempt the gain entirely if certain ownership and trading conditions are met.

## Record Keeping

Revenue requires you to keep records of all disposals and acquisitions of assets for at least 6 years after the filing date. Records should include purchase contracts and receipts, evidence of enhancement expenditure, disposal contracts, correspondence with solicitors and agents, and calculations of gains and losses.

Good record keeping is essential because indexation relief calculations (for pre-2003 assets) and base cost determinations can be complex. Without adequate records, you may not be able to support a lower base cost or claim relief.

## Summary

Capital Gains Tax at 33 per cent applies to profits from disposing of assets in Ireland, including selling a business, property, or shares. Each individual has an annual exemption of EUR 1,270, which is modest but should always be claimed.

The two most valuable reliefs for business owners are Entrepreneur Relief (10 per cent rate on the first EUR 1 million of lifetime qualifying gains) and Retirement Relief (full exemption for business owners aged 55 and over, subject to a EUR 750,000 limit for non-family disposals).

CGT payment dates are strict: 15 December for gains arising between January and November, and 31 January for December gains. Late payment attracts interest at 0.0219 per cent per day. Plan well ahead of any major disposal to understand your CGT position and ensure you meet all qualifying conditions for available reliefs.
