To operate PAYE as an employer in Ireland, you must register with Revenue, deduct income tax, USC, and PRSI from your employees' pay, and submit payroll information to Revenue on or before each pay date. Since PAYE Modernisation launched in January 2019, all payroll reporting is real-time, meaning Revenue receives your payroll data every time you pay an employee.

Employer registration, payroll deductions, and real-time reporting are mandatory from the moment you hire your first employee. Failing to register or operate PAYE correctly can result in penalties, interest, and personal liability for directors. This guide walks you through every obligation from registration to end-of-year compliance.

## Registering as an Employer with Revenue

Before you pay your first employee, you must register as an employer with the Revenue Commissioners. The registration process differs depending on your business structure.

### Sole Traders: Form TR1

If you are a sole trader hiring your first employee, you register for employer PAYE/PRSI through the TR1 form. If you are already registered for income tax or VAT, you can add employer registration to your existing tax registration by contacting Revenue through ROS or submitting an amended TR1.

### Limited Companies: Form TR2

Limited companies register using the TR2 form. Most new companies register for employer PAYE/PRSI at the time of incorporation, especially if directors will be paid through the payroll. Directors who receive a salary or other emoluments must be processed through the PAYE system.

### Registration Requirements

To register, you need:

- Your tax registration number (PPS number for sole traders, company registration number for limited companies)
- The expected date of first payment to employees
- The number of employees you expect to hire
- The payment frequency (weekly, fortnightly, monthly)
- Your bank account details for any Revenue refunds

Revenue typically processes employer registrations within 5 working days for online applications. Once registered, you receive an employer registration number and access to the employer section of ROS.

## Understanding PAYE Modernisation

PAYE Modernisation, which took effect on 1 January 2019, fundamentally changed how employers report payroll information to Revenue. Under the previous system, employers reported payroll annually through P35 returns. Under PAYE Modernisation, reporting is real-time.

### Real-Time Reporting

Every time you pay an employee, you must submit a payroll submission to Revenue on or before the pay date. This submission contains details of each employee's pay, deductions, and employer contributions. Revenue processes this information immediately, meaning they have a real-time view of all employment income in the State.

### Revenue Payroll Notifications (RPNs)

Before you can calculate deductions for an employee, you must request a Revenue Payroll Notification (RPN) for that employee through ROS. The RPN contains the employee's tax credits, standard rate cut-off point, and USC rate band information. Revenue determines these amounts based on the employee's personal circumstances, other employments, and any reliefs they have claimed.

You must use the information in the RPN to calculate the correct deductions. You cannot use different figures, even if the employee asks you to. If an employee believes their RPN is incorrect, they must contact Revenue directly to have it amended.

RPNs are updated automatically by Revenue when an employee's circumstances change. You should check for updated RPNs regularly, ideally before each payroll run.

## Employee Deductions Explained

As an employer, you must deduct three charges from each employee's gross pay: income tax, USC, and employee PRSI.

### Income Tax (PAYE)

Income tax is deducted using the cumulative basis or the week 1/month 1 basis, as specified in the employee's RPN.

**Cumulative basis:** The standard method. Each pay period, you calculate the year-to-date tax due based on total pay so far, then deduct the amount not yet collected. This ensures the employee pays the correct total tax by year-end and accounts for fluctuations in pay.

**Week 1/month 1 basis (non-cumulative):** Each pay period is treated independently, with no reference to previous periods. This basis is typically applied to new employees until Revenue issues a cumulative RPN, or in situations where Revenue wants to prevent a refund of tax.

### Universal Social Charge (USC)

USC is deducted from gross pay (before pension contributions but after certain exemptions). The rates are:

| USC Band | Rate |
|----------|------|
| First EUR 12,012 | 0.5% |
| EUR 12,012 to EUR 25,760 | 2% |
| EUR 25,760 to EUR 70,044 | 4% |
| Above EUR 70,044 | 8% |

Employees earning EUR 13,000 or less per year are exempt from USC. The employee's RPN will indicate their USC status and applicable rates.

### Employee PRSI

Employee PRSI is deducted based on the employee's PRSI class. Most private-sector employees are Class A. The employee PRSI rate for Class A workers is 4 per cent of all reckonable earnings, with no upper earnings limit.

Employees earning EUR 352 or less per week are exempt from employee PRSI. A PRSI credit applies for employees earning between EUR 352.01 and EUR 424 per week, tapering to zero at EUR 424.

## Employer PRSI

In addition to the deductions from employees' pay, you must pay employer PRSI as an additional cost to your business. Employer PRSI is not deducted from the employee's pay but is an expense borne entirely by the employer.

### Employer PRSI Rates

| Employee's Weekly Pay | Employer PRSI Rate |
|----------------------|-------------------|
| EUR 441 or less | 8.8% |
| Above EUR 441 | 11.05% |

The 8.8 per cent rate applies to all employees earning EUR 441 or less per week (approximately EUR 22,932 per year). For employees earning above this threshold, the higher rate of 11.05 per cent applies to all of their earnings, not just the amount above EUR 441.

This means that a small increase in an employee's weekly pay from EUR 441 to EUR 442 can trigger a significant jump in your employer PRSI cost. Be mindful of this threshold when setting pay rates.

### Calculating Total Employment Cost

When budgeting for a new employee, remember that employer PRSI is an additional cost on top of the gross salary. For an employee earning EUR 40,000 per year:

- Gross salary: EUR 40,000
- Employer PRSI at 11.05 per cent: EUR 4,420
- Total cost to employer: EUR 44,420

Employer PRSI is deductible as a business expense for income tax or corporation tax purposes.

## Payroll Submissions to Revenue

Under PAYE Modernisation, you must make payroll submissions to Revenue each time you pay employees. There are three types of submission.

### Payroll Submission Request (PSR)

This is the main submission you file each pay period. It contains details of each employee's pay, deductions (income tax, USC, employee PRSI), and employer PRSI. The PSR must be submitted on or before the pay date.

If you pay employees weekly, you submit a PSR every week. If you pay monthly, you submit one PSR per month. Each PSR includes a line item for every employee paid in that run.

### Payroll Submission Correction

If you discover an error in a previously submitted PSR, you can submit a correction. Corrections should be made as soon as the error is identified. Common corrections include incorrect pay amounts, wrong PRSI class, or missing employees.

### Payroll Submission Deletion

In rare cases, you may need to delete an entire submission (for example, if a submission was made in error for the wrong period). Deletions must be made through ROS.

## Operating Payroll: Step-by-Step

Here is the process for operating payroll each pay period.

### Step 1: Request RPNs

Before each payroll run, check for updated RPNs through ROS. Download the latest RPNs for all employees. If you have new employees, request RPNs for them by providing their PPS number and start date.

### Step 2: Calculate Gross Pay

Calculate each employee's gross pay for the period, including basic salary, overtime, bonuses, commissions, and any other emoluments. Include the value of any benefits in kind (BIK) that are processed through payroll.

### Step 3: Calculate Deductions

Using the RPN data, calculate income tax, USC, and employee PRSI for each employee. Most payroll software performs these calculations automatically based on the RPNs.

### Step 4: Submit PSR to Revenue

Submit the Payroll Submission Request to Revenue through ROS on or before the pay date. Most payroll software can submit directly to Revenue via the PAYE Modernisation API.

### Step 5: Pay Employees

Pay employees their net pay (gross pay minus all deductions). Provide each employee with a payslip showing gross pay, all deductions, and net pay. Payslips are a legal requirement under the Payment of Wages Act 1991.

### Step 6: Pay Revenue

Remit the total deductions (income tax, USC, employee PRSI, and employer PRSI) to Revenue. Payment is due by the 14th of the month following the pay period, extended to the 23rd for payments made through ROS. Most employers pay monthly, even if they run weekly payrolls, by accumulating the month's deductions and remitting them in a single payment.

## Benefits in Kind (BIK)

A Benefit in Kind is any non-cash benefit provided to an employee by virtue of their employment. Common examples include company cars, health insurance, accommodation, and preferential loans. BIK is taxable and must be processed through the payroll system.

### Company Cars

The taxable value of a company car is based on the original market value (OMV) of the car and the annual business mileage. The BIK rates range from 22.5 per cent of OMV (for 24,000 km or more of business travel) to 33.25 per cent (for less than 24,000 km). Electric vehicles currently benefit from a reduced OMV for BIK purposes, with a relief of up to EUR 35,000 deducted from the OMV.

### Health Insurance

If an employer pays for or contributes to an employee's health insurance, the cost is a taxable BIK. The employer deducts income tax and USC on the value through payroll. However, the employee is entitled to tax relief at the standard rate (20 per cent) on health insurance premiums, which is typically applied as a credit at source by the insurer.

### Small Benefit Exemption

Employers can provide employees with up to two small benefits per year, each with a maximum value of EUR 1,000, tax-free. The benefits must be non-cash (vouchers or tangible gifts). This exemption allows employers to reward employees without creating a tax liability, up to a combined annual limit of EUR 2,000 per employee.

## Hiring Your First Employee: Checklist

If you are hiring your first employee in Ireland, here is a comprehensive checklist of your obligations:

1. **Register as an employer** with Revenue using Form TR1 or TR2
2. **Collect the employee's PPS number** and personal details
3. **Request an RPN** for the employee through ROS
4. **Set up payroll software** or engage a payroll provider
5. **Prepare an employment contract** (required within 5 days of starting under the Employment (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2018)
6. **Register for employer's liability insurance** (compulsory under the Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005)
7. **Set up your payroll cycle** (weekly, fortnightly, or monthly)
8. **Submit your first PSR** on or before the first pay date
9. **Provide a payslip** with each payment
10. **Remit deductions to Revenue** by the 23rd of the following month (ROS)

## End-of-Year Obligations

While PAYE Modernisation has eliminated the old P35 annual return, employers still have end-of-year responsibilities.

### Verify Payroll Data

At the end of each tax year, review all payroll submissions for accuracy. Ensure that all employees have been reported correctly, all deductions are accurate, and any errors have been corrected.

### Provide Employee Summary

Although employers are no longer required to provide P60 certificates, employees can access their employment detail summary through Revenue's myAccount service. Employers should ensure their payroll data supports this by keeping all submissions up to date.

### Final Submission

Revenue considers the last payroll submission of the year as the final data for that tax year. Ensure all corrections are made before the year-end cut-off to avoid discrepancies.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Revenue takes employer PAYE compliance seriously. Non-compliance can result in:

- **Interest on late payment:** 0.0274 per cent per day on any PAYE/PRSI remitted late
- **Penalties for late filing:** Fixed penalties for late or missing payroll submissions
- **Estimation assessments:** If you fail to submit payroll data, Revenue can estimate your liability and issue an assessment, which becomes legally enforceable
- **Director liability:** In the case of a limited company, directors can be held personally liable for unpaid PAYE/PRSI under Section 1078 of the Taxes Consolidation Act 1997
- **Criminal prosecution:** In serious cases of deliberate non-compliance or fraud

## Summary

Operating PAYE as an employer in Ireland requires registration with Revenue, real-time payroll reporting under PAYE Modernisation, and accurate calculation and remittance of income tax, USC, employee PRSI, and employer PRSI. The system is designed to ensure employees pay the correct tax throughout the year and that Revenue has real-time visibility of all employment income.

Your key obligations are to request RPNs before each payroll run, submit a Payroll Submission Request on or before each pay date, provide payslips to all employees, and remit total deductions to Revenue by the 23rd of the following month via ROS.

Employer PRSI is a significant additional cost at 8.8 per cent or 11.05 per cent of employee earnings. Factor this into your employment budgets alongside gross salary. Take advantage of the small benefit exemption (two tax-free benefits of up to EUR 1,000 each per year) to reward employees cost-effectively.

If you are hiring for the first time, use the checklist in this guide and consider engaging a payroll bureau or accountant to handle your payroll obligations. The cost of professional payroll services is typically far less than the penalties for getting it wrong.
