Every company registered in Ireland must file an annual return with the Companies Registration Office (CRO) each year. The annual return is filed on the B1 form and provides the CRO with up-to-date information about your company's directors, secretary, shareholders, registered office, and financial statements. Filing on time is essential because late filing triggers penalties and, critically, the loss of audit exemption for two years.

This guide explains everything you need to know about the CRO annual return: what the B1 form contains, how to determine your filing deadline, how to file online through the CORE system, what accounts to attach, and how to avoid the common mistakes that catch Irish companies out every year.

## What Is the CRO Annual Return

The annual return is a statutory requirement under the Companies Act 2014. It is a snapshot of your company's details as at a specific date, known as your Annual Return Date (ARD). The return confirms key information about your company and attaches your financial statements for the relevant period.

The annual return is separate from your tax returns filed with Revenue. Even if your company is dormant and has no trading activity, you must still file an annual return with the CRO every year. Failure to do so can ultimately lead to the company being struck off the register.

The purpose of the annual return is to maintain the public record of companies in Ireland. Anyone can search the CRO register and view the annual returns filed by any company. This transparency supports creditors, investors, and the public in making informed decisions about the companies they deal with.

## Your Annual Return Date (ARD)

Every Irish company has a specific Annual Return Date. Your ARD is the date to which the annual return relates, and it determines your filing deadline. Understanding your ARD is the single most important aspect of CRO compliance.

### How Your ARD Is Set

When a company is first incorporated, its initial ARD is set at **six months after the date of incorporation**. For example, if your company was incorporated on 1 March 2026, your first ARD would be 1 September 2026.

For subsequent years, your ARD is set at 12 months after the previous ARD. So if your first ARD was 1 September 2026, your second ARD would be 1 September 2027.

### The 28-Day Filing Window

You have **28 days from your ARD** to file your annual return with the CRO. This means if your ARD is 1 September, your filing deadline is 29 September. If the 28th day falls on a weekend or public holiday, the deadline extends to the next business day.

This 28-day window is strict. Filing even one day late triggers the late filing penalties and loss of audit exemption. There is no grace period and no discretion to waive the deadline.

### Changing Your ARD

You can apply to change your ARD by filing a Form B73 with the CRO. This can be useful if you want to align your ARD with your financial year end or with a more convenient time of year.

You can bring your ARD forward to any earlier date. However, you can only extend your ARD by a maximum of six months, and you cannot extend it more than once in a five-year period.

Changing your ARD does not change the requirement to file -- it simply changes the date to which the return relates and therefore the filing deadline.

## What the B1 Form Contains

The B1 form requires the following information:

### Company Details

- Company name and CRO number
- Registered office address
- Principal place of business (if different from registered office)
- Type of business activity (NACE code)

### Directors and Secretary

- Full names, dates of birth, and addresses of all directors
- Full name and address of the company secretary
- Details of any changes to directors or secretary since the last return

### Shareholders and Share Capital

- Total authorised share capital
- Total issued share capital
- Details of shareholders and their shareholdings
- Any changes in share capital or shareholding since the last return

### Financial Statements

You must attach your company's financial statements to the annual return. The type of accounts you attach depends on your company's size:

**Micro companies** (meeting 2 of 3 criteria: turnover under EUR 700,000, balance sheet under EUR 350,000, fewer than 10 employees) can file abridged accounts consisting of an abridged balance sheet only.

**Small companies** (meeting 2 of 3 criteria: turnover under EUR 12 million, balance sheet under EUR 6 million, fewer than 50 employees) can file abridged accounts consisting of an abridged balance sheet and limited notes.

**Medium and large companies** must file full accounts including a balance sheet, profit and loss account, directors' report, and auditor's report.

### First Annual Return

Your first annual return after incorporation does not need to have financial statements attached. This is because the company may not have completed a full financial period by its first ARD. However, the B1 form itself must still be completed and filed within 28 days of the first ARD.

Financial statements must be attached to every subsequent annual return.

## How to File Online via CORE

The CRO's online filing system is called **CORE** (Companies Online Registration Environment). Filing online is the standard method and offers several advantages:

- Lower filing fee (EUR 20 online versus EUR 40 by post)
- Real-time validation of your form before submission
- Confirmation of receipt
- Faster processing

### Step-by-Step Filing Process

**Step 1: Register for CORE.** If you have not already, create an account on the CORE website at core.cro.ie. You will need your company's CRO number and a valid email address. The company's presenter (usually the company secretary or their accountant) files the return.

**Step 2: Log in and select Annual Return.** Navigate to the Annual Return section and select the return you need to file. CORE will display your company's current ARD and filing deadline.

**Step 3: Complete the B1 form.** Enter or confirm all required information. CORE will pre-populate some fields based on your company's existing records. Review all pre-populated information carefully and update anything that has changed.

**Step 4: Upload financial statements.** Attach your financial statements in PDF format. Ensure the PDF is legible and contains all required elements for your company's size category.

**Step 5: Pay the filing fee.** The online filing fee is EUR 20, payable by credit or debit card through the CORE system.

**Step 6: Submit.** Review all information and submit. You will receive a confirmation email with a receipt number. Keep this for your records.

**Step 7: Digital signature.** After submission, the B1 must be digitally signed by a director and the company secretary (or by two directors if there is no secretary). CORE sends signature requests to the relevant individuals, who sign electronically through the system.

## Filing Fee

The CRO annual return filing fee is:

- **EUR 20** for online filing via CORE
- **EUR 40** for paper filing by post

There is no additional charge for attaching financial statements. The fee is payable each year with each annual return.

## Consequences of Late Filing

Filing your annual return late has serious consequences:

### Late Filing Penalty

The CRO charges EUR 100 per day for each day the return is late, up to a maximum of EUR 20,000. This penalty is a debt owed by the company to the CRO and must be paid in addition to the standard filing fee.

### Loss of Audit Exemption

If your annual return is filed late, your company loses its entitlement to claim audit exemption for the financial year to which the return relates and the following financial year. This means you must engage an auditor to carry out a statutory audit of your accounts for both years, even if your company would otherwise qualify for exemption.

The cost of a statutory audit typically ranges from EUR 3,000 to EUR 8,000 for a small company. This makes late filing one of the most expensive compliance failures for Irish companies, often costing far more than the late filing penalty itself.

### Involuntary Strike-Off

If a company fails to file annual returns for two or more consecutive years, the CRO may begin involuntary strike-off proceedings. The company is placed on the gazette for strike-off, and if no action is taken within the specified period, the company is dissolved and removed from the register.

Restoring a struck-off company requires a High Court application, which involves legal fees and court costs that can run to several thousand euro.

## Common Mistakes with CRO Annual Returns

**Not knowing your ARD.** Many directors do not know their company's Annual Return Date. This is the single most common reason for late filing. Check your ARD on the CRO website or through the CORE system immediately if you are unsure.

**Confusing the ARD with the filing deadline.** Your ARD is the date to which the return relates. You then have 28 days from the ARD to file. Some directors mistakenly treat the ARD as the filing deadline, giving themselves no time to prepare.

**Forgetting the first annual return.** New companies sometimes assume they do not need to file an annual return until they have been trading for a full year. In fact, the first ARD is set at six months after incorporation, and the return must be filed within 28 days of that date.

**Not attaching accounts.** Financial statements must be attached to every annual return except the first. Filing a B1 without accounts (after the first return) means the return is incomplete, and the CRO will not accept it.

**Using the wrong accounts format.** Small companies often attach full accounts when they could file abridged accounts, or file accounts that do not meet the minimum requirements. Ensure you understand which category your company falls into and file the appropriate format.

**Not updating director or shareholder changes.** If directors have changed, shares have been transferred, or the registered office has moved since the last return, the B1 must reflect the current position. Failing to update these details means the public record is inaccurate.

**Missing digital signatures.** After submitting online, the B1 must be digitally signed. The return is not considered filed until signatures are complete. Ensure all relevant parties sign promptly after submission.

## Dormant Companies

Even if your company is dormant (not trading, no significant accounting transactions), you must still file annual returns with the CRO every year. Dormant companies can file dormant company accounts, which are simplified financial statements. Failing to file returns for a dormant company leads to the same penalties and risks as for an active company.

If you no longer need your dormant company, consider applying for voluntary strike-off rather than allowing annual returns to accumulate unpaid.

## Working with Your Accountant

Most Irish companies engage an accountant to prepare and file their CRO annual return. Your accountant will:

- Track your ARD and filing deadline
- Prepare or review the B1 form
- Prepare the financial statements in the correct format
- File through CORE on your behalf as presenter
- Ensure digital signatures are completed on time

The cost of having your accountant handle the annual return is typically included in their annual compliance fee. Given the consequences of late filing, particularly the loss of audit exemption, professional support is strongly recommended.
