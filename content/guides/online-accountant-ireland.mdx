Online accountants have transformed the accounting profession in Ireland. Where once you needed to visit an office in your town with a folder of receipts, you can now have your accounts prepared, tax returns filed, and business advice delivered entirely through digital channels. Video calls, cloud accounting software, and electronic document sharing mean that physical proximity to your accountant is no longer necessary for most small businesses.

But online accounting is not right for every situation. Some businesses benefit from face-to-face meetings, local market knowledge, and the kind of relationship that comes from sitting across a desk from someone who knows your community. This guide compares online and local accountants in Ireland, explains the practical differences in cost, service, and convenience, and helps you decide which model works best for your business.

## What Online Accountants Offer

Online accountants provide the same core services as traditional high-street practices, but deliver them through digital channels. The typical online accounting package in Ireland includes:

**Annual accounts and tax returns.** Preparation and filing of your Form 11 (sole traders) or CT1 and company accounts (limited companies) with Revenue and the CRO. Documents are shared digitally, signed electronically where possible, and filed through ROS.

**Bookkeeping.** Many online accountants include basic bookkeeping in their packages, using cloud platforms like Xero, Sage Business Cloud, or QuickBooks Online. They access your accounting data directly through the platform, reconcile transactions, and categorise expenses remotely.

**VAT returns.** Preparation and filing of your VAT3 returns based on the data in your cloud accounting software. Because they can see your records in real time, online accountants can often prepare VAT returns more quickly than traditional firms that wait for you to deliver paper records.

**Payroll.** Processing wages, calculating PRSI, USC, and income tax deductions, and submitting payroll reports to Revenue through PAYE Modernisation. Payroll is inherently digital now (Revenue requires real-time reporting), so this service works identically whether your accountant is local or online.

**Tax planning and advisory.** Video calls and screen sharing replace in-person meetings for tax planning discussions, year-end reviews, and business advisory conversations. The quality of advice depends on the accountant's expertise, not their physical location.

**Company formation and registration.** Setting up a limited company through the CRO, registering for taxes with Revenue, and establishing your company's accounting systems. All of this is done digitally, and many online accountants offer company formation packages that bundle setup with the first year of accounting services.

## Advantages of Online Accountants

### Lower Costs

Online accountants typically charge 20 to 40 per cent less than equivalent traditional firms. They achieve this through lower overheads (no expensive office in a town centre, no reception staff, no physical filing systems) and greater efficiency (cloud software automates much of the data processing).

Typical online accountant pricing in Ireland:

- **Sole trader package:** EUR 400 to EUR 1,000 per year (accounts and Form 11)
- **Limited company package:** EUR 1,000 to EUR 2,500 per year (accounts, CT1, CRO annual return)
- **Monthly bookkeeping add-on:** EUR 100 to EUR 300 per month

Compare this with traditional firm pricing of EUR 500 to EUR 1,500 for sole traders and EUR 1,500 to EUR 4,000 for limited companies, and the savings are meaningful for a small business watching its costs.

### Convenience

No office visits means no time spent travelling, parking, or waiting. You share documents through a secure portal or cloud software, sign engagement letters electronically, and have meetings by video call at a time that suits you -- including evenings or early mornings if your accountant offers flexible scheduling.

For business owners who travel frequently, work from home, or simply prefer digital communication, the convenience of online accounting is significant.

### Technology Focus

Online accountants have built their practices around cloud technology. They are typically early adopters of the latest accounting software features, automation tools, and digital workflows. This means:

- Bank feeds are connected and transactions imported automatically
- Receipt capture happens through mobile apps, not physical filing
- Management reports are generated in real time from cloud data
- You can see your financial position at any time by logging into your software

Traditional firms are increasingly adopting these tools too, but online-first practices tend to be further ahead in their use of automation and digital workflows.

### Access to Specialists

Because online accountants are not limited by geography, you can choose a specialist in your industry regardless of where they are based in Ireland. If you run a technology startup in Galway and the best accountant for tech companies is in Dublin, an online relationship eliminates the distance barrier entirely.

This is particularly valuable for businesses with niche requirements: R&D tax credits, EIIS scheme administration, international trade, film production tax credits, or agricultural reliefs. The best specialist may not be in your local area.

## Disadvantages of Online Accountants

### Less Personal Relationship

For some business owners, the personal relationship with their accountant matters. They value the ability to walk into an office, sit down, and have a conversation. They appreciate an accountant who knows their family, understands their community, and has watched their business grow over the years.

Online relationships can be warm and effective, but they are different. Video calls are not the same as face-to-face meetings. The informal exchange of ideas that happens when you bump into your accountant in town does not happen with an online provider.

### Limited Local Knowledge

A local accountant who works with dozens of businesses in your area understands the local business environment: commercial property values, local authority rates, regional grant programmes, and the particular challenges of your location. An online accountant based in another part of Ireland may not have this granular local knowledge.

This matters less for service-based businesses that operate nationally or online, but it can be relevant for businesses tied to their physical location -- retail, hospitality, construction, farming, and professional practices that serve a local community.

### Meeting Limitations

While video calls work well for routine discussions, some situations benefit from face-to-face meetings:

- **Revenue audits.** If Revenue visits your premises, having your accountant physically present is valuable. An online accountant can attend by video link in some circumstances, but an in-person presence is generally more effective.
- **Complex transactions.** Buying a property, restructuring your business, or selling your company involves detailed discussions where being in the same room, reviewing documents together, and reading body language adds value.
- **Sensitive conversations.** Discussing business difficulties, personal financial problems, or partnership disputes is often easier face-to-face.

Most online accountants will arrange in-person meetings for significant matters, but this may involve travel charges or may not be practical depending on location.

### Trust and Verification

It can be harder to assess an online accountant's practice before engaging. You cannot visit their office, see how they work, or get a sense of their professionalism from their premises. Due diligence becomes more important: check qualifications, read reviews, ask for references, and verify their professional indemnity insurance.

The risk of encountering an unqualified practitioner is the same online and offline (since anyone can call themselves an accountant in Ireland for non-audit work), but the physical separation may make it easier for less scrupulous operators to present themselves credibly.

## Irish-Specific Considerations

Several Ireland-specific factors are worth considering when choosing between online and local:

### ROS Access

Both online and local accountants can access your Revenue record through ROS using a tax agent link. This is a digital process regardless of the accountant's location. Your online accountant can file all the same returns and access all the same information as a local one.

### Company Secretarial

CRO filings (annual returns, changes of directors, changes of registered office) are all done online through the CRO's CORE system. An online accountant handles these filings exactly as a local accountant would.

### Registered Office

Every Irish company must have a registered office address. If you operate from home and do not want your home address on the public CRO register, some local accountants offer their office as your registered office. Online accountants may not offer this, though some provide a registered office service through partner arrangements.

### Tax Agent Identification Number (TAIN)

Your accountant's TAIN must be linked to your Revenue record for them to file returns on your behalf. This process is identical for online and local accountants and is managed through ROS.

### Revenue Audits

Revenue audits are typically conducted at your business premises or your accountant's office. If Revenue requests an office-based meeting, an online accountant would need to travel or arrange an alternative venue. Most Revenue correspondence and aspect queries can be handled entirely by written communication, but for full audits, physical attendance is standard practice.

## When Online Works Well

Online accounting is an excellent choice when:

- **Your business is digital or service-based** with no need for local market knowledge
- **Your transactions are straightforward** and manageable through cloud software
- **You are comfortable with technology** and prefer digital communication
- **Cost is a significant factor** and you want to minimise professional fees
- **You need a specialist** who is not available in your local area
- **You work remotely or travel frequently** and in-person meetings are impractical
- **Your compliance needs are standard** (annual accounts, tax returns, VAT, payroll)

## When Local Is Better

A local accountant may be the better choice when:

- **Your business is complex** with multiple entities, property holdings, or international operations
- **You value face-to-face relationships** and regular in-person meetings
- **Your industry requires local knowledge** (farming, hospitality, local retail, construction)
- **You anticipate Revenue audits or investigations** and want your accountant physically present
- **You need registered office services** or want your accountant to attend local meetings on your behalf
- **Your business is going through significant change** (restructuring, sale, partnership disputes) where in-person advice is more effective

## The Hybrid Model

Many accountants now offer a hybrid model: primarily online, with the option of occasional face-to-face meetings. This is increasingly the standard approach in Ireland and may be the best of both worlds.

A hybrid accountant typically:

- Delivers day-to-day services online (bookkeeping, VAT returns, payroll, correspondence)
- Conducts annual review and tax planning meetings in person (or by video, your choice)
- Attends Revenue audits in person when required
- Provides face-to-face meetings for significant decisions or complex matters

When evaluating accountants, ask whether they offer this flexibility rather than assuming they are strictly online or strictly office-based.

## How to Choose an Online Accountant

If you decide an online accountant is right for your business, follow this process:

1. **Check qualifications.** Verify membership of CAI, CPA Ireland, ACCA, or CIMA on the relevant body's website. This is non-negotiable.

2. **Review their technology stack.** Ensure they use the same cloud accounting platform you use (or are willing to migrate to their preferred platform). Xero, Sage, and QuickBooks are the most common in Ireland.

3. **Understand the service package.** Get a detailed list of what is included in the quoted fee. Ask specifically about VAT returns, payroll, management accounts, phone/email queries, and CRO filings.

4. **Ask about response times.** Online relationships depend on communication. What is their guaranteed response time for emails? Can you reach someone by phone when needed? Is there an out-of-hours service for urgent queries?

5. **Read reviews and ask for references.** Check Google reviews, Trustpilot, and ask for references from existing clients, ideally businesses similar to yours.

6. **Test the onboarding process.** The quality of the onboarding experience tells you a lot about how the ongoing relationship will work. If the setup is disorganised, the service probably will be too.

7. **Confirm professional indemnity insurance.** All practising accountants should carry professional indemnity insurance. Ask for confirmation and the level of cover.
