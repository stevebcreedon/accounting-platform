Switching from sole trader to limited company in Ireland involves incorporating a new company with the CRO, registering it for tax with Revenue, transferring your business assets and contracts to the new company, and ceasing your sole trader tax registration. The process typically takes 4 to 8 weeks from start to finish, and most accountants recommend making the switch at the start of a tax year for cleaner accounting.

The decision to incorporate is one of the most significant a small business owner in Ireland will make. It changes your tax treatment, your liability exposure, your compliance obligations, and how you interact with Revenue. This guide explains when the switch makes financial sense, how to do it step by step, and the common pitfalls that catch business owners off guard.

## When to Consider Switching

There is no single threshold that makes incorporation the right decision for every business. However, several indicators suggest it may be time to move from sole trader to limited company:

### Income Level

The most common trigger for incorporation is income. As a sole trader, your business profits are taxed at your personal marginal rate, which can reach 52 per cent (40% income tax + 8% USC + 4% PRSI) on income above approximately EUR 70,000.

A limited company pays corporation tax at 12.5 per cent on its trading profits. The tax saving is most significant when you can retain profits within the company rather than extracting everything as personal income.

**General guideline:** When your annual profits consistently exceed EUR 50,000 and you can leave at least EUR 15,000 to EUR 20,000 in the company each year, the tax savings from the lower corporation tax rate typically outweigh the higher compliance costs of running a company.

However, this is a guideline, not a rule. The exact crossover point depends on your personal tax credits, your spouse's income, your pension contributions, and other factors. Always model the numbers with your accountant before making the decision.

### Liability Concerns

As a sole trader, you have unlimited personal liability. Every debt, every claim, every legal obligation of the business is yours personally. If you are:

- Taking on larger contracts with significant financial exposure
- Hiring employees (which creates additional liability)
- Entering an industry with higher risk of professional negligence claims
- Building up personal assets that you want to protect (family home, savings, investments)

A limited company provides a legal barrier between your business obligations and your personal assets. While this protection is not absolute (directors can be liable for reckless trading, and banks may require personal guarantees), it significantly reduces your personal exposure.

### Business Growth and Credibility

Certain business opportunities are only available to limited companies:

- **Enterprise Ireland and LEO grants** often require applicants to be incorporated
- **Some large clients and government contracts** require suppliers to be limited companies
- **Investors and venture capital** can only invest in a company structure (you cannot sell shares in a sole trader business)
- **Professional credibility** -- in some industries, operating as a limited company signals permanence and seriousness

### Pension Planning

Company pension contributions offer significant tax advantages. A limited company can make employer pension contributions on behalf of a director that are:

- Fully deductible for the company (reducing corporation tax)
- Not treated as a benefit-in-kind for the director (no personal tax)
- Subject to Revenue's funding limits, but these are generous (age-related limits range from 15% to 40% of remuneration, with a maximum annual earnings cap)

As a sole trader, your pension contributions are deductible from your personal income, but the available relief is generally less flexible and the contribution limits are based on your net relevant earnings.

## The Switch: Step by Step

### Step 1: Get Professional Advice

Before starting the process, engage your accountant to model the financial implications. They should prepare:

- A tax comparison showing your projected tax liability as a sole trader vs a limited company over the next 3 to 5 years
- An analysis of the costs of running a company (higher accountant fees, company secretary, CRO filings)
- Advice on the optimal timing for the switch (usually the start of a tax year or a natural business year end)
- Guidance on how to transfer assets from your sole trader business to the company

### Step 2: Incorporate the Company

Register a new limited company with the CRO using the Form A1. This process is covered in detail in our guide on setting up a limited company in Ireland, but the key steps are:

- Choose a company name and check availability on CORE
- Appoint directors and a company secretary
- Prepare the company constitution
- File the Form A1 online (EUR 50 fee)
- Await CRO processing (approximately 5 business days)

Many business owners choose a company name that reflects their existing trading name. For example, if you traded as "Murphy Consulting" as a sole trader, you might incorporate as "Murphy Consulting Limited."

### Step 3: Register the Company for Tax

Once you receive the Certificate of Incorporation, register the company with Revenue using the TR2 form. Register for:

- **Corporation tax** (mandatory)
- **PAYE/PRSI as an employer** (mandatory -- you will be a director/employee)
- **VAT** (if your turnover exceeds the threshold, or voluntarily)
- **RCT** (if applicable, mainly construction)

Apply for a ROS digital certificate for the new company to access Revenue's online services. This takes approximately 5 to 7 working days.

### Step 4: Transfer Business Assets

The transfer of assets from your sole trader business to the new company needs careful handling. Common assets to transfer include:

**Physical assets (equipment, vehicles, stock):**
- The company purchases the assets from you at their market value
- You may have a capital gains tax (CGT) liability on any gain above the original cost
- The company can claim capital allowances (tax depreciation) on the assets purchased
- If the assets have depreciated below cost, there is no CGT liability

**Goodwill and intellectual property:**
- If your business has goodwill (brand value, customer relationships, reputation), this can be transferred to the company
- The transfer of goodwill may trigger a CGT liability at 33 per cent on the gain
- The company can amortise certain intangible assets for tax purposes

**Debtors and creditors:**
- Outstanding invoices (debtors) can be assigned to the company
- The company assumes responsibility for outstanding bills (creditors)
- Ensure all customers and suppliers are notified of the change

**Contracts and agreements:**
- Employment contracts, leases, supplier agreements, and client contracts need to be novated (formally transferred) to the company
- Some contracts may require the consent of the other party for transfer
- Insurance policies need to be transferred or new policies taken out in the company's name

**VAT considerations:**
- If you transfer a business as a going concern to a company, the transfer may be outside the scope of VAT under the transfer of business relief
- Specific conditions must be met for this relief to apply
- Your accountant should confirm whether the relief applies to your situation

### Step 5: Close Your Sole Trader Tax Registration

Once the company is trading, you need to cease your sole trader tax registration with Revenue:

- File a final Form 11 covering the period from the start of the tax year to the date of cessation
- Pay any outstanding income tax, USC, and PRSI
- Deregister from VAT (if the company will register separately)
- Notify Revenue that you have ceased trading as a sole trader

Your accountant can handle these filings. The key is to ensure there is no gap and no overlap between your sole trader and company tax registrations for the same business activities.

### Step 6: Open a Company Bank Account

The company needs its own bank account. You cannot use your personal bank account or your sole trader business account for company transactions. The company's bank account must be in the company's name, and all business income and expenses must flow through it.

Apply for a business bank account as soon as possible after incorporation, as the process can take 2 to 6 weeks.

### Step 7: Set Up Payroll

As a director of the company, you will likely draw a salary. This means the company must operate PAYE/PRSI payroll, withholding income tax, USC, and employee PRSI from your salary and paying employer PRSI.

Your accountant or a payroll service provider can set this up. Revenue's payroll reporting requirements are now in real-time under the Enhanced Reporting Requirements, so each time you are paid, the company must report the details to Revenue electronically.

## Tax Implications of the Switch

### Final Sole Trader Tax Year

Your final year as a sole trader requires a cessation return. The rules for your final year include:

- You are assessed on actual profits from 1 January to the date of cessation
- Revenue may revise assessments for the penultimate and pre-penultimate years under the cessation rules
- Any unused trading losses from your sole trader business cannot be transferred to the company (they remain with you personally, potentially available against other personal income)

### Capital Gains Tax on Asset Transfer

Transferring assets at above their tax base cost triggers a capital gains tax (CGT) liability at 33 per cent. However, several reliefs may be available:

- **Retirement relief (Section 598/599 TCA):** Available if you are 55 or over and have owned the business for at least 10 years. This can exempt gains up to EUR 750,000 (if transferring to a child) or EUR 500,000 (transferring to anyone else).
- **Revised entrepreneur relief (Section 597AA TCA):** A reduced CGT rate of 10 per cent applies to qualifying business disposals up to a lifetime limit of EUR 1 million.

These reliefs have specific conditions and should be discussed with your accountant well before the transfer.

### Company Tax Treatment Going Forward

Once operating as a limited company:

- Trading profits are subject to corporation tax at 12.5 per cent
- Your salary as a director is deductible for the company but subject to personal tax through PAYE
- Dividends paid to you are subject to income tax at your marginal rate plus USC
- Company pension contributions are deductible and tax-efficient
- The close company surcharge may apply if profits are retained beyond 18 months without distribution

## Common Pitfalls

**Switching too early.** If your income is below EUR 40,000 to EUR 50,000, the higher compliance costs of a limited company may outweigh the tax savings. Model the numbers before committing.

**Switching too late.** If your income has been above EUR 80,000 for several years and you have been paying 52 per cent marginal tax on retained profits that could have been taxed at 12.5 per cent, you have been leaving significant money on the table. Review annually.

**Not transferring assets properly.** Informally moving assets from your sole trader business to the company without proper documentation can create tax issues. Each transfer should be documented with a transfer agreement at market value.

**Forgetting to cease sole trader registration.** If you do not formally notify Revenue that your sole trader business has ceased, they may continue to expect sole trader tax returns, leading to estimated assessments and surcharges.

**Using personal accounts for company transactions.** Every business transaction must go through the company bank account. Mixing personal and company finances undermines the separation of the company as a legal entity and can compromise your limited liability protection.

**Ignoring the annual return date.** The CRO sets an Annual Return Date (ARD) 6 months after incorporation. Missing this deadline triggers the loss of audit exemption. Put it in your calendar on day one.

**Not setting up payroll from the start.** If you draw any income from the company, even informally, PAYE/PRSI obligations apply. Operating without payroll registration can lead to penalties and interest from Revenue.

**Overlooking stamp duty.** The transfer of certain assets (particularly property and goodwill) to the company may attract stamp duty. The rates are 1 per cent on amounts up to EUR 1 million and 2 per cent on amounts above that for non-residential property.

## Timeline for the Switch

| Step | Timeframe |
|------|-----------|
| Accountant consultation and tax modelling | 1-2 weeks |
| CRO incorporation | 1-2 weeks |
| Revenue registration (TR2 and ROS certificate) | 1-2 weeks |
| Bank account opening | 2-6 weeks |

| Payroll setup | 1 week |

The most common approach is to plan the switch 2 to 3 months before the intended start date. Many accountants recommend switching at the start of a calendar year (1 January) for cleaner accounting, but this is not a strict requirement.
