If you sell goods or services online from Ireland, you are running a taxable business and must comply with Irish tax law. Revenue treats e-commerce income the same as any other business income. You must register for tax, keep proper records, file returns, and pay income tax (or corporation tax if you operate through a company) on your profits. This guide covers the specific accounting and tax issues that Irish e-commerce businesses face, from VAT on online sales and the EU One-Stop Shop scheme to managing multi-platform revenue and customs obligations.

The e-commerce landscape creates unique accounting challenges. You may sell through your own website, through marketplaces like Amazon, Etsy, or eBay, or through a combination of channels. Each platform has different fee structures, payment timings, and reporting formats. Getting your bookkeeping right from the start saves significant time and money when tax return season arrives.

## Business Structure for E-Commerce

The first decision is your business structure. Most small e-commerce businesses in Ireland start as sole traders. This is the simplest option: you trade in your own name, register with Revenue using a TR1 form, and pay income tax on your profits.

As your business grows, a limited company may become more appropriate. The 12.5 per cent corporation tax rate on trading profits is significantly lower than the higher rate of income tax at 40 per cent. A company also provides limited liability, which is important if you sell physical products that could potentially cause harm or if you hold significant stock.

The decision depends on your expected profits, your personal tax position, and whether you plan to reinvest profits in the business. If you are consistently earning over EUR 80,000 in annual profits, a limited company is usually more tax-efficient. Below that level, the administrative costs and compliance burden of a company may outweigh the tax savings.

## VAT on B2C Online Sales in Ireland

If you sell goods or services to consumers in Ireland and your turnover exceeds the VAT registration threshold (EUR 80,000 for goods, EUR 40,000 for services), you must register for VAT and charge the appropriate rate on your sales.

For most physical goods sold online, the standard VAT rate of 23 per cent applies. Some categories qualify for reduced rates: children's clothing and footwear are zero-rated, books are zero-rated, and certain food items are zero-rated.

When selling to Irish consumers through your own website, you are responsible for charging, collecting, and remitting VAT. Your prices should clearly indicate whether they include or exclude VAT. For B2C sales, it is standard practice in Ireland to display VAT-inclusive prices.

## EU One-Stop Shop (OSS) Scheme

The EU One-Stop Shop scheme simplifies VAT compliance for businesses selling goods to consumers in other EU member states. Before the OSS, you would have needed to register for VAT in every EU country where your sales exceeded the local distance selling threshold. The OSS consolidates this into a single registration and return.

### How the OSS Works

Under the OSS, you register in Ireland (your member state of establishment) and file a single quarterly OSS return covering all your B2C sales to consumers across the EU. You charge the VAT rate of the consumer's country, collect it, and remit it to Irish Revenue through the OSS return. Revenue then distributes the VAT to the relevant member states.

### When You Must Use the OSS

The OSS becomes relevant when your total cross-border B2C sales to EU consumers exceed EUR 10,000 per calendar year. Below this threshold, you can continue to charge Irish VAT on all EU sales. Once you exceed EUR 10,000, you must either register for the OSS or register for VAT individually in each member state where you have customers.

For most Irish e-commerce businesses selling across the EU, the OSS is the obvious choice. It eliminates the need for multiple VAT registrations and returns.

### OSS Registration

Register for the OSS through ROS. Revenue provides an online portal for filing quarterly OSS returns. The return periods are Q1 (January to March), Q2 (April to June), Q3 (July to September), and Q4 (October to December). Returns and payments are due by the end of the month following each quarter.

## Import One-Stop Shop (IOSS)

The Import One-Stop Shop applies to goods imported into the EU from non-EU countries with a value not exceeding EUR 150. If you sell goods directly to EU consumers and those goods are shipped from outside the EU (for example, from a supplier in China), the IOSS allows you to charge and collect VAT at the point of sale rather than at the point of import.

Without the IOSS, your customers would be charged VAT and potentially customs handling fees when the goods arrive in their country, creating a poor customer experience. The IOSS eliminates this by allowing you to collect the VAT upfront.

To use the IOSS, you register through Revenue and file monthly returns. The IOSS only applies to consignments valued at EUR 150 or less. For higher-value goods, standard import VAT and customs procedures apply.

## Marketplace Facilitator Rules

Major online marketplaces such as Amazon, Etsy, and eBay are considered deemed suppliers for VAT purposes in certain circumstances. This means the marketplace, not you, is responsible for charging and collecting VAT on the sale.

The marketplace is deemed the supplier when:

- A non-EU seller sells goods to EU consumers through the marketplace, regardless of the value
- Goods are imported into the EU from non-EU countries with a consignment value not exceeding EUR 150

If you are an Irish-based seller on Amazon or Etsy selling to Irish and EU consumers, you (not the marketplace) are generally responsible for VAT on your sales. However, if Amazon or another marketplace collects VAT on your behalf under the deemed supplier rules (for example, on goods stored in Amazon's EU warehouses and sold cross-border), you should not also charge VAT, as this would result in double taxation.

Understanding which sales the marketplace accounts for and which you must account for is crucial. Review your platform's VAT reports carefully and reconcile them with your own records.

## Recording Sales from Multiple Platforms

Many e-commerce businesses sell through multiple channels: their own Shopify or WooCommerce website, Amazon, Etsy, eBay, and possibly wholesale channels. Each platform provides its own sales reports, often in different formats with different levels of detail.

To maintain accurate accounts, you need a system that consolidates all sales data into a single set of books. Practical approaches include:

**Use accounting software with platform integrations.** Xero, QuickBooks, and other accounting packages offer integrations with Shopify, Amazon, and other platforms. These integrations automatically import sales data, reducing manual entry and the risk of errors.

**Reconcile platform payouts to bank deposits.** Platforms typically pay out net of their fees. Your bank deposit will not match your gross sales. You need to record the gross sale, the platform fee as an expense, and the net deposit. This is essential for accurate profit reporting.

**Maintain a channel summary.** Create a monthly summary showing gross sales, fees, refunds, and net income by channel. This helps you understand which channels are profitable and identifies any discrepancies.

## Allowable Expenses for E-Commerce

E-commerce businesses can deduct a wide range of expenses from their income:

### Platform and Transaction Fees

Fees charged by Amazon, Etsy, Shopify, Stripe, PayPal, and other platforms and payment processors are fully deductible. These include monthly subscription fees, per-transaction commission, listing fees, and payment processing charges.

### Shipping and Packaging

Postage, courier charges, packaging materials, labels, tape, and bubble wrap are all deductible. If you use a fulfilment service, those fees are deductible too.

### Cost of Goods Sold

The cost of purchasing or manufacturing the products you sell is deductible. This includes raw materials, finished goods purchased for resale, and any import duty or customs charges paid on goods brought into Ireland.

### Advertising and Marketing

Digital advertising costs (Google Ads, Facebook Ads, Instagram promotions), SEO services, content creation, photography, and influencer fees are all deductible business expenses.

### Software and Technology

E-commerce platform subscriptions (Shopify, WooCommerce hosting), email marketing tools, inventory management software, design tools, and any other software used in the business are deductible.

### Home Office and Storage

If you work from home and store stock at home, you can claim a proportion of your household costs. If you rent a separate warehouse or storage unit, the full cost is deductible.

### Professional Services

Accountancy fees, legal fees for terms and conditions or IP protection, and business consultancy are deductible.

## Bookkeeping for High-Volume Transactions

E-commerce businesses can generate hundreds or thousands of transactions per month, making manual bookkeeping impractical. Effective approaches include:

**Daily or weekly summaries.** Rather than recording every individual transaction, record daily or weekly summary totals by channel. This is acceptable to Revenue provided you can produce the underlying detail if requested.

**Automated data feeds.** Use accounting software integrations to automatically import transaction data. Most modern accounting packages can handle high volumes and categorise transactions automatically.

**Regular reconciliation.** Reconcile your accounting records to your bank statements and platform reports at least monthly. Discrepancies are much easier to resolve when they are caught early.

**Stock records.** If you hold inventory, maintain accurate stock records showing opening stock, purchases, sales, and closing stock. This is essential for calculating your cost of goods sold and for your annual accounts.

## Customs and Import Duty

If you import goods from outside the EU (or from Great Britain post-Brexit), you must account for customs duty and import VAT. The rates depend on the type of goods and their country of origin.

Customs duty is a cost of the goods and is added to the cost of goods sold in your accounts. Import VAT at 23 per cent is charged on the value of the goods plus the customs duty. If you are VAT-registered, you can reclaim the import VAT as input VAT on your VAT return, so it is a cash flow consideration rather than a cost.

You will need an EORI number (Economic Operators Registration and Identification) to import goods into Ireland. Apply for one through Revenue's online system. Processing typically takes a few days.

Since Brexit, goods imported from Great Britain (England, Scotland, and Wales) are subject to customs procedures. Northern Ireland remains in the EU customs territory for goods under the Windsor Framework, so goods moving from Northern Ireland to Ireland are not subject to customs checks.

## Quarterly VAT Considerations

E-commerce businesses with seasonal sales patterns should pay close attention to their VAT timing. Your VAT liability is based on the bi-monthly period in which the sale occurs (or in which you issue the invoice), regardless of when you receive payment.

This means that if you have a strong November and December (Black Friday, Christmas), your November/December VAT return (due by 23 January) could be significantly larger than other periods. Plan for this by setting aside VAT funds throughout the peak season.

If your sales are consistently seasonal, you can apply to Revenue for a different VAT return frequency. Some businesses request quarterly or annual filing, which can reduce the administrative burden. However, annual returns require you to make interim direct debit payments, so the cash flow benefit is limited.

## Record Keeping for E-Commerce

Revenue requires you to keep comprehensive records for at least 6 years. For e-commerce businesses, this includes:

- Platform sales reports and payout statements
- Invoices and receipts for all purchases and expenses
- Bank statements for all business accounts
- Import documentation (customs declarations, duty receipts)
- VAT records including OSS returns if applicable
- Stock records and inventory counts
- Correspondence with Revenue

Digital records are acceptable and, for e-commerce businesses, are often the most practical format. Ensure your records are backed up and accessible if Revenue requests an audit.
