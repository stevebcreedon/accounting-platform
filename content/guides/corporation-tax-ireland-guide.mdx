Corporation tax in Ireland is charged at 12.5 per cent on trading profits for most companies, making it one of the lowest headline rates in the OECD. Irish-resident companies pay corporation tax on their worldwide profits, while non-resident companies pay only on profits attributable to an Irish branch or agency.

Every Irish company must file an annual CT1 return with Revenue and pay preliminary tax during the accounting period. Getting these obligations right is critical because late filing and underpayment of preliminary tax result in surcharges, interest, and potential restriction of loss relief. This guide covers everything an Irish company director needs to know about corporation tax, from calculating your liability to claiming reliefs and meeting your deadlines.

## Corporation Tax Rates in Ireland

Ireland operates a multi-rate corporation tax system. The rate that applies to your company depends on the nature of the income.

### The 12.5 Per Cent Trading Rate

The headline 12.5 per cent rate applies to trading income, which is the profit your company earns from carrying on a trade or business in Ireland. This rate has been in place since 2003 and is a cornerstone of Ireland's industrial policy, attracting significant foreign direct investment.

Trading income includes profits from selling goods, providing services, manufacturing, and other active business operations. The key test is whether the income arises from an active trade carried on by the company, as opposed to passive investment returns.

### The 25 Per Cent Passive Income Rate

A higher rate of 25 per cent applies to non-trading income. This includes investment income such as rental income (where the company is not trading as a property rental business), interest income, royalties, and income from foreign trades not carried on through a branch.

The distinction between trading and non-trading income is sometimes contentious. Revenue will examine the nature of the activity, the level of organisation, the regularity of transactions, and other factors to determine whether a particular income stream constitutes a trade.

### The 15 Per Cent Pillar Two Minimum Rate

Since January 2024, Ireland has implemented the OECD Pillar Two global minimum tax. This introduces a 15 per cent effective minimum rate for multinational groups with consolidated revenue exceeding EUR 750 million. For the vast majority of Irish SMEs, this rate is irrelevant, but it is important to be aware of if your company is part of a larger group.

The Pillar Two rules work through a top-up tax mechanism. If the effective tax rate in Ireland falls below 15 per cent for a qualifying group, a top-up tax brings the effective rate to 15 per cent. Ireland implemented the Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax (QDMTT) to ensure this top-up is collected in Ireland rather than by another jurisdiction.

### The 6.25 Per Cent Knowledge Development Box Rate

Ireland offers a reduced rate of 6.25 per cent on qualifying profits arising from certain intellectual property developed in Ireland. This Knowledge Development Box (KDB) applies to patents, copyrighted software, and certain other IP. The relief is calculated using a modified nexus approach, linking the reduced rate to the proportion of qualifying R&D expenditure incurred in Ireland.

## Filing the CT1 Return

Every company registered in Ireland must file an annual corporation tax return, known as the CT1, with Revenue. The CT1 is a comprehensive return that covers not only corporation tax but also information about the company's directors, shareholders, transactions with connected parties, and claims for reliefs and credits.

### What the CT1 Covers

The CT1 return requires detailed information about your company's financial position, including:

- Trading profits and losses for the accounting period
- Non-trading income (investment income, rental income)
- Capital gains and losses
- Charges and deductions claimed
- Details of directors' remuneration and benefits
- Information on connected party transactions
- Claims for reliefs, credits, and exemptions
- Details of distributions (dividends) paid
- Close company surcharge calculations (if applicable)

### Filing Deadline

The CT1 return must be filed within 9 months of the end of the company's accounting period. For a company with a 31 December year-end, the CT1 is due by 23 September of the following year (the ROS extended deadline). The standard deadline is 21 September, with a two-day extension for online filing through ROS.

Late filing of the CT1 attracts a surcharge on your corporation tax liability. The surcharge is 5 per cent (up to EUR 12,695) if the return is filed within 2 months of the deadline, increasing to 10 per cent (up to EUR 25,390) if filed more than 2 months late.

### Filing Through ROS

All CT1 returns must be filed electronically through the Revenue Online Service. Revenue no longer accepts paper CT1 returns. You must have an active ROS digital certificate, and your company's financial statements must be uploaded in iXBRL format as part of the filing.

iXBRL (inline eXtensible Business Reporting Language) is a standardised format for financial statements. Most accounting software and accountancy firms can generate iXBRL-tagged accounts for CT1 filing. The requirement applies to all companies, regardless of size.

## Preliminary Tax

Preliminary tax is the system Revenue uses to collect corporation tax during the accounting period rather than waiting until after the year-end. Every company must pay preliminary tax before the filing deadline, and underpayment results in interest charges.

### When to Pay Preliminary Tax

For companies with accounting periods ending on the last day of a month, preliminary tax is due by the 23rd of the month preceding the month in which the accounting period ends (with ROS extension). For a company with a 31 December year-end, preliminary tax is due by 23 November.

### How Much to Pay

There are two methods for calculating preliminary tax, and you must meet at least one to avoid interest charges:

**Method 1: 90 per cent of the current year's liability.** Pay at least 90 per cent of the corporation tax that will ultimately be due for the current accounting period. This requires you to estimate your profits before the year ends.

**Method 2: 100 per cent of the prior year's liability.** Pay an amount equal to 100 per cent of your corporation tax liability for the preceding accounting period. This is the simpler and safer method for companies with relatively stable profits.

### Small Company Rules

Companies with a corporation tax liability of EUR 200,000 or less for the preceding period can pay their preliminary tax in a single instalment, using either the 90 per cent or 100 per cent method described above.

### Large Company Rules

Companies with a prior year corporation tax liability exceeding EUR 200,000 must pay preliminary tax in two instalments:

- **First instalment:** Due in month 6 of the accounting period (by the 23rd of that month). Must equal at least 50 per cent of the prior year's liability or 45 per cent of the current year's liability.
- **Second instalment:** Due in month 11 of the accounting period (by the 23rd of that month). The total of both instalments must equal at least 90 per cent of the current year's liability.

## Calculating Taxable Profits

Corporation tax is charged on a company's taxable profits, which are calculated by adjusting the company's accounting profit for tax purposes.

### Starting Point: Accounting Profit

Begin with the profit shown in the company's financial statements, prepared in accordance with Irish GAAP or IFRS. This is the profit before taxation as shown in the profit and loss account or income statement.

### Add Back Non-Deductible Expenses

Certain expenses that reduce accounting profit are not deductible for corporation tax purposes. You must add these back when calculating taxable profits. Common add-backs include:

- Entertainment expenses (client entertainment is not deductible)
- Depreciation (replaced by capital allowances for tax purposes)
- Provisions for future liabilities (unless specific and quantifiable)
- Fines, penalties, and legal costs related to offences
- Political donations exceeding EUR 200

### Deduct Capital Allowances

Capital allowances replace accounting depreciation for tax purposes. The standard rate for plant and machinery is 12.5 per cent per annum on a straight-line basis over 8 years. Industrial buildings qualify for a 4 per cent annual allowance over 25 years.

Accelerated capital allowances are available for certain energy-efficient equipment, allowing the full cost to be written off in the first year. This incentivises investment in environmentally sustainable assets.

### Other Adjustments

Additional adjustments include adding back any provisions that are not tax-deductible, adjusting for stock valuations, and accounting for foreign exchange gains and losses. The calculation can be complex for larger companies, and professional advice is recommended.

## Close Company Surcharge

The majority of Irish companies are close companies. A close company is one that is controlled by five or fewer participators (shareholders) or by any number of participators who are directors. Most owner-managed businesses fall within this definition.

Close companies are subject to a surcharge on undistributed investment and estate income. The surcharge rate is 20 per cent on undistributed investment income and 15 per cent on undistributed professional services income.

The surcharge is designed to prevent shareholders from sheltering income within the company to avoid personal income tax. To avoid the surcharge, the company must distribute sufficient dividends within 18 months of the end of the accounting period.

### Avoiding the Close Company Surcharge

The most common approach is to pay dividends to shareholders sufficient to eliminate the undistributed income that would attract the surcharge. The dividend amount needed is calculated by dividing the undistributed income subject to surcharge by a factor that accounts for the dividend withholding tax rate.

Alternatively, directors may take the income as salary, which is deductible for the company and therefore reduces the income available for surcharge. The choice between salary and dividends depends on the personal tax position of the director/shareholder.

## Loss Relief

Companies that incur trading losses can use those losses to reduce their corporation tax liability. Irish tax law provides several mechanisms for loss relief.

### Carry Forward of Trading Losses

Trading losses can be carried forward indefinitely against future trading profits from the same trade. There is no time limit on how long losses can be carried forward, making this a valuable relief for businesses that experience cyclical trading patterns.

### Carry Back of Trading Losses

Trading losses can be carried back to the immediately preceding accounting period of the same length. This allows you to claim a refund of corporation tax already paid. The carry-back is particularly useful for businesses that were previously profitable but have experienced a downturn.

### Group Relief

Companies that are members of a group (75 per cent ownership relationship) can surrender trading losses to other group companies. This allows profitable group members to use the losses of loss-making members, reducing the overall group tax liability.

### Restriction of Loss Relief for Late Filers

If a company files its CT1 return late, the amount of losses that can be carried forward is restricted. The restriction is 50 per cent of the loss for returns filed within 2 months of the deadline, and 100 per cent for returns filed more than 2 months late. This is a significant incentive to file on time.

## R&D Tax Credit

The Research and Development (R&D) tax credit is one of the most valuable reliefs available to Irish companies. It provides a 25 per cent tax credit on qualifying R&D expenditure incurred by a company in carrying on R&D activities.

### Qualifying Activities

R&D activities must seek to achieve scientific or technological advancement and involve the resolution of scientific or technological uncertainty. The activities must be systematic, investigative, and experimental in nature. Routine testing, market research, and quality control do not qualify.

### Calculating the Credit

The credit is calculated as 25 per cent of qualifying expenditure. Qualifying expenditure includes staff costs (salaries, PRSI), materials consumed, and certain overhead costs directly related to R&D. Expenditure on buildings used for R&D is also eligible.

### How the Credit Works

The R&D tax credit is first used to reduce the company's corporation tax liability for the accounting period. Any excess credit can be carried back to the preceding period, used to generate a cash refund, or carried forward. The cash refund is paid in three instalments over 33 months.

## Start-Up Company Relief

New companies that commence trading may be eligible for a three-year exemption from corporation tax. This relief is available to companies that begin a new qualifying trade and meet certain conditions.

### Eligibility Criteria

The company must be a new company (not a successor to an existing trade). The corporation tax liability for each of the first three accounting periods must not exceed EUR 40,000. Where the liability is between EUR 40,000 and EUR 60,000, marginal relief applies.

### How the Relief Works

The relief operates by giving the company a credit against its corporation tax liability equal to the amount of employer PRSI paid by the company, up to a maximum of EUR 40,000 per year. Effectively, if a company's total employer PRSI exceeds its corporation tax liability, no corporation tax is payable.

The relief encourages new businesses to invest in employment, as the benefit is directly linked to employer PRSI contributions.

## Filing Deadlines Summary

Meeting your corporation tax deadlines is essential to avoid surcharges, interest, and restriction of reliefs. Here is a summary of the key dates for a company with a December year-end.

| Obligation | Deadline | ROS Deadline |
|------------|----------|--------------|
| Large company preliminary tax (1st instalment) | 21 June | 23 June |
| Small company preliminary tax | 21 November | 23 November |
| Large company preliminary tax (2nd instalment) | 21 November | 23 November |
| CT1 return filing | 21 September (following year) | 23 September (following year) |
| Balance of tax payment | 21 September (following year) | 23 September (following year) |

For companies with non-December year-ends, adjust these dates accordingly. The preliminary tax deadline is always in the month before the final month of the accounting period, and the CT1 filing deadline is 9 months after the period end.

## Summary

Corporation tax in Ireland centres on the 12.5 per cent trading rate, which applies to the vast majority of Irish SME profits. The 25 per cent rate applies to passive and investment income, while the Pillar Two 15 per cent minimum rate affects only large multinational groups.

Every Irish company must file a CT1 return through ROS within 9 months of its accounting period end, with financial statements uploaded in iXBRL format. Preliminary tax must be paid during the accounting period, with the amount depending on whether your company qualifies as a small or large company for these purposes.

Valuable reliefs are available, including R&D tax credits at 25 per cent, start-up company relief for the first three years, and flexible loss relief provisions. However, late filing can restrict your access to these reliefs and trigger surcharges of up to 10 per cent.

Work with a qualified accountant to ensure your CT1 is accurate, your preliminary tax is sufficient, and you are claiming all available reliefs.
