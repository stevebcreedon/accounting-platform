Allowable business expenses in Ireland are costs you can deduct from your income when calculating your tax liability. The fundamental rule, established in tax law and applied consistently by the Revenue Commissioners, is that an expense must be incurred "wholly and exclusively" for the purposes of your trade or profession to be tax-deductible. Understanding which expenses qualify and how to claim them correctly can significantly reduce your tax bill while keeping you on the right side of Revenue.

This guide covers every category of allowable expense relevant to Irish sole traders and limited companies, explains the "wholly and exclusively" rule, addresses common grey areas like home office expenses and motor costs, and outlines what you need to keep as evidence.

## The Wholly and Exclusively Rule

The "wholly and exclusively" test is the cornerstone of expense deductibility in Ireland. An expense is allowable if it was incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of your trade, profession, or business. This means:

- The expense must have a clear business purpose
- It must not be a personal expense disguised as a business cost
- If an expense has both a personal and business element, only the business portion is deductible

Revenue applies this rule strictly. If you cannot demonstrate that an expense was genuinely for business purposes, it will be disallowed, and you may face penalties if Revenue considers the claim to have been made carelessly or deliberately.

The test does not require the expense to be "necessary" or "reasonable." If you choose to fly business class to a genuine business meeting when economy would have sufficed, the full cost is still allowable because it was wholly and exclusively for business. However, Revenue may question unusually high expenses during an audit.

## Common Allowable Expenses

The following categories of expenses are generally allowable for Irish businesses, subject to the wholly and exclusively rule:

### Rent and Rates

Rent paid for your business premises is fully deductible. This includes rent for offices, shops, workshops, and warehouses. Commercial rates charged by your local authority are also deductible. If you operate from a serviced office or co-working space, the full membership or desk rental fee is allowable.

### Light, Heat, and Utilities

Electricity, gas, heating oil, water charges, and waste collection costs for your business premises are all allowable. If you work from home, you can claim a proportion of these costs (see Home Office Expenses below).

### Insurance

Business insurance premiums are fully deductible. This includes public liability insurance, professional indemnity insurance, employer liability insurance, business property insurance, and stock insurance. Personal insurance policies (life insurance, health insurance, income protection) are not allowable business expenses, though some may qualify for separate tax relief.

### Office Supplies and Equipment

Stationery, printer ink, paper, postage, computer peripherals, and general office supplies are all allowable. Equipment costing less than a few hundred euro is typically treated as a revenue expense and deducted in full in the year of purchase. More expensive equipment (computers, furniture, machinery) is treated as a capital asset and written off over time through capital allowances.

### Professional Fees

Fees paid to your accountant, solicitor, and other professional advisers for business purposes are deductible. This includes accountancy fees for preparing accounts and tax returns, legal fees for commercial contracts, and fees for professional consultancy. However, legal fees for acquiring a capital asset (such as a property) are not revenue expenses and are instead added to the cost of the asset.

### Advertising and Marketing

All genuine advertising and marketing costs are allowable. This includes website development and hosting, online advertising (Google Ads, social media advertising), print advertising, signage, branded merchandise, networking event costs, and trade show fees. Content marketing, SEO services, and email marketing platform subscriptions are also deductible.

### Travel and Subsistence

Business travel expenses are deductible, including:

- **Motor fuel** for business journeys (not commuting between home and a fixed place of work)
- **Public transport fares** for business travel
- **Flights and accommodation** for business trips
- **Subsistence (meals and accommodation)** when travelling overnight for business purposes
- **Parking and tolls** incurred on business journeys

Revenue publishes civil service subsistence rates that provide a benchmark for reasonable claims. Using these rates simplifies record-keeping, as Revenue generally accepts claims at or below these levels without further evidence of the actual expenditure.

The daily subsistence rates for travel within Ireland (as at 2026) are:

- **10 hours or more:** Day rate for meals
- **Overnight:** Night rate covering accommodation and meals

For travel outside Ireland, higher rates apply depending on the country visited.

### Motor Expenses

Motor expenses are one of the most common and most scrutinised categories of business expense in Ireland. If you use a vehicle for business purposes, you can claim the business proportion of the running costs, including fuel, insurance, road tax, servicing, repairs, and tolls.

**Sole traders** claiming actual motor expenses must keep a detailed mileage log showing business and private mileage. The business proportion of total running costs is then deductible. For example, if you drive 20,000 km per year and 12,000 km is for business, 60 per cent of your motor expenses are deductible.

Alternatively, sole traders and employees can claim civil service mileage rates (flat-rate expenses) instead of actual costs. The current civil service mileage rates are:

| Band | Distance | Rate per km |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Band 1 | 0 - 1,500 km | 39.12 cent |
| Band 2 | 1,501 - 5,500 km | 21.22 cent |
| Band 3 | 5,501 - 25,000 km | 11.45 cent |
| Band 4 | 25,001 km and above | 10.31 cent |

**Limited companies** that own the vehicle claim the actual running costs (apportioned for business use) and capital allowances on the vehicle. If the company provides a vehicle to a director or employee for private use, benefit-in-kind (BIK) applies.

### Staff Costs

Salaries, wages, bonuses, commissions, employer PRSI (currently 11.05 per cent), and pension contributions for employees are all deductible. This includes the salary of a sole trader's spouse if they are genuinely employed in the business and the salary is commensurate with the work performed.

Training costs for staff that are relevant to the business are also deductible. This includes courses, conferences, and professional development.

### Telephone and Internet

Business telephone line rental, mobile phone bills, and broadband or internet costs are deductible. If you use a personal phone or home broadband for business, you can claim the business proportion. A 50/50 split is commonly used where a more precise calculation is not practical.

### Bank Charges and Interest

Bank charges, credit card fees, merchant service charges, and interest on business loans and overdrafts are all deductible. Interest on a loan to purchase business premises or equipment qualifies, as does interest on a working capital overdraft.

However, interest on a loan used for personal purposes (even if taken out through the business) is not deductible.

### Subscriptions and Memberships

Subscriptions to trade associations, professional bodies, industry publications, and business networks are deductible. Examples include Chartered Accountants Ireland membership, ISME (Irish Small and Medium Enterprises Association), local chamber of commerce fees, and subscriptions to trade journals.

### Software and Technology

Software subscriptions (accounting software, CRM, project management tools, email marketing platforms), domain name registration, web hosting, and cloud storage are all allowable. SaaS subscriptions are treated as revenue expenses and deducted in full in the year they are paid.

### Bad Debts

If a customer fails to pay an invoice and you have taken reasonable steps to collect the debt, you can write off the amount as a bad debt and deduct it from your taxable income. You must be able to demonstrate that the debt is genuinely irrecoverable. Specific bad debts that have been individually identified and pursued are deductible. General provisions (setting aside a percentage of debtors "just in case") are not allowable for tax purposes.

## Home Office Expenses

Many Irish sole traders and company directors work from home, either full-time or for part of their working week. You can claim a proportion of your home running costs as a business expense, calculated based on the proportion of the home used for business.

The simplest approach is to calculate the proportion by room count. If your home has six rooms and you use one exclusively as an office, one-sixth (approximately 17 per cent) of your home running costs can be claimed. Relevant costs include:

- Rent (if renting) or mortgage interest (not capital repayments)
- Electricity and gas
- Heating oil
- Home insurance (the business proportion)
- Broadband and telephone (the business proportion)
- Cleaning (the business proportion)

Alternatively, Revenue allows employees (not sole traders) to claim a flat-rate deduction of EUR 3.20 per day for days worked from home, covering electricity, heating, and broadband. This simplified approach requires less record-keeping.

**Important consideration for homeowners:** If you claim a home office deduction, be aware of the potential capital gains tax implications when you sell your home. Your principal private residence exemption from capital gains tax could be partially reduced if part of the home was used exclusively for business. Discuss this with your accountant before claiming, as the tax saved on home office expenses may be less than the CGT exposure created.

## Non-Allowable Expenses

Certain expenses are specifically not deductible, regardless of their connection to your business:

### Entertainment

The cost of entertaining clients, customers, or suppliers is not an allowable expense in Ireland. This includes restaurant meals with clients, tickets to sporting events, corporate hospitality, and gifts to clients. This is one of the strictest rules in Irish tax law, and Revenue applies it without exception.

The only exception is staff entertainment, such as a Christmas party for employees. Revenue allows a reasonable staff party cost (generally up to EUR 500 per head per year) without triggering a benefit-in-kind charge.

### Personal Expenses

Clothing (unless it is protective clothing or a uniform with the business logo), personal grooming, commuting costs between your home and a fixed place of work, and personal food and drink are not deductible.

### Fines and Penalties

Speeding fines, parking fines, late filing penalties, and any other fines or penalties are not tax-deductible, even if incurred during business activity.

### Political Donations

Donations to political parties are not allowable business expenses.

### Capital Expenditure (Directly)

The purchase price of capital assets (vehicles, machinery, equipment, property) is not deducted as a revenue expense. Instead, these assets qualify for capital allowances, which spread the tax deduction over the useful life of the asset.

## Capital Allowances

Capital allowances are the tax equivalent of depreciation. When you purchase a capital asset for your business, you cannot deduct the full cost in the year of purchase. Instead, you claim capital allowances over the asset's tax life.

### Plant and Machinery

The standard rate of capital allowances for plant and machinery in Ireland is **12.5 per cent per year over 8 years** on a straight-line basis. This covers equipment, computers, office furniture, vehicles, and other business assets.

For example, if you buy a computer for EUR 2,000, you can claim EUR 250 per year for 8 years as a capital allowance against your taxable profits.

### Motor Vehicles

Capital allowances on motor vehicles are subject to a cost cap. The maximum cost on which allowances can be claimed depends on the vehicle's CO2 emissions category:

- **Category A/B (0-140 g/km):** Full cost up to EUR 24,000
- **Category C (141-155 g/km):** Cost limited to EUR 24,000
- **Category D/E (over 155 g/km):** Reduced limit applies

Electric vehicles benefit from an accelerated capital allowance scheme, allowing the full cost (up to the relevant limit) to be claimed in the year of purchase.

### Industrial Buildings

Industrial buildings qualify for capital allowances at 4 per cent per year over 25 years. This applies to factories, warehouses, and certain other qualifying structures. Commercial offices and retail premises do not qualify.

## Keeping Receipts and Records

Revenue requires you to retain all records supporting your expense claims for a minimum of 6 years. This includes:

- Receipts for all purchases
- Bank statements showing payments
- Mileage logs for motor expense claims
- Home office calculations
- Invoices from suppliers
- Payroll records

Digital records are acceptable. Many Irish business owners use apps like Dext (formerly Receipt Bank), Expensify, or their accounting software's receipt capture feature to photograph and store receipts digitally.

If you are audited and cannot produce evidence for an expense claim, Revenue will disallow the expense. This results in additional tax, interest (at 0.0274 per cent per day), and potential penalties.

## Flat-Rate Expenses

Revenue maintains a list of flat-rate expenses for certain employments and trades. These are fixed deductions that can be claimed without receipts, covering the cost of tools, equipment, or clothing specific to certain occupations.

For example, nurses can claim a flat-rate deduction for uniforms and shoes, while electricians can claim for tools. The amounts vary by occupation and are published on Revenue's website. If you are a sole trader in a qualifying occupation, check whether a flat-rate deduction applies to your trade.

## Revenue's Approach to Mixed-Use Expenses

When an expense has both a personal and a business element, Revenue expects you to apportion the cost and claim only the business proportion. Common examples include:

- A mobile phone used for both business and personal calls (claim the business proportion)
- A car used for business and personal journeys (claim based on mileage records)
- Home broadband used for business and personal use (claim a reasonable proportion)

The key is to be reasonable and to be able to justify your apportionment method if questioned. Revenue does not expect mathematical precision, but it does expect an honest and defensible approach.

- Capital assets like vehicles and equipment are not deducted in full when purchased -- instead, claim capital allowances at 12.5 per cent per year over 8 years for plant and machinery
