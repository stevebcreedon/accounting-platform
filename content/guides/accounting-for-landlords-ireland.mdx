Rental income in Ireland is taxable. If you own a property and receive rent from tenants, you must declare that income to the Revenue Commissioners and pay income tax, USC, and potentially PRSI on the profit. Whether you own one buy-to-let apartment or a portfolio of houses, the tax rules are the same. This guide covers every accounting obligation for Irish landlords, from declaring rental income and claiming allowable expenses to understanding CGT on disposal, rent-a-room relief, and the specific requirements for short-term lettings.

The key principle is straightforward: your taxable rental income is the total rent received minus allowable expenses. Revenue allows you to deduct a wide range of costs associated with owning and maintaining a rental property, but there are important distinctions between what qualifies as an expense and what is treated as a capital improvement.

## How Rental Income Is Taxed in Ireland

Rental income is assessed under Case V of Schedule D. The tax treatment depends on whether you are a PAYE worker with rental income on the side or whether rental property is your primary source of income.

### PAYE Workers with Rental Income

If you are a PAYE employee who also earns rental income, you have two options for declaring it:

- **Form 12 (simplified return):** If your non-PAYE income is under EUR 5,000, you may be able to declare rental income through a simplified Form 12 via myAccount on Revenue's website. Revenue can collect any additional tax due by adjusting your tax credits for the following year.
- **Form 11 (self-assessment):** If your non-PAYE income exceeds EUR 5,000, or if you prefer to self-assess, you must file a Form 11. This gives you access to the full range of deductions and credits available to self-assessed taxpayers.

Once your gross non-PAYE income exceeds EUR 5,000, you are obliged to file a Form 11 and you become a self-assessed taxpayer. This means you must also pay preliminary tax by 31 October each year (or the ROS extended deadline in November).

### Self-Assessed Landlords

If rental property is your primary income source, or if you are already self-assessed for another reason (such as being self-employed), your rental income is included in your annual Form 11 return. You declare all rental properties, the income received from each, and the expenses incurred.

### Tax Rates on Rental Income

Rental income is added to your other income and taxed at your marginal rate. For the 2026 tax year:

- **Income tax:** 20 per cent on the first EUR 42,000 (single person) and 40 per cent on income above that threshold
- **USC:** Rates from 0.5 per cent to 8 per cent depending on total income
- **PRSI:** Class S at 4 per cent applies if you are self-assessed, though PAYE workers already paying Class A may not have an additional PRSI liability on rental income

The effective tax rate on rental income for higher-rate taxpayers can exceed 50 per cent, making it essential to claim every allowable expense.

## Allowable Expenses for Landlords

Revenue allows landlords to deduct the following expenses from rental income, provided they are incurred wholly and exclusively in earning that income:

### Mortgage Interest

You can deduct 100 per cent of the mortgage interest paid on the rental property. This is interest only, not the capital repayment element of your mortgage. The interest deduction is one of the most significant deductions available to landlords.

To qualify for the full interest deduction, you must be registered with the Residential Tenancies Board (RTB). If the property is not registered with the RTB, you lose the right to deduct mortgage interest entirely.

### Repairs and Maintenance

The cost of repairing and maintaining the property in its current condition is deductible. This includes plumbing repairs, electrical work, painting and decorating, replacing broken windows, fixing the roof, servicing the boiler, and general upkeep.

The distinction between repairs and improvements is critical. A repair restores the property to its previous condition. An improvement enhances the property beyond its original state. Replacing a broken window with a like-for-like window is a repair. Replacing single-glazed windows with double-glazed units is an improvement and is not deductible as a revenue expense (though it may qualify for capital allowances in certain circumstances).

### Insurance

Landlord insurance premiums, including building insurance, contents insurance (if the property is furnished), and landlord liability insurance, are fully deductible.

### Management Fees

If you use a letting agent or property management company to manage the property, their fees are deductible. This includes fees for finding tenants, collecting rent, managing maintenance, and handling tenant issues.

### Advertising Costs

The cost of advertising the property for rent, whether through online platforms such as Daft.ie or MyHome.ie, or through newspaper advertisements, is deductible.

### RTB Registration Fee

The Residential Tenancies Board registration fee (currently EUR 40 per tenancy when registered online) is deductible.

### Legal and Professional Fees

Legal fees for drawing up tenancy agreements, debt collection, and resolving disputes are deductible. Accountancy fees for preparing your rental accounts and tax return are also deductible.

### Wear and Tear on Furnished Properties

If you let a furnished property, you can claim a wear and tear allowance of 12.5 per cent per year for 8 years on the cost of furniture, fittings, and appliances provided with the property. This covers items such as beds, sofas, tables, white goods, curtains, and carpets.

### Service Charges

Management company service charges for apartments or managed developments are deductible.

## Non-Allowable Expenses

Certain expenses cannot be deducted from rental income:

- **Capital improvements:** Extensions, renovations that enhance the property, new additions, and improvements are not revenue expenses. They are capital expenditure and may be relevant when calculating CGT on disposal.
- **Your own labour:** If you personally carry out repairs or maintenance, you cannot charge for your own time.
- **Pre-letting vacancy costs:** Mortgage interest during periods when the property is vacant between tenants is deductible, but some costs incurred before the property was first let may not qualify.
- **Personal use:** If you use the property personally for any period, expenses must be apportioned.

## Pre-Letting Expenses

If you incur expenses in preparing a property for letting after it has been vacant for at least 12 months, you can claim a deduction for pre-letting expenses up to a maximum of EUR 5,000 per property. This covers costs such as repairs, cleaning, and advertising incurred in the 12 months before a new tenancy begins. The property must have been vacant for at least 12 consecutive months, and the tenancy must be for a minimum of 12 months.

## RTB Registration Requirement

All private residential tenancies in Ireland must be registered with the Residential Tenancies Board within one month of the commencement of the tenancy. Failure to register is an offence and can result in penalties. More importantly for tax purposes, you cannot claim the mortgage interest deduction if the property is not RTB-registered.

The RTB provides dispute resolution services, maintains a register of tenancies, and enforces landlord and tenant obligations under the Residential Tenancies Acts.

## Local Property Tax

The Local Property Tax (LPT) is payable annually on all residential properties in Ireland. As the property owner, you are liable for LPT. The tax is based on the valuation of the property and is not deductible against rental income for income tax purposes. However, it is a cost of owning the property that you should factor into your overall return calculations.

## Capital Gains Tax on Disposal

When you sell a rental property, you may be liable for Capital Gains Tax (CGT) on any gain. The current CGT rate in Ireland is 33 per cent.

The gain is calculated as the sale price minus the original purchase price, minus allowable costs. Allowable costs include:

- **Acquisition costs:** Legal fees, stamp duty, and surveyor fees paid when you purchased the property
- **Enhancement expenditure:** Capital improvements made during ownership (the items you could not deduct as revenue expenses)
- **Disposal costs:** Estate agent fees, legal fees, and advertising costs for the sale
- **Inflation relief:** Indexation relief applies to properties acquired before 2003

CGT is due by 15 December if the property is sold between 1 January and 30 November, or by 31 January of the following year if sold in December. You must file a CGT return as part of your annual Form 11.

## Rent-a-Room Relief

If you rent a room (or rooms) in your principal private residence, you may qualify for rent-a-room relief. Under this scheme, you can earn up to EUR 14,000 per year from renting a room in your home without paying any tax on that income. The relief covers rent plus any payments for food, laundry, or other services.

Key conditions for rent-a-room relief:

- The property must be your sole or main residence
- You must occupy the property as your home during the letting period
- The total income from the letting must not exceed EUR 14,000
- If total income exceeds EUR 14,000, the entire amount becomes taxable (not just the excess)
- The relief does not apply to short-term holiday lettings (Airbnb-style) where the property is your home

The relief is claimed by including the income on your tax return and claiming the exemption. You do not need to register with the RTB for rent-a-room arrangements.

## Short-Term Lettings and Airbnb

Short-term lettings through platforms such as Airbnb, Booking.com, and VRBO have specific tax and regulatory considerations in Ireland.

From a tax perspective, income from short-term lettings is taxable in the same way as traditional rental income. You declare the income, deduct allowable expenses, and pay tax on the profit. The key difference is that rent-a-room relief does not apply to short-term tourist lettings in your home.

From a regulatory perspective, planning permission may be required for short-term letting in certain areas. In Rent Pressure Zones (RPZs), which include Dublin and most major cities, you must register with your local authority and comply with the Short-Term Letting Regulations 2019. These regulations restrict the number of days you can let your entire home and require reporting to the local authority.

Platforms such as Airbnb are required to report income to Revenue under the DAC7 directive, so you should ensure that you are declaring all short-term letting income on your tax return.

## Non-Resident Landlord Withholding Tax

If you are a non-resident landlord (you live outside Ireland but own rental property in Ireland), your tenant or letting agent is required to withhold 20 per cent of the gross rent and remit it directly to Revenue. This withholding tax is a payment on account of your Irish tax liability.

As a non-resident landlord, you must still file an Irish tax return declaring the rental income and claiming any allowable expenses. The withholding tax already paid is credited against your final tax liability.

If you appoint a collection agent (a person resident in Ireland who collects rent on your behalf), the withholding obligation transfers to the agent. Many non-resident landlords appoint a letting agent as their collection agent for this reason.

## Record Keeping for Landlords

You must retain records of all rental income and expenses for at least 6 years. This includes:

- Tenancy agreements and RTB registration confirmations
- Rent receipts or bank statements showing rent received
- Mortgage interest certificates from your lender
- Receipts for all repairs, maintenance, and other expenses
- Insurance policy documents and premium receipts
- Letting agent statements and invoices
- Capital expenditure receipts (for CGT purposes)

Organised record keeping is essential. Revenue may request these records during an audit, and incomplete records can result in estimated assessments that are typically higher than your actual liability.

## Tax Planning Tips for Landlords

Several strategies can help Irish landlords manage their tax liability:

**Claim every allowable expense.** Many landlords miss deductions, particularly for management fees, insurance, RTB registration, and professional fees. Review the full list of allowable expenses each year.

**Time repairs strategically.** If you have a high-income year, bringing forward planned repairs into that year maximises the tax relief you receive at your marginal rate.

**Consider a property company.** For larger portfolios, holding properties in a limited company can be tax-efficient because rental profits are taxed at the 25 per cent passive income corporation tax rate (not 12.5 per cent, as rental income is not trading income). However, extracting profits from the company triggers additional tax, so the overall benefit depends on your personal circumstances.

**Plan for CGT.** If you are planning to sell, consider the timing of the disposal and whether you can use your annual CGT exemption (EUR 1,270 per person) or offset losses from other disposals.
