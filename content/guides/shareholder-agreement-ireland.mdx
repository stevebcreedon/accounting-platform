A shareholder agreement is a private contract between the shareholders of an Irish company that sets out their rights, obligations, and how they will manage their relationship. Unlike the company constitution, which is a public document filed with the CRO, a shareholder agreement is confidential and is not registered anywhere. It supplements the constitution by covering matters that the constitution either does not address or cannot address in sufficient detail.

Every Irish company with more than one shareholder should have a shareholder agreement. Without one, disputes between shareholders can escalate quickly, and the only recourse is the company's constitution, the default rules of the Companies Act 2014, and ultimately the courts. This guide explains what a shareholder agreement should contain, why it is different from the constitution, and when you need one.

## Why You Need a Shareholder Agreement

Many business owners assume that the company's constitution is sufficient to govern the relationship between shareholders. It is not. Here is why a separate shareholder agreement is essential:

**The constitution is public.** Any person can request a copy of a company's constitution from the CRO by paying a small fee. Commercial terms, profit-sharing arrangements, exit provisions, and restrictive covenants are matters that most shareholders want to keep confidential. A shareholder agreement is a private contract that is not filed with the CRO or any other public body.

**The constitution has legal constraints.** The constitution must comply with the Companies Act 2014 and can only contain provisions that are within the scope of company law. A shareholder agreement, as a private contract, can include a much wider range of provisions, including restrictive covenants, non-compete clauses, and specific commercial arrangements.

**The constitution is harder to amend.** Changing the constitution requires a special resolution passed by at least 75 per cent of the shareholders. A shareholder agreement can be amended by whatever mechanism the parties agree (commonly unanimous consent or a specified majority).

**Default rules may not suit your business.** Without a shareholder agreement, the relationship between shareholders is governed by the constitution and the default provisions of the Companies Act 2014. These defaults may not reflect what the shareholders actually intended. For example, the default position is that shares can be freely transferred (subject to the constitution), which may not be appropriate if the shareholders want to control who can become a member of the company.

**Dispute resolution.** A well-drafted shareholder agreement includes dispute resolution mechanisms (mediation, arbitration) that are faster and cheaper than going to court. Without such provisions, disputes may have to be resolved through expensive and time-consuming litigation.

## Key Clauses to Include

A comprehensive shareholder agreement for an Irish company should address the following areas:

### Share Ownership and Capital

- **Initial shareholdings:** The number and class of shares held by each shareholder
- **Future share issues:** How new shares will be issued and whether existing shareholders have pre-emption rights (the right to subscribe for new shares in proportion to their existing holdings before shares are offered to third parties)
- **Share capital contributions:** Whether shareholders are required to make additional capital contributions and under what circumstances
- **Share valuation methodology:** How shares will be valued for the purposes of transfers, exits, or disputes (common methods include net asset value, earnings multiples, or independent valuation)

### Management and Decision-Making

- **Board composition:** How many directors will there be, and how will they be appointed? In a 50/50 company, each shareholder might have the right to appoint one director. In a company with a majority shareholder, the agreement might give minority shareholders the right to appoint one director as a board observer.
- **Reserved matters:** Certain significant decisions that require unanimous shareholder approval (or a supermajority) rather than a simple board majority. Common reserved matters include:
  - Issuing new shares or changing share capital
  - Borrowing above a specified threshold
  - Selling or acquiring major assets
  - Entering into contracts above a specified value
  - Changing the nature of the business
  - Appointing or removing directors or key employees
  - Declaring dividends
  - Amending the constitution
  - Entering into litigation above a specified value
  - Winding up the company

- **Day-to-day management:** Who is responsible for the day-to-day running of the business, and what decisions can be made without board or shareholder approval
- **Information rights:** What financial and management information shareholders are entitled to receive, and how often

### Pre-emption Rights

Pre-emption rights are one of the most important provisions in a shareholder agreement. They give existing shareholders the right of first refusal before shares can be transferred to a third party. A typical pre-emption clause works as follows:

1. The selling shareholder notifies the other shareholders of their intention to sell, the number of shares, and the proposed price
2. The remaining shareholders have a specified period (commonly 28 to 60 days) to decide whether to purchase the shares at the proposed price
3. If the remaining shareholders do not exercise their pre-emption rights, the selling shareholder can transfer the shares to a third party, but only at a price no lower than the price offered to the existing shareholders
4. If multiple shareholders want to buy, the shares are allocated pro rata to their existing holdings

Pre-emption rights protect shareholders from finding themselves in business with an unknown or unwanted third party.

### Drag-Along and Tag-Along Rights

**Drag-along rights** allow a majority shareholder (or a specified majority, such as 75 per cent) to force minority shareholders to sell their shares on the same terms if the majority shareholder receives a bona fide offer from a third party to purchase the entire company.

This protects the majority shareholder from a minority shareholder blocking a sale that would benefit all shareholders. Without drag-along rights, a minority shareholder could hold out for a higher price or simply refuse to sell, potentially killing the deal.

**Tag-along rights** protect minority shareholders. If a majority shareholder agrees to sell their shares to a third party, tag-along rights give the minority shareholders the right to sell their shares on the same terms and at the same price.

This protects the minority from being left in a company with a new majority shareholder they did not choose, potentially on less favourable terms.

### Deadlock Resolution

Deadlock occurs when the shareholders cannot agree on a matter that requires unanimous or supermajority consent. In a 50/50 company, deadlock is a constant risk because neither shareholder can outvote the other.

Common deadlock resolution mechanisms include:

- **Escalation:** Refer the dispute to a senior representative of each shareholder (if the shareholders are corporate entities) or to a nominated mediator
- **Mediation:** A neutral mediator facilitates discussion and attempts to find a resolution
- **Expert determination:** For financial or valuation disputes, appoint an independent expert (such as an accountant or valuer) whose decision is binding
- **Russian roulette (shotgun clause):** One shareholder offers to buy the other's shares at a specified price. The other shareholder must either accept the offer or buy the first shareholder's shares at the same price. This mechanism forces fair pricing because the offering shareholder does not know whether they will end up buying or selling
- **Texas shoot-out (sealed bid):** Both shareholders submit sealed bids. The higher bidder buys the other shareholder's shares at the higher bid price
- **Winding up:** As a last resort, the agreement may provide for the company to be wound up if deadlock cannot be resolved through other mechanisms

### Dividend Policy

The shareholder agreement should set out the company's approach to dividends:

- Whether the company will pay dividends and how frequently
- The minimum or maximum percentage of profits to be distributed
- Whether dividends will be paid equally to all shareholders or in proportion to shareholdings
- Whether the company will retain a specified amount of profits before distributing dividends
- How the dividend decision will be made (board decision, shareholder resolution, or automatic based on a formula)

In Ireland, dividends are subject to income tax at the shareholder's marginal rate plus USC. The close company surcharge may apply if trading profits are not distributed within 18 months, which makes the dividend policy particularly important for tax planning.

### Restrictive Covenants

To protect the company and the remaining shareholders, the agreement should include restrictive covenants that apply to shareholders who leave the company:

**Non-compete:** The departing shareholder cannot compete with the company's business for a specified period (commonly 12 to 24 months) within a specified geographic area
**Non-solicitation:** The departing shareholder cannot solicit the company's customers, clients, or suppliers for a specified period
**Non-poaching:** The departing shareholder cannot recruit or attempt to recruit the company's employees for a specified period
**Confidentiality:** Ongoing obligation not to disclose the company's confidential information, trade secrets, or business plans

Restrictive covenants must be reasonable to be enforceable under Irish law. Courts will not enforce covenants that are excessively broad in scope, duration, or geographic area. A solicitor experienced in commercial law should draft these provisions.

### Exit Provisions

The agreement should address how a shareholder can exit the company:

- **Voluntary exit:** The procedure for a shareholder who wants to sell their shares and leave the company. This typically triggers the pre-emption rights described above.
- **Death or incapacity:** What happens to a deceased shareholder's shares. The agreement may require the personal representatives to offer the shares to the remaining shareholders, or it may allow the shares to pass to the deceased's estate. Key person insurance is often used to fund the purchase of a deceased shareholder's shares.
- **Compulsory transfer (bad leaver):** Circumstances in which a shareholder can be compelled to transfer their shares. Common triggers include conviction of a criminal offence, bankruptcy, material breach of the shareholder agreement, or dismissal for gross misconduct (if the shareholder is also an employee).
- **Good leaver vs bad leaver:** Different valuation methods for departing shareholders depending on the circumstances. A "good leaver" (retirement, long service, ill health) might receive full market value for their shares, while a "bad leaver" (dismissal, breach, competition) might receive nominal value or a discounted price.

## Shareholder Agreement vs Company Constitution

| Aspect | Shareholder Agreement | Company Constitution |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Legal status | Private contract | Public document |
| Filed with CRO | No | Yes |
| Who can see it | Parties to the agreement only | Anyone (public record) |
| Amendment | As agreed by the parties | Special resolution (75% of shareholders) |
| Enforcement | Contract law (breach of contract) | Company law (court can enforce) |
| Scope | Wide -- any lawful commercial terms | Limited to matters within company law |
| Binding on new shareholders | Only if they sign it | Automatically binds all members |
| Survives insolvency | May not survive liquidation | Continues until company dissolved |

One important limitation of a shareholder agreement: it only binds the shareholders who are party to it. If a new shareholder acquires shares without signing the agreement, they are not bound by its terms. For this reason, most agreements require that any new shareholder must sign a deed of adherence (joining the agreement on the same terms) as a condition of their share transfer being registered.

## When to Get a Shareholder Agreement

The best time to put a shareholder agreement in place is before the company starts trading. At this stage, the relationship between the shareholders is positive, expectations are aligned, and it is much easier to agree on terms that seem hypothetical.

However, it is never too late to put an agreement in place. If your company already has multiple shareholders and no agreement, the risk of a dispute increases with every year that passes. The longer you wait, the more likely it is that circumstances will change (different income levels, different time commitments, personal disagreements) that make it harder to reach consensus on the terms.

**Situations that make a shareholder agreement urgent:**
- Two or more unrelated individuals are shareholders
- 50/50 ownership structures (highest deadlock risk)
- One shareholder is investing capital while another is contributing expertise
- The company is about to raise external investment
- A key employee is being given shares as part of their remuneration
- The shareholders have different expectations about the company's direction

## The Cost of a Shareholder Agreement

A solicitor will typically charge between EUR 1,500 and EUR 5,000 to draft a shareholder agreement for an Irish company, depending on the complexity of the arrangements and the number of shareholders involved.

For a straightforward two-shareholder company with simple equal arrangements, a basic agreement might cost EUR 1,500 to EUR 2,500. For more complex arrangements involving multiple shareholders, different share classes, vesting schedules, or investment terms, costs can be EUR 3,000 to EUR 5,000 or more.

This is a significant investment for a small company, but it is a fraction of the cost of resolving a shareholder dispute through litigation, which can easily run to tens of thousands of euros, and in some cases hundreds of thousands.

Some accountants and company formation agents offer template shareholder agreements at lower cost (EUR 300 to EUR 800). While these can be a useful starting point, they are generic and may not address the specific circumstances of your business. For any arrangement involving significant value or complexity, a solicitor-drafted agreement is strongly recommended.
