Medical professionals in Ireland face unique accounting and tax challenges that differ significantly from other self-employed individuals. Whether you are a GP with a GMS contract, a hospital consultant with both public and private income, a dentist running your own practice, or a locum working across multiple facilities, the tax rules are the same in principle but the practical application varies considerably. This guide covers the specific accounting considerations for Irish medical professionals, from choosing the right business structure through to pension planning and managing multiple income sources.

Revenue is well aware of the earning potential in the medical sector and conducts targeted audits of medical professionals. Maintaining accurate records and claiming all allowable expenses is essential not only for tax efficiency but also for audit readiness.

## Business Structure for Medical Professionals

The choice of business structure depends on your role, income level, and long-term plans.

### Sole Trader

Most GPs, dentists, and consultants operating their own practice start as sole traders. This is the simplest structure: you trade under your own name (or a registered business name), register with Revenue using a TR1 form, and file an annual Form 11 self-assessed tax return. All practice income is taxed as your personal income.

The sole trader structure is appropriate for medical professionals earning under EUR 80,000 to EUR 100,000 per year in practice profits. Above this level, the tax savings from incorporating may justify the additional administration.

### Limited Company

A medical practice can be operated through a limited company. The company pays corporation tax at 12.5 per cent on trading profits, and you draw a salary (subject to PAYE, USC, and PRSI) and potentially dividends. This dual structure can reduce your overall tax bill when profits are high.

However, certain regulatory considerations apply. Medical Council rules may affect how you structure your practice, and some professional indemnity insurers have specific requirements for incorporated practices. Take advice from both your accountant and your professional body before incorporating.

### Associate vs Principal GP

Many GPs work as associates in an existing practice rather than setting up their own. As an associate, you are typically self-employed (not an employee of the principal GP) and receive a share of practice income or a fixed fee per session. You are responsible for your own tax registration, returns, and pension contributions.

As a principal GP, you run the practice, hold the GMS contract (if applicable), employ staff, and bear the overhead costs. Associates typically pay a contribution towards overheads, or the income split already accounts for the principal's higher costs. The accounting for a principal GP is more complex as you must manage payroll, practice expenses, and GMS reconciliation.

## GMS Payments and HSE Contracts

GPs holding GMS (General Medical Services) contracts receive payments from the HSE for providing services to medical card holders and GP visit card holders. These payments are structured as:

### Capitation Payments

Monthly payments based on the number of patients on your panel. Rates vary by patient age, gender, and distance from the practice. Capitation is the primary income stream for GMS GPs and is fully taxable.

### Fee-Per-Item Payments

Additional payments for specific services such as cervical screening, childhood immunisations, chronic disease management, and out-of-hours cover. These are invoiced to or paid automatically by the HSE and must be recorded as income.

### Practice Support Allowances

Payments towards practice costs including nursing support, secretarial support, and practice management. These allowances are taxable income but are typically offset by the corresponding expenses they are intended to cover.

### Special Items of Service

Payments for specific medical examinations, reports, or procedures requested by the HSE or other state bodies. Each payment must be recorded and matched to the service provided.

All GMS and HSE payments must be reconciled with your records. The HSE provides periodic statements of payments made, and you should verify these against your own records of services provided. Discrepancies should be queried promptly.

## Allowable Expenses for Medical Professionals

Medical professionals can claim a wide range of expenses against their practice income:

### Medical Equipment and Supplies

The cost of medical equipment, instruments, consumables, and supplies used in the practice is deductible. Items with a short useful life or low cost can be expensed in full. More expensive items such as dental chairs, imaging equipment, or diagnostic machines are subject to capital allowances at 12.5 per cent per year over 8 years.

### Continuing Professional Development (CPD)

Medical Council registration requires ongoing CPD. The cost of courses, conferences, seminars, medical journals, textbooks, and online learning subscriptions directly related to your professional practice is fully deductible. Travel and accommodation costs for attending relevant conferences (including international conferences) are also deductible.

### Professional Indemnity Insurance

Medical indemnity insurance is a mandatory requirement for practising medical professionals in Ireland. The premium, which can be substantial (particularly for surgeons and obstetricians), is fully deductible against practice income.

### Professional Body Subscriptions

Annual subscriptions to the Medical Council, Irish Medical Organisation (IMO), Irish College of General Practitioners (ICGP), Dental Council, and other relevant professional bodies are deductible.

### Practice Premises

Rent, rates, insurance, utilities, cleaning, and maintenance costs for your practice premises are deductible. If you use part of your home as a consulting room, you can claim a proportion of household costs as a business expense.

### Staff Costs

Salaries and wages paid to practice nurses, receptionists, secretaries, practice managers, and locums are deductible. Employer PRSI contributions are also deductible.

### Locum Fees

If you hire a locum to cover your practice during holidays, illness, or CPD, the locum's fees are deductible. If you work as a locum yourself, the income is taxable and should be included in your annual return.

### Motor Expenses

If you use your car for practice purposes (house calls, nursing home visits, hospital rounds), you can claim motor expenses. You can either claim actual costs (fuel, insurance, maintenance, depreciation) apportioned by business use, or claim Revenue's civil service mileage rates. Keep a mileage log showing the date, destination, purpose, and distance for each business journey.

### Accountancy Fees

Fees paid to your accountant for preparing practice accounts, tax returns, and providing advice are fully deductible.

## VAT for Medical Professionals

Medical services are generally exempt from VAT in Ireland. This means you do not charge VAT on consultations, treatments, diagnoses, and other medical services. However, the exemption does not apply to all services provided by medical professionals:

- **Exempt services:** Medical care, diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of disease or health conditions provided by registered medical practitioners, dentists, and other qualified health professionals
- **Taxable services:** Cosmetic procedures that are not medically necessary, medico-legal reports, occupational health assessments for employers, and certain non-therapeutic services may be subject to VAT at the standard rate of 23 per cent

If you provide a mix of exempt and taxable services, you need to account for VAT on the taxable element. You can reclaim VAT on purchases related to your taxable supplies but not on purchases related to exempt supplies. This requires apportionment, which can be complex.

Most medical professionals providing primarily clinical services will not need to register for VAT. However, if you earn significant income from non-exempt activities (medico-legal work, corporate health services, cosmetic dentistry), you should discuss VAT registration with your accountant.

## Pension Planning for Medical Professionals

Pension planning is a critical area for medical professionals, particularly because many have multiple income sources with different pension arrangements.

### Superannuation for Public Sector Doctors

Hospital consultants and GPs with GMS contracts may be members of the HSE superannuation scheme. Contributions to this scheme are deducted at source and provide a defined benefit pension based on years of service and salary. The scheme provides a retirement lump sum and an annual pension.

If you are a member of a public sector pension scheme, your scope for additional private pension contributions may be limited by the overall contribution cap. However, you can still make Additional Voluntary Contributions (AVCs) to top up your public sector pension.

### Private Pension for Self-Employed Practitioners

Self-employed medical professionals not in a public sector scheme should contribute to a Personal Retirement Savings Account (PRSA) or a Retirement Annuity Contract (RAC). Contributions qualify for tax relief at your marginal rate (up to 40 per cent), subject to age-related percentage limits and a maximum earnings cap of EUR 115,000.

Given the high earnings potential of medical professionals, maximising pension contributions is one of the most effective tax planning strategies available. A GP or dentist earning EUR 150,000 per year who is aged 40 to 49 can contribute up to 25 per cent of their earnings (capped at EUR 115,000), giving a maximum deductible contribution of EUR 28,750. At the 40 per cent marginal rate, the tax relief on this contribution is EUR 11,500.

### Multiple Income Sources

Medical professionals frequently have income from multiple sources: private practice, HSE contracts, hospital sessions, medico-legal work, and teaching. Each source may have different pension arrangements. It is essential to have a coordinated pension strategy that accounts for all income sources and maximises the available tax relief without exceeding the overall limits.

## Managing Multiple Income Streams

Many medical professionals earn income from several different activities:

- Private practice consultations
- GMS/HSE contract payments
- Hospital sessions (as an employee or contractor)
- Medico-legal reports and court appearances
- Teaching and examining
- Research grants

Each income stream must be properly categorised and reported on your tax return. Income from employment (hospital salary, for example) is taxed under PAYE, while self-employed income is reported on your Form 11.

Where you have both PAYE employment and self-employment income, you must file a Form 11 and are subject to self-assessment. Your preliminary tax must account for the total tax liability across all income sources, not just your self-employed income.

Keep separate records for each income stream. This makes tax return preparation simpler and provides clarity if Revenue queries any aspect of your return.

## Locum Doctor Tax Treatment

Locum doctors are generally treated as self-employed for tax purposes. As a locum, you provide services to multiple practices or hospitals, you have flexibility in accepting or declining assignments, and you bear your own expenses.

As a self-employed locum, you must:

- Register with Revenue as a sole trader (TR1 form)
- File an annual Form 11
- Pay preliminary tax
- Keep records of all income and expenses
- Pay Class S PRSI

Your allowable expenses as a locum include travel between assignments, accommodation when working away from home, professional indemnity insurance, CPD costs, and professional subscriptions. You do not have access to employer pension contributions or employer PRSI, so pension planning is your own responsibility.

Some locum agencies may attempt to engage you as an employee. Be clear about your employment status from the outset, as this affects your tax treatment, your PRSI class, and your employment rights. Revenue's Code of Practice on Determining Employment Status provides guidance.

## Revenue Audits of Medical Professionals

Revenue conducts targeted compliance interventions in the medical sector. Common areas of focus include:

- **Completeness of income:** Revenue cross-references your declared income with GMS payment records, hospital payroll data, and third-party reports. All income must be declared.
- **Private cash payments:** Revenue may query the proportion of private consultations paid in cash and compare it to industry norms.
- **Capital allowances:** Claims for expensive medical equipment are scrutinised to ensure the equipment is used for business purposes.
- **Motor expenses:** Revenue auditors frequently challenge motor expense claims, particularly where the business-to-personal use split appears generous.

The best preparation for a Revenue audit is maintaining complete, accurate, and well-organised records throughout the year. If you are notified of an audit, contact your accountant immediately.

<FAQSection>
<FAQItem question="Do doctors need to register for VAT in Ireland?">
Most doctors do not need to register for VAT because medical services are exempt from VAT in Ireland. This exemption covers consultations, diagnoses, treatments, and other clinical services provided by registered medical practitioners. However, if you provide non-exempt services such as cosmetic procedures, medico-legal reports, or corporate health assessments, and your turnover from these taxable services exceeds EUR 40,000, you must register for VAT on that portion of your income.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="How are GMS payments taxed for Irish GPs?">
All GMS payments, including capitation, fee-per-item, practice support allowances, and special items of service, are taxable income. They are included in your self-employed farming income on your annual Form 11. You can deduct all allowable practice expenses from this income to arrive at your taxable profit. The HSE provides payment statements that should be reconciled with your own records.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="Should a GP or dentist incorporate their practice?">
Incorporation can be tax-efficient when practice profits consistently exceed EUR 80,000 to EUR 100,000 per year, as the 12.5 per cent corporation tax rate is lower than the higher personal income tax rate of 40 per cent plus USC and PRSI. However, incorporating a medical practice has regulatory considerations, professional indemnity implications, and higher administrative costs. Consult both your accountant and your professional body before making the decision.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="What expenses can a locum doctor claim in Ireland?">
Self-employed locum doctors can claim travel costs between assignments (mileage or public transport), accommodation when working away from home, professional indemnity insurance, CPD courses and conferences, Medical Council registration, professional body subscriptions, equipment and consumables, and accountancy fees. Keep records and receipts for all expenses for at least 6 years.
</FAQItem>
