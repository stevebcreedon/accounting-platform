Starting a business in Ireland involves a series of financial decisions that affect your tax liability, personal exposure, and growth potential for years to come. The business structure you choose, the way you pay yourself, the tax reliefs you claim, and the accounting systems you set up in your first year create the foundation everything else builds on. Getting these decisions right from the start is significantly cheaper than correcting them later.

An accountant who understands startups is not just someone who files your tax return at year end. They are an advisor who helps you make the structural decisions that determine how much tax you pay, how investors view your business, and how efficiently you can scale. This guide explains what accounting services Irish startups actually need, how to find a startup-friendly accountant, and what to expect in your first year.

## Why Startups Need Specialist Advice

General practice accountants handle tax returns and annual accounts perfectly well for established businesses. But startups face decisions that most established businesses settled years ago:

**Business structure selection.** Should you start as a sole trader or a limited company? The answer depends on your expected income, whether you will seek investment, your risk appetite, and your long-term plans. The wrong choice can cost thousands per year in unnecessary tax.

**Company formation.** If you choose a limited company, the formation process involves decisions about share structure, company constitution, financial year end, and registered office. These decisions have long-term consequences for tax planning, investor readiness, and compliance costs.

**Revenue registrations.** You need to register for the right taxes at the right time. Income tax or corporation tax, PAYE (if you will have employees), and VAT registration (mandatory above the threshold, but voluntary registration may benefit you earlier). An accountant who understands startup timing ensures you do not register too early (creating unnecessary compliance burden) or too late (incurring penalties).

**Pre-trading expenses.** Expenses incurred in the three years before you start trading can be claimed as a tax deduction in your first trading period. Many startups miss this because they do not keep records of costs incurred before the business formally begins. An accountant who understands this rule helps you capture these deductions from day one.

**Startup tax reliefs.** Ireland offers several tax incentives specifically designed to support new businesses. Missing them in your first year means they are gone forever. An accountant who works with startups knows these reliefs and ensures you claim them.

## Key Startup Tax Reliefs in Ireland

An accountant who specialises in startups will ensure you do not miss these critical reliefs:

### Start-Up Relief for New Companies (Section 486C TCA)

New companies that commence trading can qualify for a three-year exemption from corporation tax if their total tax liability does not exceed EUR 40,000 per year. Where the tax liability is between EUR 40,000 and EUR 60,000, marginal relief applies.

This relief applies to the first three years of trading and is linked to the amount of employer PRSI the company pays. In effect, the relief encourages companies to create employment. If your startup is a limited company with employees, this relief can save you tens of thousands in corporation tax over three years.

### Employment and Investment Incentive Scheme (EIIS)

EIIS allows investors to claim income tax relief on investments in qualifying companies. Investors can claim relief at their marginal tax rate (up to 40 per cent) on investments up to EUR 250,000 per year (EUR 500,000 for investments held for more than 4 years). The company can raise up to EUR 15 million through EIIS over its lifetime.

For startups seeking investment, EIIS makes your company significantly more attractive to investors. The tax relief effectively reduces the investor's risk. However, the scheme has detailed conditions regarding qualifying trades, minimum holding periods, and connected-party restrictions. An accountant experienced with EIIS can structure the investment correctly and prepare the compliance documentation.

### Start-Up Refunds for Entrepreneurs (SURE)

SURE (formerly the Seed Capital Scheme) allows individuals who leave employment to start a business to claim a refund of income tax paid in the six years before they set up the company. The refund is based on the amount invested in the new company, up to EUR 100,000 per year for a maximum claim of EUR 700,000.

This can provide a substantial cash injection to fund your startup. If you paid income tax as a PAYE employee for the past six years and are now investing your savings into a new limited company, SURE can refund a significant portion of that tax. Your accountant needs to understand the qualifying conditions, prepare the claim, and submit it to Revenue.

### R&D Tax Credit

The R&D tax credit in Ireland allows companies to claim a 30 per cent tax credit on qualifying research and development expenditure. This is in addition to the normal tax deduction for the expenditure, making the effective benefit worth up to 42.5 per cent of qualifying spend.

For technology startups, this is one of the most valuable reliefs available. Qualifying expenditure includes salaries of staff engaged in R&D, materials consumed in R&D activities, and certain overhead costs. The key is to identify and document R&D activity from day one -- retrospective claims are possible but harder to support.

Your accountant should help you set up time tracking and project documentation systems that capture R&D expenditure as it happens, rather than trying to reconstruct it at year end.

### Key Employee Engagement Programme (KEEP)

KEEP allows qualifying companies to grant share options to employees with favourable tax treatment. When employees exercise KEEP options, they pay capital gains tax (33 per cent) rather than income tax, USC, and PRSI (up to 52 per cent) on the gain. This makes equity compensation more attractive and tax-efficient for both the company and the employee.

KEEP is particularly valuable for startups that cannot compete with established companies on salary but want to attract talent through equity participation. The conditions are specific: the company must be a qualifying SME, the option exercise price must be at least market value at the date of grant, and the options must be exercised between one and ten years after grant.

An accountant who understands KEEP can structure the scheme correctly, determine the market value of shares (which requires a formal valuation), and ensure compliance with the detailed conditions.

## Key Decisions Your Accountant Helps With

### Sole Trader vs Limited Company

This is the first and most consequential decision. The considerations include:

**Tax rates.** Sole traders pay income tax at 20 per cent on the first EUR 42,000 (2025 rate band for a single person) and 40 per cent on income above that, plus USC (up to 8 per cent) and PRSI (4 per cent). Limited companies pay corporation tax at 12.5 per cent on trading profits, but you then pay income tax on salary and dividend withholding tax on distributions.

**Personal liability.** A sole trader is personally liable for all business debts. A limited company provides a legal separation between personal and business assets, though directors can still face personal liability in certain circumstances.

**Investment readiness.** If you plan to raise investment, you need a limited company. Investors buy shares, and the EIIS, SURE, and KEEP reliefs are only available to limited companies.

**Administrative burden.** Limited companies have significantly more compliance obligations (CRO filings, company accounts, corporation tax returns, company secretarial duties) and higher accounting costs.

Your accountant should model the numbers for your specific situation: projected income, planned investment, growth expectations, and personal circumstances. The right answer is different for every startup.

### Financial Year End

For limited companies, choosing the right financial year end affects cash flow, tax planning, and compliance timing. Key considerations:

- A year end that falls shortly before a major seasonal sales period gives you more time to plan
- Corporation tax is due 6 months plus 23 days after your year end (for online filers)
- CRO annual return is due within 56 days of your annual return date
- Aligning your year end with the calendar year (31 December) or tax year simplifies some calculations but may create a deadline cluster

Your accountant advises on the optimal year end based on your business cycle and personal tax position.

### VAT Registration Timing

Voluntary VAT registration before reaching the threshold makes sense if your customers are VAT-registered businesses (they reclaim the VAT you charge) and you have significant VAT-ratable purchases (equipment, software, professional services). It does not make sense if most of your customers are consumers who cannot reclaim VAT, as it effectively increases your prices by the VAT rate.

Your accountant models the VAT position for your specific business to determine the optimal registration timing.

## What to Expect in Your First Year

### Month 1-3: Setup Phase

Your accountant handles:

- Company formation (if applicable) or sole trader registration with Revenue
- Tax registrations (corporation tax/income tax, PAYE, VAT as appropriate)
- Setting up cloud accounting software (Xero, Sage, or QuickBooks)
- Establishing a chart of accounts appropriate for your business
- Advice on business bank account setup
- Review of pre-trading expenses to capture deductions

### Month 4-6: Operations Phase

Your accountant supports:

- Monthly or quarterly bookkeeping review
- First VAT return preparation (if VAT registered)
- Payroll setup and first PAYE submissions (if you have employees)
- R&D tax credit documentation setup (if applicable)
- Cash flow forecasting and financial projections

### Month 7-12: Growth and Compliance Phase

Your accountant provides:

- Ongoing bookkeeping support and review
- VAT return preparation and filing
- Pre-year-end tax planning meeting
- Preliminary tax calculation and payment
- Year-end accounts preparation
- Corporation tax return (CT1) or income tax return (Form 11)
- CRO annual return (for limited companies)
- Review of the first year and planning for year two

## Startup-Specific Fee Structures

Accountants who work with startups often offer pricing that recognises the cash constraints of new businesses:

**Startup packages.** Fixed annual fees that bundle company formation, initial registrations, bookkeeping setup, and first year compliance. Typical range: EUR 1,500 to EUR 3,000 for a limited company's first year.

**Discounted first year.** Some firms offer a reduced fee for the first year with the expectation that fees will increase to standard rates as the business grows. This can be 20 to 40 per cent below their normal rates.

**Monthly payment plans.** Spreading the annual fee into monthly instalments rather than a lump sum helps startup cash flow. Most accountants offering startup services provide this option.

**Equity-linked arrangements.** In rare cases, some accountants accept a small equity stake in exchange for reduced fees. This is uncommon and carries tax implications for both parties, but it happens in the startup ecosystem.

**Pay-as-you-grow pricing.** Fee scales linked to turnover or employee count, starting low and increasing as the business grows.

Be wary of very low startup fees that exclude essential services. A headline fee of EUR 500 that covers only the CT1 and excludes accounts preparation, CRO filing, VAT returns, and payroll is not a startup package -- it is a compliance-only service that leaves gaps.

## How to Find a Startup-Friendly Accountant

Not every accountant understands the startup world. Here is how to find one who does:

**Ask the startup community.** Enterprise Ireland, Local Enterprise Offices, startup incubators, and co-working spaces often have recommended accountant lists. Fellow founders are the best source of referrals.

**Check their client base.** Ask potential accountants what percentage of their clients are startups or early-stage businesses. An accountant whose clients are mostly established sole traders and small companies may not have the startup-specific knowledge you need.

**Test their knowledge.** Ask about EIIS, SURE, KEEP, R&D tax credits, and startup corporation tax relief. If they cannot explain these clearly, they are not a startup specialist.

**Assess their technology.** Startup-friendly accountants use cloud accounting software, offer digital document sharing, and are comfortable with video calls and messaging apps. If they require you to deliver paper records to their office, they are probably not a good fit for a startup.

**Look for proactive advice.** The best startup accountants do not wait for you to ask questions. They proactively identify tax reliefs, flag compliance deadlines, and suggest structural improvements. During your initial meeting, note whether they ask questions about your business plans and growth ambitions, or just ask about your current turnover.

## Equity and Share Structures

One area where startup accounting diverges significantly from general practice is share structures. If your startup involves co-founders, investors, or equity-based employee compensation, your accountant needs to understand:

**Share classes.** Ordinary shares, preference shares, and different classes with different rights (voting, dividend, redemption). The share structure affects control, profit distribution, and exit scenarios.

**Share valuation.** KEEP options require a formal valuation of shares at the date of grant. EIIS investments require shares to be issued at fair market value. Your accountant should either provide valuations or work with a specialist valuer.

**Founder agreements.** While primarily a legal matter, the financial implications of vesting schedules, good leaver/bad leaver provisions, and anti-dilution clauses all need accounting input.

**Share incentive schemes.** Beyond KEEP, there are other ways to incentivise employees with equity. Each has different tax treatment, and the wrong choice can create unexpected tax liabilities for both the company and the employee.

Your accountant works alongside your solicitor on these matters, ensuring the financial and tax implications of share arrangements are properly considered.
