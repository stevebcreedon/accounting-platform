To start a business in Ireland, you need to choose a legal structure (sole trader or limited company), register with the Revenue Commissioners for tax, and depending on your structure, register with the Companies Registration Office (CRO). Most new businesses can be operational within 2 to 4 weeks.

Ireland ranks as one of the best countries in Europe for starting a business, with a straightforward registration process, one of the lowest corporation tax rates in the world at 12.5 per cent, and a strong support ecosystem through Local Enterprise Offices, Enterprise Ireland, and Microfinance Ireland. This guide walks you through every step of getting your business off the ground, from choosing the right structure to meeting your first tax deadlines.

## Step 1: Choose Your Business Structure

The first decision you need to make is how your business will be legally structured. In Ireland, the three most common structures for new businesses are sole trader, limited company, and partnership. Your choice affects your tax obligations, personal liability, compliance requirements, and how much it costs to set up and run your business.

### Sole Trader

A sole trader is the simplest structure. You and your business are the same legal entity. There is no separation between your personal and business assets, which means you are personally liable for all business debts. Setting up costs nothing beyond registering for tax with Revenue.

A sole trader is ideal if you are starting small, testing a business idea, or working as a freelancer or contractor. You file a single Form 11 income tax return each year and pay income tax, USC, and PRSI on your business profits at your personal marginal rates.

### Limited Company

A limited company (LTD) is a separate legal entity from you. It has its own tax registration, bank account, and legal obligations. Your personal liability is limited to the amount you invest in the company. The company pays corporation tax at 12.5 per cent on trading profits.

A limited company is suitable if you expect to earn over EUR 50,000, want liability protection, plan to bring on investors, or need a company structure for grant applications. The setup costs are higher (EUR 300 to EUR 600 through a formation agent) and ongoing compliance is more complex.

### Partnership

A partnership involves two or more people carrying on a business together. Each partner is personally liable for the business debts (joint and several liability). Partners share profits according to the partnership agreement and each files their own Form 11 return.

Partnerships are common in professional services (solicitors, doctors, accountants) but less common for general trading businesses. If you want limited liability with multiple owners, incorporate a limited company instead.

### Which Structure Should You Choose

For most new businesses in Ireland, the decision comes down to sole trader versus limited company. Here is a quick decision guide:

| Factor | Choose Sole Trader | Choose Limited Company |
|---|---|---|
| Expected annual profit | Under EUR 50,000 | Over EUR 50,000 |
| Risk level | Low (services, consulting) | Higher (contracts, employees, stock) |
| Setup speed | Start immediately | 1-2 weeks for CRO registration |
| Compliance cost | EUR 300-800/year accountant | EUR 1,500-3,500/year accountant |
| Liability | Unlimited personal | Limited to investment |
| Investors needed | No | Yes |
| Grant eligibility | Some grants available | More grants available |

You can always start as a sole trader and incorporate a limited company later when your business grows. Many Irish businesses follow this path.

## Step 2: Register with the Revenue Commissioners

Every business in Ireland must register for tax with the Revenue Commissioners. This is a mandatory step regardless of your business structure.

### Sole Trader Tax Registration

As a sole trader, you register using the **TR1 form** (Tax Registration Form for Individuals). You can submit this online through the Revenue Online Service (ROS) at ros.ie. To access ROS, you need a digital certificate, which you can apply for on the ROS website. Revenue posts a verification PIN to your address within 5 to 7 working days.

The TR1 form registers you for:

- **Income tax** (mandatory for all sole traders)
- **VAT** (mandatory if turnover exceeds EUR 80,000 for goods or EUR 40,000 for services; optional below these thresholds)
- **Employer PAYE/PRSI** (mandatory if you hire employees)

You will need your PPS number, business address, a description of your business activity, estimated annual turnover, and your bank details.

### Limited Company Tax Registration

For a limited company, you register using the **TR2 form** (Tax Registration Form for Companies). You will need the company's CRO number, which you receive after registration with the Companies Registration Office.

The TR2 form registers the company for:

- **Corporation tax** (mandatory for all companies)
- **VAT** (mandatory if turnover exceeds the relevant threshold)
- **Employer PAYE/PRSI** (mandatory if the company pays salaries, including director salaries)

Revenue typically processes online registrations within 3 to 5 working days. Paper applications take longer.

## Step 3: Register with the CRO (Limited Companies Only)

If you are setting up a limited company, you must register with the Companies Registration Office (CRO) before you can begin trading. Sole traders do not need CRO registration unless they trade under a business name.

### Company Formation Process

1. **Choose a company name:** Check availability on the CRO website at core.cro.ie. Your name must not be identical or misleadingly similar to an existing company. It must end in "Limited" or "Ltd."

2. **Prepare a company constitution:** This single document sets out the company's regulations, share structure, and objects. Template constitutions are available from the CRO.

3. **Complete the A1 form:** This requires details of the registered office, directors (at least one EEA-resident director required), secretary, shareholders, and share capital.

4. **Submit and pay:** Online filing through CORE costs EUR 50. Standard processing takes 5 to 10 working days. Express processing (EUR 100 additional) takes 2 working days.

5. **Receive your CRO number:** After registration, you receive a certificate of incorporation and your unique CRO number. You need this number to register for tax with Revenue.

Most founders use a company formation agent (EUR 200 to EUR 500 including CRO fees) to handle the paperwork. Many accountancy firms offer this as part of a startup package.

### Business Name Registration (Sole Traders)

If you trade under a name other than your own personal name, you must register the business name with the CRO. For example, if Sarah Murphy trades as "Murphy Consulting," the business name "Murphy Consulting" must be registered.

Registration costs EUR 20 online or EUR 40 by post. This does not give you exclusive rights to the name -- it simply records who trades under it. For trademark protection, apply separately to the Intellectual Property Office of Ireland.

## Step 4: Open a Business Bank Account

While sole traders are not legally required to have a separate business bank account, it is strongly recommended. Mixing personal and business transactions makes bookkeeping difficult, tax preparation more expensive, and Revenue audits more problematic.

Limited companies must have a separate bank account in the company's name.

To open a business bank account in Ireland, you typically need:

- Proof of identity (passport or driving licence) for all directors/owners
- Proof of address (utility bill or bank statement, less than 6 months old)
- Your CRO certificate of incorporation (limited companies)
- A business plan or description of your business activity
- Proof of your business address

The main Irish banks (AIB, Bank of Ireland, Permanent TSB) all offer business accounts. Digital banks like Revolut Business and N26 Business also operate in Ireland and may offer faster account opening. Opening a traditional bank account can take 2 to 4 weeks, while digital banks may process applications in a few days.

Watch out for monthly fees, transaction charges, and minimum balance requirements. Compare at least two or three options before committing.

## Step 5: Get a Tax Clearance Certificate

A tax clearance certificate confirms that your tax affairs are in order with Revenue. While not required to start trading, you will need one for:

- Applying for government grants (LEO, Enterprise Ireland)
- Tendering for public contracts
- Applying for certain licences (liquor, haulage, PSA)
- Claiming certain tax reliefs (e.g., R&D tax credit)

You can apply for tax clearance through ROS. Revenue issues the certificate electronically, usually within 5 working days if all your tax returns are filed and taxes are paid. The certificate is valid for one year and includes a unique tax clearance access number that can be verified by third parties online.

If you are a brand new business with no prior tax history, Revenue will generally issue a first-year tax clearance certificate after processing your TR1 or TR2 registration, provided you have no outstanding personal tax issues.

## Step 6: Register for VAT (If Required)

VAT registration is mandatory if your turnover exceeds EUR 80,000 for goods or EUR 40,000 for services in any 12-month period. Below these thresholds, registration is optional.

You register for VAT as part of your TR1 (sole trader) or TR2 (company) submission. If you initially registered without VAT and later need to add it, you can update your registration through ROS.

Voluntary VAT registration can be worthwhile if you:

- Incur significant VAT on business purchases (equipment, fit-out, stock)
- Sell primarily to other VAT-registered businesses
- Trade with businesses in other EU member states

Once registered, you must charge VAT on your sales, file bi-monthly VAT3 returns through ROS, and maintain detailed VAT records. The main Irish VAT rates are 23 per cent (standard), 13.5 per cent (reduced), 9 per cent (hospitality), and 0 per cent (food, children's clothing, exports).

## Step 7: Register as an Employer (If Hiring)

If you plan to hire employees or, in the case of a limited company, pay yourself a director's salary, you must register as an employer with Revenue for PAYE (Pay As You Earn), PRSI (Pay Related Social Insurance), and USC (Universal Social Charge).

As an employer, you are responsible for:

- Deducting income tax, USC, and employee PRSI from each employee's pay
- Paying employer PRSI (currently 11.05 per cent of gross pay) on top of the employee's salary
- Reporting payroll information to Revenue in real time through the **Enhanced Reporting Requirements** system
- Filing payroll submissions on or before each pay date
- Issuing employees with payslips showing all deductions

Payroll in Ireland operates under real-time reporting, meaning you must submit payroll details to Revenue on or before each pay date, not at the end of the month or year. Most businesses use payroll software (Sage Payroll, BrightPay, Thesaurus Payroll) or outsource payroll to their accountant.

The employer PRSI cost of 11.05 per cent is an additional expense on top of the employee's gross salary. Budget for this when planning your staffing costs.

## Step 8: Arrange Business Insurance

While not all insurance is legally required, certain types of cover are essential or mandatory for Irish businesses:

**Employers' liability insurance** is legally required if you have any employees. It covers claims by employees for workplace injuries or illnesses. Minimum cover of EUR 13 million is typically required.

**Public liability insurance** covers claims by third parties (customers, visitors, members of the public) for injury or property damage caused by your business activities. While not legally mandatory in most cases, it is a practical necessity and is often required by clients, landlords, and event organisers.

**Professional indemnity insurance** is required for regulated professions (accountants, solicitors, architects, engineers) and is strongly recommended for any business providing professional advice or services. It covers claims arising from errors, omissions, or negligence in your professional work.

**Motor insurance** for business use is required if you use a vehicle for business purposes. Personal motor insurance typically does not cover commercial use.

Get quotes from at least three insurance brokers. Cover levels and premiums vary significantly between providers.

## Step 9: Set Up Your Bookkeeping

Every business in Ireland is legally required to keep adequate accounting records. Under the Taxes Consolidation Act 1997, sole traders must maintain records sufficient to enable a true and complete return of income to be made. Under the Companies Act 2014, limited companies must keep "adequate accounting records" that correctly record and explain the company's transactions.

Revenue requires you to retain all business records for at least 6 years.

### What Records to Keep

At minimum, you should maintain:

- All sales invoices issued
- All purchase invoices and receipts
- Bank statements for all business accounts
- Records of all business expenses with supporting receipts
- Mileage logs (if claiming motor expenses)
- Payroll records (if you have employees)
- VAT records (if VAT-registered)

### Bookkeeping Software

For most new businesses, a cloud-based bookkeeping system is the most practical option. Popular choices in Ireland include:

- **Xero:** EUR 15 to EUR 50 per month. Well-established in Ireland, integrates with most Irish banks and accounting firms.
- **Sage Business Cloud:** EUR 12 to EUR 35 per month. Strong Irish market presence with local support.
- **QuickBooks Online:** EUR 12 to EUR 32 per month. Popular internationally, good for businesses trading overseas.
- **Excel / Google Sheets:** Free. Adequate for very simple businesses with few transactions, but lacks automation and becomes unwieldy as volume grows.

Ask your accountant what software they prefer before choosing. Using the same system as your accountant reduces the time (and cost) of year-end accounts preparation.

## Step 10: Find an Accountant

While you can manage your own tax affairs, most Irish business owners engage an accountant from the start. A good accountant helps you:

- Choose the right business structure and tax registration
- Set up your bookkeeping system correctly
- Identify allowable expenses and tax deductions
- File returns on time and avoid penalties
- Plan for tax payments so you are not caught short
- Advise on when to register for VAT, hire employees, or incorporate

Typical accountant fees for new businesses are EUR 300 to EUR 800 per year for sole traders and EUR 1,500 to EUR 3,500 per year for limited companies. Many accountants offer discounted first-year startup packages.

When choosing an accountant, look for:

- Membership of a recognised body (Chartered Accountants Ireland, CPA Ireland, or ACCA)
- Experience with businesses in your industry
- Clear, fixed-fee pricing with a written engagement letter
- Responsive communication (important during your first year when you have many questions)
- Cloud-accounting capability (compatible with your bookkeeping software)

## Grants and Supports for New Businesses

Ireland has a strong support ecosystem for new businesses. The main sources of funding and support are:

### Local Enterprise Office (LEO)

Every county in Ireland has a Local Enterprise Office that provides free mentoring, training, and grants for small businesses with fewer than 10 employees. LEO supports include:

- **Feasibility study grant:** Up to EUR 15,000 towards the cost of researching your business idea
- **Priming grant:** Up to EUR 150,000 for businesses in the first 18 months of trading (for businesses with growth and export potential)
- **Business expansion grant:** Up to EUR 150,000 for established businesses
- **Technical assistance for micro-exporters:** Up to EUR 2,500 for international trade fair participation
- **Free mentoring:** Access to experienced business mentors at no cost
- **Training programmes:** Subsidised courses on finance, marketing, digital skills, and management

LEO grants typically cover 50 per cent of eligible costs and are paid in arrears (you spend first, then claim). You must have a viable business plan and demonstrate that the grant will contribute to job creation or business growth.

### Enterprise Ireland

Enterprise Ireland supports businesses with high growth and export potential. Supports include:

- **Competitive Start Fund:** EUR 50,000 equity investment for innovative startups
- **Startup showcase:** Accelerator programme providing mentoring, networking, and investor access
- **Innovation vouchers:** EUR 5,000 towards working with a research institution on an innovation project
- **Online retail scheme:** Up to EUR 40,000 towards developing your e-commerce capability
- **Going global grants:** Funding for market research and business development in export markets

Enterprise Ireland investments are equity-based, meaning they take a small shareholding in your company in exchange for funding.

### Microfinance Ireland

Microfinance Ireland provides loans from EUR 2,000 to EUR 25,000 for small businesses and sole traders who cannot access bank finance. Loans are unsecured, with fixed interest rates and repayment terms of up to 5 years. Microfinance Ireland is a not-for-profit lender supported by the government.

### Other Supports

- **Revenue's Start Your Own Business relief:** If you were unemployed for at least 12 months, you may qualify for income tax exemption of up to EUR 40,000 per year for the first 2 years of trading.
- **Back to Work Enterprise Allowance:** Allows you to keep a percentage of your social welfare payment for up to 2 years while starting a business.
- **SURE (Startup Refund for Entrepreneurs):** Refund of income tax paid in the previous 6 years (up to EUR 700,000) for founders investing in a new qualifying company.

## Key First-Year Deadlines

Missing deadlines in your first year can result in penalties that cost more than a year of accounting fees. Here are the critical dates:

### Sole Trader Deadlines

| Deadline | What | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 31 October (year 1) | Preliminary tax payment | Pay estimated tax for current year; may be reduced in year 1 |
| 31 October (year 2) | Form 11 + balance of tax | File return for first trading year + pay remaining tax |
| Mid-November (year 2) | Extended deadline (ROS) | Applies only if filing and paying through ROS |

### Limited Company Deadlines

| Deadline | What | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 6 months after incorporation | Annual return date (ARD) | First B1 + accounts due 28 days after this date |
| 9 months after year end | Form CT1 | Corporation tax return for first accounting period |
| Day 21 of month 6 | Preliminary tax (small company) | One instalment for small companies |
| Each pay date | Payroll submission | Real-time reporting to Revenue |

**Critical warning:** Missing the CRO annual return deadline by even one day costs your company the audit exemption for two financial years. Set calendar reminders well in advance.

## Common Mistakes When Starting a Business

Avoid these frequently made errors that cost Irish business owners time and money:

**Not separating personal and business finances.** Open a dedicated business bank account from day one. Mixed accounts make bookkeeping expensive and Revenue audits problematic.

**Forgetting to register for tax.** Revenue expects you to register before you start trading. Late registration can result in penalties and backdated tax obligations.

**Not keeping receipts.** Revenue can disallow expense claims without supporting documentation. Photograph or scan every receipt and store them digitally.

**Underestimating tax obligations.** As a self-employed person, you must save for your own tax payments. A common guideline is to set aside 25 to 30 per cent of your net profit for income tax, USC, and PRSI.

**Ignoring VAT thresholds.** Monitor your turnover throughout the year. If you breach the threshold and have not registered, Revenue can backdate your obligation.

**Not engaging an accountant early enough.** Fixing mistakes after your first year is more expensive than setting up correctly from the start.

**Missing the CRO annual return date.** For limited companies, this is the most expensive deadline to miss.
