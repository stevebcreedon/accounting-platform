A business plan for an Irish business should include an executive summary, market analysis, operations plan, marketing strategy, financial projections for at least 3 years, and details of the founding team. If you are applying for a Local Enterprise Office (LEO) grant or a bank loan, the plan also needs to demonstrate job creation potential and a clear explanation of how funding will be used.

Writing a business plan forces you to think critically about every aspect of your business before you invest significant time and money. It is not just a document for banks and grant bodies -- it is the roadmap that turns a business idea into a viable operation. In Ireland specifically, where LEO grants, Enterprise Ireland funding, and Microfinance Ireland loans all require a business plan, having a well-prepared document can directly unlock thousands of euros in support. This guide explains what to include, how Irish evaluators assess plans, and practical tips for getting it right.

## Why You Need a Business Plan

### For Funding Applications

Every state funding programme in Ireland requires a business plan. Whether you are applying for a LEO feasibility study grant, a priming grant, a Trading Online Voucher, or Enterprise Ireland's Competitive Start Fund, the business plan is the core document against which your application is assessed.

Banks also require business plans for loan applications. AIB, Bank of Ireland, and Microfinance Ireland all expect a written plan that demonstrates the viability of your business and your ability to repay the loan.

### For Personal Clarity

Even if you never apply for a grant or loan, writing a business plan forces you to confront the hard questions: Is there actually a market for this? How much will it cost to get started? How long until I break even? What happens if my main supplier raises prices by 20 per cent? Answering these questions on paper before you commit your savings is far cheaper than learning the answers through experience.

### For Decision Making

A business plan gives you benchmarks to measure against. If your plan projected EUR 5,000 per month in revenue by month six and you are actually at EUR 2,000, that is an early warning signal. Without a plan, you have no baseline against which to evaluate your progress.

## The Key Sections of a Business Plan

### 1. Executive Summary

The executive summary is the most important section because it is the first thing any reader sees. It should be written last, after all other sections are complete, and should be no more than 2 pages.

The executive summary must answer:

- What does your business do?
- What problem does it solve or what need does it meet?
- Who are your customers?
- What is your revenue model?
- How much funding do you need and what will you use it for?
- What are your projected revenues and profits for the first 3 years?
- What is the opportunity size?

For LEO grant applications, the executive summary should also mention the employment you plan to create and the growth potential of the business. Evaluators read dozens of applications, and a clear, compelling executive summary makes the difference between a plan that gets serious attention and one that does not.

### 2. Business Description

This section describes your business in detail:

- **Legal structure:** Sole trader, partnership, or limited company (and why you chose this structure)
- **Business activity:** What you sell -- products, services, or both
- **Location:** Where you are based and why (include any significance, such as proximity to customers, suppliers, or transport links)
- **Stage of development:** Pre-startup, startup, or established (and key milestones achieved so far)
- **Mission and vision:** What you are trying to achieve and the values that guide your business

For Irish-specific context, mention your CRO registration status, Revenue registration, and any regulatory requirements specific to your industry.

### 3. Market Analysis

The market analysis demonstrates that you understand your target market and that there is genuine demand for your product or service. This section should cover:

**Market size and growth.** How large is the market in Ireland? Is it growing, stable, or declining? Use data from the Central Statistics Office (CSO), industry reports, or trade associations to support your claims.

**Target customers.** Who specifically will buy from you? Define your customer segments with as much detail as possible -- demographics, location, spending habits, pain points, and buying behaviour.

**Competitor analysis.** Who are your main competitors in Ireland? What are their strengths and weaknesses? How does your offering differ? Be honest here -- grant evaluators and bank managers will see through claims of "no competition." Every business has competitors, even if they are indirect.

**Market trends.** What trends are affecting your market? Are there regulatory changes, technology shifts, or consumer behaviour changes that create opportunities or threats? In Ireland, consider trends such as remote working (creating demand for rural services), sustainability requirements, digital transformation, and post-pandemic behavioural shifts.

### 4. Products and Services

Describe what you are selling in clear, non-technical language:

- What is the product or service?
- What problem does it solve for the customer?
- How is it different from what competitors offer?
- What is the pricing structure and why?
- What is the production or delivery process?
- Are there any intellectual property considerations (patents, trademarks, trade secrets)?

If you are in a technical field, include enough detail for a non-specialist to understand your offering. LEO evaluators come from diverse backgrounds and may not have expertise in your specific industry.

### 5. Marketing Strategy

Your marketing strategy explains how you will reach customers and generate sales. Cover:

**Pricing strategy.** How did you determine your prices? Are they competitive, premium, or value-oriented? How do they compare to competitors?

**Distribution channels.** How will customers buy from you? Direct sales, online, through retailers, through agents?

**Promotion.** How will you make potential customers aware of your business? Consider online marketing (website, SEO, social media, email), offline marketing (networking, trade shows, print advertising), PR, and referral strategies.

**Sales process.** How does a prospect become a customer? What are the steps, and what is the typical sales cycle length?

For an Irish context, mention specific channels relevant to your market. If you are B2B, consider industry events such as the Ploughing Championships for agri-businesses or Web Summit for tech. If you are targeting consumers, consider local marketing through community groups, local newspapers, and platforms such as DoneDeal or Adverts.ie.

### 6. Operations Plan

The operations plan explains how your business will function on a day-to-day basis:

- **Premises:** Where will you operate? Home office, rented space, co-working space, or commercial premises?
- **Equipment and technology:** What equipment, tools, or software do you need?
- **Suppliers:** Who are your key suppliers? Are there backup suppliers?
- **Production process:** If you make a product, describe the process from raw materials to finished product
- **Quality control:** How will you ensure consistent quality?
- **Staffing:** Who will do what? If you plan to hire, when and what roles?

### 7. Management Team

Who is running the business? This section should cover:

- **Founder background:** Your relevant experience, qualifications, and skills
- **Key personnel:** Anyone else involved in the business and their roles
- **Skill gaps:** What expertise is missing and how will you address it (hiring, training, outsourcing, mentoring)?
- **Advisers:** Accountant, solicitor, mentor, or industry adviser

Grant evaluators pay close attention to the management team. They are investing in people as much as ideas. If you lack direct experience in your industry, explain how your transferable skills and your plan to acquire industry knowledge mitigate this.

### 8. Financial Projections

The financial projections are where many business plans fall down. They must be realistic, internally consistent, and based on stated assumptions. Include:

**Startup costs.** A detailed breakdown of everything you need to spend before you start trading, including equipment, fit-out, stock, deposits, legal fees, marketing launch costs, and working capital.

**Projected profit and loss.** Monthly for year one, quarterly or annually for years two and three. Show revenue, cost of goods sold, gross profit, operating expenses (rent, utilities, insurance, marketing, wages, professional fees), and net profit.

**Cash flow projection.** Monthly for at least the first year. This is critically important because a profitable business can still fail if it runs out of cash. Show when money comes in (customer payments), when it goes out (supplier payments, rent, wages, tax), and the resulting cash position.

**Break-even analysis.** At what point do your revenues cover your total costs? How many units do you need to sell, or how many clients do you need, to break even?

**Funding requirements.** If you are seeking a grant or loan, clearly state how much you need, what you will spend it on (itemised), and how the investment will generate a return.

### Financial Projection Format for LEO Applications

LEO evaluators expect financial projections to follow a standard format. Your local LEO typically provides a template, but the key elements are:

- Year 1 monthly cash flow showing realistic revenue build-up (not full capacity from month one)
- Salary costs including employer's PRSI at 11.05 per cent
- Tax provisions (income tax or corporation tax, VAT if registered, preliminary tax)
- Clear assumptions stated separately (for example, "average order value EUR 150 based on competitor analysis," "customer acquisition cost EUR 30 based on pilot results")

## Common Mistakes in Irish Business Plans

**Unrealistic revenue projections.** The most common error is projecting revenue that is too high too quickly. A new restaurant does not operate at 80 per cent capacity from day one. A consultant does not bill 40 hours per week from month one. Start conservatively and show a realistic ramp-up.

**Ignoring competition.** Claiming "there is no competition" undermines your credibility. Every business has competitors, even if they are in a different format or location. Demonstrate that you understand the competitive landscape and explain your differentiation clearly.

**Missing cash flow analysis.** Many plans include a profit and loss projection but skip the cash flow. For LEO and bank evaluators, the cash flow is often more important than the P&L because it shows whether the business can survive month to month.

**No market evidence.** Stating "the market is huge" without data is not convincing. Use CSO statistics, industry reports, survey results, or evidence from a pilot or test trading period to support your market claims.

**Vague use of funds.** "We will use the grant for business development" tells the evaluator nothing. "EUR 5,000 for a commercial espresso machine (model X from supplier Y), EUR 3,000 for 6 months of Google Ads targeting 'coffee shop Dublin 8'" is specific and credible.

**Not accounting for tax.** Many first-time business owners forget to include tax in their financial projections. Your cash flow must account for VAT payments (if registered), preliminary income tax or corporation tax, and employer's PRSI if hiring.

**Over-designed, under-written.** A business plan is not a marketing brochure. Evaluators want substance, not glossy graphics. Focus on clear writing, solid analysis, and realistic projections. A well-reasoned 20-page plan beats a 50-page document padded with stock photos.

## Resources and Templates

### LEO Resources

Your local LEO is the best source of practical support for writing a business plan. They offer:

- **Start Your Own Business programme:** A free multi-week course covering all aspects of starting a business, including business plan writing
- **Business plan templates:** Most LEOs provide a template that matches their evaluation criteria
- **One-to-one mentoring:** Free sessions with experienced mentors who can review and refine your plan
- **Financial projection templates:** Spreadsheet templates for cash flow and P&L projections

### Online Templates

Several Irish organisations provide free business plan templates:

- **LEO website (localenterprise.ie):** Downloadable business plan template and guide
- **Enterprise Ireland:** Business plan templates for technology and export-focused businesses
- **Microfinance Ireland:** Simplified business plan template for loan applications
- **Revenue:** While not a business plan template, Revenue's guide to record keeping for new businesses helps inform the financial sections

### Professional Support

If your business plan is for a significant grant application (LEO priming grant) or a substantial bank loan, consider engaging a professional:

- **Accountants:** Can prepare or review your financial projections to ensure they are realistic and properly formatted
- **Business plan consultants:** Can help structure and write the entire plan, though be aware that evaluators can often tell when a plan has been written entirely by a consultant rather than the business owner
- **LEO mentors:** Free and available through your local LEO -- often the most practical and cost-effective option
