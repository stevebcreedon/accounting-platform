Every business in Ireland must issue invoices that meet specific legal requirements. If you are VAT-registered, the Value-Added Tax Consolidation Act 2010 sets out exactly what must appear on your invoices. Even if you are not VAT-registered, issuing proper invoices protects your business, ensures you get paid, and keeps your records in order for Revenue.

Getting your invoices right from the start avoids problems later. Incorrect or incomplete invoices can delay payment from customers, cause issues during a Revenue audit, and prevent your VAT-registered customers from reclaiming the VAT you charge. This guide covers everything you need to know about invoicing in Ireland, from the mandatory information to credit notes, electronic invoicing, and record retention.

## What Must Appear on an Invoice in Ireland

Every business invoice in Ireland should contain certain basic information, regardless of whether you are VAT-registered. The following details are considered best practice for all invoices and are legally required for VAT invoices:

### Mandatory Information for All Invoices

- **Your business name** as registered with Revenue (or your trading name if different)
- **Your business address** (principal place of business)
- **The date the invoice was issued**
- **A unique sequential invoice number** (each invoice must have a number that follows a logical sequence, such as INV-001, INV-002, or 2026-0001, 2026-0002)
- **The customer's name and address**
- **A clear description of the goods or services supplied**
- **The quantity of goods or extent of services**
- **The total amount due**
- **Payment terms** (when payment is due)

### Additional Requirements for VAT Invoices

If you are VAT-registered, your invoices must also include:

- **Your VAT registration number** (format: IE followed by 7 digits and 1 or 2 letters)
- **The customer's VAT number** (if they are VAT-registered and entitled to deduct input VAT)
- **The unit price exclusive of VAT** for each item or service
- **The VAT rate applied** to each item (23%, 13.5%, 9%, or 0%)
- **The total VAT amount** at each rate
- **The total amount payable including VAT**
- **The date of supply** if different from the invoice date
- **A notation for any special VAT treatment**, such as "reverse charge" for intra-EU B2B services or "margin scheme" for second-hand goods

If you supply goods or services at different VAT rates on the same invoice, you must show each rate separately with the corresponding net amount and VAT amount for each rate. Lumping everything together at a single rate is not permitted.

## VAT Invoice vs Non-VAT Invoice

The distinction between a VAT invoice and a non-VAT invoice is important for both you and your customers.

**VAT invoice:** Issued by a VAT-registered business. It must contain all the information listed above, including your VAT number, the VAT rate, and the VAT amount. Your VAT-registered customers need a proper VAT invoice to reclaim the input VAT on their own VAT returns.

**Non-VAT invoice:** Issued by a business that is not VAT-registered. It does not include a VAT number, VAT rate, or VAT amount. You must not charge VAT or show a VAT amount if you are not VAT-registered. Doing so is a Revenue offence.

If you are not VAT-registered, it is good practice to state this on your invoices with a note such as "Not VAT registered" to avoid any confusion with your customers. This prevents them from attempting to claim a VAT deduction on your invoice.

## Simplified Invoices

For supplies of less than EUR 100 (including VAT), you may issue a simplified invoice. A simplified invoice must include:

- Your name, address, and VAT number
- The date of issue
- A description of the goods or services
- The total amount including VAT
- The VAT rate applied

Simplified invoices do not require the customer's name and address or a separate breakdown of the VAT amount. They are commonly used in retail situations, restaurants, and other businesses where individual customers make small purchases.

However, if a customer requests a full VAT invoice for a transaction below EUR 100, you must provide one.

## Credit Notes

A credit note is a document issued to correct or cancel a previously issued invoice. You issue a credit note when:

- You overcharged the customer
- The customer returns goods
- You agreed a discount or rebate after issuing the invoice
- There was an error on the original invoice
- The supply was cancelled

### What Must Appear on a Credit Note

A credit note must contain:

- The words "credit note" prominently displayed
- A unique sequential credit note number
- The date of issue
- Your business name, address, and VAT number
- The customer's name and address
- A reference to the original invoice number and date
- A description of the reason for the credit
- The amount being credited (exclusive of VAT)
- The VAT rate and VAT amount being credited
- The total amount including VAT being credited

Credit notes reduce the VAT you owe to Revenue. On your VAT3 return, you deduct the VAT on credit notes issued from your output VAT (T1). Your customer must correspondingly reduce their input VAT claim.

## Debit Notes

A debit note is issued by the customer to the supplier, typically when goods are returned or when the customer identifies an overcharge. Debit notes serve a similar function to credit notes but are initiated by the buyer rather than the seller. In practice, most Irish businesses handle adjustments through credit notes issued by the supplier, but debit notes are recognised under Irish VAT law.

## Electronic Invoicing

Irish law permits electronic invoicing provided certain conditions are met. Electronic invoices are valid if:

- The customer accepts electronic invoicing (consent can be implied through business practice)
- The authenticity of the origin and integrity of the content are guaranteed (you can prove the invoice came from you and has not been altered)
- The invoice is legible and can be reproduced in human-readable format

In practice, PDF invoices sent by email, invoices generated by accounting software (such as Xero, Sage, or QuickBooks), and invoices issued through online invoicing platforms all meet these requirements.

There is no requirement in Ireland to use a specific electronic invoicing format or platform. However, the EU is progressively moving towards structured electronic invoicing, and it is likely that Ireland will adopt more specific e-invoicing requirements in the coming years, particularly for B2B and B2G (business-to-government) transactions.

### Benefits of Electronic Invoicing

Moving to electronic invoicing offers several advantages for Irish businesses:

- **Faster delivery:** Email delivery is instant compared to postal delays
- **Faster payment:** Studies consistently show that electronic invoices are paid faster than paper invoices
- **Lower cost:** No printing, postage, or paper storage costs
- **Better records:** Digital invoices are easier to search, organise, and back up
- **Integration:** Cloud accounting software can automatically generate, send, and track invoices
- **Environmental:** Reduced paper and transport

## Invoice Numbering

Your invoice numbers must follow a sequential, unbroken series. Revenue expects to see a logical progression in your invoice numbering and will question gaps in the sequence during an audit.

You can use any numbering system that works for your business, provided it is sequential. Common formats include:

- Simple numeric: 001, 002, 003
- Year-prefixed: 2026-001, 2026-002
- Prefix-based: INV-001, INV-002

You may start a new sequence at the beginning of each financial year (for example, 2026-001, then 2027-001) provided you are consistent. What you must not do is skip numbers, reuse numbers, or issue invoices out of sequence.

If you void an invoice for any reason, keep it in your records marked as void rather than deleting it. Revenue needs to see the complete sequence, including voided invoices.

## Payment Terms on Invoices

Your invoice should clearly state when payment is due. In Ireland, common payment terms include:

- **Due on receipt:** Payment expected immediately
- **Net 7 / Net 14 / Net 30:** Payment due within 7, 14, or 30 days of the invoice date
- **End of month (EOM):** Payment due at the end of the month in which the invoice is dated
- **End of month plus 30:** Payment due 30 days after the end of the invoice month

If your invoice does not state payment terms, the default under the European Communities (Late Payment in Commercial Transactions) Regulations 2012 is 30 days from receipt of the invoice.

### Late Payment Interest

Under the 2012 Regulations, you are entitled to charge interest on late commercial payments automatically, without needing to send a reminder or demand. The interest rate is the ECB reference rate plus 8 percentage points. You are also entitled to a fixed sum of EUR 40 as compensation for recovery costs for each late payment, plus any reasonable additional recovery costs.

While many Irish businesses do not enforce these provisions to maintain customer relationships, including a note about late payment interest on your invoices can encourage prompt payment.

## Record Retention

Revenue requires you to retain copies of all invoices, credit notes, and debit notes for a minimum of **6 years**. This applies to both invoices you issue and invoices you receive from suppliers.

For VAT-registered businesses, the retention requirement starts from the date of the last transaction covered by the records. In practice, this means you may need to keep records for longer than 6 years if they relate to an ongoing contract or asset.

Records can be kept in paper or electronic format. If you keep electronic records, you must ensure they are:

- Complete and accurate
- Accessible and readable on request
- Backed up regularly
- Protected against unauthorised alteration

Many Irish businesses now use cloud accounting software that automatically stores invoices and makes them accessible for the full retention period. If you use such software, ensure your subscription remains active for the required period, or export and store your records independently.

## Common Invoicing Mistakes

**Missing VAT number.** If you are VAT-registered but forget to include your VAT number on an invoice, your customer cannot reclaim the input VAT. This can damage your relationship and delay payment.

**Incorrect VAT rate.** Charging the wrong VAT rate is a compliance issue. If you charge too little, you owe Revenue the difference. If you charge too much, you may need to issue a credit note and refund the excess.

**Non-sequential numbering.** Gaps in your invoice sequence will raise questions during a Revenue audit. Always maintain a complete, unbroken sequence.

**Vague descriptions.** A description like "services rendered" is not sufficient. Your invoice should clearly describe what you supplied, such as "website design and development for company brochure site, completed 15 March 2026."

**No payment terms.** Without stated payment terms, you have less grounds to chase late payment. Always include clear terms and the due date.

**Mixing currencies without conversion.** If you invoice in a foreign currency, include the EUR equivalent for VAT purposes, using the ECB exchange rate on the date of supply.

## Invoicing Tips for Irish Businesses

Keep your invoices professional and clear. A well-designed invoice reflects well on your business and reduces queries from customers. Use accounting software such as Xero, Sage, QuickBooks, or the Irish-developed Surf Accounts or Big Red Cloud to automate your invoicing process.

Set up recurring invoices for regular clients to save time and ensure consistency. Most accounting software supports this feature. For project-based work, consider sending a pro-forma invoice before starting work and a final invoice on completion.

Review your outstanding invoices weekly. The longer an invoice goes unpaid, the less likely you are to collect the full amount. A structured follow-up process, starting with a polite reminder at 21 days and escalating from there, significantly improves collection rates.
