Tradespeople in Ireland, whether plumbers, electricians, carpenters, builders, plasterers, or any other trade, have specific accounting and tax obligations that differ from many other self-employed individuals. The construction industry in particular is subject to Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT), which means tax is deducted at source from payments for construction operations. Understanding RCT, VAT rates for construction, and the range of allowable expenses is essential for any tradesperson running their own business in Ireland.

Revenue pays close attention to the construction and trades sectors. Cash transactions, subcontracting chains, and the line between employment and self-employment are all areas where Revenue conducts regular compliance checks. Getting your accounting right protects you from penalties and ensures you are not paying more tax than necessary.

## Sole Trader vs Limited Company for Tradespeople

Most tradespeople in Ireland start as sole traders. You register with Revenue using a TR1 form, file an annual Form 11, and pay income tax, USC, and Class S PRSI on your profits. The sole trader structure is simple, low-cost, and suitable for most tradespeople in the early years.

As your income grows, a limited company may offer tax advantages. The 12.5 per cent corporation tax rate on trading profits compares favourably with the 40 per cent higher rate of income tax. However, a company has higher running costs: CRO annual return fees, accountancy fees for company accounts, and payroll administration for paying yourself a salary.

For tradespeople consistently earning over EUR 80,000 per year in profits, a conversation with your accountant about incorporation is worthwhile. Below that level, the sole trader structure is usually more cost-effective.

There is another consideration specific to trades. A limited company provides limited liability, which can be important if you work on projects where there is a risk of claims. However, many clients (particularly in domestic construction) require personal guarantees or specific insurance cover regardless of your business structure.

## Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT)

RCT is a withholding tax that applies to payments for construction, forestry, and meat processing operations in Ireland. If you work as a subcontractor in the construction industry, your principal (the person who engages you) must operate RCT on payments to you.

### How RCT Works

When a principal contractor engages a subcontractor for construction operations, the principal must:

1. Notify Revenue of the contract through the electronic RCT system (eRCT)
2. Deduct RCT from each payment at the rate determined by Revenue
3. Submit a deduction summary to Revenue
4. Pay the RCT deducted to Revenue

Revenue determines the deduction rate based on the subcontractor's compliance history:

- **0 per cent:** For subcontractors with a good compliance record (three years of tax compliance, up-to-date returns, and no outstanding liabilities)
- **20 per cent:** The standard rate for subcontractors who are registered with Revenue and have a reasonable compliance record
- **35 per cent:** For subcontractors who are not registered, have poor compliance, or where Revenue cannot verify their identity

### Getting to 0 Per Cent

Achieving the 0 per cent RCT rate should be a priority for every subcontractor. To qualify, you must:

- Have been registered with Revenue for at least three years
- Have filed all tax returns on time
- Have no outstanding tax liabilities (or have a payment arrangement in place)
- Have a good compliance history with no serious penalties

If you are deducted at 20 or 35 per cent, the RCT deducted is credited against your income tax liability on your annual return. If the RCT deducted exceeds your tax liability, you can claim a refund. However, having tax deducted at source creates a cash flow disadvantage, so maintaining a 0 per cent rate is important for your business finances.

### What Counts as Construction Operations

RCT applies to a wide range of construction activities, including:

- Building, alteration, repair, extension, and demolition of structures
- Installation of heating, lighting, power, drainage, water supply, and ventilation systems
- Road construction, bridge building, and civil engineering works
- Painting, decorating, and cleaning of buildings (as part of construction)
- Site preparation, excavation, and land reclamation

If you are a plumber, electrician, or other tradesperson working on construction projects, your work is likely within the scope of RCT. Some maintenance work outside of the construction context may not be subject to RCT, but the distinction can be complex. When in doubt, ask your accountant.

### If You Are a Principal Contractor

If you engage subcontractors yourself (for example, a builder who hires a plumber and an electrician for a project), you become a principal contractor and must operate RCT on payments to your subcontractors. This means registering as a principal with Revenue, notifying contracts, deducting tax at the appropriate rate, and filing deduction summaries. Failure to operate RCT correctly can result in you being held personally liable for the tax that should have been deducted.

## VAT for Tradespeople

VAT is a significant consideration for tradespeople. You must register for VAT if your turnover exceeds the relevant threshold (EUR 80,000 for goods, EUR 40,000 for services). Most tradespeople provide services, so the EUR 40,000 threshold applies. If you also supply materials as part of your service, you need to consider whether your supply is primarily goods or services.

### VAT Rates in Construction

The construction sector has specific VAT rate considerations:

- **13.5 per cent:** This reduced rate applies to the supply of construction services, including building, plumbing, electrical, plastering, carpentry, and painting services. It also applies to the supply of ready-to-pour concrete, concrete blocks, and similar building materials when supplied as part of a construction service.
- **23 per cent:** The standard rate applies to most building materials when sold separately (timber, pipes, wiring, fixtures), professional services (architectural, surveying), and plant hire.

The distinction between a 13.5 per cent construction service and a 23 per cent supply of goods can be complex, particularly for tradespeople who supply materials as part of their work. The general rule is that if you supply and install materials as part of a construction contract, the entire supply is treated as a construction service at 13.5 per cent. If you simply sell materials without installation, the standard 23 per cent rate applies.

### Two-Thirds Rule

Revenue applies the two-thirds rule to determine the VAT rate on a composite supply. If the cost of materials exceeds two-thirds of the total charge, the supply is treated as a supply of goods (23 per cent on the materials). If materials are less than two-thirds, the entire supply is treated as a service (13.5 per cent). This rule is particularly relevant for kitchen fitters, bathroom installers, and other trades where material costs are high relative to labour.

## Common Allowable Expenses for Tradespeople

Tradespeople can claim a wide range of business expenses:

### Tools and Equipment

Hand tools, power tools, testing equipment, and safety equipment are all deductible. Items costing under a few hundred euro are typically expensed in full in the year of purchase. More expensive equipment such as industrial tools, generators, and scaffolding is subject to capital allowances at 12.5 per cent per year over 8 years.

### Van and Vehicle Costs

A van is essential for most tradespeople. The costs of running a business vehicle are deductible, but you must distinguish between business and personal use. Options for claiming motor expenses include:

**Actual costs method:** Claim the actual costs of fuel, insurance, tax, maintenance, and depreciation (or lease payments), apportioned between business and personal use. You must keep a detailed mileage log showing the proportion of business use.

**Fixed mileage rates:** Claim Revenue's civil service mileage rates for business kilometres driven. These rates cover all motoring costs, so you cannot claim additional expenses on top. You must keep a mileage log.

For a van used primarily for business (carrying tools, materials, and equipment to job sites), a business use percentage of 80 to 90 per cent is common and generally accepted by Revenue, provided you can demonstrate it with a mileage log.

### Materials

The cost of materials used on jobs is deductible. This includes consumables (solder, adhesives, fixings, cables), components installed as part of your work, and any materials not recharged to the client. Keep all receipts from builders' merchants, plumbing suppliers, electrical wholesalers, and hardware stores.

### Protective Clothing and Safety Equipment

Safety boots, hi-vis vests, hard hats, gloves, goggles, and other protective clothing and equipment required for your work are fully deductible. Revenue specifically lists protective clothing as an allowable deduction for construction workers.

### Insurance

Public liability insurance, professional indemnity insurance, employers' liability insurance (if you have employees), van insurance, and tool insurance are all deductible. Trade insurance premiums can be substantial, so ensuring you claim the full amount is important.

### Training and Certification

The cost of maintaining your trade qualifications, including Safe Pass, CSCS cards, confined spaces training, working at heights certification, and any other mandatory training, is deductible. Fees for courses that enhance your existing skills (such as advanced electrical qualifications or renewable energy installation courses) are also deductible.

### Accountancy and Professional Fees

Fees paid to your accountant for preparing your accounts, filing tax returns, and providing advice are deductible.

## Invoicing Requirements for Tradespeople

Every invoice you issue should include:

- Your business name and address
- Your tax registration number (and VAT number if registered)
- The customer's name and address
- A unique sequential invoice number
- The date of the invoice
- A description of the work carried out
- The labour charge and materials charge (separately if possible)
- The VAT rate and amount (if VAT-registered)
- The total amount payable

For construction contracts subject to RCT, include your RCT reference number. If you are deducted RCT, the principal should issue a deduction authorisation for each payment.

Keeping a copy of every invoice issued (numbered sequentially) is a Revenue requirement. Gaps in your invoice sequence will be queried during an audit.

## Cash Flow Management

Cash flow is one of the biggest challenges for tradespeople. You may need to purchase materials upfront, wait for jobs to be completed, and then wait for the client to pay. Meanwhile, you have ongoing costs for your van, insurance, tools, and personal living expenses.

Practical tips for managing cash flow:

- **Request deposits.** It is standard practice to request a deposit of 20 to 30 per cent before starting a job, particularly for larger projects.
- **Invoice promptly.** Issue invoices as soon as the work is complete. The longer you wait, the longer you wait to be paid.
- **Set payment terms.** State your payment terms clearly on your invoices (for example, payment due within 14 or 30 days).
- **Separate tax money.** Set aside 30 to 35 per cent of every payment received into a dedicated savings account for income tax, USC, and PRSI. If VAT-registered, set aside VAT separately.
- **Chase late payments.** Do not let debts age. Follow up promptly when payments are overdue.

## Keeping Receipts for Materials

Revenue expects tradespeople to retain receipts for all materials purchased. This is one of the most common audit issues in the trades sector. Without receipts, Revenue may disallow your materials deduction, significantly increasing your tax bill.

Best practices include:

- Get a receipt for every purchase, no matter how small
- Use a trade account with builders' merchants, which provides monthly statements and itemised purchase records
- Photograph receipts using your phone (thermal paper receipts fade over time)
- Store digital copies in organised folders by month or by job
- Reconcile material purchases to specific jobs where possible

A shoebox of faded receipts at year end is not adequate. Revenue auditors expect organised, legible records that can be traced to specific jobs or expenses.

## Common Revenue Issues with Tradespeople

Revenue focuses on several areas when auditing tradespeople:

**Cash transactions.** The trades sector has historically involved significant cash payments. Revenue uses various methods to identify undeclared cash income, including lifestyle checks (comparing declared income to living expenses and assets) and deposit analysis (examining bank lodgements against declared turnover).

**Subcontractor vs employee.** Revenue regularly challenges the employment status of workers in the construction sector. If you engage workers who should properly be classified as employees, you may face backdated PAYE, PRSI, and USC assessments plus penalties.

**RCT compliance.** Both principals and subcontractors are subject to RCT compliance checks. Ensure your RCT notifications, deductions, and returns are up to date.

**Motor expense claims.** Disproportionate business use claims on vehicles, particularly where a tradesperson has only one vehicle used for both business and personal purposes, are frequently challenged.

Maintaining complete, accurate, and well-organised records is the best protection against Revenue assessments and penalties.

## Employing Apprentices

If you take on an apprentice, you become an employer and must register for PAYE, PRSI, and USC as an employer. Apprentice wages are set by the relevant Sectoral Employment Order or industry agreement, and you must pay at least the prescribed rate.

As an employer, you must operate PAYE on the apprentice's wages, pay employer PRSI (currently 11.05 per cent), and file payroll submissions to Revenue through the real-time PAYE reporting system.

The cost of employing an apprentice, including wages, employer PRSI, training contributions, and any materials provided for training, is fully deductible against your business income. Some employer incentive schemes may also be available to offset the cost of apprenticeship training.
