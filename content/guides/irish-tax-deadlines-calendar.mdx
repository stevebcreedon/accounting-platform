The most important tax deadline in Ireland is 31 October (or mid-November if you file and pay through ROS) for income tax returns. Corporation tax returns are due within 9 months of your accounting period end. Missing these deadlines triggers automatic surcharges of up to 10% of your tax liability, plus interest charges from Revenue.

Whether you are a sole trader, limited company director, or employer, keeping track of your tax deadlines is essential. Ireland has a complex web of filing obligations spread throughout the year -- income tax, corporation tax, VAT, PAYE, CRO returns, and more. Missing even one deadline can result in penalties, surcharges, and unwanted attention from the Revenue Commissioners.

This guide sets out every key tax and filing deadline for Irish businesses in 2026 and 2027, explains what each return involves, and provides practical advice on planning your tax year to avoid late filing penalties.

## Annual Income Tax: Pay and File

The cornerstone of the Irish tax calendar is the annual **pay-and-file** deadline for income tax. This applies to sole traders, landlords, directors with non-PAYE income, and anyone with income not fully taxed at source.

### Key Dates

- **31 October 2026:** Paper filing deadline for 2025 income tax return (Form 11) and payment of balance of tax for 2025 plus preliminary tax for 2026
- **Mid-November 2026 (typically 16 November):** Extended deadline for taxpayers who both file and pay through Revenue's Online Service (ROS). Revenue confirms the exact ROS extension date each year
- **31 October 2027:** Deadline for 2026 income tax return and preliminary tax for 2027

### What You Must File

The **Form 11** is the annual income tax return for self-assessed taxpayers. It covers all your income for the tax year (1 January to 31 December) including:

- Self-employment income (sole trader profits)
- Rental income
- Investment income (dividends, interest, foreign income)
- Capital gains
- Director's fees or other non-PAYE income
- PAYE income (if you also have other income sources)

### What You Must Pay

On the same date, you must pay:

1. **Balance of tax for the previous year:** The difference between your actual tax liability and the preliminary tax you already paid
2. **Preliminary tax for the current year:** An estimate of your tax liability for the year in progress. This must be at least 90% of your actual liability for the current year, or 100% of your liability for the previous year, or 105% of your liability for the pre-preceding year (direct debit only)

Getting your preliminary tax calculation right is important. If you underpay, Revenue charges interest at 0.0219% per day (approximately 8% per annum) on the shortfall.

## Corporation Tax

Limited companies have their own filing obligations with different deadlines based on their accounting period end date.

### CT1 Return

The **CT1 (Corporation Tax Return)** must be filed within **9 months of the end of your accounting period**. For example:

- Accounting period ending 31 December 2025: CT1 due by 23 September 2026 (ROS)
- Accounting period ending 31 March 2026: CT1 due by 23 December 2026 (ROS)
- Accounting period ending 30 June 2026: CT1 due by 23 March 2027 (ROS)

The CT1 covers corporation tax on trading profits, passive income, capital gains, and close company surcharges. The standard corporation tax rate in Ireland is **12.5%** on trading income and **25%** on passive income (rental, investment).

### Preliminary Corporation Tax

Companies must pay preliminary corporation tax in advance of filing the CT1:

- **Small companies** (tax liability under EUR 200,000 in the preceding period): One payment due within 6 months of the accounting period end, equal to at least 90% of the current period liability or 100% of the previous period liability
- **Large companies** (tax liability of EUR 200,000 or more): Two payments required. The first instalment is due within 6 months of the start of the accounting period (at least 50% of the previous period liability or 45% of the current period liability). The second instalment is due within 11 months of the start of the period, bringing the total to at least 90% of the current period liability

### Close Company Surcharges

If your company is a **close company** (controlled by five or fewer participators, which includes most Irish owner-managed businesses), additional surcharges may apply on undistributed investment income (20%) and undistributed professional services income (15%). These are reported on the CT1.

## VAT Return Dates

VAT-registered businesses must file regular VAT returns. The standard filing frequency is **bi-monthly**, though some businesses may be assigned monthly or quarterly filing periods based on their VAT liability.

The **VAT3 return** reports your output VAT (VAT charged to customers) and input VAT (VAT paid on business purchases). The difference is either paid to Revenue (if output exceeds input) or claimed as a refund (if input exceeds output).

### Annual Return of Trading Details (RTD)

In addition to bi-monthly VAT3 returns, VAT-registered businesses must file an **annual RTD** summarising their total supplies and purchases at each VAT rate. The RTD deadline is the same as the VAT3 return for the final period of the year (January of the following year for calendar-year businesses).

Failure to file the RTD can result in Revenue restricting your VAT refunds until the return is submitted.

## PAYE and Employer Obligations

If you employ staff, you have monthly and annual PAYE obligations to Revenue.

### Monthly PAYE Returns

Under **PAYE Modernisation** (introduced in 2019), employers must report payroll information to Revenue in real time. Each time you run payroll, you submit a **payroll submission** to Revenue through ROS or your payroll software. This includes:

- Gross pay for each employee
- Tax deducted (income tax, USC, PRSI, LPT)
- Net pay
- Employee PPS numbers and Revenue Payroll Notifications (RPNs)

### Monthly Payment Deadline

The **14th of each month** is the deadline for paying PAYE/PRSI/USC to Revenue for the previous month. If you pay through ROS, you get an extension to the **23rd of the month**.

For example, PAYE deducted in January 2026 must be paid to Revenue by 14 February 2026 (or 23 February via ROS).

### Employer Annual Returns

At the end of each tax year, employers must submit:

- **P35 Return** (now part of the Enhanced Reporting Requirements): Annual summary of all employee earnings and deductions. Due by **15 February** following the end of the tax year
- **Benefit-in-Kind (BIK) returns** for company cars, health insurance, and other non-cash benefits provided to employees

### Enhanced Reporting Requirements (ERR)

Since 2024, employers must report certain tax-free payments and benefits through ERR submissions. These include:

- Travel and subsistence payments
- Small benefit exemption payments
- Remote working daily allowances

ERR submissions must be made on or before the date of payment.

## CRO Annual Return

Every Irish company registered with the Companies Registration Office (CRO) must file an **annual return (Form B1)**, regardless of whether it is trading.

### Key Rules

- Each company has a specific **Annual Return Date (ARD)** -- the anniversary of the company's incorporation or its elected ARD
- The Form B1 must be filed within **28 days** of the ARD
- Financial statements must be attached to the annual return (unless the company qualifies for an audit exemption and files an abridged return)

### Deadlines

- **ARD + 28 days:** Filing deadline. For example, if your ARD is 1 June, your B1 must be filed by 29 June
- **Late filing penalties:** EUR 100 per day, up to a maximum of EUR 1,200 per return. But the real cost is losing your **audit exemption** for two years -- meaning you must pay for a full statutory audit even if your company is small

### Filing Tips

- You can change your ARD once per year (within certain rules) to align it with your accounting year-end
- CRO now accepts electronic filing through CORE (Companies Online Registration Environment)
- Set a reminder at least 6 weeks before your ARD to ensure financial statements are ready

## Local Property Tax (LPT)

If you own property in Ireland (including business premises held personally or through a company), you may have LPT obligations.

- **Valuation date:** Revenue issues LPT valuations periodically (the most recent revaluation was for 2022-2025)
- **Payment options:** Annual payment (by January) or phased payments (direct debit, deduction from salary/pension)
- **LPT returns:** Revenue contacts property owners when revaluation is required. Returns must be submitted by the deadline specified in Revenue's correspondence

For business owners who own their premises personally, LPT is a separate obligation from their business taxes.

## Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT)

RCT applies to payments in the **construction, forestry, and meat processing** industries. If you are a **principal contractor** making payments to subcontractors, you must:

1. **Notify Revenue** of each contract before making payments
2. **Deduct RCT** at the appropriate rate from payments to subcontractors
3. **File monthly RCT returns** through ROS by the **23rd of each month**
4. **Pay the deducted RCT** to Revenue with the monthly return

### RCT Rates

- **0%:** Subcontractor has an excellent compliance record and meets Revenue's conditions
- **20%:** Standard rate for registered subcontractors with a satisfactory record
- **35%:** Applied to unregistered subcontractors or those with compliance issues

Subcontractors can claim credit for RCT deducted against their income tax or corporation tax liability. Revenue determines the applicable rate through the electronic RCT system on ROS.

## Dividend Withholding Tax (DWT)

If your company pays dividends to shareholders, you must deduct **Dividend Withholding Tax at 25%** and remit it to Revenue.

### Filing Requirements

- **DWT return:** Due within **14 days of the end of the month** in which the dividend was paid
- If your company pays a dividend on 15 March, the DWT return and payment are due by 14 April

Certain shareholders may be exempt from DWT (Irish resident companies, pension funds, charities). Exemption declarations must be on file before paying dividends without DWT deduction.

## October/November Pay-and-File Season

October and November are the busiest months in the Irish tax calendar. Here is why:

### 31 October (Paper Filing)

On this single date, self-assessed taxpayers must:

1. File their income tax return (Form 11) for the previous year
2. Pay any balance of tax due for the previous year
3. Pay preliminary tax for the current year
4. File a capital gains tax return for gains in the period January-September of the current year
5. Pay CGT on gains from January-September

### Mid-November (ROS Extension)

Taxpayers who both file and pay through ROS receive an extension -- typically to **16 November** (Revenue confirms the exact date each year). To qualify, you must:

- File your Form 11 electronically through ROS (not paper)
- Pay the full amount due through ROS (by direct debit, debit card, or credit card through the system)

This extension applies to the Form 11 return, the balance of tax, preliminary tax, and CGT for the January-September period.

### Planning for Pay-and-File

- Start gathering your records in July or August
- Provide your accountant with all necessary information by September at the latest
- Do not leave everything to the last week of October -- accountants are swamped, and errors are more likely under time pressure
- If you cannot afford to pay your full liability, contact Revenue before the deadline to discuss a phased payment arrangement. Voluntary engagement is always treated more favourably than default

## Penalties for Late Filing and Payment

Revenue enforces strict penalties for late filing and late payment. Understanding these helps motivate timely compliance.

### Late Filing Surcharge

If you file your income tax return after the deadline:

- **Up to 2 months late:** 5% surcharge on your tax liability, up to a maximum of EUR 12,695
- **More than 2 months late:** 10% surcharge on your tax liability, up to a maximum of EUR 63,485

These surcharges are in addition to any interest charges. The surcharge applies even if you have paid all tax due on time but filed the return late.

### Interest on Late Payment

Revenue charges interest on late payment at **0.0219% per day** (approximately 8% per annum). Interest runs from the due date until the date of payment. There is no minimum threshold -- even a one-day delay triggers interest.

### Other Penalties

- **VAT:** Penalties of up to EUR 4,000 per return for late filing, plus interest on late payment
- **PAYE:** Penalties for late payment of employer taxes range from EUR 100 to EUR 4,000 depending on the amount and duration of the delay
- **CRO:** EUR 100 per day late filing fee (max EUR 1,200) plus loss of audit exemption for two years
- **Revenue audits:** If Revenue discovers underreporting during an audit, penalties range from 3% to 100% of the tax underpaid, depending on whether the error was careless, deliberate, or fraudulent

### Voluntary Disclosures

If you discover an error in a previous return, making a **qualifying disclosure** to Revenue before they contact you about an audit significantly reduces penalties. A qualifying disclosure can eliminate publication on the Revenue defaulters' list and reduce financial penalties.

## Setting Up ROS for Pay-and-File

Revenue's Online Service (ROS) is essential for modern tax compliance in Ireland. Here is what you need to know:

### Registering for ROS

1. **Apply for a digital certificate** through the Revenue website
2. Revenue posts a **ROS Administrator Password** to your registered address
3. Complete registration by creating your digital certificate
4. Download and install your certificate on your computer or device

### Benefits of ROS

- Extended filing deadlines (typically an extra 2-3 weeks)
- Real-time calculation of tax liabilities
- Access to previous returns and correspondence
- Electronic payment options
- Instant acknowledgement of filed returns
- Access to PAYE employee records (RPNs)

### ROS Agent Access

If you use an accountant, they can file returns and make payments on your behalf through their own ROS tax agent account. You grant agent access through ROS -- this does not require you to share your own login credentials.

## Planning Your Tax Year

Effective tax planning is not a once-a-year exercise. Here is a month-by-month approach for Irish business owners:

### January to March

- Review the previous year's financial performance
- Begin preparing information for your annual tax return
- File bi-monthly VAT returns (January/February period)
- File P35 employer annual return (by 15 February)
- Pay preliminary corporation tax (small companies with December year-end: due by 21 June, but plan early)

### April to June

- Finalise previous year's accounts with your accountant
- Review your preliminary tax estimate for the current year
- Consider making pension contributions before year-end for additional tax relief
- File CRO annual return if your ARD falls in this period
- File bi-monthly VAT returns (March/April period)

### July to September

- Gather all records and information for your annual tax return
- Provide your accountant with everything they need by September at the latest
- File bi-monthly VAT returns (May/June and July/August periods)
- Review cash flow to ensure you can meet October's pay-and-file obligations

### October to December

- **31 October / mid-November:** File Form 11, pay balance of tax, pay preliminary tax
- **15 December:** Pay CGT on gains from October-December (the "later period")
- File bi-monthly VAT returns (September/October and November/December periods)
- Review your business structure and tax position for the coming year
- Consider year-end tax planning measures (pension contributions, capital expenditure, timing of income)

## Summary

The Irish tax calendar is demanding, with deadlines spread throughout the year for income tax, corporation tax, VAT, PAYE, CRO returns, and more. The most critical date for most business owners is 31 October (or mid-November via ROS) for the annual pay-and-file. Missing deadlines is expensive -- surcharges of up to 10% plus daily interest charges add up quickly. The best approach is to use a qualified accountant, file through ROS to benefit from extended deadlines, and start preparing well in advance of each deadline. A proactive approach to tax compliance saves money, reduces stress, and keeps your business in good standing with Revenue.
