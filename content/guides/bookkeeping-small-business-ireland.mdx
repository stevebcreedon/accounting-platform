Every business in Ireland is legally required to keep adequate accounting records under the Companies Act 2014 and the Taxes Consolidation Act 1997. Revenue requires you to retain all business records for at least 6 years from the end of the tax year to which they relate, and failure to maintain proper records can result in penalties of up to EUR 3,000 per offence.

Good bookkeeping is not just a legal requirement. It is the foundation of a well-run business. Accurate records tell you whether your business is profitable, help you plan for tax payments, make it easier and cheaper to work with an accountant, and protect you in the event of a Revenue audit. This guide covers everything Irish small business owners need to know about bookkeeping, from what records to keep and how long to retain them, to choosing the right software and preparing for your accountant.

## What Is Bookkeeping and Why Does It Matter

Bookkeeping is the systematic recording of all financial transactions in your business. Every sale, purchase, expense, and bank transaction is recorded so you have a complete picture of the money flowing in and out of your business.

Bookkeeping is distinct from accounting. Bookkeeping is the day-to-day recording of transactions. Accounting is the interpretation of those records for tax compliance, financial reporting, and business decision-making. Your accountant needs accurate bookkeeping data to prepare your tax returns and financial statements. If the underlying records are incomplete or disorganised, your accountant spends more time (and charges more) to sort them out before they can even begin the compliance work.

For Irish small businesses, bookkeeping matters for four key reasons:

**Legal compliance.** Revenue and the CRO require you to maintain adequate records. Non-compliance can result in penalties, loss of tax reliefs, and difficulties obtaining a tax clearance certificate.

**Tax accuracy.** Accurate records ensure you pay the correct amount of tax -- no more, no less. Overpaying tax because you missed legitimate deductions is as much a problem as underpaying and facing penalties.

**Cash flow management.** Knowing what you are owed by customers, what you owe to suppliers, and when payments are due helps you manage your cash flow and avoid running out of money at critical moments.

**Business decisions.** Reliable financial data helps you set prices, control costs, decide when to hire, plan for growth, and assess whether your business is genuinely profitable after all expenses are accounted for.

## Revenue's Record-Keeping Requirements

Revenue's requirements for business record keeping are set out in Section 886 of the Taxes Consolidation Act 1997. The key requirements are:

### What Records You Must Keep

Every business in Ireland must maintain sufficient records to enable a complete and accurate tax return to be prepared. At minimum, this includes:

- **All invoices issued** to customers, numbered sequentially
- **All invoices received** from suppliers
- **All receipts** for business expenses
- **Bank statements** for all business bank accounts
- **Cash register records** (if applicable)
- **Records of all business assets** purchased and sold (for capital allowances and capital gains tax)
- **Payroll records** (if you have employees)
- **VAT records** (if VAT-registered), including a summary of VAT charged on sales and VAT paid on purchases
- **Stock records** (if you hold inventory)
- **Motor expense records** including mileage logs, fuel receipts, and the business-to-personal use ratio

### How Long to Keep Records

Revenue requires you to keep all business records for at least **6 years** from the end of the tax year to which they relate. For example, records relating to the tax year 2026 (ending 31 December 2026) must be retained until at least 31 December 2032.

For limited companies, the Companies Act 2014 requires accounting records to be kept for 6 years from the end of the financial year to which they relate.

In practice, it is wise to keep records for 7 years, as Revenue has 4 years from the end of the year of assessment to raise an assessment (or 6 years in cases of fraud or neglect), and you may need records from the prior year for comparison purposes.

### Digital vs Paper Records

Revenue accepts both paper and electronic records, provided they are legible, complete, and can be produced on request during an audit. If you keep electronic records, you must be able to provide Revenue with access to the system and, if requested, printed copies of any records.

Most Irish accountants and business advisors now recommend digital record keeping. Photographing or scanning receipts and invoices and storing them in the cloud (via your bookkeeping software, Google Drive, or a dedicated receipt app) is more reliable than keeping paper copies that can fade, be lost, or be damaged.

**Important:** If you choose to keep only digital copies of receipts, the digital image must be a true and accurate copy of the original. Revenue has confirmed that photographed receipts are acceptable provided they are legible and stored securely.

## What Records to Keep: A Practical Guide

### Sales Records

For every sale your business makes, you should record:

- The date of the sale
- The customer's name and address (for invoiced sales)
- A description of the goods or services sold
- The amount charged (exclusive of VAT)
- The VAT charged (if VAT-registered)
- The total amount
- The payment method and date payment was received

Issue sequentially numbered invoices for all sales. This is a legal requirement if you are VAT-registered, and it is best practice regardless. Gaps in your invoice numbering sequence can raise questions during a Revenue audit.

### Purchase and Expense Records

For every business purchase and expense, keep:

- The supplier's name and address
- The date of purchase
- A description of what was purchased
- The amount paid (exclusive of VAT)
- The VAT amount (if applicable)
- A copy of the invoice or receipt

Common business expenses for Irish businesses include:

- Office rent and utilities
- Telephone and broadband
- Professional services (accountant, solicitor)
- Insurance premiums
- Advertising and marketing
- Software subscriptions
- Travel and subsistence (subject to Revenue's civil service rates)
- Motor expenses (fuel, insurance, maintenance, leasing -- proportioned for business use)
- Home office expenses (if you work from home -- Revenue allows a proportion of household costs)
- Bank charges and interest
- Training and professional development
- Stationery and office supplies

### Bank Reconciliation Records

Bank reconciliation is the process of matching the transactions in your bookkeeping records against your bank statements to ensure they agree. Any discrepancy must be investigated and resolved.

Perform bank reconciliation at least monthly. This catches errors, identifies missing transactions, and ensures your bookkeeping records are accurate. Most bookkeeping software can import bank transactions directly from your bank, making reconciliation significantly faster.

### Payroll Records

If you have employees (including directors taking a salary from a limited company), you must keep:

- Each employee's name, address, and PPS number
- The Employment Detail Summary for each employee
- Gross pay, deductions (income tax, USC, employee PRSI), and net pay for each pay period
- Employer PRSI calculations
- Revenue payroll submissions and receipts
- P45s (for employees who leave)

Under Ireland's Enhanced Reporting Requirements, payroll information must be submitted to Revenue on or before each pay date. Your payroll software handles this automatically if configured correctly.

### VAT Records

If you are VAT-registered, you must maintain additional records including:

- A VAT account showing total output VAT (charged on sales) and total input VAT (paid on purchases) for each return period
- Copies of all VAT invoices issued and received
- Credit notes and debit notes
- Import and export documentation
- Intra-Community supply and acquisition records
- The annual Return of Trading Details (RTD) summary

## Single-Entry vs Double-Entry Bookkeeping

There are two fundamental methods of bookkeeping, and the one you use depends on the size and complexity of your business.

### Single-Entry Bookkeeping

Single-entry bookkeeping records each transaction once, typically in a simple income and expense format. You record money coming in and money going out, and the difference is your profit or loss. This is essentially a list of transactions, similar to a personal bank statement with categories.

Single-entry bookkeeping is suitable for sole traders with simple businesses, low transaction volumes, and no significant assets or liabilities. It is easy to set up and maintain, and an Excel spreadsheet can handle it for very small businesses.

The main limitation is that single-entry bookkeeping does not track assets, liabilities, or equity. It tells you your income and expenses for the period, but it does not tell you the overall financial position of your business (what you own, what you owe, and what your business is worth).

### Double-Entry Bookkeeping

Double-entry bookkeeping records every transaction twice: once as a debit and once as a credit. This method produces a complete set of financial statements including a profit and loss account and a balance sheet.

Every transaction affects at least two accounts. For example, if you buy office supplies for EUR 100 cash:
- Debit: Office Supplies Expense (increase in expenses) -- EUR 100
- Credit: Bank Account (decrease in asset) -- EUR 100

The total debits always equal the total credits, which provides a built-in error-checking mechanism. If your accounts do not balance, there is an error somewhere that needs to be found and corrected.

Double-entry bookkeeping is required for limited companies (which must prepare full financial statements) and is recommended for any sole trader with a growing or complex business. All modern bookkeeping software uses double-entry bookkeeping by default.

## Cash Basis vs Accrual Basis Accounting

Another fundamental choice in bookkeeping is when to recognise income and expenses: when cash changes hands (cash basis) or when the transaction occurs (accrual basis).

### Cash Basis

Under the cash basis, you record income when you receive payment and expenses when you make payment. If you issue an invoice in December but the customer does not pay until January, the income is recorded in January.

The cash basis is simpler and gives you a clear picture of actual cash flow. It is commonly used by sole traders and small service businesses with straightforward transactions.

### Accrual Basis

Under the accrual basis, you record income when you earn it (when you issue the invoice or deliver the goods) and expenses when you incur them (when you receive the supplier's invoice), regardless of when cash changes hands. This matches income with the expenses incurred to generate it, giving a more accurate picture of profitability.

The accrual basis is required for limited companies under generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) and is recommended for any business that extends credit to customers or receives credit from suppliers.

### Which Should You Use

Most sole traders in Ireland use the cash basis for its simplicity. Most limited companies use the accrual basis because it is required for their statutory financial statements. If you are a sole trader but want a more accurate picture of your profitability, you can adopt the accrual basis voluntarily.

Your accountant can advise which basis is most appropriate for your business and will adjust your figures if needed when preparing your tax return.

## Chart of Accounts Basics

A chart of accounts is a categorised list of all the accounts used in your bookkeeping system. Think of it as the filing system for your financial transactions. Every transaction is assigned to a specific account, and the chart of accounts determines how your financial data is organised and reported.

A typical chart of accounts for an Irish small business has four main sections: **Assets** (bank accounts, equipment, accounts receivable), **Liabilities** (accounts payable, VAT liability, loans), **Income** (sales revenue, service fees), and **Expenses** (rent, utilities, insurance, professional fees, motor expenses, advertising).

Most bookkeeping software comes with a default chart of accounts that you can customise. Ask your accountant to review it during setup to ensure it captures the categories they need for your tax return.

## Bookkeeping Software Options for Ireland

Choosing the right bookkeeping software is one of the most impactful decisions for your business administration. The right software saves time, reduces errors, and makes it easier and cheaper to work with your accountant.

### Xero

**Price:** EUR 15 to EUR 50 per month (Starter, Standard, Premium tiers)

Xero is one of the most widely used cloud bookkeeping platforms in Ireland. It integrates with Irish banks (AIB, Bank of Ireland, Permanent TSB, Revolut) for automatic transaction importing and connects with most Irish accountancy firms. Key features include invoicing, expense tracking, bank reconciliation, VAT return preparation, payroll (via add-on), and multi-currency support.

Xero is ideal for service businesses, freelancers, and small companies. Its user interface is clean and relatively intuitive for non-accountants.

### Sage Business Cloud Accounting

**Price:** EUR 12 to EUR 35 per month

Sage has a long history in the Irish market and is well-known among Irish accountants. Sage Business Cloud Accounting offers invoicing, expense management, bank feeds, VAT compliance, and reporting. Sage also offers a more advanced product, Sage 50, for larger businesses that need inventory management and more detailed reporting.

Sage is particularly strong for businesses that need Irish-specific compliance features and local support.

### QuickBooks Online

**Price:** EUR 12 to EUR 32 per month (Simple Start, Essentials, Plus tiers)

QuickBooks Online is a global platform with a growing presence in Ireland. It offers invoicing, expense tracking, bank reconciliation, mileage tracking, and project profitability tracking. QuickBooks integrates with many third-party apps and offers strong mobile apps for iOS and Android.

QuickBooks is well-suited for businesses that trade internationally and need multi-currency capabilities, but its Irish bank integrations are less extensive than Xero's.

### Surf Accounts

**Price:** EUR 25 to EUR 50 per month

Surf Accounts is an Irish-developed cloud accounting platform designed specifically for the Irish market. It handles Irish VAT, payroll, and CRO compliance natively.

### Microsoft Excel or Google Sheets

**Price:** Free (Google Sheets) or included with Microsoft 365

For very small businesses with fewer than 50 transactions per month, a simple spreadsheet can work. However, spreadsheets lack automatic bank reconciliation, invoicing, and reporting. Most businesses outgrow spreadsheets within the first year.

### How to Choose

Ask your accountant what software they prefer before making a decision. Using the same system as your accountant reduces the time and cost of year-end accounts preparation, as they can access your data directly rather than re-entering it from exported files.

Consider your business needs:

- **Low volume, simple business:** Xero Starter or QuickBooks Simple Start
- **Growing business with employees:** Xero Standard or Sage Business Cloud
- **Irish-specific requirements:** Surf Accounts
- **International trade:** QuickBooks Online or Xero Premium
- **Just starting out, minimal budget:** Google Sheets (upgrade to software when transaction volume grows)

## Managing Expenses and Receipts

Expense management is one of the most common bookkeeping pain points for Irish small businesses. The key is to establish a simple system and follow it consistently.

### Receipt Capture

The moment you make a business purchase, capture the receipt. Options include:

- **Bookkeeping software receipt capture:** Most platforms (Xero, Sage, QuickBooks) have mobile apps that let you photograph a receipt and attach it directly to a transaction.
- **Dedicated receipt app:** Apps like Dext or AutoEntry scan receipts, extract key data, and push it into your bookkeeping software automatically (EUR 20 to EUR 50 per month).
- **Manual scan and file:** Photograph receipts and save them in a dated folder on Google Drive or Dropbox.

### Expense Categories

Categorise every expense when you record it, not at the end of the year. Common categories for Irish businesses are:

- Rent and rates
- Light and heat
- Insurance
- Repairs and maintenance
- Motor expenses
- Telephone and broadband
- Postage and stationery
- Advertising and marketing
- Professional fees
- Bank charges
- Travel and subsistence
- Staff costs
- Depreciation / capital allowances
- Entertainment (note: generally not tax-deductible in Ireland)

### The Shoe Box Problem

The biggest single driver of excessive accountant fees is poor record keeping. Handing your accountant a carrier bag of unsorted receipts at year end means they must spend hours sorting, categorising, and reconciling before they can begin preparing your accounts. This time is billed to you.

The solution is simple: spend 15 to 30 minutes per week entering transactions, categorising expenses, and reconciling your bank account. This weekly habit saves you hundreds of euros in accountant fees and gives you a real-time picture of your financial position.

## Preparing for Your Accountant

At year end, your accountant needs specific information to prepare your tax return and financial statements. Having this information organised in advance reduces their time (and your fees) and avoids last-minute stress.

### What Your Accountant Needs from You

1. **Access to your bookkeeping software** (or a backup/export of your data)
2. **Bank statements** for all business accounts for the full year (12 months)
3. **A summary of any transactions not captured in the bookkeeping** (cash transactions, personal card purchases for business)
4. **Motor expense details:** total mileage, business mileage, fuel costs, insurance, maintenance
5. **Home office details** (if applicable): total household costs (rent/mortgage interest, electricity, broadband, heating), percentage of home used for business, number of days working from home
6. **Details of any assets purchased or sold** during the year (equipment, vehicles, property)
7. **Loan statements** showing interest paid during the year
8. **Pension contribution certificates**
9. **Medical expense receipts** (for personal tax credits on the director's Form 11)
10. **Confirmation of stock levels at year end** (if applicable)

### Year-End Checklist

Before sending your records to your accountant, confirm: all transactions are entered, bank accounts are reconciled to your year-end date, outstanding invoices and bills are recorded (accrual basis), mileage logs are complete, all receipts are captured, and any personal transactions through the business account are identified.

## Common Bookkeeping Mistakes

Avoiding these common mistakes will keep your records accurate and your accountant fees manageable:

**Not recording cash transactions.** If you receive cash payments or make cash purchases, record them immediately. Cash transactions without receipts are easy to forget and difficult to reconstruct later.

**Mixing personal and business expenses.** Keep your personal and business finances completely separate. If you accidentally pay for a personal expense from the business account, record it as a drawing (sole trader) or director's loan (limited company) and reimburse the business.

**Ignoring bank reconciliation.** Reconcile your bank account at least monthly. Unreconciled accounts accumulate errors that become increasingly difficult to find and correct over time.

**Not categorising expenses correctly.** Putting every expense into a "miscellaneous" category makes it impossible to analyse your costs and may result in missed tax deductions.

**Forgetting to record depreciation.** If you have purchased business assets (equipment, vehicles, computers), you need to record capital allowances (depreciation for tax purposes) in your accounts. The standard capital allowance rate in Ireland is 12.5 per cent per year over 8 years for most assets.

**Not backing up data.** If you use desktop software or spreadsheets, back up your data regularly. Cloud-based software handles this automatically.

**Leaving bookkeeping until year end.** Reconstructing 12 months of transactions in January is stressful and error-prone. Stay current throughout the year.

## When to Hire a Bookkeeper vs DIY

As your business grows, there comes a point where doing your own bookkeeping is no longer the best use of your time. Consider hiring a bookkeeper when:

- You are spending more than 5 hours per week on bookkeeping
- Your transaction volume exceeds 100 to 200 per month
- You have employees and need to manage payroll
- You are VAT-registered and filing bi-monthly returns
- Your accountant is spending significant time correcting or completing your records
- You would rather spend the time on revenue-generating activities

A part-time bookkeeper in Ireland typically costs EUR 15 to EUR 30 per hour, or EUR 200 to EUR 500 per month for a small business. Many bookkeepers work remotely and can access your cloud bookkeeping software from anywhere.

Alternatively, your accountant may offer a monthly bookkeeping service as part of a retainer package. The cost of a bookkeeper is a tax-deductible business expense.
