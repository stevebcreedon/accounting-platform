Setting up a limited company in Ireland involves registering with the Companies Registration Office (CRO) using the Form A1, appointing at least one director and a company secretary, adopting a company constitution, and paying a registration fee of EUR 50 online. The entire process takes approximately 5 business days when filed electronically through CORE, the CRO's online filing system.

Most small businesses in Ireland choose to incorporate as an LTD (private company limited by shares), which is the standard company type under the Companies Act 2014. This guide walks you through every step of the process, from choosing a company name to completing your post-incorporation obligations with Revenue.

## LTD vs DAC: Choosing Your Company Type

Before you begin the registration process, you need to decide which type of limited company to form. The two most common types for small businesses are:

**LTD (Private Company Limited by Shares):** This is the default and most popular company type in Ireland. An LTD can have between 1 and 149 shareholders, requires at least one director and a separate company secretary, and operates under a single-document constitution. There is no restriction on the types of business activities an LTD can carry on.

**DAC (Designated Activity Company):** A DAC is similar to the old-style private limited company. It has an objects clause in its constitution that restricts the activities the company can carry on. DACs are typically used for regulated businesses, joint ventures, or situations where lenders or regulators require a restriction on the company's activities. Most small businesses do not need a DAC.

For the vast majority of Irish small businesses, sole traders incorporating, freelancers, and startup founders, an LTD is the correct choice. This guide focuses on the LTD formation process.

## What You Need Before You Start

Before filing your Form A1 with the CRO, gather the following:

**Company name.** You need a unique name that is not identical or misleadingly similar to any existing company on the CRO register. Check availability at core.cro.ie. The name must end in "Limited" or "Ltd" (or the Irish equivalents "Teoranta" or "Teo"). You cannot use certain restricted words like "bank," "insurance," or "university" without ministerial consent.

**Registered office address.** Every Irish company must have a registered office address in the Republic of Ireland. This is the address where official correspondence and legal documents will be delivered. It can be your home address, your business premises, or a registered office service provider. The address is publicly available on the CRO register.

**Directors.** An LTD company must have at least one director. You can have up to 25 directors. At least one director must be resident in the European Economic Area (EEA). If no director is EEA-resident, the company must hold a Section 137 bond (approximately EUR 2,000 per year) to cover potential penalties. Each director needs a Personal Public Service (PPS) number.

**Company secretary.** Every Irish company must have a company secretary. The secretary cannot be the sole director of the company -- if you are a single-director company, you must appoint a separate person or a corporate body as secretary. The secretary can be another individual, an accountant, or a company formation agent.

**Shareholders.** An LTD company must have at least one shareholder. The maximum is 149. Shareholders can be individuals or corporate bodies. You need to decide on the share capital structure, including the number of shares, their nominal value, and how they will be allocated among the shareholders.

**Constitution.** An LTD company operates under a single-document constitution that replaces the old memorandum and articles of association. The CRO provides template constitutions, and most company formation agents include a standard constitution in their service. The constitution sets out the company's internal regulations, including how directors are appointed, how meetings are conducted, and how shares can be transferred.

## CRO Registration: The Form A1

The Form A1 is the application to incorporate a company in Ireland. It is submitted to the Companies Registration Office and contains all the essential details about your new company.

### Filing Online via CORE

The CRO's online filing system is called CORE (Companies Online Registration Environment), accessible at core.cro.ie. Online filing is faster, cheaper, and recommended by the CRO.

To file online, you need a CORE account. Registration is free and straightforward. Once logged in, you can complete the Form A1 electronically, upload your company constitution, and pay the registration fee by credit or debit card.

The Form A1 requires the following information:

1. **Company name** (as approved or checked for availability)
2. **Company type** (LTD for most small businesses)
3. **Registered office address** in Ireland
4. **Details of each director:** full name, date of birth, nationality, residential address, occupation, and PPS number
5. **Details of the company secretary:** full name and address (or corporate name and registered office if a body corporate)
6. **Details of each shareholder:** name, address, number of shares, and nominal value
7. **Share capital:** total authorised share capital and how it is divided into shares
8. **Confirmation of EEA residency** for at least one director, or details of the Section 137 bond
9. **Company constitution** (uploaded as a PDF or created using the CRO's online template)

### Registration Fees

- **Online filing (CORE):** EUR 50
- **Paper filing:** EUR 100
- **Express processing (optional):** EUR 100 additional for 2-business-day turnaround

### Processing Times

Standard online applications are processed within approximately 5 business days. Paper applications take 10 to 15 business days. Express processing reduces the online timeline to 2 business days.

The CRO may reject an application if the proposed name is too similar to an existing registered name, if the form is incomplete, or if the constitution does not comply with the Companies Act 2014. Rejections require correction and resubmission, which adds to the timeline.

## After Incorporation: Post-Registration Steps

Once the CRO approves your application, you receive a Certificate of Incorporation. This confirms that your company legally exists. The certificate includes your company's CRO number, which you will use for all future filings.

### Revenue Registration (TR2 Form)

Your new company must register for tax with the Revenue Commissioners. This is done using the TR2 form, which registers the company for:

- **Corporation tax** (mandatory for all companies)
- **Employer PAYE/PRSI** (mandatory if you have employees or pay director salaries)
- **VAT** (mandatory if turnover exceeds the relevant threshold, otherwise voluntary)
- **Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT)** (if applicable, mainly construction industry)

You can submit the TR2 online through the Revenue Online Service (ROS) at ros.ie. To access ROS for the company, you need to apply for a digital certificate, which Revenue posts to your registered office address. This process takes approximately 5 to 7 working days.

Until your ROS digital certificate arrives, you can submit a paper TR2 form to your local Revenue office. Many accountants recommend submitting the TR2 immediately after incorporation to avoid delays in starting to trade.

### Opening a Company Bank Account

A limited company must have its own bank account, separate from any personal accounts. Irish banks require the following to open a business account:

- Certificate of Incorporation from the CRO
- Company constitution
- Board resolution authorising the opening of the account and appointing signatories
- Identification and proof of address for all directors and beneficial owners
- Details of the company's business activities and expected turnover

The process can take 2 to 6 weeks depending on the bank, as they carry out anti-money laundering (AML) due diligence. Apply as early as possible after incorporation to avoid delays in trading.

### Register of Beneficial Ownership (RBO)

Since 2019, all Irish companies must register their beneficial owners on the Register of Beneficial Ownership, maintained by the RBO at rbo.gov.ie. A beneficial owner is any individual who directly or indirectly holds more than 25 per cent of the shares or voting rights, or who otherwise exercises control over the company.

You must file your beneficial ownership information within 5 months of incorporation. Failure to register is a criminal offence, with penalties of up to EUR 500,000.

## Company Secretary Requirement

The role of company secretary is a legal requirement under the Companies Act 2014. The secretary is responsible for ensuring the company complies with its filing obligations, maintaining statutory registers, and handling administrative duties.

In a single-director company, the sole director cannot also be the secretary. You must appoint a separate individual or body corporate. Options include:

- A family member, friend, or business associate willing to take on the role
- Your accountant (many offer company secretary services for EUR 150 to EUR 400 per year)
- A company formation agent or corporate services provider

The secretary does not need any formal qualifications, although they should understand their duties and the consequences of non-compliance.

## Share Capital Structure

When setting up your company, you need to decide on the share capital. Most small single-owner companies start with a simple structure:

- **1 ordinary share with a nominal value of EUR 1:** This is the simplest possible structure. The sole shareholder holds the single share and has 100 per cent ownership and voting rights.

- **100 ordinary shares at EUR 1 each:** This provides flexibility for future share transfers or bringing in additional shareholders without needing to create new shares.

The nominal value of shares does not represent their market value. It is simply the minimum price at which shares can be issued. Most small companies use EUR 1 per share for simplicity.

If you plan to have multiple shareholders, consider how the shares will be split and whether different classes of shares (ordinary, preference) are needed. A shareholder agreement is strongly recommended for multi-shareholder companies.

## Common Mistakes When Setting Up a Company

**Not checking name availability thoroughly.** The CRO checks for identical or misleadingly similar names. Even if a name passes the online check, the CRO examiner may reject it during processing. Check the register carefully and consider phonetic similarities.

**Appointing the sole director as secretary.** This is not permitted under the Companies Act 2014 for LTD companies. If you are a single-director company, you must appoint a separate secretary.

**Forgetting the RBO filing.** The Register of Beneficial Ownership filing is often overlooked because it is a separate process from the CRO incorporation. Missing the 5-month deadline is a criminal offence.

**Delaying Revenue registration.** You cannot trade, issue invoices, or pay yourself a salary until you have a tax registration. Submit the TR2 form as soon as you receive your Certificate of Incorporation.

**No shareholder agreement.** If there are multiple shareholders, operating without a shareholder agreement is a significant risk. Disputes between shareholders without a clear agreement can be expensive and destructive.

**Ignoring the annual return date.** The CRO assigns an Annual Return Date (ARD) 6 months after incorporation. Missing this date by even one day triggers the loss of audit exemption for two financial years. Put the date in your calendar immediately.

## Using a Company Formation Agent

Many business owners choose to use a company formation agent or their accountant to handle the incorporation process. A formation agent typically provides:

- Preparation and filing of the Form A1
- A standard company constitution
- Company secretary services
- Registered office address
- Initial statutory registers and minute book
- Guidance on post-incorporation steps

Formation agents charge between EUR 200 and EUR 500 for the basic incorporation service. The main advantage is convenience and the assurance that everything is filed correctly. The main disadvantage is the cost, given that the CRO's online system makes DIY incorporation straightforward.

If you plan to use an accountant for your annual compliance anyway, many offer a discounted incorporation service as part of an ongoing client relationship. This can be the most cost-effective approach.

## Timeline: From Decision to Trading

Here is a realistic timeline for incorporating and starting to trade as a limited company in Ireland:

| Step | Timeframe |
|------|-----------|
| Gather information and choose name | 1-3 days |
| File Form A1 on CORE | 1 day |
| CRO processing | 5 business days (2 with express) |
| Apply for ROS digital certificate | 1 day |
| Receive ROS certificate | 5-7 working days |
| Submit TR2 and register for taxes | 1 day |
| Revenue processing | 3-5 working days |
| Open bank account | 2-6 weeks |

The main bottleneck is usually the bank account opening. You can begin trading once you have your tax registration, even before the bank account is fully set up, but you will need the account to receive payments and pay expenses.
