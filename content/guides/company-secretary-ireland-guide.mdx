Every limited company in Ireland must have a company secretary. This is a legal requirement under Section 129 of the Companies Act 2014, and there are no exceptions. The company secretary is responsible for ensuring the company meets its statutory filing obligations, maintains proper registers, and complies with company law. In a single-director company, the sole director cannot also serve as the company secretary -- a separate person or body corporate must be appointed.

Despite its name, the role of company secretary in an Irish company is not administrative in the traditional sense. It is a statutory officer position with legal responsibilities and potential personal liability for certain filing failures. This guide explains who can be a company secretary, what the role involves, and how to manage this obligation effectively for your Irish business.

## The Legal Requirement

The requirement for a company secretary is set out in the Companies Act 2014. Section 129 states that every company must have a secretary, and Section 130 provides that a sole director cannot also be the secretary. These provisions apply to all company types, including LTD, DAC, CLG, and PLC companies.

The company secretary must be named on the company's incorporation documents (Form A1) when the company is first registered with the Companies Registration Office (CRO). If you do not name a secretary on the Form A1, the CRO will reject your application.

There is no requirement for the company secretary to hold any formal qualifications for private limited companies (LTD and DAC). However, for public limited companies (PLCs), the directors must ensure the secretary has the necessary knowledge and experience to carry out the role, and the secretary must hold certain professional qualifications.

For the vast majority of Irish small businesses operating as LTD companies, the company secretary can be any individual over 18 years of age, or a body corporate (another company or a professional services firm).

## Who Can Be a Company Secretary

The flexibility around who can serve as company secretary for private companies gives business owners several options:

**A co-director.** If your company has two or more directors, one director can also serve as company secretary. This is the simplest arrangement for multi-director companies and costs nothing.

**A family member or trusted associate.** You can appoint a spouse, partner, family member, or friend as company secretary. This is common for small owner-managed businesses. The person should understand the basic obligations of the role, even if they rely on the company's accountant for guidance.

**Your accountant.** Many Irish accountancy firms offer company secretary services as part of their annual compliance package. This is one of the most popular options because your accountant already handles the company's annual returns and financial statements, so combining the secretary role is a natural fit. Typical fees range from EUR 150 to EUR 400 per year.

**A company formation agent or corporate services provider.** Dedicated company secretarial firms provide the service on an ongoing basis. They handle all filings, maintain registers, and ensure compliance. This is common for companies with non-resident directors or for businesses that want to keep the role entirely separate from their internal team.

**A body corporate.** Another company can act as company secretary. This is how corporate services firms typically provide the service. The body corporate is named as secretary, and the firm's staff handle the duties on its behalf.

**Who cannot be a company secretary:**

- The sole director of a single-director company. This is explicitly prohibited by Section 130 of the Companies Act 2014.
- A person who has been disqualified from acting as a company director or secretary by a court order.
- A body corporate whose sole director is also the sole director of the company requiring a secretary. This would circumvent the prohibition on a sole director acting as secretary.

## Duties and Responsibilities

The company secretary's duties are primarily administrative and compliance-focused. While the directors are responsible for the strategic management of the company, the secretary ensures that the company's legal and filing obligations are met.

### Statutory Filing Obligations

The most critical duty of the company secretary is ensuring that the company's filings are made on time. The key filings include:

**Annual return (Form B1).** Every Irish company must file an annual return with the CRO within 28 days of its annual return date (ARD). The annual return includes details of the company's directors, secretary, registered office, shareholders, and share capital. Financial statements must be attached to the annual return.

Missing the annual return deadline has severe consequences. A late filing attracts a penalty of EUR 100 per day, up to a maximum of EUR 5,000 per year. More significantly, late filing results in the loss of the company's audit exemption for two financial years, which typically costs EUR 3,000 to EUR 10,000 or more in audit fees. This is one of the most expensive compliance mistakes a small Irish company can make.

**Changes in company details (various forms).** Whenever there is a change in the company's directors, secretary, registered office, share capital, or other registrable details, the company secretary must file the appropriate form with the CRO. Common forms include:

| Form | Purpose | Filing Deadline |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| B10 | Change of director or secretary | 14 days of change |
| B2 | Change of registered office | 14 days of change |
| B5 | Return of allotment of shares | 30 days of allotment |
| B73 | Special resolution | 15 days of passing |
| B74 | Ordinary resolution (where required) | 15 days of passing |

**Annual financial statements.** The secretary ensures that the company's financial statements are prepared, approved by the directors, and filed with the annual return. For small companies availing of the audit exemption, this includes an abridged balance sheet, an abridged profit and loss account, and the directors' report.

### Maintaining Statutory Registers

The Companies Act 2014 requires every company to maintain several statutory registers. The company secretary is responsible for keeping these up to date:

- **Register of members (shareholders):** Names, addresses, dates of becoming and ceasing to be a member, and number of shares held
- **Register of directors and secretary:** Names, addresses, dates of birth, nationalities, and occupations
- **Register of directors' and secretary's interests:** Any shares or debentures held by directors or secretary in the company or related companies
- **Register of charges:** Details of any mortgages or charges on the company's assets
- **Minutes of general meetings and directors' meetings:** Records of decisions made and resolutions passed

These registers must be kept at the company's registered office or at another address notified to the CRO. They must be available for inspection by members of the company and, in some cases, by the public.

### Organising Meetings

The company secretary is responsible for the administrative aspects of company meetings:

- Issuing notices of general meetings to shareholders (21 days' notice for an AGM, 7 days for other general meetings, unless shorter notice is agreed)
- Preparing agendas and circulating relevant documents
- Recording minutes of meetings and ensuring they are signed by the chairperson
- Filing any resolutions passed at meetings with the CRO where required

For small owner-managed companies, the meeting requirements are often streamlined. An LTD company can pass written resolutions without holding a physical meeting, provided all shareholders sign the resolution. Many small companies use written resolutions for routine matters.

### Company Seal and Statutory Books

While the use of a company seal is no longer mandatory for LTD companies under the Companies Act 2014, many companies still use one. If the company has a seal, the secretary is typically responsible for its custody and proper use.

The secretary also ensures that the company's statutory books (registers, minute books, and accounting records) are properly maintained and stored securely.

## The Secretary's Liability

The company secretary can be personally liable for certain failures. Under the Companies Act 2014:

**Filing penalties.** If the company fails to file its annual return on time, the secretary (along with the directors) can be subject to prosecution. The CRO can initiate prosecution proceedings for persistent default.

**Failure to maintain registers.** If the company fails to maintain its statutory registers, the secretary and every officer in default are guilty of a category 3 offence, which can result in a fine.

**Incorrect filings.** Filing false or misleading information with the CRO is a criminal offence. The secretary who files the documents can be held responsible.

In practice, prosecution of company secretaries of small companies is relatively rare. The CRO tends to focus enforcement on persistent non-filers and companies with significant compliance failures. However, the potential liability underscores the importance of taking the role seriously.

## Appointing and Changing a Company Secretary

### Initial Appointment

The first company secretary is named on the Form A1 when the company is incorporated. Their appointment takes effect from the date of incorporation.

### Changing the Secretary

To change the company secretary after incorporation, the company must:

1. **Pass a board resolution** appointing the new secretary and accepting the resignation of the outgoing secretary (or removing them)
2. **File Form B10** with the CRO within 14 days of the change
3. **Update the register of directors and secretary** in the company's statutory books
4. **Notify the company's accountant and bank** of the change

The CRO filing fee for a Form B10 is EUR 15 online. The new secretary's details become part of the public record.

### Resignation

A company secretary can resign at any time by giving notice to the company (usually in writing to the board of directors). However, the company must immediately appoint a replacement, as it is illegal for a company to operate without a secretary.

If a secretary resigns and no replacement is appointed, the directors become personally responsible for the secretary's duties and are in breach of the Companies Act 2014.

## Outsourcing the Company Secretary Role

For most small Irish businesses, outsourcing the company secretary role to an accountant or corporate services firm is the most practical approach. Here is why:

**Your accountant already handles compliance.** If your accountant prepares your financial statements and files your annual return, adding the secretary role is a natural extension. They already have all the company's information and understand its filing obligations.

**It ensures nothing is missed.** Professional secretarial services maintain compliance calendars, send reminders for filing deadlines, and ensure that changes in company details are filed promptly. This reduces the risk of late filings and the associated penalties.

**It frees up your time.** As a business owner, your time is better spent on running and growing your business than on filing CRO forms and maintaining statutory registers.

**Typical costs.** Company secretary services from an accountant or formation agent typically cost EUR 150 to EUR 400 per year. This is a modest investment compared to the potential cost of missing a filing deadline, which can run into thousands of euros if audit exemption is lost.

When outsourcing, ensure your agreement clearly sets out what is included. A basic company secretary service should cover the annual return filing, maintenance of statutory registers, filing of changes (B10, B2, etc.), and correspondence with the CRO.

## Company Secretary vs Director: What Is the Difference

Many small business owners confuse the roles of director and company secretary. While both are statutory officers, their responsibilities are distinct:

| Aspect | Director | Company Secretary |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| Primary role | Strategic management and decision-making | Administrative compliance and filings |
| Liability | Personal liability for reckless/fraudulent trading | Liability for filing failures |
| Qualifications | No formal requirement (LTD companies) | No formal requirement (LTD companies) |
| Minimum number | 1 | 1 |
| Can be same person | Yes (if not sole director) | Yes (if not sole director of single-director company) |
| Registered with | CRO (Form A1 / B10) | CRO (Form A1 / B10) |
| Listed on public register | Yes | Yes |

In many small companies, the distinction between the two roles is blurred in practice. The director makes all the business decisions and the accountant handles the secretary's duties behind the scenes. This is perfectly acceptable as long as a formal secretary is appointed and named on the CRO register.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Failing to comply with company secretary obligations can result in:

- **Late annual return:** EUR 100 per day penalty (up to EUR 5,000/year) plus loss of audit exemption for two financial years
- **Failure to file Form B10 within 14 days:** Category 3 offence, fine on summary conviction
- **Failure to maintain statutory registers:** Category 3 offence, fine on summary conviction
- **Operating without a secretary:** Breach of Section 129, the directors are personally responsible
- **Filing false information:** Criminal offence, potential imprisonment

The most common and costly penalty in practice is the late annual return, which triggers the loss of audit exemption. For a small company, the cost of a statutory audit can be EUR 3,000 to EUR 10,000, making it one of the most expensive administrative failures in Irish company law.
