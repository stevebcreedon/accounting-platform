Bank reconciliation is the process of comparing your business's accounting records against your bank statements to ensure they match. It is one of the most fundamental bookkeeping tasks for any Irish business, and yet it is one of the most commonly neglected. A regular bank reconciliation catches errors, prevents fraud, and ensures your financial records are accurate. Revenue auditors routinely check bank reconciliations during an audit, and an unreconciled set of books is one of the first red flags they look for.

Whether you do your own bookkeeping or outsource it, understanding the bank reconciliation process is essential. This guide walks you through the process step by step, explains common issues you will encounter, and shows you how modern accounting software simplifies what was once a tedious manual task.

## What Bank Reconciliation Is

Bank reconciliation is the process of matching the transactions in your accounting records (your books) against the transactions on your bank statement to identify and explain any differences. When you reconcile, you are answering one question: does the balance in your books match the balance on your bank statement?

In a perfect world, these two figures would always match. In practice, they rarely do at any given point in time because of timing differences, errors, and transactions that appear in one record but not the other. The reconciliation process identifies these differences, explains each one, and ensures they resolve over time.

### Why the Balances Differ

There are several legitimate reasons why your book balance and bank balance may differ:

**Unpresented cheques:** You have written a cheque and recorded it in your books, but the recipient has not yet lodged it at their bank. The payment has reduced your book balance but not your bank balance.

**Lodgements in transit:** You have recorded a customer payment or cash lodgement in your books, but it has not yet appeared on your bank statement because it was lodged late in the day or on a weekend.

**Bank charges and interest:** Your bank deducts charges and adds interest directly to your account. These may not appear in your books until you see the bank statement.

**Direct debits and standing orders:** Regular payments such as insurance, loan repayments, and utility bills may appear on your bank statement before you record them in your books.

**Errors:** Either your books or the bank statement (rarely) contain an error. Common errors include entering the wrong amount, recording a transaction twice, or missing a transaction entirely.

## Why Bank Reconciliation Matters

### Accuracy of Financial Statements

Your financial statements (profit and loss account and balance sheet) are only as accurate as the underlying data. If your bank balance in your books does not match reality, your balance sheet is wrong. If transactions are missing from your books, your profit and loss account is incomplete. Regular reconciliation ensures your financial statements reflect the true position of your business.

### Fraud Detection

Bank reconciliation is one of the primary controls for detecting fraud. If someone with access to your bank account makes an unauthorised payment, the reconciliation process should catch it because the payment will appear on the bank statement but not in your books (if the fraudster did not record it) or the books will show a different payee.

For this reason, the person performing the bank reconciliation should ideally be someone other than the person who authorises payments. In a small business where the same person does both, review the reconciliation carefully and question any unfamiliar transactions.

### Revenue Audit Readiness

During a Revenue audit, the auditor will typically request your bank statements and ask to see your bank reconciliations. They use the reconciliation to verify that all income has been recorded (by checking lodgements) and that all expense deductions are genuine (by matching payments to invoices).

An unreconciled set of books tells the Revenue auditor that your records may be unreliable, which increases the likelihood of a more extensive and intrusive audit. A clean, up-to-date reconciliation demonstrates good financial management.

### Cash Flow Visibility

You cannot manage your cash flow effectively if you do not know your true bank balance. The balance in your accounting software might be different from your actual bank balance because of timing differences. Regular reconciliation keeps you informed of your actual cash position.

## Step-by-Step Bank Reconciliation Process

### Step 1: Obtain Your Bank Statement

Get your bank statement for the period you are reconciling. Most Irish banks (AIB, Bank of Ireland, Permanent TSB, Ulster Bank) provide statements online through their business banking portals. Download the statement in PDF or CSV format. If your accounting software has bank feeds, the transactions may already be imported.

### Step 2: Compare Opening Balances

Start by confirming that the opening balance on your bank statement matches the closing balance from your previous reconciliation. If these do not match, you have an issue from a previous period that needs to be resolved first.

### Step 3: Match Transactions

Go through each transaction on your bank statement and match it to the corresponding entry in your books. In practice, you are looking for transactions that appear in both places with the same date (approximately), amount, and description.

**Matched transactions** are those that appear correctly in both your books and your bank statement. These need no further action.

**Unmatched bank transactions** appear on the bank statement but not in your books. These need to be recorded. Common examples include bank charges, interest, direct debits you forgot to enter, and customer payments you have not yet recorded.

**Unmatched book transactions** appear in your books but not on the bank statement. These are usually timing differences (unpresented cheques or lodgements in transit). They should appear on a future bank statement. If they remain unmatched for an extended period, investigate.

### Step 4: Record Missing Transactions

For any bank statement transactions that are not in your books, create the appropriate accounting entries:

- Bank charges: debit bank charges expense, credit bank account
- Interest received: debit bank account, credit interest income
- Direct debits: debit the appropriate expense account, credit bank account
- Customer payments not yet recorded: debit bank account, credit trade debtors

### Step 5: Investigate Discrepancies

If the balances do not match after completing the steps above, you have a discrepancy that needs investigation. Common causes include:

- A transaction entered with the wrong amount (e.g., EUR 1,250 instead of EUR 1,520)
- A transaction recorded twice in your books
- A transaction missing entirely from your books
- A payment or receipt allocated to the wrong bank account (if you have multiple accounts)

Check each unmatched item systematically. For small discrepancies, check for transposition errors (swapping digits, such as 54 instead of 45). For larger discrepancies, review the individual transactions that do not match.

### Step 6: Prepare the Reconciliation Statement

Once you have identified all differences, prepare a formal reconciliation statement. This document starts with the bank statement balance and adjusts for the known timing differences to arrive at your book balance (or vice versa).

A standard reconciliation statement looks like this:

**Balance per bank statement:** EUR XX,XXX.XX

**Add: Lodgements in transit** (recorded in books, not yet on statement)
- Customer A payment: EUR X,XXX.XX
- Cash lodgement 28th: EUR XXX.XX

**Less: Unpresented cheques** (recorded in books, not yet cleared)
- Cheque 1234 to Supplier B: EUR X,XXX.XX
- Cheque 1235 to Supplier C: EUR XXX.XX

**Adjusted bank balance:** EUR XX,XXX.XX

**Balance per books:** EUR XX,XXX.XX (should match the adjusted bank balance)

If the adjusted bank balance equals your book balance, your reconciliation is complete.

### Step 7: File the Reconciliation

Save your reconciliation statement, the bank statement it relates to, and any notes about unusual items. Keep these records for at least 6 years, consistent with Revenue's record retention requirements. Many businesses file reconciliations monthly in a dedicated folder (physical or digital).

## How Often to Reconcile

### Monthly (Minimum)

Every Irish business should reconcile its bank accounts at least monthly. This is the standard expected by accountants, banks, and Revenue. Monthly reconciliation catches errors and missing transactions within a reasonable timeframe and ensures your monthly management accounts are accurate.

### Weekly

Businesses with high transaction volumes (retail, hospitality, construction) benefit from weekly reconciliation. The more transactions you process, the more likely errors or omissions will occur, and the harder they are to find if left for a month.

### Daily

Very high-volume businesses (shops with multiple tills, online retailers) should reconcile daily. This is also recommended for businesses that have experienced fraud or have concerns about unauthorised transactions.

The key principle is: reconcile frequently enough that you can easily identify and resolve discrepancies while they are still fresh.

## Bank Feeds and Automation

Modern cloud accounting software has transformed bank reconciliation from a manual, time-consuming process into a largely automated one. Bank feeds import transactions from your bank account directly into your accounting software, typically overnight.

### How Bank Feeds Work

Your accounting software connects to your bank through a secure data feed (using open banking APIs or aggregation services). Transactions from your bank account are imported into the software automatically, usually once per day.

Once imported, the software suggests matches between bank transactions and existing entries in your books. You review the suggestions and confirm or amend the matches. Transactions with no match (such as bank charges or unexpected payments) are flagged for you to categorise.

### Benefits of Bank Feeds

- **Time saving:** Eliminates manual data entry of bank transactions
- **Accuracy:** Reduces transcription errors (the amounts come directly from the bank)
- **Timeliness:** Transactions are available the next day, not when you collect a paper statement
- **Matching suggestions:** The software learns your patterns and becomes more accurate over time

### Bank Feed Availability in Ireland

Most cloud accounting platforms support bank feeds for the major Irish banks:

- **AIB:** Supported by Xero, Sage, QuickBooks
- **Bank of Ireland:** Supported by Xero, Sage, QuickBooks
- **Permanent TSB:** Supported by most major platforms
- **Ulster Bank/NatWest:** Supported
- **Revolut Business:** Supported by Xero and others
- **N26 Business:** Supported through open banking

Check with your specific software provider for the most up-to-date list of supported banks.

### Automation Does Not Replace Review

Even with bank feeds and automatic matching, you must still review the reconciliation. Automation handles the matching, but you need to:

- Confirm that the suggested matches are correct
- Categorise unmatched transactions
- Investigate any discrepancies
- Approve the reconciliation

Never blindly accept all suggested matches. A few minutes of review each week prevents errors from compounding.

## Common Issues and How to Resolve Them

### Unpresented Cheques That Never Clear

If you wrote a cheque months ago and it still has not been presented, contact the payee to confirm they received it. Cheques in Ireland are valid for 6 months from the date of issue. If a cheque expires without being presented, you may need to reissue it or reverse the entry in your books.

### Duplicate Entries

If a transaction appears twice in your books (perhaps entered manually and also imported via bank feed), one entry needs to be deleted. This is a common issue when transitioning to bank feeds from manual entry.

### Foreign Currency Transactions

If you receive payments or make purchases in a currency other than euro, your bank may convert the amount at a different rate from the one you used in your books. The small difference (exchange rate gain or loss) needs to be recorded to bring the reconciliation into balance.

### Multiple Bank Accounts

If your business has multiple bank accounts (current account, savings account, credit card, foreign currency account), each one needs to be reconciled separately. Transfers between your own accounts need to be recorded carefully to avoid double-counting.

### Year-End Reconciliation

Your year-end bank reconciliation is particularly important because it directly affects your balance sheet. Ensure it is complete and accurate before your accountant prepares your year-end accounts. Provide your accountant with the bank statement and reconciliation statement for the year-end date.

## Signs of Errors or Fraud

During your bank reconciliation, watch for these warning signs:

- **Round number payments** that do not match any invoice or known expense
- **Payments to unfamiliar payees** that no one in the business can explain
- **Duplicate payments** to the same supplier for the same amount
- **Unexplained cash withdrawals**
- **Direct debits you did not authorise**
- **A growing list of old unmatched items** that nobody investigates

If you discover a suspicious transaction, do not alter the records. Note it, secure the evidence (bank statement, transaction details), and contact your bank immediately if you suspect fraud. For significant amounts, also notify An Garda Siochana.

## Revenue Audit Preparation

During a Revenue audit, the auditor uses your bank reconciliations to verify the completeness and accuracy of your records. They will:

1. Obtain bank statements directly from your bank (not just the ones you provide)
2. Check that all lodgements can be traced to sales or other income recorded in your books
3. Verify that expense deductions match payments on the bank statement
4. Look for cash lodgements that do not correspond to recorded income
5. Investigate personal transactions passing through the business account

Having clean, up-to-date bank reconciliations with supporting documentation for every reconciling item demonstrates that your records are reliable and well-maintained. This can significantly reduce the scope and duration of the audit.
