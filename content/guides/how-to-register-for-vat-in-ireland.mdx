To register for VAT in Ireland, you submit a TR1 form (sole trader) or TR2 form (company) to the Revenue Commissioners online via ROS. Registration is mandatory once your turnover exceeds EUR 80,000 for goods or EUR 40,000 for services in any continuous 12-month period.

Even if you fall below these thresholds, voluntary VAT registration can work in your favour by allowing you to reclaim VAT on business purchases. This comprehensive guide walks you through every aspect of VAT in Ireland, from determining whether you must register to filing your first VAT3 return, handling intra-EU trade, and avoiding the most common mistakes that trigger Revenue audits.

## Who Must Register for VAT in Ireland

You must register for VAT in Ireland if your turnover from taxable supplies exceeds, or is likely to exceed, the relevant threshold in any continuous 12-month period. Registration is mandatory once you breach the threshold. There is no grace period, and Revenue expects you to register before exceeding the limit if it is clear you will do so.

The obligation applies to all business structures operating in the Republic of Ireland, including sole traders, partnerships, and limited companies. Whether you sell goods, provide services, or do both, the same rules apply. The only difference is which threshold figure is relevant to your particular business activity.

Certain types of supply trigger a registration obligation regardless of turnover. If you receive services from a supplier based in another EU member state that are subject to the reverse charge mechanism, you must register for VAT in Ireland even if your domestic turnover is zero. Similarly, if you make intra-Community acquisitions of goods exceeding EUR 41,000 in a calendar year, you must register.

Non-established traders (businesses based outside Ireland that make taxable supplies in the State) must also register for Irish VAT, with no threshold exemption. This applies to overseas businesses selling goods or services directly to Irish consumers.

## Current VAT Registration Thresholds

The VAT registration thresholds in Ireland are set by the Finance Act and reviewed periodically. The current thresholds, which have been in place since 2008 for goods and 2006 for services, are as follows:

- **Goods: EUR 80,000** in turnover over any continuous 12-month period
- **Services: EUR 40,000** in turnover over any continuous 12-month period
- **Intra-Community acquisitions: EUR 41,000** in a calendar year
- **Distance selling into Ireland: EUR 10,000** (cross-EU OSS threshold)

If your business supplies both goods and services, the lower threshold of EUR 40,000 applies unless at least 90 per cent of your turnover comes from the supply of goods. Revenue takes a strict view on this 90 per cent test, so if you have any doubt about which threshold applies, seek professional advice.

These thresholds apply to your total taxable turnover, not your profit. Revenue considers the full value of your sales when determining whether you have exceeded the limit. Exempt supplies, such as financial services and medical services, are excluded from the calculation because they are outside the scope of VAT.

It is important to monitor your turnover continuously. You cannot simply check at the end of a calendar year. Revenue looks at any rolling 12-month window, so if your turnover in any consecutive 12-month span exceeds the threshold, you are obligated to register.

## How to Register for VAT: Step by Step

The registration process depends on your business structure. In all cases, you register through the Revenue Commissioners, and the recommended method is online via the Revenue Online Service (ROS).

### Step 1: Determine Your Registration Requirement

Before beginning the registration process, confirm whether you are required to register (mandatory) or choosing to register (voluntary). This distinction matters because voluntary registrants must demonstrate genuine business activity. Revenue may refuse voluntary registration if they believe the application is speculative or if you cannot show that you are carrying on, or intend to carry on, a taxable business.

### Step 2: Gather Your Documentation

For sole traders using the TR1 form, you will need:

- Your PPS number
- Your business name and trading address
- A description of your business activity and NACE code
- Estimated annual turnover (goods and services separately)
- The date you exceeded or expect to exceed the threshold
- Your bank account details (IBAN and BIC)
- Proof of business activity (invoices, contracts, lease agreements)

For limited companies using the TR2 form, you will additionally need:

- Your company's CRO number from the Companies Registration Office
- The company's registered office address
- Details of all directors (PPS numbers, addresses)
- The company's financial year end date
- Details of any connected companies

### Step 3: Submit via ROS

Log in to the Revenue Online Service at ros.ie using your digital certificate. Navigate to the tax registration section and select the appropriate form. ROS provides real-time validation and will flag any missing or inconsistent information before submission.

If you do not yet have a ROS digital certificate, you can apply for one on the ROS website. The process takes approximately 5 to 7 working days as Revenue posts a verification PIN to your registered address.

Alternatively, you can submit a paper TR1 or TR2 form to your local Revenue office, but this method is slower and Revenue strongly encourages online submission.

### Step 4: Await Revenue Processing

Once you submit your registration application, Revenue typically processes it within 3 to 5 working days for straightforward online applications. During busy periods, such as the start of a new tax year or following Budget changes, processing times may extend to 2 to 3 weeks.

Revenue may contact you to request additional information or clarification. Common queries relate to the nature of the business, the expected turnover figures, or the reason for voluntary registration. Responding promptly helps avoid delays.

### Step 5: Receive Your VAT Number

After approval, Revenue issues your VAT registration number. This is a unique identifier in the format IE followed by 7 digits and 1 or 2 letters (for example, IE1234567T). This number must appear on all invoices you issue. You will also receive confirmation of your VAT return filing frequency, which for most small businesses is bi-monthly.

## Irish VAT Rates Explained

Once registered, you must charge the correct VAT rate on your goods and services. Charging the wrong rate can lead to assessments and penalties during a Revenue audit. Ireland currently operates four main VAT rates:

### Standard Rate: 23%

The 23 per cent standard rate applies to most goods and services in Ireland. This is the default rate, and if a supply does not qualify for a reduced or zero rate, the standard rate applies. Examples include professional services, consultancy, most retail goods, telecommunications, and digital services.

### Reduced Rate: 13.5%

The 13.5 per cent reduced rate applies to certain goods and services, including:

- Building and construction services (labour element)
- Short-term car hire
- Repair and maintenance of immovable goods
- Certain energy products (gas and electricity for domestic use)
- Cleaning and laundry services
- Hairdressing services
- Newspapers and periodicals (print)

### Second Reduced Rate: 9%

The 9 per cent rate applies to hospitality and tourism-related services:

- Restaurant and catering services (food for consumption on the premises)
- Hotel and guest house accommodation
- Admission to cinemas, theatres, and museums
- Use of sporting facilities
- Printed newspapers and periodicals

This rate has been subject to changes over recent years, with temporary reductions during and after the COVID-19 pandemic. Always verify the current rate with Revenue or your accountant.

### Zero Rate: 0%

The zero rate means VAT is charged at 0 per cent, but crucially, the supply is still within the VAT system, meaning you can reclaim input VAT on related purchases. Zero-rated supplies include:

- Most food and drink (not restaurant meals or alcohol)
- Children's clothing and footwear
- Oral medicines
- Exports of goods outside the EU
- Books (including audiobooks from January 2024)
- Certain animal feeding stuffs and seeds

### Exempt Supplies

Some supplies are exempt from VAT entirely. Unlike zero-rated supplies, exempt supplies do not allow you to reclaim input VAT. Exempt supplies include financial services, insurance, medical services, education, and passenger transport.

Understanding the distinction between zero-rated and exempt is critical. If your business makes primarily exempt supplies, you may not be able to reclaim the VAT on your business purchases, which can significantly affect your costs.

## Voluntary VAT Registration

Even if your turnover is below the mandatory thresholds, you can choose to register for VAT voluntarily. This is common among new businesses, exporters, and B2B service providers. Voluntary registration makes sense in these situations:

**You incur significant VAT on purchases.** If you buy materials, equipment, or services that include VAT, registration allows you to reclaim that VAT as input credit. This is particularly common for businesses in the early stages that invest heavily before generating revenue. For example, a new restaurant fitting out premises might spend EUR 200,000 on equipment and fit-out, paying EUR 46,000 in VAT. Registering voluntarily would allow the business to reclaim some or all of this VAT.

**You sell primarily to VAT-registered businesses.** Your customers can reclaim the VAT you charge as input VAT, so it does not affect their costs. Being VAT-registered also makes your business appear more established and professional.

**You trade within the EU.** An Irish VAT number is required for intra-Community trade. Without one, you cannot use the VIES (VAT Information Exchange System) and you cannot apply the zero rate to intra-Community supplies of goods.

**You want to claim back VAT on startup costs.** If you registered for VAT within 6 months of incurring startup costs, you may be able to reclaim VAT on goods purchased up to 4 years before registration and services purchased up to 6 months before registration, subject to certain conditions.

However, voluntary registration also means you must charge VAT on all your taxable sales, file regular VAT returns, maintain detailed records, and face the administrative burden of VAT compliance. If most of your customers are private consumers who cannot reclaim VAT, adding 23 per cent to your prices could make you less competitive.

## VAT Returns: Filing and Payment

After registration, your primary ongoing obligation is to file VAT returns and pay any VAT due. The standard filing frequency for Irish businesses is bi-monthly, meaning you file six returns per year.

### VAT3 Returns

The VAT3 return is the standard VAT return filed by most Irish businesses. It covers a two-month period and requires you to report:

- **T1:** Total VAT charged on your sales (output VAT)
- **T2:** Total VAT you paid on your purchases (input VAT)
- **T3:** The difference (T1 minus T2). If positive, you owe VAT to Revenue. If negative, you are due a refund.
- **T4:** Total sales at each VAT rate
- **E1/E2:** Intra-Community supplies and acquisitions

### Filing Deadlines

VAT3 returns are due by the 19th of the month following the end of each bi-monthly period. If you file and pay through ROS, you receive an automatic extension to the 23rd of that month. The bi-monthly periods are:

| Period | Covers | Due Date (ROS) |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| January/February | Jan 1 - Feb 28/29 | March 23 |
| March/April | Mar 1 - Apr 30 | May 23 |
| May/June | May 1 - Jun 30 | July 23 |
| July/August | Jul 1 - Aug 31 | September 23 |
| September/October | Sep 1 - Oct 31 | November 23 |
| November/December | Nov 1 - Dec 31 | January 23 |

### Annual Return of Trading Details (RTD)

In addition to your bi-monthly VAT3 returns, you must file an annual RTD. This return summarises your total supplies and purchases for the year, broken down by VAT rate. The RTD is due by the 23rd day of the month following your annual accounting period end.

### Payment Methods

VAT can be paid via ROS using direct debit, single debit instruction, or debit/credit card. Revenue encourages direct debit as it ensures timely payment and reduces the risk of late payment surcharges.

## The Reverse Charge Mechanism

The reverse charge mechanism is a critical concept for Irish businesses that buy services from suppliers in other EU countries or from outside the EU. Under the reverse charge, the customer (rather than the supplier) accounts for the VAT on the transaction.

If you receive services from an EU-based supplier that fall under the B2B general rule in Article 44 of the VAT Directive, you must self-account for Irish VAT on those services. You include the VAT on your VAT3 return as both output VAT (T1) and input VAT (T2), resulting in a net-zero effect if you are fully taxable.

Common examples include software subscriptions from EU providers, consulting services from UK firms (post-Brexit, treated as third country), and digital marketing services from platforms based in other EU states.

The reverse charge also applies to certain domestic supplies, including construction services under the Construction Industry Reverse Charge and supplies of gas and electricity by non-established traders.

## Intra-EU Trade and VIES

If you supply goods to VAT-registered businesses in other EU member states, you can zero-rate those supplies provided certain conditions are met. You must:

1. Verify the customer's VAT number using the VIES (VAT Information Exchange System) at ec.europa.eu/taxation_customs/vies
2. Include the customer's VAT number on your invoice
3. Ensure the goods physically leave Ireland
4. Report the supply on your VAT3 return and your VIES return

Failure to verify the customer's VAT number or to properly document the movement of goods can result in Revenue denying the zero rate and assessing you for Irish VAT on the full value of the supply.

### Post-Brexit: Trade with the United Kingdom

Since 1 January 2021, the United Kingdom (excluding Northern Ireland) is treated as a third country for VAT purposes. Goods exported to Great Britain are zero-rated as exports. Goods imported from Great Britain are subject to Irish VAT at the point of import.

Northern Ireland retains its position within the EU VAT system for goods under the Northern Ireland Protocol. Services supplied to Northern Ireland businesses are treated the same as services to any other UK business (third country rules apply).

## VAT Invoice Requirements

Once registered for VAT, every invoice you issue must contain specific information required by Irish VAT legislation. A compliant VAT invoice must include:

- Your business name and address
- Your VAT registration number
- The customer's name and address
- A unique sequential invoice number
- The date of issue
- The date of supply (if different from the invoice date)
- A description of the goods or services supplied
- The quantity and unit price (exclusive of VAT)
- The VAT rate applied to each item
- The total VAT amount
- The total amount payable (inclusive of VAT)

For supplies to other EU businesses, you must also include the customer's VAT number. For simplified invoices (under EUR 100), reduced requirements apply, but it is good practice to include all information on every invoice.

## Common VAT Registration Mistakes

Having helped Irish businesses with VAT for many years, certain mistakes appear repeatedly. Avoiding these can save you time, money, and stress:

**Late registration.** If you exceed the threshold and fail to register promptly, Revenue can backdate your registration. You will owe VAT on all taxable sales from the date you should have registered, plus interest at 0.0274 per cent per day and potential penalties.

**Wrong threshold applied.** Businesses that supply both goods and services sometimes apply the higher EUR 80,000 threshold when the EUR 40,000 threshold is the correct one. The 90 per cent goods test is strictly applied.

**Not reclaiming input VAT.** Some newly registered businesses forget to reclaim VAT on their purchases, effectively overpaying. Always ensure you claim all allowable input VAT on your returns.

**Claiming VAT on non-deductible expenses.** VAT on entertainment, food and drink (unless you are in the hospitality business), and certain motor expenses cannot be reclaimed. Claiming these incorrectly can trigger penalties.

**Missing the RTD.** Many businesses file their bi-monthly VAT3 returns on time but forget the annual Return of Trading Details. Late filing of the RTD results in automatic penalties.

**Incorrect treatment of exempt supplies.** If your business makes both taxable and exempt supplies, you may need to apportion your input VAT using a special method agreed with Revenue. Getting this wrong can lead to over-claiming or under-claiming.

**Not reporting reverse charge transactions.** Failing to self-account for VAT on services received from abroad is a common compliance gap that Revenue auditors specifically look for.

## What Happens If You Do Not Comply

Revenue takes VAT compliance seriously. Non-compliance can result in:

- **Interest on late payments:** 0.0274 per cent per day (approximately 10 per cent per annum)
- **Fixed penalties:** EUR 4,000 for failure to register; EUR 4,000 for failure to file a return
- **Tax-geared penalties:** Up to 100 per cent of the underpaid tax in cases of deliberate default
- **Publication:** Revenue publishes a quarterly list of tax defaulters who have settled for amounts exceeding EUR 50,000
- **Criminal prosecution:** In serious cases of fraud or evasion

Revenue conducts both desk audits (reviewing your returns and records remotely) and field audits (visiting your premises). Newly registered businesses are frequently selected for early compliance checks within the first 12 to 18 months of registration.

The best defence against penalties is to maintain accurate records, file on time, and seek professional advice when you are unsure about a VAT treatment.

## Summary

VAT registration in Ireland is straightforward once you understand the thresholds and forms involved. Sole traders use the TR1 form, limited companies use the TR2 form, and both can register online through the Revenue Online Service (ROS). Revenue typically processes online applications within 3 to 5 working days.

After registration, you must charge the correct VAT rate (23 per cent standard, 13.5 per cent reduced, 9 per cent hospitality, or 0 per cent zero-rated), file bi-monthly VAT3 returns, maintain detailed records for at least 6 years, and file an annual Return of Trading Details.

If you trade with businesses in other EU member states, ensure you understand the reverse charge mechanism and VIES requirements. Post-Brexit, treat Great Britain as a third country for VAT purposes on goods.

Whether VAT registration is mandatory or voluntary for your business, compliance is essential. Late registration, incorrect returns, and poor record keeping are the most common triggers for Revenue audits and penalties. When in doubt, speak with a qualified accountant who specialises in Irish VAT to ensure your business is fully compliant from day one.
