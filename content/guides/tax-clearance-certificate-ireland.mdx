A tax clearance certificate is an electronic confirmation from Revenue that your tax affairs are in order. You apply for one through the Revenue Online Service (ROS) using the eTax Clearance system, and if all your tax returns have been filed and you have no outstanding liabilities, the certificate is usually issued immediately or within a few working days.

Tax clearance is required in a wide range of situations in Ireland, from tendering for government contracts to renewing certain licences. Many business owners encounter the requirement for the first time when they apply for a public sector contract or a Local Enterprise Office (LEO) grant and discover they need to produce evidence of tax compliance. This guide explains who needs a tax clearance certificate, how to apply, what can go wrong, and how to resolve issues if your application is refused.

## What Is a Tax Clearance Certificate?

A tax clearance certificate is confirmation from the Revenue Commissioners that your tax affairs are, to the best of Revenue's knowledge, in order at the date of issue. It means that all required tax returns have been filed and all tax liabilities have been paid or satisfactorily arranged (for example, through an agreed phased payment arrangement).

Since 2016, Ireland operates an electronic tax clearance (eTax Clearance) system. There is no physical certificate. Instead, you receive a Tax Clearance Access Number (TCAN), which you provide to the requesting party (a government department, a licensing authority, or a grant body). They use this number along with your tax reference number to verify your tax clearance status directly through Revenue's online verification system.

This electronic system replaced the old paper-based certificates and provides real-time verification. It means the requesting party always sees the current status of your tax clearance rather than relying on a certificate that may have been issued months ago.

## Who Needs a Tax Clearance Certificate?

Tax clearance is required in numerous situations under Irish law and administrative practice. The most common include:

### Government Contracts and Public Procurement

Any business tendering for a government or public body contract valued at EUR 10,000 or more (inclusive of VAT) must hold a valid tax clearance certificate. This applies to all government departments, local authorities, the HSE, state agencies, and other public bodies. The requirement is set out in Department of Finance Circular 43/2006 and applies to the successful bidder, not all tenderers -- though in practice many contracting authorities request evidence of tax clearance at the tender stage.

### Grants and State Funding

If you apply for a grant from a Local Enterprise Office (LEO), Enterprise Ireland, IDA Ireland, or any other state agency, you will need to provide your TCAN. This applies to feasibility grants, priming grants, business expansion grants, the Trading Online Voucher, and all other state-funded business supports. The grant body verifies your tax clearance before releasing any funds.

### Licence Renewals

Numerous licences in Ireland require tax clearance for renewal. These include:

- **Pub licences (intoxicating liquor licences):** Tax clearance is required when applying to the District Court for renewal or transfer of a liquor licence
- **Bookmaker's licences:** Required for new applications and renewals
- **Gaming licences:** Required for amusement hall and similar licences
- **Road haulage licences:** Required by the Road Safety Authority (RSA)
- **Waste collection permits:** Required by local authorities
- **Taxi licences (SPSV):** Required by the National Transport Authority for new applications

### Standards in Public Office

Holders of certain public offices, including TDs, Senators, MEPs, judges, and senior public servants, are required to provide tax clearance certificates under the Standards in Public Office Acts. This ensures that individuals in positions of public trust are tax compliant.

### Property Transactions

In certain property transactions, particularly those involving the transfer of development land or buildings subject to the reverse charge for VAT purposes, tax clearance may be relevant. Professional advisers involved in commercial property transactions often require tax clearance from all parties.

### Other Situations

Tax clearance is also required for:

- Applicants for the Employment Investment Incentive Scheme (EIIS)
- Companies seeking approval as qualifying companies under various tax relief schemes
- Businesses applying for certain Revenue approvals or reliefs

## How to Apply for Tax Clearance

### Step 1: Ensure All Tax Returns Are Filed

Before applying, verify that all your tax returns have been filed with Revenue. The most common returns that must be up to date include:

- **Income tax (Form 11):** For sole traders and self-assessed individuals
- **Corporation tax (Form CT1):** For limited companies
- **VAT (VAT3 returns):** If VAT-registered
- **PAYE/PRSI (P30/P35):** If you have employees
- **Relevant Contracts Tax (RCT):** If operating in the construction sector
- **Annual Return of Trading Details (RTD):** Required alongside VAT registration

Even one outstanding return will prevent tax clearance from being granted. This is the most common reason for refusal or delay.

### Step 2: Pay All Outstanding Liabilities

All tax liabilities must be paid in full. If you have an outstanding balance with Revenue, it must be cleared before tax clearance will be issued. If you cannot pay the full amount immediately, contact Revenue's Collector General to discuss a phased payment arrangement. Tax clearance can be granted where a payment arrangement is in place and being honoured.

### Step 3: Apply Through ROS

Log in to the Revenue Online Service at ros.ie using your digital certificate. Navigate to the eTax Clearance section and submit your application. The system checks your tax records in real time and, if everything is in order, issues your Tax Clearance Access Number (TCAN) immediately.

To find the eTax Clearance application in ROS:

1. Log in to ROS
2. Select "Manage Tax Clearance" from the main menu
3. Click "Apply for Tax Clearance"
4. Confirm your details and submit

### Step 4: Receive Your TCAN

If your application is successful, you receive a Tax Clearance Access Number (TCAN) on screen. This is a unique alphanumeric code that you provide to any party that needs to verify your tax clearance. They combine your TCAN with your tax reference number (PPS number for individuals, company tax reference for companies) to verify your status online.

The TCAN does not expire on a fixed date. Instead, it remains valid as long as your tax affairs remain in order. Revenue continuously monitors compliance, and your tax clearance status can change if you fall behind on returns or payments. The requesting party always sees the real-time status when they verify.

## How Long Does It Take?

For straightforward cases where all returns are filed and all liabilities are paid, the eTax Clearance system processes applications instantly. You receive your TCAN immediately upon submission through ROS.

If there are issues with your tax records, the processing time depends on the nature of the issue:

- **Missing returns:** You need to file the outstanding returns first. Once filed and processed by Revenue, you can reapply for tax clearance.
- **Outstanding liabilities:** You need to pay the balance or agree a phased payment arrangement with Revenue.
- **Queries from Revenue:** If Revenue needs additional information or clarification, they will contact you. Response times depend on your promptness in providing the requested information and Revenue's processing capacity.
- **Appeals or disputes:** If you are disputing a tax assessment, tax clearance may be withheld until the dispute is resolved, unless Revenue exercises discretion.

As a general rule, allow at least 2 to 4 weeks before you need the certificate to deal with any unexpected issues. If you are tendering for a government contract or applying for a grant, do not leave the tax clearance application to the last minute.

## Common Reasons for Refusal

The most common reasons for tax clearance being refused or delayed are:

**Outstanding tax returns.** This is by far the most frequent issue. Even if you owe no tax, failing to file a return on time will block tax clearance. Common culprits include unfiled annual RTDs, late P35s (employer's annual return), or missing VAT returns for dormant periods.

**Outstanding tax liabilities.** Any unpaid tax, interest, or penalties will prevent clearance. This includes balances arising from Revenue assessments, audit settlements, and surcharges for late filing.

**Incomplete registration.** If you are not registered for a tax that Revenue believes you should be (for example, VAT or employer's PAYE), this can block clearance until the registration issue is resolved.

**Connected party issues.** Revenue may refuse tax clearance if you are connected to another business or individual whose tax affairs are not in order. This can apply to company directors, shareholders, and related entities.

**Ongoing Revenue audit or investigation.** If Revenue is conducting an audit or investigation into your tax affairs, they may withhold tax clearance until the audit is concluded.

## What to Do If Refused

If your tax clearance application is refused, Revenue will inform you of the reason. The steps to resolve the issue depend on the specific reason:

1. **File outstanding returns immediately.** If the refusal is due to missing returns, file them through ROS as a priority. Once processed, reapply for tax clearance.
2. **Pay outstanding balances.** If you have unpaid liabilities, settle them or contact Revenue to agree a phased payment arrangement.
3. **Contact your Revenue office.** If the reason is unclear or you believe the refusal is incorrect, contact your local Revenue office or the Collector General to discuss. Errors in Revenue's records do occur, and they can be corrected.
4. **Engage your accountant.** If the issue is complex -- such as a disputed assessment or a connected party problem -- your accountant or tax adviser can liaise with Revenue on your behalf.
5. **Appeal.** If you believe the refusal is unjust, you can appeal through Revenue's internal review process or to the Tax Appeals Commission.

## Tax Clearance for Directors and Connected Persons

For limited companies applying for tax clearance, Revenue may also check the personal tax affairs of directors and significant shareholders (those holding more than 15 per cent of the company). If a director's personal tax affairs are not in order, this can prevent the company from obtaining tax clearance.

This means that if you are a company director applying for a government contract, both the company's and your personal tax affairs must be compliant. In practice, this catches many director-managed companies where the director has outstanding personal returns or liabilities.

Similarly, if you are a sole trader and also a director of a limited company, issues with either your personal or company tax affairs can affect tax clearance for both.

## Verifying Someone Else's Tax Clearance

If you are a government body, grant agency, licensing authority, or contracting party that needs to verify someone's tax clearance, you can do so online through Revenue's eTax Clearance verification system. You need the applicant's TCAN and their tax reference number (PPS number or company registration number).

The verification system returns a simple confirmation of whether the person or entity currently has tax clearance. It does not provide any details about their tax affairs -- only the clearance status.

## Maintaining Your Tax Clearance

Tax clearance is not a one-time event. Because the system operates in real time, your clearance status can change at any point. To maintain continuous tax clearance:

- File all tax returns on time, every time
- Pay all tax liabilities by the due date
- Respond promptly to any Revenue correspondence
- Keep your contact details and registration information up to date with Revenue
- If you cannot pay a liability on time, contact Revenue proactively to arrange a phased payment plan before the deadline passes

For businesses that regularly tender for government contracts or rely on state grants, maintaining continuous tax clearance should be a core part of your financial administration. A lapsed clearance at the wrong time can cost you a contract or delay critical funding.
