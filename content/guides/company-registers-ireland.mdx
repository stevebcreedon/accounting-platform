Every company registered in Ireland must maintain certain statutory registers under the Companies Act 2014. These registers record essential information about the company's ownership, management, and activities. They must be kept up to date, stored at the company's registered office (or another notified location), and made available for inspection. Failure to maintain these registers is a criminal offence and can result in fines for the company and its officers.

This guide explains which registers you must keep, what information each register contains, where they must be stored, who can inspect them, and the consequences of non-compliance.

## Why Statutory Registers Matter

Statutory registers serve several purposes in Irish company law. They provide a clear record of who owns and controls the company, which is essential for transparency and corporate governance. They allow members (shareholders), creditors, and the public to verify key information about the company. They also provide evidence of corporate decisions and changes over the life of the company.

Revenue, the CRO, the Director of Corporate Enforcement, and other regulatory bodies may request to inspect your registers. Having them in order is a basic requirement of running a compliant Irish company.

## Register of Members

The register of members is the most fundamental of the statutory registers. It records the details of every person who holds shares in the company.

### Required Information

For each member, the register must include:

- **Full name and address** of the member
- **Date of becoming a member** (date on which they were entered on the register)
- **Date of ceasing to be a member** (if applicable)
- **Number and class of shares held** by each member
- **Amount paid or agreed to be paid** on the shares of each member
- **Details of share transfers** (including the date of registration of each transfer)

### Joint Holders

Where shares are held jointly, all joint holders must be named in the register. The company typically communicates with the first-named joint holder for matters such as dividend payments and meeting notices.

### Updating the Register

The register of members must be updated promptly when:

- New shares are allotted
- Shares are transferred from one person to another
- A member's details change (name change, change of address)
- A member ceases to hold shares

A company cannot refuse to register a valid share transfer unless the company's constitution provides specific grounds for refusal. The registration of a transfer must be completed within two months of the transfer document being lodged with the company.

### Location and Inspection

The register of members must be kept at the company's registered office or at another address within Ireland that has been notified to the CRO. Any member of the company can inspect the register free of charge. Any other person can inspect the register for a fee not exceeding EUR 6.35.

## Register of Directors and Secretaries

Every Irish company must maintain a register of its directors and company secretary. This register must be kept up to date whenever there are changes to the officeholders.

### Required Information for Directors

For each director, the register must include:

- **Full name** (and any former name)
- **Date of birth**
- **Residential address** (or an alternative address, subject to certain conditions introduced by the Companies (Corporate Governance, Enforcement and Regulatory Provisions) Act)
- **Nationality**
- **Business occupation** (if any)
- **Other directorships** held in Irish companies (past and present within the previous five years)
- **Date of appointment**
- **Date of cessation** (if applicable)

### Required Information for Company Secretary

For the company secretary, the register must include:

- **Full name** (and any former name)
- **Residential address**
- **Date of appointment**
- **Date of cessation** (if applicable)

If the secretary is a body corporate, the register must include the corporate name and registered office address.

### Shadow Directors

If a person acts as a shadow director (someone in accordance with whose directions or instructions the directors are accustomed to act), they must also be included in the register. Shadow directorship has important legal implications, including personal liability for the company's obligations in certain circumstances.

## Register of Directors' and Secretaries' Interests

The Companies Act 2014 requires directors and secretaries to disclose their interests in shares or debentures of the company or any related company. The company must maintain a register of these disclosed interests.

### What Must Be Disclosed

Directors and secretaries must notify the company of:

- Any shares or debentures they hold in the company or a related company
- Any right to subscribe for or convert securities into shares
- Any contract to acquire shares
- The interests of their spouse, civil partner, or children under 18 in shares of the company

The notification must be made within five days of the relevant event (acquisition, disposal, or other change in interest).

### Register Contents

The register of interests must record:

- The name of the director or secretary
- The nature and extent of the interest
- The date of the notification
- The date on which the interest arose or changed

## Minutes Books

While not technically a "register," every Irish company must maintain minutes of all proceedings at:

### Board Meetings

Minutes of directors' meetings must record:

- The date, time, and location of the meeting
- The names of directors present and absent
- All resolutions passed
- The substance of discussions on material matters
- Any declarations of interest made by directors

Minutes must be prepared and approved at the next meeting. Once approved and signed by the chairperson, they constitute evidence of the proceedings.

### General Meetings

Minutes of general meetings (annual general meetings and extraordinary general meetings) must record:

- The date, time, and location of the meeting
- The number of members present (in person and by proxy)
- All resolutions put to the meeting and whether they were passed or rejected
- The results of any polls
- Any other business transacted

Members have the right to inspect the minutes of general meetings. The minutes of board meetings are available for inspection by directors only.

### Written Resolutions

Where decisions are made by written resolution instead of at a meeting, the company must retain the resolution and a record of the signatures. Written resolutions must be maintained in the same manner as minutes.

## Register of Debenture Holders

If the company has issued debentures (a form of debt instrument), it must maintain a register of debenture holders. This register records:

- The name and address of each debenture holder
- The amount of debentures held
- The date of issue
- Any transfers of debentures

Many small companies do not issue debentures, in which case this register is not required.

## Register of Charges

The company must maintain a register of charges (also called a register of mortgages). This records details of any charges or security interests granted by the company over its assets, including:

- A description of the property charged
- The amount of the charge
- The person entitled to the charge
- The date of creation

Charges must also be registered with the CRO within 21 days of creation. Failure to register a charge with the CRO renders it void against a liquidator or creditor of the company.

## Common Seal Register

If the company has a common seal (not mandatory under the Companies Act 2014 but still used by many companies), it should maintain a register recording each occasion on which the seal is used, including:

- The date of use
- The document to which the seal was affixed
- The signatures of the persons who witnessed the sealing

## Where Registers Must Be Kept

All statutory registers must be kept at the company's **registered office** or at another location within Ireland. If registers are kept at a location other than the registered office, the company must notify the CRO of that location using the appropriate form.

Registers must be available for inspection during business hours. If you move your registered office, ensure the registers move with it (or update the CRO notification accordingly).

## Inspection Rights

Different registers have different inspection rights:

| Register | Members | Public |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Register of members | Free of charge | Fee up to EUR 6.35 |
| Register of directors and secretaries | Free of charge | Free of charge |
| Register of directors' interests | Free of charge | Free of charge |
| Minutes of general meetings | Free of charge | Not available |
| Minutes of board meetings | Directors only | Not available |
| Register of debenture holders | Free of charge | Fee up to EUR 6.35 |
| Register of charges | Free of charge | Free of charge |

A person who is refused inspection or is not provided with a copy within 10 days of a proper request can apply to the court for an order directing the company to comply.

## Electronic Register Keeping

The Companies Act 2014 permits registers to be maintained in electronic form, provided:

- The information can be reproduced in legible form
- The electronic records are adequately backed up and protected against loss, damage, and unauthorised access
- The register can be made available for inspection in a readable format

Most companies now maintain their registers electronically, whether in dedicated company secretarial software, spreadsheets, or document management systems. Regardless of format, the information must be complete, accurate, and promptly updated.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Failure to maintain statutory registers is a **category 3 offence** under the Companies Act 2014. This means:

- On summary conviction: a fine of up to **EUR 5,000**
- On conviction on indictment: a fine of up to **EUR 50,000**

The offence applies to the company and to every officer of the company who is in default. This means directors and the company secretary can be personally liable for failing to maintain registers.

In addition to fines, failure to maintain proper registers can:

- Complicate share transfers and corporate transactions
- Create difficulties during due diligence if the company is being sold or is seeking investment
- Lead to disputes about ownership and control
- Result in adverse findings during a CRO inspection or Director of Corporate Enforcement investigation

## Practical Tips for Maintaining Registers

**Set up registers at incorporation.** Establish all required registers when the company is first incorporated. Do not leave it until later.

**Update promptly.** Changes to directors, shareholders, and other registerable matters should be recorded as they happen, not months later when you are preparing the annual return.

**Use a company secretary service.** Many Irish accountancy firms and company secretarial service providers will maintain your statutory registers as part of their annual compliance service. This ensures registers are kept up to date and in the correct format.

**Keep digital and physical copies.** While electronic registers are permitted, consider maintaining backup copies. Ensure electronic records are regularly backed up and stored securely.

**Review annually.** Before filing your CRO annual return, review all registers to ensure they are consistent with the information being filed on the B1 form.

<FAQItem question="What statutory registers must an Irish company keep?">
At minimum, an Irish company must maintain a register of members (shareholders), register of directors and secretaries, register of directors' and secretaries' interests in shares, minutes books for board and general meetings, and a register of charges. If the company has issued debentures, a register of debenture holders is also required. These are mandatory under the Companies Act 2014.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="Can I keep company registers electronically?">
Yes. The Companies Act 2014 permits statutory registers to be maintained in electronic form, provided the information can be reproduced in legible form, is adequately backed up, and can be made available for inspection in a readable format. Most companies now maintain their registers using software or spreadsheets.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="Who can inspect a company's registers?">
Members (shareholders) can inspect the register of members free of charge. The register of directors and secretaries and the register of charges are open to public inspection free of charge. Minutes of general meetings can be inspected by members, while board meeting minutes are restricted to directors. Non-members can inspect the register of members for a small fee.
</FAQItem>
<FAQItem question="What happens if I do not maintain my company's registers?">
Failure to maintain statutory registers is a criminal offence under the Companies Act 2014. The company and its officers (directors and company secretary) can be fined up to EUR 5,000 on summary conviction or EUR 50,000 on conviction on indictment. It can also complicate share transfers, corporate transactions, and due diligence processes.
</FAQItem>
