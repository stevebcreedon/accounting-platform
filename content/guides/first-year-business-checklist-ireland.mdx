Your first year in business in Ireland involves a defined set of registrations, tax filings, and compliance milestones that you need to complete on time to avoid penalties. The critical items are registering with Revenue (TR1 or TR2 form), filing your first VAT returns if registered, making your first preliminary tax payment by 31 October, and filing your first Form 11 or CT1 tax return.

This guide provides a month-by-month walkthrough of what you need to do in your first 12 months of business in Ireland. It covers the tasks most new business owners need to complete, from the initial registrations through to your first tax return. Some items will apply to every business, while others depend on your structure (sole trader or limited company), whether you have employees, and whether you are VAT-registered. Use this as a working checklist and tick off each item as you complete it.

## Before You Start Trading

Before you make your first sale or issue your first invoice, complete these foundational tasks.

### Register with Revenue

Submit your TR1 form (sole traders) or TR2 form (limited companies) through the Revenue Online Service (ROS). This registers you for income tax or corporation tax, and optionally for VAT and employer's PAYE if applicable. Processing takes 5 to 10 working days. You cannot legally trade without being registered with Revenue.

### Register Your Business Name (If Applicable)

If you are trading under a name other than your own legal name (sole traders) or your registered company name (limited companies), register the business name with the CRO using the RBN1 form on the CORE system. The filing fee is EUR 20 online.

### Open a Business Bank Account

Choose a bank and open a dedicated business account. Keep your personal and business finances separate from day one. This makes bookkeeping, tax returns, and potential Revenue audits vastly simpler.

### Set Up Your Record-Keeping System

Choose how you will track your income and expenses. Options range from a simple spreadsheet to cloud accounting software such as Sage, Xero, or QuickBooks. Whatever you choose, set it up before you start trading so you capture every transaction from the beginning.

### Arrange Business Insurance

At minimum, consider public liability insurance. If you have employees, employer's liability insurance is legally required. Professional services providers should consider professional indemnity insurance. Get quotes from a broker or directly from insurers such as Allianz, Zurich, or AXA.

### Check If You Need Any Licences or Permits

Depending on your industry, you may need specific licences or permits before you can legally trade. Common examples include food business registration with the HSE, a pub or restaurant licence, a waste collection permit, a taxi (SPSV) licence, or a building trade registration. Check with your local authority and relevant regulatory body.

## Month 1-3: Getting Established

### Issue Compliant Invoices

Every invoice you issue must include your name and address, the customer's name and address, a unique sequential invoice number, the date, a description of the goods or services, the amount due, and your VAT number and VAT amounts if VAT-registered. Keep copies of all invoices issued.

### Collect and Store Receipts

Keep a receipt for every business expense from the very first day. Revenue requires you to retain receipts and records for 6 years. Digital copies are acceptable -- many business owners photograph receipts immediately and store them in the cloud or their accounting software.

### Monitor Your VAT Position

If you are not yet VAT-registered, track your cumulative turnover. You must register for VAT if your turnover exceeds EUR 80,000 for goods or EUR 40,000 for services in any continuous 12-month period. If you expect to breach these thresholds, register proactively rather than waiting until you exceed them.

### File Your First VAT Return (If VAT-Registered)

If you registered for VAT, your first VAT3 return covers the two-month period in which you registered. The return is due by the 19th of the month following the end of that period (23rd if filing via ROS). For example, if you registered in January, your first return covers January/February and is due by 23 March via ROS.

### Set Up a Tax Savings Routine

Open a separate savings account and transfer a percentage of every payment you receive -- typically 25 to 35 per cent -- into it. This money covers your income tax, USC, PRSI, and VAT obligations. New business owners who do not save for tax are often caught out by their first preliminary tax bill in October.

### Explore LEO Supports

Contact your local Local Enterprise Office early. They offer free training programmes, mentoring, and information about grants you may be eligible for. The Start Your Own Business programme, the Trading Online Voucher, and mentor assignments are all available from the early stages.

## Month 4-6: Building Momentum

### Review Your Financial Position

After 3 to 6 months of trading, review your actual performance against your business plan projections. Are revenues on track? Are your costs where you expected them? This is the time to adjust your pricing, marketing, or cost structure if things are not going to plan.

### Consider Registering for VAT Voluntarily

If you are below the mandatory VAT thresholds but are spending significantly on VAT-inclusive purchases, voluntary VAT registration may save you money by allowing you to reclaim input VAT. Discuss this with your accountant.

### File Ongoing VAT Returns

If VAT-registered, continue filing your bi-monthly VAT3 returns on time. Late filing results in automatic interest charges and can affect your tax clearance status. Set calendar reminders for each filing deadline.

### Submit PAYE Returns (If You Have Employees)

If you have hired employees, you must operate PAYE in real time through Revenue's Enhanced Reporting Requirements (ERR). Each time you pay an employee, you submit a payroll submission to Revenue reflecting the tax, USC, PRSI, and Local Property Tax (LPT) deducted. Monthly employer returns are also required.

### Review Your Insurance

After a few months of trading, review whether your insurance cover is still appropriate. Has your turnover or headcount changed? Are you providing services you did not originally plan for? Adjusting your cover mid-year is better than discovering a gap after an incident.

## Month 7-9: Preparing for Tax Season

### Understand Your Preliminary Tax Obligation

If your first tax year straddles a 31 October deadline, you may need to pay preliminary tax for the current year. For sole traders in their first year, preliminary tax rules can be complex. If you started trading on or after 1 January, your first preliminary tax payment is due on 31 October of that year (or mid-November via ROS).

Preliminary tax must be at least 90 per cent of your current year liability or 100 per cent of your prior year liability. Since you have no prior year liability in your first year, you must estimate your current year's tax. Discuss this with your accountant to avoid underpayment penalties.

### Start Preparing Your Accounts

Do not wait until the filing deadline to organise your records. Reconcile your bank statements, ensure all invoices and receipts are recorded, and address any gaps or queries while the transactions are still fresh in your memory.

### Review Expenses You May Have Missed

Common deductible expenses that new business owners often overlook:

- **Home office expenses:** If you work from home, you can claim a proportion of your heating, electricity, broadband, and insurance based on the area used for business
- **Motor expenses:** Keep a mileage log for business travel. You can claim either actual costs (fuel, insurance, tax, maintenance) proportioned to business use, or the civil service mileage rate
- **Professional development:** Training courses, books, and conferences related to your business are deductible
- **Professional fees:** Accountant fees, legal fees, and consulting fees
- **Pre-trading expenses:** Certain expenses incurred up to 3 years before you started trading can be claimed as if they were incurred on day one of trading

### Engage an Accountant

If you have not already, this is the time to engage an accountant to prepare your first tax return. Finding an accountant in October when the filing deadline is weeks away is stressful and more expensive. Choose an accountant who specialises in small businesses and understands your industry.

## Month 10-12: First Tax Deadlines

### 31 October (Mid-November via ROS): The Pay-and-File Deadline

This is the most important date in the Irish tax calendar for self-employed individuals. On this date (extended to approximately 14 November for ROS filers who pay and file online), three things are due simultaneously:

1. **Form 11 tax return** for the preceding tax year (if you traded during the prior year)
2. **Balance of tax** due for the preceding year (the difference between your final liability and any preliminary tax already paid)
3. **Preliminary tax** for the current tax year

For a business that started on 1 January 2026, by 31 October 2026 (or mid-November via ROS) you must pay preliminary tax for 2026. You will file your actual Form 11 for 2026 by 31 October 2027.

### Late Filing Consequences

Missing the pay-and-file deadline triggers:

- **Surcharge on the return:** 5 per cent of your tax liability if filed within 2 months of the deadline, 10 per cent if later (capped at specific maximums)
- **Interest on late payment:** 0.0274 per cent per day (approximately 10 per cent per annum) on any unpaid tax
- **Restriction of tax credits:** Your personal tax credits may be restricted if returns are consistently late
- **Loss of tax clearance:** Outstanding returns or liabilities will prevent you from obtaining a tax clearance certificate

### File Your Annual Return of Trading Details (RTD)

If you are VAT-registered, you must file an RTD summarising your total supplies and purchases for the year, broken down by VAT rate. This is due by the 23rd of the month following your accounting period end. Many businesses forget this return, and late filing results in automatic penalties.

### Company Annual Return (Limited Companies Only)

If you are operating as a limited company, you must file an annual return with the CRO within 56 days of your Annual Return Date (ARD). The first ARD is typically 6 months after incorporation. Late filing results in penalties and the loss of audit exemption for that year and the following year. The CRO filing fee is EUR 20 online.

## End of Year Review

### Evaluate Your First Year

At the end of your first year, conduct a thorough review:

- **Revenue vs projection:** How did actual revenue compare to your business plan?
- **Profitability:** Are you profitable? If not, when do you expect to reach break-even?
- **Cash flow:** Do you have enough cash reserves to cover the next quarter's expenses plus your upcoming tax payments?
- **Customer base:** How many customers do you have? What is the customer acquisition cost? What is the retention rate?
- **Operations:** What processes are working well? What needs improvement?

### Plan for Year Two

Based on your first-year performance, update your business plan and financial projections for the next 12 months. Consider:

- Whether your pricing needs adjustment
- Whether you need to hire your first employee (or additional staff)
- Whether it is time to move from home to commercial premises
- Whether your business structure is still appropriate (sole trader considering incorporation)
- What marketing channels delivered the best return

### Common First-Year Mistakes

**Not saving for tax.** The number one mistake. Your first preliminary tax payment can be a shock if you have not been setting money aside throughout the year. Save 25 to 35 per cent of every payment you receive.

**Poor record keeping.** Shoebox accounting -- throwing receipts in a box and hoping your accountant can sort it out in October -- is expensive and risky. It increases your accountant's fees and the chance of errors on your return.

**Not separating personal and business finances.** Even if you are a sole trader with no legal obligation to maintain a separate account, mixing finances creates problems with bookkeeping, tax returns, and Revenue audits.

**Underestimating costs.** Most new businesses underestimate their costs in the first year. Insurance, professional fees, marketing, and working capital needs are commonly underestimated. Build a contingency buffer into your financial plan.

**Ignoring marketing.** Many new business owners focus entirely on their product or service and neglect marketing. Consistent marketing from month one -- even if it is just building a website, creating social media profiles, and networking -- builds the pipeline you will need by month six.

**Not seeking professional advice early enough.** Engaging an accountant, a solicitor, and a LEO mentor early saves money in the long run. They can prevent costly mistakes and help you access supports you might not know about.

**Trying to do everything yourself.** Delegating tasks outside your core competence -- bookkeeping, IT, marketing -- frees you to focus on what generates revenue. Even a few hours per week of outsourced bookkeeping can make a significant difference.

- Engage an accountant by month 7 to 9, well before the October pay-and-file deadline, to avoid last-minute stress and higher fees
